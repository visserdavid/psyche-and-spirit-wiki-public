# Active Imagination and the Red Book

**Summary**: Jung's 1913–1917 "confrontation with the unconscious" — a deliberate, sustained engagement with waking fantasy that produced the *Black Books*, the calligraphed *Red Book* (*Liber Novus*), and the privately printed *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos*, and from which Jung says the whole of his later theoretical work (archetypes, individuation, the Self) was drawn.

## Origin: A Deliberate Descent

After breaking with [Freud](../thinkers/sigmund-freud.md) in 1913, Jung describes a period of acute disorientation — "suspended in mid-air" — that he resolved not by suppressing but by deliberately submitting to. He resigned his university lectureship and began, essentially as "a scientific experiment" on himself, letting fantasy images arise and recording them without prior theoretical premises. His method: return to childhood play (rebuilding block villages on the lakeshore) as a way back into direct contact with unconscious material, then track and write down the fantasies that followed.

## Publication History: A 48-Year Withholding

Jung never published the *Red Book* in his lifetime and left explicit instructions only that it and the *Black Books* "remain in the family," without further guidance. His heirs — the Society of the Heirs of C. G. Jung — treated the manuscript for decades as private, showing it to almost no one; it was placed in a bank safe-deposit box in 1983 and photographically duplicated for family use only in 1984. Historian of psychology **Sonu Shamdasani** began working with the family from the mid-1990s, and in 2000 the Society formally handed him the task of preparing it for publication. The full facsimile and a text-only "Reader's Edition" both appeared in October 2009 — 48 years after Jung's death, and 96 years after he began writing it. Shamdasani's extensive scholarly introduction (drawing on the *Black Books*, correspondence, and unpublished seminar transcripts) is itself now a primary source for reconstructing the exact chronology of Jung's 1913–1930 "confrontation," used throughout this page.

## Key Figures

**Philemon**: a winged old man with the horns of a bull and a bunch of keys, who first appeared in a dream (preceded by a vision of a kingfisher-winged figure flying across a blue sky) and then as a recurring inner interlocutor Jung met in imagined conversations in a garden. Jung treated Philemon as psychologically objective — "he said things which I had not consciously thought" — and credited him with the crucial insight that **psychic contents are not manufactured by the ego but have a life of their own**. Jung called Philemon his inner "guru," comparing the relationship to the Indian guru-chela bond; Shamdasani notes Philemon evolved out of an earlier figure, Elijah, and combined "an Egypto-Hellenic atmosphere with a Gnostic coloration."

**Salome and Elijah**: encountered together during an imagined "descent," Elijah representing intellect/knowledge (**Logos**) and the blind Salome representing the erotic (**Eros**) — Jung's earliest encounter with what he later formalized as the [anima](the-self.md) figure, accompanied by a black serpent he read as a mark of the hero-myth pattern. Jung's own contemporaneous commentary (preserved in the Red Book's Appendix B) frames the pair as one of psychology's recurring primordial images: Logos as the ordering, form-giving principle, Eros as the dissolving, form-fulfilling one — "two fundamental psychic powers form[ing] a pair of opposites, one requiring the other." He explicitly flags Salome-as-Elijah's-daughter as an *incomplete* allegory of Eros (true Eros is not simply subordinate to Logos), a self-critical move characteristic of how the book works through its own symbolism rather than simply reporting it.

**Ka**: a later, more earth-bound, "Mephistophelian" figure — a counterpart to Philemon's spiritual aspect representing chthonic, material nature.

## The Precognitive War Fantasies

Beginning in October 1913, Jung recorded roughly a dozen fantasies of catastrophic flood, seas of blood, and mass death, accompanied by an inner voice insisting they would "become real." He initially read them, fearfully, as a personal psychosis. When the First World War broke out in August 1914 — shortly after his final vision in this series, in late July — Jung and Shamdasani both treat this as the turning point that convinced him the material was not purely personal pathology but had tapped something collective: "I understood that my dreams and visions came from the subsoil of the collective unconscious... I remained inwardly uncertain no longer." Jung later told Mircea Eliade directly that the war's outbreak resolved his fear that he had been "menaced by a psychosis." Whatever one makes of the precognition claim itself (see Critique below), it is the specific episode Jung repeatedly cites as decisive evidence, to himself, that the fantasy material was archetypal/collective rather than idiosyncratic.

## Septem Sermones and the Poltergeist Episode

In 1916, Jung reports a genuinely uncanny household episode: his eldest daughter saw a white figure pass through the room, his son had a nightmare and drew a picture (unprompted) of a fisherman being told by a devil-figure to keep only the "bad fish," his second daughter dreamed of an angel removing bad spirits from the house, and the family's doorbell rang insistently with no one present. Jung describes the house as "crammed full of spirits" until, over three evenings, he wrote the *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos* — a pseudo-Gnostic text voiced as the 2nd-century teacher Basilides — after which "the haunting was over." The *Sermones* centers on **Abraxas**, a god uniting the Christian God and Satan as a single *complexio oppositorum* — the same argument Jung would only make openly, decades later, in *Answer to Job* (1952).

## A Collective, Not Solitary, Experiment

Shamdasani's introduction stresses a point easy to miss from *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* alone: Jung was not working in isolation. His companion Toni Wolff underwent a closely related process alongside him; several analysands (Tina Keller among them) report Jung showing them Red Book pages in session as evidence that this kind of inner work was a legitimate, shareable therapeutic method, not a private eccentricity; and members of Jung's circle in Zürich (including Maria Moltzer and Hans Schmid) produced comparable visionary material of their own, some of it published pseudonymously. Jung's circle also had real, if uneasy, contact with the Zürich Dada movement (several members of his Psychological Club attended early Cabaret Voltaire/Gallery Dada events) — Shamdasani frames the whole period as continuous with a wider early-20th-century experimentation across psychology, spiritualism, and the avant-garde in exploring non-rational, imagistic modes of knowing, not an episode isolated to Jung alone.

## The Black Books, the Red Book, and Septem Sermones

Jung recorded the raw fantasy material in six black leather notebooks (the *Black Books*), then transcribed and elaborated them, with his own paintings, into a large calligraphed folio (the *Red Book*, or *Liber Novus*) — an aesthetic project he ultimately abandoned unfinished, concluding that further "estheticizing" would substitute art for the harder work of understanding. In 1916, under what he describes as compulsive pressure — his house reportedly felt "haunted," his children reported apparitions — he wrote the *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos* ("Seven Sermons to the Dead") in three evenings, a pseudo-Gnostic text voiced as the second-century teacher Basilides, centered on the god Abraxas as a *complexio oppositorum* uniting the pairs of opposites (life/death, good/evil, light/dark). Jung had it privately printed and later called publishing it, even privately, "a sin of my youth" he regretted — but included an account of it in this book "for the sake of honesty."

## From Fantasy to Theory: Mandalas and the Self

Beginning around 1916, Jung started spontaneously drawing circular *mandala* images, initially without understanding their function. Over several years he came to read them as **a state-of-the-self report drawn daily** — "cryptograms concerning the state of the self" — and, by 1918–1920, as showing that individuation is not a linear achievement toward a distant goal but a **circumambulation of a center**, the [Self](the-self.md). A 1927 dream ("Liverpool — the Pool of Life") crystallized this: a radially organized city with a luminous flowering tree on a central island, which Jung read directly as an image of the Self as the point of orientation and meaning. See [Individuation](individuation.md) for the full theoretical elaboration this experience produced.

His first mandala of the series, the **Systema Munditotius** (January 1916), is an explicit cosmological diagram of opposites: the winged child-god Erikepaios/Phanes above, the dark world-creator Abraxas below, a tree of life and a seven-branched fire/Eros candelabra, art and science as a winged serpent and a winged mouse, and a horizontal split between a dark serpent-and-blood "Satanas" side and a bright dove-of-the-Holy-Ghost/Sophia side. Jung published it anonymously only in 1955, decades later, describing it (in a letter reproduced in the Red Book's appendices) as portraying "the antinomies of the microcosm and... the macrocosmic world of antinomies" — a direct visual precursor to the [privatio boni](privatio-boni.md) argument he would only make discursively in *[Aion](../sources/aion.md)*.

## Theoretical Distillation: Persona and the Danger of "Godlikeness"

Jung's 1916 lecture "The Structure of the Unconscious" (first published in French, in Flournoy's *Archives de Psychologie*) is the earliest published theoretical residue of the Red Book period, and it names a specific danger the fantasy-work exposed him to. Distinguishing the **personal unconscious** (acquired in one's own lifetime) from the **impersonal/collective unconscious** (inherited), Jung warned that a person who "annexes" contents of the collective psyche and mistakes them for personal attributes experiences extreme states of superiority or inferiority he called, borrowing the term from Goethe and Adler, **"godlikeness."** He describes only three ways out of an activated collective unconscious: regressively restoring the [persona](individuation.md) (impossible once the unconscious has been engaged); accepting the inflated, godlike state (psychologically dangerous); or — the path Jung himself took — the **hermeneutic treatment of the fantasies as creative material**, differentiating and integrating them consciously. This third path is, in miniature, the theoretical justification for the entire Red Book project, and the direct origin of the technical distinction between persona and [anima](the-self.md) that Jung formalized in this same essay.

## Method and Status: "Not Science, But Art" — Then Neither

An inner voice, which Jung attributed to the [anima](the-self.md), told him during this period that his fantasy-work was "art." Jung's considered rejection of that framing — "No, not art! On the contrary, nature" — became methodologically important: treating the material as **spontaneous psychic nature to be understood, not aesthetic material to be worked up**, is what allowed him to later derive general psychological theory from it rather than a personal artistic mythology. He describes the technique of consciously "personifying" unconscious contents (writing letters to and from figures like the anima) as a way to differentiate and relate to them without being either swamped or simply performing.

## Historical Grounding: The Turn to Alchemy

Jung reports that his fantasy material long lacked any historical precedent, leaving him unable to verify whether it reflected something universal or was simply personal. A 1926 dream of being "caught" while driving through a seventeenth-century Italian courtyard, combined with Richard Wilhelm sending him the Taoist alchemical text *The Secret of the Golden Flower* in 1928, led him into a fifteen-year study of alchemical texts, which he came to read as an independent historical record of individuation symbolism — see *[Aion](../sources/aion.md)* and *[Mysterium Coniunctionis](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md)*.

## Critique

**Psychiatric risk read as method**: Jung himself frames the 1913–1917 period as bordering on the psychotic — he explicitly worried he was suffering a psychic disturbance, and later commentators (notably Richard Noll, whose *The Jung Cult* and *The Aryan Christ* argue Jung's entire system originated in a period of severe psychological crisis he subsequently mythologized as scientific method, rather than in disciplined self-observation) have debated whether the *Red Book* material represents controlled psychological experimentation or an episode more accurately described as a self-managed psychotic break that Jung's professional standing and family/work stability allowed him to survive and later theorize. Shamdasani's own introduction pushes back with the point that Jung was not working alone or in secret from everyone: Toni Wolff underwent a comparable process, several patients report Jung showing them Red Book material as a demonstrated method, and members of his circle produced similar visionary work of their own — evidence, on this reading, of a shared clinical technique rather than a solitary breakdown. Jung's own stated view — that "the same fund of unconscious images which fatally confuse the mental patient" also underlies mythopoeic imagination generally, and that the difference lies in whether the material can be integrated and understood — is a claim about degree and outcome, not a claim that no risk was present. This wiki holds the question open rather than resolving it.

**The precognition claim**: Jung and Shamdasani both treat the pre-1914 "war fantasies" as evidence the material was collective/archetypal rather than personal — but this rests on treating the fantasies as genuinely precognitive of a specific historical event, a claim with no accepted scientific mechanism and vulnerable to the ordinary explanation that fin-de-siècle Europe (per Shamdasani's own cultural-context discussion, citing Kandinsky, Meidner, and Hugo Ball) was already saturated with apocalyptic expectation before 1914; a vision of catastrophe was a common cultural mood, not a specific prediction. This does not resolve whether the fantasies were archetypal in Jung's sense, but it weakens the specific evidentiary weight Jung himself placed on their being precognitive.

**Unverifiable, unrepeatable evidence base**: the entire theoretical apparatus built from this period (archetypes, the Self, individuation-as-circumambulation) rests on Jung's own introspective and largely unwitnessed experience. Mainstream psychology does not treat this as an empirical evidence base in the modern sense; its status here is clinical-theoretical and biographical, not experimentally validated. See the parallel caution already noted on [Individuation](individuation.md).
