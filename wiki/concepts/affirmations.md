# Affirmations

**Summary**: Positive statements made in the present tense, repeated intensively to replace limiting beliefs with supportive ones; a practice central to Louise Hay's self-healing system and appearing across multiple traditions in the wiki under different names.

**Sources**:
- [Hay, L. L. (1984). *You can heal your life*. Hay House.](../sources/you-can-heal-your-life.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Definition and Structure

An affirmation is any statement made in the present tense that describes a desired reality as if it is already true. The present tense is essential: "I am loved" rather than "I will be loved"; "Money flows to me freely" rather than "I hope to attract money." Hay holds that the mind responds to declarations in the present tense and treats them as instructions.

Affirmations work by repetition: the goal is to crowd out the habitual negative self-talk ("I'm not good enough," "I'll never have money") with its positive counter. Over time, the affirmation becomes the dominant thought, reshapes belief, and — on Hay's model — reshapes experience.

---

## Hay's System

Louise Hay treats affirmations as the primary tool for changing thought patterns that generate illness and suffering. Her core affirmation is: **"I love and approve of myself."**

Her recommendations:
- Repeat key affirmations 300–400 times a day
- Write them 10–20 times in a notebook each day
- Say them aloud while looking in a mirror (see [Mirror Work](mirror-work.md))
- Sing them — lightening the procedure makes resistance easier to meet
- Use them as a "walking mantra" during ordinary activities

Hay identifies the emergence of resistance as confirmation that the affirmation is working: if an affirmation provokes anxiety, anger, or "I can't say that — it's not true," this reveals the limiting belief that is causing the problem. The resistance is what needs attention.

She frames affirmations in three categories:
1. **Self-worth**: "I deserve to have all my needs met," "I love and approve of myself"
2. **Healing**: "Every hand that touches me is a healing hand," "My body knows how to heal itself"
3. **Prosperity and relationships**: "Money flows to me in abundance," "I attract loving, kind people"

## Relation to the Disease Index

The disease index in *You Can Heal Your Life* (Chapter 15) pairs each ailment with a "new thought pattern" — which is the affirmation prescribed to heal that condition. For example:
- Cancer: "I lovingly forgive and release all of the past. I choose to fill my world with joy."
- Arthritis: "I am love. I now choose to love and approve of myself."
- Back problems: "I trust the process of life. All I need is always taken care of."

These affirmations are the practical interface between the [thought-causes-illness](thought-causes-illness.md) framework and daily practice.

## Parallels in Other Wiki Sources

Affirmations appear across the wiki under different names and in different registers:

- **[A Course in Miracles](../sources/a-course-in-miracles.md)**: The 365 Workbook lessons are affirmations — "I am not a body. I am free," "God is the Love in which I forgive." ACIM's structure is identical to Hay's in that it provides a daily practice of repeating statements designed to shift the mind from one thought system to another.
- **[Exceptional Cancer Patients](exceptional-cancer-patients.md)** ([Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md)): Visual imagery functions as a non-verbal affirmation — the Simonton method asks patients to visualise their immune system defeating cancer cells, which is a present-tense declaration in image form.
- **[Relaxation Response](relaxation-response.md)** ([Benson](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md)): The repetition of a focus word (including secular or spiritual phrases) during relaxation practice has measurable physiological effects. Benson's work provides the closest empirical grounding for the mechanism underlying affirmation practice.
- **Prayer**: [Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md) distinguishes directed prayer (asking for a specific outcome) from non-directed (surrendering to divine will). Both share the structure of an intentional statement held in consciousness — a verbal affirmation with metaphysical intent.

## Critique

**The evidence base**: No randomised controlled trial has specifically tested Hay's affirmation method against controls. The closest relevant evidence is from Benson's relaxation response research, which demonstrates that repetitive mental focus produces measurable physiological changes (reduced cortisol, heart rate, blood pressure). This supports affirmations as a stress-reduction tool; it does not validate the thought-disease mappings in the disease index.

**Positive psychology research**: The related construct of "positive self-talk" and "self-compassion" has a moderate evidence base in psychology (Neff; Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory), suggesting that self-critical thoughts impair cognitive flexibility and performance while compassionate self-talk supports it. This is consistent with Hay's general claim without endorsing the specific disease mappings.

**The suppression risk**: Repeated affirmations can function as a form of emotional suppression if they are used to avoid genuine negative emotions rather than transform them. Hay addresses this by combining affirmations with forgiveness and anger-release work, but the risk of bypassing emotional reality through positive repetition ("toxic positivity") is a real and documented phenomenon.

**Note**: Mainstream clinical psychology does not recommend affirmations as a treatment for serious illness. Cognitive-behavioural therapy uses a related technique — cognitive restructuring — in which distorted beliefs are identified and challenged through evidence, rather than repeated until they feel true.
