# Ashtanga Yoga (Eight-Limbed Path)

**Summary**: Patanjali's classical eight-limbed Yoga system — yama, niyama, asana, pranayama, pratyahara, dharana, dhyana, samadhi — the systematic path for purifying body and mind, activating the central nadi (Sushumna), and guiding Kundalini to liberation; the structural framework underlying Johari's account of Kundalini Yoga practice.

**Sources**:
- [Johari, H. (2000). *Chakras: Energy centers of transformation* (Rev. expanded ed.). Destiny Books.](../sources/chakras-energy-centers.md)

## Overview

*Ashtanga* (Sanskrit: *asht* = eight; *anga* = limb) Yoga is the eight-part system codified by Patanjali in the *Yoga Sutras* (c. 400 CE), the foundational text of classical Yoga philosophy. [Harish Johari](../thinkers/harish-johari.md) in [*Chakras: Energy Centers of Transformation*](../sources/chakras-energy-centers.md) presents Ashtanga Yoga as the necessary practical foundation for the entire Kundalini Yoga path: without systematic ethical and physical preparation, deliberate arousal of [Kundalini](kundalini.md) is dangerous and counterproductive.

Lord Shiva is described in the Tantric tradition as expounding twelve forms of Yoga (Mantra, Hatha, Laya, Raja, Bhakti, Kriya, Gyana, Lakshya, Vasana, Para, Amanaska, Sahaj). Johari treats Ashtanga as the integrating framework — the four foundational forms (Mantra, Hatha, Laya, Raja) together constitute *Maha Yoga*. The eight limbs span the full journey from ethical conduct to liberation.

## The Eight Limbs

### 1. Yama — Ethical Restraints

Restraints governing how the practitioner relates to the outer world. The classic five yamas (non-violence/ahimsa, truth/satya, non-stealing/asteya, continence/brahmacharya, non-possessiveness/aparigraha) are expanded in the text to ten, including honesty, forbearance, fortitude, kindness, and moderation in diet. Johari frames yamas as the code of conduct that helps accomplish the remaining steps: living within yama gradually purifies the personality and reduces the expenditure of energy that would otherwise be dissipated in reactive behavior.

### 2. Niyama — Observances

Golden rules of conduct enhancing internal development: bodily purity (Shauca), contentment (Santosha), austerity (Tapas), self-study (Svadhyaya), and surrender to God (Ishvara Pranidhana). The *niyama* attitude (witnessing one's actions without attachment) begins to awaken the witness consciousness (*sakshi*) — the first step toward understanding the mind from outside its own fluctuations. Following yama and niyama together means the practitioner's energy starts working without the distortion of reactive habits.

### 3. Asana — Postures

Physical postures that train the body to be still and non-obstructive. Patanjali's requirement: a posture should be *sthira-sukha* — steady and comfortable — and sustained without discomfort for extended periods. Johari follows the Shandilyopanishad: "Sicknesses are destroyed by practising postures; poisons are assimilated." The aim is not flexibility or fitness but the training of the musculature to cease creating distractions, so that the mind can concentrate without the body pulling attention to itself. Proper asana also activates nerve centres, helps the body secrete growth hormones, and produces antibodies.

### 4. Pranayama — Breath Control

Control of prana (life force) through disciplined breathing. The three phases: *puraka* (inhalation), *kumbhaka* (retention/suspension), *rechaka* (exhalation), in a 1:4:2 ratio. Pranayama slows the rhythm of the mind (since breath and mind are interdependent), cleans the [nadi system](prana-and-nadis.md), and — when the suspension is prolonged — creates *kevali kumbhaka* (spontaneous breath suspension), at which point Ida and Pingala Nadis cease their alternation and Sushumna activates. The three bandhas (Mula, Uddiyana, Jalandhara) are employed with pranayama to lock and redirect prana into Sushumna. When properly mastered, pranayama stops the aging process and can arouse Kundalini.

Three stages of pranayama are described: (1) perspiration, (2) body shaking, (3) levitation — signs of increasingly successful activation.

### 5. Pratyahara — Withdrawal of Sensory Perceptions

The turning of the senses inward, away from their objects. As pranayama deepens, prana is withdrawn from the sense organs, which naturally follow and cease to register external signals. Pratyahara has two steps: (1) prolonged kumbhaka (breath holding), which draws the mind inward; (2) mind absorption — concentrating attention on the Self rather than on sensory input. The effect: the duality between perceiver and perceived is suspended; the mind approaches concentration (*dharana*). In Tantric practice, Pratyahara is sometimes identified with the moment Kundalini energy is drawn from Muladhara upward into Sushumna.

### 6. Dharana — Concentration

One-pointed fixing of the mind on a single object. The classical eighteen *adharas* (concentration points) include the foot, ankle, knee, thigh, anus, genitals, navel, heart, neck, throat, palate, nose, eyes, space between the eyebrows, forehead, and head — or more specifically the four central points: navel, heart, space between the eyebrows (*trikuti*), and Sahasrara. In Kundalini Yoga, dharana on the bija mantras of each chakra and on its presiding deity and yantra is the primary practice. Dharana depends on and follows asana (body stillness) and pranayama (breath suspension reduces neuromuscular activity, slowing mental fluctuations). Concentration on the chakras, done in ascending order from Muladhara, is one of the primary methods for guiding Kundalini's ascent.

### 7. Dhyana — Meditation

Uninterrupted flow of awareness toward the object of concentration — the point where dharana deepens past effort into effortlessness. When sustained concentration on a chakra, deity, or mantra finally dissolves the separation between concentrator and concentrated, dhyana arises. Two types:
- *Saguna dhyana*: meditation on a form, deity, or symbol (with attributes)
- *Nirguna dhyana*: meditation on the formless, nameless Supreme (without attributes)

In dhyana, inner dialogue ceases, the sense of I-consciousness vanishes, visualization stops, and bliss begins. Consciousness enters *turiya* — the fourth state, beyond waking, dreaming, and sleep. Nirguna dhyana leads naturally to samadhi.

### 8. Samadhi — Absorption

The culmination of the eight-limbed path. *Sama* = complete equilibrium; *dhi* = cognition/knowledge. In samadhi, I-consciousness dissolves into pure consciousness — the object, the act of knowing, and the knower merge into undivided awareness. Time and space cease to operate. Two types:

- *Samprajnata samadhi*: conscious samadhi — concentration dissolves into consciousness; duality is suspended but some cognitive seed remains. This is the *turiya* state fully realised.
- *Asamprajnata samadhi*: beyond-conscious samadhi — even the seed of cognitive activity is dissolved; pure God-consciousness without illumination as a state. Not experiencing anything, but being existence-consciousness-bliss (*sat-chit-ananda*).

From asamprajnata samadhi, the yogi may descend into *sahaja samadhi* — ordinary life experienced with undivided awareness; no need to close the eyes, no detachment from the world, every action becomes worship.

## The Kshata Karmas — Six Purification Acts

Johari supplements the eight limbs with the Kshata Karmas (Hatha Yoga's six physical purification acts), which are a prerequisite for effective pranayama and nadi purification:

1. **Dhauti** — throat/stomach cleansing (swallowing a cloth strip)
2. **Vasti** — colon cleansing (enema or Ganesha Kriya, rinsing the anus)
3. **Neti** — nasal cleansing (thread drawn through one nostril; or jala neti, water through nostrils)
4. **Trataka** — eye gazing (fixed gaze at one point without blinking until tears flow; develops one-pointedness)
5. **Nauli** — abdominal churning (contracting and rotating the abdominal muscles; energises Vishvodara Nadi and digestion)
6. **Kapalabhati** — bellows breathing (rapid, forced exhalation clearing phlegm disorders)

These are traditionally kept secret and taught only with a master's guidance.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks in the Wiki

Ashtanga Yoga provides a systematic path that other frameworks in the wiki address more selectively:
- [Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s concept of [personal power](personal-power.md) and self-responsibility parallels yama/niyama — the ethical self-governance that is the foundation of both health and spiritual practice
- [Barbara Ann Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md)'s healing work operates primarily at the level of pranayama and dharana — working with the energy field and the healer's concentration on the client's field
- [Stanley Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md)'s [HOw Process](how-process.md) resembles pratyahara (withdrawing attention inward to the somatic felt sense) and dharana (concentrated attention to the body's image)
- [Mindfulness-based approaches in Western medicine](medical-intuition.md) echo pratyahara and dharana without the liberative cosmological context

## Critique

**From Yoga research**: Controlled studies confirm benefits of pranayama (reduced anxiety, improved autonomic regulation), asana (flexibility, strength, cardiovascular effects), and meditation (attention, stress reduction). These findings are supportive of the first six limbs' health claims. The higher states (dhyana, samadhi) are studied as contemplative experiences but not confirmed as the metaphysical events Patanjali describes.

**From different Yoga schools**: The Ashtanga formulation is Raja Yoga's approach. Bhakti Yoga (devotion), Jnana Yoga (knowledge), and Karma Yoga (action) offer alternative eight-limbed or non-limbed routes to the same goal. Johari integrates these as converging streams rather than competitors.

**From Western concerns**: The emphasis on suppression of mental fluctuations (*vritti nirodha*) in classical Yoga has been criticised by Western therapists as potentially dissociative in people with trauma histories. The practitioner is instructed to dissolve the ego — a goal that conflicts with therapeutic approaches that first strengthen the ego before working on its transcendence.
