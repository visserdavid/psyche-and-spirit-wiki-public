# Attitude and Health

**Summary**: The evidence-informed principle that specific emotional and psychological attitudes — particularly forgiveness, love, tolerance, and serenity — promote health and healing, while anger, fear, guilt, resentment, and depression deplete vitality and contribute to illness.

## Overview

[Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) regards attitude as the single most important determinant of health. This is not merely a motivational claim: he draws on psychoneuroimmunology, stress biology ([Selye](../thinkers/hans-selye.md)'s General Adaptation Syndrome), and clinical observations of thousands of patients to argue that the emotional life is physiologically potent.

The same framework is foundational to [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md) thesis — but where Myss frames it in terms of energy centres and archetypal patterns, Shealy frames it in terms of biochemistry and stress physiology.

## The Two Personality Natures

Shealy presents a taxonomy of attitudes, distinguishing two "natures":

**Nature of Limited Personality** (health-depleting):
- Negativity, pessimism, materialism
- Pride, need for authority, self-deception
- Intolerance, separatism, cruelty, arrogance
- Selfishness, prejudice, impulsiveness, impatience
- Anger, resentment, hatred, jealousy
- Sadness, self-pity, guilt
- Possessiveness, reactiveness, rebellion
- Greed, irresponsibility, judgmentalism, dishonesty
- Uncertainty, focus on the past

**Nature of Higher Personality** (health-promoting):
- Love, joy, detachment, wisdom
- Thought, reason, knowing, enlightenment
- Acceptance, unity, benevolence, nobility
- Tolerance, peace, serenity
- Motivation, cooperation, adaptability
- Responsiveness to spirit
- Self-determination, harmony, cooperation, charity
- Productivity, purpose, responsibility
- Endurance, beauty, honesty, optimism
- Patience, dignity, focus on the present

The physiology behind this distinction: the Limited Personality activates the sympathetic nervous system (increased adrenaline, elevated blood sugar, magnesium loss, eventual exhaustion), while the Higher Personality activates the parasympathetic system (rest, restoration, anabolic processes).

## The Fourteen Healing Attitudes

Shealy distils the health-promoting attitudes to fourteen core qualities:

1. **Forgiveness** — releasing resentment is the foundational healing attitude; carrying a grudge is "taking poison and expecting someone else to die."
2. **Tolerance and acceptance** of differences.
3. **Serenity** — the ability to find peace in turmoil.
4. **Love** — not preference or desire, but the genuine wish for others' wellbeing.
5. **Compassion** for those in trauma.
6. **Charity** — the desire to share.
7. **Motivation** — drive to accomplish something beyond oneself.
8. **Joy and appreciation** of beauty and life.
9. **Faith** — a sense of purpose and meaning.
10. **Hope** — orientation toward the future.
11. **Confidence and belief** in oneself.
12. **Courage** to face obstacles.
13. **Reason and logic** — rationality as a health factor.
14. **Wisdom** — the ability to choose the right response in each situation.

## Edgar Cayce on Attitude

[Edgar Cayce](../thinkers/edgar-cayce.md)'s readings articulated attitude-illness links with remarkable specificity:

> "No one can hate his neighbor and not have stomach and liver trouble." *(Reading 4021-1)*

> "Hate, malice, and jealousy create poisons in the minds, souls, and bodies of people." *(Reading 3312-1)*

These aphorisms anticipate decades of psychoneuroimmunological research showing that hostile attitudes elevate inflammatory markers, cortisol, and blood pressure.

## The Spiritual Attitude Inventory

Shealy includes a fifty-item questionnaire — the Spiritual Attitude Inventory — designed to help people assess their own attitude profile across categories including forgiveness, tolerance, compassion, faith, prayer, service, and willingness to help others. He frames this as a diagnostic tool: attitude is not simply a philosophical preference but a clinically relevant variable.

## Attitude vs. Belief

Shealy distinguishes between **attitude** and **belief**. Beliefs about doctrine (reincarnation, sin, hell) are secondary. Attitude — how one actually orients toward the world moment by moment — is the operative health variable. This means that someone with orthodox religious beliefs but chronic resentment is physiologically in a worse position than a non-believer with genuine forgiveness and love.

## Conscientiousness as Attitude in Action (Living Bliss)

In *Living Bliss* (2014), Shealy [(Shealy, 2014)](../sources/living-bliss.md) extends his attitude framework by grounding it in the *Longevity Project* (Friedman & Martin, 2011), the most robust longitudinal dataset in personality research. The key finding: **conscientiousness** — not attitude in the abstract, but the specific personality trait of being organized, purposeful, responsible, and self-disciplined — is the single strongest personality predictor of longevity.

This sharpens the earlier two-natures framework. The "Nature of Higher Personality" traits (motivation, purpose, responsibility, endurance, honesty, patience) are precisely the behavioural expression of conscientiousness. Conscientiousness is not a separate concept but the integrated form of health-promoting attitudes — what they look like when enacted over a lifetime.

Shealy's addition in *Living Bliss* is the **neurochemical substrate**: he proposes that the emotional security required for genuinely conscientious behaviour rests on a well-functioning oxytocin system, established in the first seven years of life through adequate nurturing. This bridges the attitude framework to biochemistry: the person who lacks conscientiousness is not simply choosing poorly — they may be neurochemically impaired. See [Conscientious Psychology](conscientious-psychology.md) and [Oxytocin and Health](oxytocin-and-health.md).

The **Ten Steps to Bliss** (Living Bliss, Chapter 6) are the practical expression of this integrated framework: weight, nutrition, exercise, non-smoking, sleep, daily self-regulation (Autogenic Training/Biogenics), positive attitude, social network, loving sex (oxytocin-raising), and quality supplements. Each step addresses one of the attitude-physiology-neurochemistry pathways identified across Shealy's career.

## Borysenko's Clinical Framework: Fear vs. Love

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) at Harvard's Mind/Body Clinic [(Borysenko & Rothstein, 1987)](../sources/minding-the-body.md) arrives at the same essential distinction through [psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md) rather than through Shealy's bioelectromagnetics or Myss's energy anatomy. Her clinical taxonomy of emotions:

**Fear-based emotions** (anger, resentment, guilt, grief, anxiety): activate the sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight), raise cortisol and adrenalin, and directly suppress immune function — reducing T-lymphocyte activity and natural killer cell function.

**Love-based emotions** (joy, compassion, humour, forgiveness): activate the parasympathetic system, allow the [relaxation response](relaxation-response.md), and create the physiological conditions in which immunity is restored.

This is not merely a philosophical observation: the psychoneuroimmunological research Borysenko draws on — Ader and Cohen's conditioned immune suppression, the Jemmott/Benson exam-stress/IgA studies, Kiecolt-Glaser's natural killer cell findings — establishes measurable immune consequences of sustained fear-based emotional states.

**Awfulizing** is Borysenko's clinical term for the specific cognitive pattern that most reliably generates unnecessary fear: catastrophising future events, using the mind's imaging capacity to produce fight-or-flight responses to imagined threats. Because the body cannot distinguish imagined threats from real ones (imagining a lemon produces real salivation), awfulizing generates real cortisol release and real immune suppression from entirely mental events.

**Hardiness** — Kobasa's three-attitude cluster of commitment, control, and challenge — is the positive attitudinal counterpart: the disposition that maintains engagement and autonomy under stress without escalating to fight-or-flight. See [Hardiness](hardiness.md) for the full research picture.

## Relation to Other Frameworks

The attitude-health connection is a thread running through the entire wiki:
- Myss's [woundology](woundology.md) describes how wound identity — using past suffering as currency — constitutes a chronically health-depleting attitude.
- Myss's [power and healing](power-and-healing.md) framework shows how self-esteem and inner authority determine whether healing is possible.
- Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome provides the stress-physiological mechanism by which attitude becomes biology.

## Siegel's Contribution: The Four Questions and the Survivor Personality

[Bernie Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md) extends the attitude-health framework with two practical tools: a set of four diagnostic questions and a description of the survivor personality.

### The Four Diagnostic Questions

Siegel uses four questions to assess the patient's will to live, surface unconscious attitude toward illness, and identify what changes the illness is demanding:

1. **Do you want to live to be 100?** Only 15–20% of general audiences say yes spontaneously — the same proportion as Siegel's exceptional patient group. The question tests whether the patient has a future orientation and a reason to continue.
2. **What happened in the year or two before your illness?** Identifies the precipitating loss or stressor — the biographical material that correlates with the onset of disease. Connects directly to the [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md) principle.
3. **What does your illness mean to you?** Surfaces the patient's operative belief about their situation (cancer = death sentence; cancer = release; cancer = challenge). This belief functions as a suggestion that the body enacts.
4. **Why do you need your illness?** Identifies the needs the illness is meeting — permission to say no, time off, love and attention, escape from a destructive relationship or career. If these needs can be met by other means, the illness loses its function.

These questions operationalise the attitude-health link as a clinical tool. [(Siegel, 1986)](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

### The Survivor Personality

Drawing on psychologist Al Siebert's research on paratrooper veterans who survived combat and on subsequent studies of crisis survivors, Siegel describes a cluster of characteristics — the survivor personality — that distinguishes patients who outlive their prognoses from those who don't. See [Exceptional Cancer Patients](exceptional-cancer-patients.md) for the full description.

The clinically relevant point for attitude-and-health is that the survivor personality is not primarily a matter of optimism (the attitude most associated with "positive thinking"). It is a matter of authenticity — the willingness to express genuine emotion (including fear, anger, and despair), to assert genuine needs, and to form genuine relationships. The long-term cancer survivors in Derogatis's 1979 study were rated as difficult by their physicians precisely because they expressed difficult emotions rather than performing the cheerful compliance that most people associate with a good attitude.

This finding complicates the simple equation of "positive attitude = better health outcomes." The healthy attitude, in Siegel's framework, is one of authentic self-expression, not performed positivity.

## Hay's Contribution: Self-Love as the Root Attitude

[Louise Hay](../thinkers/louise-hay.md) in [*You Can Heal Your Life*](../sources/you-can-heal-your-life.md) (1984) argues that beneath all the specific attitudes and emotional states that affect health lies a single foundational variable: **the degree to which a person loves and approves of themselves**.

The four health-depleting attitudes Hay identifies — resentment, criticism, guilt, and fear — are, in her framework, all expressions of the absence of self-love:
- **Resentment** arises from the belief that one has been denied what one deserves — which presupposes a prior belief in one's own unworthiness.
- **Criticism** (of self and others) is the habit of a person who measures everyone, including themselves, against standards they are pre-convinced cannot be met.
- **Guilt** is self-punishment — the belief that one deserves to suffer for failing some standard.
- **Fear** is the anticipation of further rejection or failure — the stance of a self that does not believe it is safe.

**Self-love as the single cure**: "There is only one thing that heals every problem, and that is: to know how to love yourself." Hay's claim goes beyond the PNI finding that positive emotions support immunity — she argues that self-love is the *root* from which all healing attitudes grow, and that its absence is the root of all health-depleting ones. Self-love is not a pleasant addition to good health practices; it is their prerequisite.

**Practical attitude tools**: Hay's primary instruments for cultivating the self-love attitude are [mirror work](mirror-work.md) (looking in one's own eyes and saying "I love you") and [affirmations](affirmations.md) — repeated present-tense positive declarations ("I love and approve of myself," repeated 300–400 times a day). These practices directly address the internalised critical voice formed in childhood and replace it with a self-approving stance. [(Hay, 1984)](../sources/you-can-heal-your-life.md)

**The self-love → health chain**: Hay proposes: self-love → positive self-talk → reduced fear and resentment → reduced cortisol and adrenalin → restored immunity → improved health. While she does not cite PNI research, this chain is structurally consistent with the Borysenko-Siegel PNI findings and adds the specific psychological root (self-love/self-hate as the primary variable) that those frameworks leave implicit.

## Critique

The link between psychological attitudes and physical health is one of the better-supported claims in this literature. Chronic anger is associated with cardiovascular disease; depression with immune suppression and cancer progression; social isolation with increased mortality across virtually all disease categories. These relationships are documented in mainstream epidemiology and psychoneuroimmunology.

However:
- The specific disease-emotion mappings (hatred causing liver trouble; resentment causing diabetes) are not supported at the level of controlled research.
- The implied direction of causation (attitude causes illness rather than illness causing negative attitudes) is difficult to establish.
- The claim that attitude change can reverse established organic disease — while suggested by some case reports — has not been demonstrated in controlled trials.

**Note**: Mainstream medicine accepts that psychological states significantly affect health outcomes. The specific claims about which attitudes cause which diseases, and whether attitude change can reliably cure physical illness, remain areas of active investigation with inconsistent evidence.
