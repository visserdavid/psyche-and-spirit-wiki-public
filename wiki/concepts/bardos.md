# The Four Bardos

**Summary**: The Tibetan Buddhist framework dividing existence into four transitional states (bardos): the natural bardo of life, the bardo of dying, the bardo of dharmata (intrinsic radiance), and the bardo of becoming — each presenting specific opportunities for liberation that meditation practice during life is designed to prepare for.

**Sources**:
- [Sogyal Rinpoche. (1992). *The Tibetan book of living and dying* (P. D. Gaffney & A. Harvey, Eds.). HarperSanFrancisco.](../sources/tibetan-book-of-living-and-dying.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Overview

The Tibetan term *bardo* (literally "between two") denotes any transitional or intermediate state. While the popular association is with the state between death and rebirth — as in the famous *Bardo Thodol* (Tibetan Book of the Dead) — Tibetan Buddhist teachings describe four distinct bardos that together map the entire arc of conscious existence.

[Sogyal Rinpoche](../thinkers/sogyal-rinpoche.md) in *[The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying](../sources/tibetan-book-of-living-and-dying.md)* presents the four bardos as the organising framework of the entire book. The key insight: each bardo is characterised by a specific kind of uncertainty in which the mind's habitual patterns can be interrupted and liberation is possible. Ordinary life is itself a bardo — the ordinary waking state is perpetually transitional, perpetually uncertain, and therefore perpetually an opportunity.

## The Four Bardos

### 1. The Natural Bardo of Life

The bardo of life spans from birth to the beginning of the dying process — what we normally call "life." From the bardo perspective, this is not a stable state but a prolonged transition: the mind is constantly in flux, nothing lasts, and every moment presents the choice between entrenching habitual patterns or recognising their empty nature.

The primary spiritual task of the bardo of life is to **recognise [Rigpa](rigpa-nature-of-mind.md)** — the fundamental nature of mind — through meditation practice, so that this recognition is available at the much more challenging moments of dying, death, and becoming.

Sogyal's teaching on [impermanence](../sources/tibetan-book-of-living-and-dying.md) belongs here: the contemplation of death as a daily practice not to generate anxiety but to dissolve the illusion of permanence that prevents the present moment from being fully inhabited. Milarepa's formulation: practice so that you can die without regret.

### 2. The Bardo of Dying

The bardo of dying begins with the onset of the dying process — whether sudden (accident) or gradual (illness) — and ends with the dawning of [Ground Luminosity](ground-luminosity.md) at the moment of actual death.

During this bardo, the four elements of the body dissolve in sequence:
- **Earth dissolves into water**: the sense of solidity fails; the dying person feels heavy, sinking, as if the body is crushing them; inner sign is a mirage-like shimmer
- **Water dissolves into fire**: fluids dry up; the body cools; inner sign is smoke
- **Fire dissolves into air**: body heat fades; breathing becomes laboured; inner sign is fireflies or sparks
- **Air dissolves into space**: breathing ceases; consciousness withdraws from the sense faculties; inner sign is a sputtering butter lamp

As each element dissolves, a corresponding level of mind dissolves, until what remains is bare awareness — which, at the culminating moment, reveals the Ground Luminosity.

Practical implication: the state of mind of the dying person at this moment is of great consequence. This is why Sogyal emphasises clearing unfinished business, managing pain to preserve clarity of consciousness, and providing a calm, sacred environment. The bardo of dying is the final examination for which the bardo of life is preparation.

**Correspondence**: [Kübler-Ross's five stages](five-stages-of-dying.md) describe the *emotional* process of the bardo of dying from the perspective of the personality. The Tibetan teaching describes the *energetic* dissolution process from the perspective of consciousness itself. Both point to the same need: meeting death with openness rather than resistance.

### 3. The Bardo of Dharmata (Intrinsic Radiance)

The bardo of dharmata begins after the Ground Luminosity has been glimpsed (or passed unrecognised) and the mind re-establishes activity. *Dharmata* means "the nature of things" — this bardo is the unmediated display of mind's nature as sound, colour, and light.

In the traditional Tibetan teaching (particularly the Dzogchen-based texts from which the *Bardo Thodol* derives), the bardo of dharmata unfolds in four phases:

1. **Ground Luminosity phase**: the first dawning of the Clear Light — see [Ground Luminosity](ground-luminosity.md)
2. **Manifestation of radiance**: mind's energy appears as undifferentiated light and sound
3. **Peaceful deity appearances**: the five wisdoms manifest as five brilliantly coloured lights in a mandala of peaceful deities, each paired with a dimmer light corresponding to a samsaric realm; liberation occurs by moving *toward* the brilliant lights (wisdom) rather than being drawn toward the dim lights (familiar habits)
4. **Wrathful deity appearances**: the same energies manifest in fiercer, more overwhelming form; liberation still possible for those who recognise these as mind's own energy

Sogyal explicitly connects this phase to David Bohm's physics: Bohm's observation that "matter is condensed light" resonates with the bardo teaching that the physical world is a condensation of the luminous energy that manifests fully in the bardo of dharmata. The [M-1/M-2/M-3 metaphysics](m-1-m-2-m-3-metaphysics.md) framework (Harman) parallels this: M-3 (consciousness primary) is the ground from which the bardo of dharmata's radiance arises; M-1 (matter primary) is the most condensed expression.

**NDE correspondence**: The "being of light" and the overwhelming love reported in [near-death experiences](near-death-experiences.md) correspond to the early phases of the bardo of dharmata: Ground Luminosity or the first peaceful deity appearances, glimpsed briefly before the consciousness returns to the body.

### 4. The Bardo of Becoming

If liberation does not occur in the bardo of dharmata, the bardo of becoming begins: a period of increasingly solid-feeling experience shaped by karmic tendencies, which eventually culminates in rebirth. The consciousness that enters the bardo of becoming has no physical body but experiences visions, emotional turbulence, and eventually the draw toward a new life.

The duration of the bardo of becoming is described in traditional teachings as up to forty-nine days, though this is understood as symbolic of a variable process. The key teaching: even in the bardo of becoming, liberation remains possible through recognition of Rigpa. Phowa practice (consciousness transference) performed by living practitioners for the deceased is understood to provide ongoing support during this period.

## The Bardos as Opportunity Structure

The bardo framework differs from most Western psychological and spiritual maps in one crucial respect: it provides a *graduated* map of multiple moments of liberation. Rather than a single final judgment, the bardos present repeated opportunities — at the moment of death (Ground Luminosity), in the early bardo of dharmata (peaceful lights), in the later bardo of dharmata (wrathful lights), and throughout the bardo of becoming. Each opportunity is less accessible than the previous one, however, which is why practice in the bardo of life is so emphasized.

Sogyal's teaching: "Whether we are familiar with it or not is entirely up to us. But in one case it liberates, and in the other it does not."

## Connections to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Grof's Perinatal Matrices](crisis-as-initiation.md)**: Stanislav Grof's four Basic Perinatal Matrices — the psychological experiences of birth as death/rebirth templates — parallel the bardo structure. BPM IV (the emergence into light) corresponds to Ground Luminosity; BPM I (oceanic undifferentiated bliss) corresponds to the state prior to the bardo of dying. Both frameworks map an arc from contraction through dissolution to expansion.

**[NDE Transformation](nde-transformation.md)**: Ring's systematic findings — value shifts, spiritual universalism, loss of death fear — are understood in the bardo framework as the consequence of having briefly touched the bardo of dharmata. The permanent change that follows an NDE corresponds to what would happen in a full bardo traversal.

**[Five Stages of Dying](five-stages-of-dying.md)**: Kübler-Ross's stages describe the psychological-emotional process of coming to terms with death in the bardo of dying. The acceptance stage corresponds to the moment of readiness that enables recognition of Ground Luminosity.

**[Chakra System](chakra-system.md)**: The element-dissolution sequence in the bardo of dying (earth → water → fire → air → space) mirrors the upward movement of Kundalini through the chakras. Muladhara (earth), Svadhisthana (water), Manipura (fire), Anahata (air), and Vishuddha (space/ether) are the five lower chakras corresponding to the five elements whose dissolution is mapped in the bardo of dying.

**[The Real World (ACIM)](real-world-acim.md)**: The bardo of dharmata's display of Ground Luminosity corresponds structurally to what ACIM describes as the Real World: the mind's natural state when the accumulation of ego-thought falls away, revealing a perception of reality through the lens of love rather than fear.

## Critique

**Empirical status**: The bardo teachings are based on centuries of meditative observation within a specific cultural and doctrinal tradition. They cannot be empirically validated in the ordinary scientific sense. Sogyal acknowledges this; his consistent position is that the framework is experientially accessible through meditation practice and partially corroborated by NDE research.

**Cultural specificity**: The peaceful and wrathful deity appearances in the bardo of dharmata are drawn from Nyingma Tantric iconography. The framework assumes a cultural and initiatory context that Western readers typically lack. Sogyal simplifies significantly from the technical source texts.

**Sequential certainty**: Traditional teachings describe the bardos as a specific sequential process. Modern consciousness researchers (including some Buddhist scholars) suggest this may be a pedagogical map rather than a literal sequence — the valuable teaching being the *attitude* of recognition, not a specific timeline.
