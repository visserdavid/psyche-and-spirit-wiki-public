# Body Scanning

**Summary**: W. Brugh Joy's technique for detecting energy fields radiating from the body surface using trained hand sensitivity — developed through clinical medical practice in 1973 and refined over years of conference work — which he used to locate disease processes and assess chakra activity without physical examination.

**Sources**:
- [Joy, W. B. (1979). *Joy's way: A map for the transformational journey — An introduction to the potentials for healing with body energies*. J. P. Tarcher.](../sources/joys-way.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Discovery and Origins

Joy discovered body scanning accidentally in 1973, during a routine abdominal examination. Moving his hand toward a patient's abdomen during a liver palpation, he felt the impulse to pass his hand several inches above the body surface rather than touching it. He detected a field of energy radiating from the solar plexus — a cylinder approximately four inches in diameter extending three to four feet from the body surface. The patient's eyes were closed; Joy confirmed the sensation by repeated passes.

Over the following three months, Joy systematically scanned patients at a distance during examinations, mapping energy field locations, diameters, and projection distances. When he encountered a Tantra Yoga book showing a figure with marked chakra points, he recognised that he had independently mapped the chakra system — without prior knowledge of it. He subsequently extended his mapping to approximately forty field locations, including several not described in traditional texts.

## Technique

The scanner moves the dominant hand slowly above the body surface of a person lying face up on a table, at an initial height of approximately eight inches. The hand moves slowly enough to allow the brain's lag-time between stimulus and sensation to be compensated. The scanning consciousness centres in the hand — not in expectations about what should be there — while the scanner maintains a witness state.

Sensations reported include warmth, tingling, vibration, pressure, and (less commonly) visual impressions of light or colour. Joy notes that individuals differ in which sensory modality predominates; most people, after training, sense fields as warmth or tingling.

**Practical notes from Joy's training:**
- The scanning hand can become overcharged (tingling, aching); resting it or flicking the fingers relieves this.
- Metal tables and metal jewellery on the subject can interfere with detection; subjects should remove watches and belt buckles.
- Eyes closed in the scanner increases concentration; eyes closed in the subject reduces the suggestibility problem.
- A beginning practitioner should start with chest and abdomen areas (strongest fields) and contrast the sensation over a chakra area against the sensation over an adjacent non-chakra area.

## What Can Be Detected

Joy reports two distinct categories of detectable field:

**Chakra fields**: The energy centres corresponding to the classical Hindu system (and his extended mapping) radiate fields detectable at varying distances from the body. He notes that the intensity and quality of a chakra field indicates the level of awareness and activity associated with that chakra — not merely its anatomical strength. A highly intense chakra field does not indicate development; quality (felt as refinement, clarity, or colour by sensitives) matters more than intensity.

**Disease fields**: Illness creates anomalous energy fields — radiating from the location of the diseased organ, not from a chakra centre. Joy found he could detect cancer, hepatitis (liver area), kidney disease, heart disease, and appendicitis as distinct field anomalies. These disease fields differ from chakra fields in their texture, projection pattern, and location.

He notes clear limits to what scanning detects:
- Multiple sclerosis and leukaemia (no localised organ involvement) do not produce detectable disease fields by this method.
- He cannot determine the specific nature of the pathology, only the presence of anomalous energy in a location.
- He cannot distinguish cancer from inflammation (both produce elevated field signatures).
- He cannot determine whether a disease process is progressing or subsiding.

**Posthumous persistence**: Joy reports detecting energy fields in cadavers refrigerated for up to twenty-four hours after death, and claims to have attempted scanning Egyptian mummies at the Cairo Museum. He concludes that the fields do not depend on circulation or the nervous system, and are not heat radiation.

## Physical Properties of the Fields

Joy's observations about the physical nature of the detected fields:
- They are **not heat radiation**: the fields pass through four-inch styrofoam insulation and half-inch steel plates, which block thermal radiation.
- They are **not imaginary**: Valerie Hunt (UCLA) measured energy field frequencies correlating with what trained clairvoyants (Rosalyn Bruyere, Carolyn Conger) described visually — providing independent instrumental and perceptual convergence.
- They are **trainable**: In Joy's conference groups, 99% of participants reported detecting some form of field sensation after brief training sessions.
- They relate to the **Kirlian / Kilner tradition**: Wilhelm Kilner's dioptric screens (London, Warr Laboratory) and Kirlian photography had already documented field emissions around the body; Joy's work adds clinical mapping.
- Harold Saxon Burr's (Yale) bioelectric field research, showing that a developing cell is surrounded by an energy field that guides its growth axis, provides a scientific analogue Joy cites as corroboration.

## Critique

**Anecdotal base**: Joy's disease-detection claims rest entirely on clinical observations without controlled blinding. A sceptic could attribute his sense of "finding" a tumour to unconscious pattern recognition from the physical examination that preceded or informed the scan.

**Reproducibility**: The 99% success rate in training 500 conference participants is not a blinded controlled study. Participants were motivated seekers in an intensive two-week immersive environment — highly susceptible to expectation effects. No independent replication of this training result has been published.

**Thermography**: Joy correctly notes that thermography does not detect his described fields, which he interprets as evidence that the fields are not heat. A mainstream interpretation is that no such fields exist to be detected — that the sensation is proprioceptive artefact, expectation, or warmth from proximity to the body.

**Mummy scanning**: Joy describes attempting to scan mummies at the Cairo Museum and reporting confusion due to glass cases and guard interventions. No data was collected.

**Note**: Mainstream medicine does not recognise body scanning of this type as a clinical diagnostic tool. The energy field system Joy describes remains outside the framework of evidence-based medicine.
