# Calling

**Summary**: The soul's innate orientation toward a specific mode of being in the world — its character, purpose, and way of making meaning — distinct from career or role; in Hillman's framework it is inseparable from fate and character, in Myss's it is the outward expression of the Sacred Contract.

## Calling in Hillman

For [James Hillman](../thinkers/james-hillman.md), calling is the outward expression of the [acorn](acorn-theory.md) — the soul's unique image asserting itself in the world. It is not equivalent to vocation, job, or career, though it may find expression through these. Calling is the persistent, characteristic way a soul insists on being what it is.

Hillman distinguishes calling from several common reductions:

- **Not motivation in the psychological sense** (drive, need, reward-seeking). The acorn theory explicitly rejects Adler's compensation theory — the idea that greatness emerges from inferiority overcome. Superiorities are not reformed inferiorities; they emerge from a source that is prior to both.
- **Not talent alone**. Talent is the instrument the calling uses; calling is the *purpose* for which the instrument exists. Barbara McClintock's talent for genetics served her calling for a certain kind of deeply attentive, intimate knowing of living things; Yehudi Menuhin's violin technique served his calling for the music he could already hear in his mind at age four.
- **Not chosen by the person consciously**. Calling chooses the person — it arrives as annunciation, compulsion, obsession, or undeniable certainty.

### Signals of Calling

Hillman identifies several characteristic appearances of calling in a biography:

- **The annunciation**: A specific moment in childhood when the calling arrives — Ella Fitzgerald's spontaneous switch from dancing to singing at Amateur Night; the eight-year-old Collingwood's encounter with Kant; Manolete's sudden fascination with bulls at age eleven.
- **The tool demand**: Children with strong callings demand the instruments of their vocation at ages when their bodies cannot yet handle them — McClintock wanting adult tools, Menuhin demanding a real violin.
- **The obsession**: The persistent, non-rational return to a theme, image, or activity that exceeds educational or circumstantial explanation — Colette's lifelong obsession with stationery and writing tools before she wrote a word.
- **The refusal**: The equal and opposite — the calling refusing to be what circumstances prescribe, the child resisting the school, the profession, or the parents' vision.
- **The mentor encounter**: A person who *sees* the image (see [Esse Percipi](esse-percipi.md)) and names it — giving the calling permission to exist by recognising it.

### Calling, Character, and Fate

Hillman grounds his theory in Heraclitus: *ethos anthropoi daimon* — variously translated as "character is fate," "man's character is his daimon/guardian," or "habit is a person's daimon." Hillman reads this not as determinism but as the claim that calling, character, and fate are inseparable expressions of the same underlying image. To follow the calling *is* to live in accord with one's character; to express one's character *is* to participate in one's fate.

Fate in this sense is not imposed from outside but is the soul's own pattern asserting itself through circumstances.

### Fantasy and Calling

One of Hillman's practical arguments: the fantasies and obsessions of children — even "useless" ones like penny dreadfuls, pulp fiction, and violent adventures — are often the calling's first language. Cole Porter reading adventure tales while hiding them in his music bag; Frank Lloyd Wright reading Jules Verne; Quentin Tarantino saturated in crime films and comics. The fantasy is not an escape from the calling but its earliest articulation.

## Calling in Myss

For [Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md), calling is the outward expression of the [Sacred Contract](sacred-contracts.md) — what the soul agreed to do and be before birth. It is one of the primary reasons illness occurs (when calling is unfulfilled or denied), and one of the primary conditions for healing (when calling is discovered and followed).

In Myss's framework, calling operates through the archetypal companions of the Sacred Contract — each archetype carrying a specific domain of calling (the Healer's call is to serve others' wholeness; the Artist's call is to bring beauty into form; the Warrior's call is to defend the vulnerable). The calling is also organized through the chakra system — the third chakra (personal power) is specifically the seat of authentic calling and self-definition.

Myss's fifth stage of Sacred Contract (*surrender*) is the moment when calling is fully embraced — the individual gives up the attempt to manage and control the terms of their life purpose and allows the Contract's intelligence to direct them.

## Comparison: Hillman and Myss

| Dimension | Hillman | Myss |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Source | Soul's pre-birth image (acorn) | Pre-birth covenant with the Divine |
| Agent | Daimon | Archetypal companions + grace |
| Framework | Psychological/imaginal | Theological/spiritual |
| Moral dimension | Neutral (can produce Hitler) | Oriented toward divine potential |
| Method of discovery | Biographical reading backward | Archetypal Wheel, symbolic sight |
| Relation to illness | Blocked calling = distress | Unfulfilled Contract = illness |

Both frameworks agree that calling is prior to circumstance, that it persists regardless of obstacles, and that it requires a witness (mentor, guide) to fully manifest.

## Calling vs. Career

A central practical distinction in both Hillman and Myss: **calling is not career**. A person may pursue a calling through multiple careers, or express a calling entirely outside professional life. The janitor who loves and tends his building with the care of an artist is following a calling; the doctor who hates medicine but earns well is not.

Myss argues that illness frequently occurs precisely at the juncture between career and calling — when the person's biography has diverged from their soul's agreement. The cellular energy invested in maintaining a life contrary to calling is withdrawn from health.

## Critique

**Privilege and accessibility**: Not everyone has the circumstances, stability, or resources to follow a calling even if they recognise it. The frameworks of Hillman and Myss tend toward the individual and assume a level of freedom to choose that is not universally available. Calling in conditions of poverty, oppression, or survival-mode living is a different thing than calling for those with leisure and resources.

**The grandiosity risk**: Calling as a concept can be appropriated by the ego to license self-absorption, refusal of ordinary obligation, or resistance to productive feedback. "My daimon is calling me to this" can become a way of avoiding accountability.

**Mainstream perspective**: Psychology recognizes calling as a construct — in research on work motivation, purpose, and meaning (see Wrzesniewski, Frankl, Deci & Ryan). These empirical frameworks do not require a pre-birth image; they locate calling in the interaction between individual strengths, values, and environmental contexts.
