# Centering Prayer

**Summary**: Thomas Keating's contemplative practice in which a "sacred word" is used not as mantra but as pointer — a gentle reminder of the meditator's intention to dwell in God's presence; the word is not repeated but reintroduced when attention wanders, allowing the practitioner to identify with the "river of presence" (consciousness itself) rather than with the thoughts that float on it; Borysenko's preferred form of contemplative practice and the culmination of her practices framework in *Fire in the Soul*.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Overview

Centering prayer is a form of Christian contemplative practice developed and systematised by Father Thomas Keating, a Trappist monk and abbot who resides at St. Benedict's Monastery in Snowmass, Colorado. Keating is one of the founders of the Centering Prayer movement, which seeks to recover the contemplative dimension of Christian prayer that was largely eclipsed in Western Christianity following the Renaissance and Reformation.

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) encountered centering prayer after years of teaching and practising mantra-based meditation (Herbert Benson's relaxation response method) and Buddhist mindfulness. She describes centering prayer as the form of meditation she settled into for its "clarity, simplicity, and effectiveness." It appears in [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md) as the culminating contemplative practice — the deepest available to the ordinary person, requiring no special training or altered states.

---

## The Central Distinction: Pointer, Not Mantra

The essential difference between centering prayer and mantra-based meditation is the function of the repeated word:

**Mantra-based meditation** (Benson's relaxation response; Transcendental Meditation): a word, phrase, or sound is repeated rhythmically, typically on the out-breath, throughout the meditation period. The repetition serves as a focus point that crowds out discursive thought and elicits physiological calm. The word is the content of the practice.

**Centering prayer**: the sacred word is not repeated continuously but is used as a *pointer* — introduced only when the meditator notices that attention has drifted into active thinking. The word says, in effect: "I intend to return to silence and presence." Once the return is made, the word is released. The silence is the content of the practice; the word is the door back to it.

Keating's formulation: the sacred word is not about the word's characteristics (sound, meaning, or tradition) but about the *intent* it expresses. The meditator chooses any word that "feels like home" — peace, Jesus, love, shalom, Mary, let go — and uses it as a symbol of their will to remain in divine presence. "The word is not what strengthens [the practice] but the intent it expresses."

## The River of Presence

Keating offers a central metaphor that Borysenko adopts and develops:

**Ordinary consciousness**: attention is fixed on the boats (thoughts, images, emotions, sensations, plans, memories). The river — the consciousness that contains them — is invisible, taken for granted, unnoticed.

**Centering prayer practice**: the meditator learns to identify with the *river* rather than with any particular boat. As Keating describes it, a thought is "any perception that crosses the screen of consciousness." Instructions are not "empty your mind" (impossible) but "resist no thought, hang on to no thought, react to no thought." When a thought passes, let it go. When attention catches on a thought (a strong emotion, a compelling idea, the judgment that meditation is "going well" or "going badly"), gently return to the sacred word — and then release even the word as attention settles again into the river.

The metaphor's clinical significance: identifying with the river rather than the boats is not a metaphysical claim but a phenomenological description of a trainable shift in awareness. The boats are still there; the threats, plans, worries, and longings are still present. What changes is whether the meditator is *constituted by* them or *contains* them. In the river, thoughts and emotions are events in consciousness; they no longer define consciousness.

## Stages of the Practice

### Setting
Borysenko describes her own meditation space in detail — not as decoration but as an understanding that the environment participates in the practice. A shrine table with objects that evoke love and gratitude (a heart-shaped stone, a sandalwood box, a photograph, a candle); incense that "makes memory and brings mind to remembrance." The setting's purpose is not to impress God — "God is the Cosmic Birther; it is not to woo God" — but to help the practitioner's own mind enter the practice state.

### Letting Go
Close the eyes, take several letting-go breaths. Relax the body. Check posture — spine upright.

### Connecting to the Higher Self
Before introducing the sacred word, Borysenko recommends a brief connecting practice: recall a moment of genuine love, peace, or awe (a sunset, a beloved person, a moment in nature). Let the body feel the memory's residue. This quality of presence is what the centering prayer aims to deepen and extend.

### The Sacred Word
Choose one word. The tradition of Sanskrit mantras (*om nama shivaya*, *hamsa*), Hebrew words (*shalom*, *shem*), or Aramaic (*Abba* — the word Jesus used for God, translated by Neil Douglas-Klotz as "Birther! Father-Mother of the Cosmos") all carry the vibration of traditions in which they have been used for centuries. Borysenko uses the *aah* sound on the in-breath and *bwoooooon* on the out-breath as a simple Aramaic form.

The word is introduced when attention drifts — gently, without self-judgment or frustration, and then released as silence returns.

### Establishing Practice
Minimum twenty minutes. It takes approximately fifteen minutes for the ordinary mind to quiet sufficiently for the silence to become accessible. "If you sit for fifteen minutes, you get ten minutes of peace." Daily practice at a consistent time and place. Keating: "Don't look for results; look for intention."

## Relationship to Benson's Relaxation Response

Herbert Benson's relaxation response model uses a similar technique (focusing on a repeated word to produce physiological calm) but aims at a different outcome and operates through a different mechanism:

| | Benson's Relaxation Response | Centering Prayer |
|---|---|---|
| **Aim** | Physiological calm; stress reduction | Communion with divine presence |
| **Word function** | Focus point; repeated continuously | Pointer; used only when needed |
| **Outcome measure** | Decreased heart rate, cortisol, oxygen consumption | Spiritual transformation; deepened presence |
| **Theoretical frame** | Parasympathetic nervous system | Contemplative theology |
| **Tradition** | Secular, cross-traditional | Christian contemplative |

Borysenko's synthesis: the relaxation response clears the physiological noise — the aroused nervous system that makes quiet attention nearly impossible — that stands between ordinary life and contemplative practice. Centering prayer opens the deeper silence in which the divine can be received. The two practices are not alternatives but layers: relaxation response first (weeks to months of establishing the physiological baseline), then centering prayer as the practice matures.

## Centering Prayer and Mindfulness

[Mindfulness](mindfulness.md) (Kabat-Zinn's clinical application; Thich Nhat Hanh's Buddhist practice) and centering prayer share the foundational move of identifying with the observer rather than with the observed — the Witness rather than the thoughts the Witness observes. They diverge in:

**Orientation**: Mindfulness aims at present-moment awareness of whatever is arising; centering prayer aims at surrender to a specific presence (God's) in the silence beyond thought.

**Tradition**: Mindfulness is Buddhist in origin, secularised for clinical use. Centering prayer is explicitly Christian contemplative.

**Activity**: Mindfulness cultivates *noticing*; centering prayer cultivates *releasing*.

**Relationship to thought**: In mindfulness, thoughts are observed without judgment — the aim is to notice them clearly. In centering prayer, thoughts are released as gently as possible — the aim is to move through them into the silence they disturb.

Both practices converge in their clinical effects (stress reduction, emotional regulation, increased compassion) and in their phenomenological description of the observer position. Borysenko practises both and does not see them as mutually exclusive.

## Thomas Keating: Context and Teaching

Thomas Keating (1923–2018) entered the Cistercian (Trappist) order in 1944 and became abbot of St. Joseph's Abbey in Spencer, Massachusetts, where he hosted meetings of Buddhist, Hindu, and Jewish teachers with Christian monks — an unprecedented ecumenical encounter that shaped his understanding of contemplative practice. He co-founded the Centering Prayer movement in the 1970s and wrote its principal text, *Open Mind, Open Heart: The Contemplative Dimension of the Gospel* (1986).

Keating's insight was that contemplative prayer — the direct experiential encounter with God beyond words and concepts — had been the living heart of the Christian tradition in the desert fathers and mothers, the medieval mystics, and the Carmelite tradition (John of the Cross, Teresa of Ávila), but had been largely inaccessible to ordinary Christians who lacked the monastic context in which it was transmitted. Centering prayer is his attempt to make the core practice available to non-monastics.

The phrase "open mind, open heart" reflects Keating's understanding: the mind is opened by releasing its grip on concepts, theologies, and expectations (including spiritual expectations — the desire for consolation, visions, or experiences of any kind); the heart is opened by surrendering to the divine presence that is always already present. The practice does not create the divine presence; it removes the obstacles to noticing it.

## Critique

**Theological critique (Protestant)**: Some Protestant theologians resist the centering prayer framework on the grounds that it imports Buddhist and Hindu contemplative techniques under Christian language — a syncretism that, in their view, compromises the uniqueness of Christian revelation. Keating responds that the tradition he is recovering is pre-Protestant and pre-Reformation; it belongs to the Christian heritage before the divisions.

**The emptying question**: Some critics argue that prayer is by definition addressed *to* God, not a state of emptiness *before* God. Keating's response: centering prayer is not emptiness for its own sake but availability — a practiced willingness to receive what God offers rather than filling the space with one's own agenda.

**Efficacy evidence**: The specific claim that centering prayer produces encounters with divine presence is not empirically testable by any currently available method. Its physiological effects (comparable to other forms of meditation in reducing stress markers) are measurable; its spiritual claims are not.

**Accessibility**: Sitting in silence for twenty minutes twice daily requires significant life circumstances — protected time, physical stability, absence of acute crisis. For people in acute distress, trauma, or poverty, the preconditions for centering prayer may not be available.
