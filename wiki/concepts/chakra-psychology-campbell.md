# Chakra Psychology (Campbell)

**Summary**: Campbell's comparative psychological reading of the seven-chakra system in Part IV of *The Mythic Image* — each chakra is interpreted as a mode of motivation, a level of consciousness at which the life energy is primarily invested; Kundalini's ascent through the chakras is the story of how consciousness frees itself from successively subtler levels of bondage; Western art, mysticism, and philosophy are shown to encode the same seven-stage map.

**Sources**:
- [Campbell, J. (1974). *The mythic image*. Princeton University Press.](../sources/the-mythic-image.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

---

## Overview

Part IV of *The Mythic Image*, "Transformations of Inner Light," is Campbell's most original contribution to the interpretation of the chakra system. Where [Johari](../thinkers/harish-johari.md)'s [*Chakras: Energy Centers of Transformation*](../sources/chakras-energy-centers.md) provides the traditional Hindu-Tantric attribute system (deities, mantras, yantras, lokas), Campbell provides a *psychological* reading: each chakra describes a mode of motivation or a level of consciousness in which a person's life energy can be primarily invested.

Campbell draws on:
- Sir John Woodroffe's translations of Tantric texts (especially *The Serpent Power*, 3rd ed. 1931)
- Sri Ramakrishna's first-person accounts of Kundalini experiences
- C. G. Jung's observations about Eastern psychology
- Western art (Dante, William Blake, Meister Eckhart) read through the chakra lens

He makes the argument that the chakra system is not merely a Hindu sectarian teaching but a universal psychological map — and that once one knows the map, the same stages can be recognised in Western mystical literature and art.

## The Psychology of Yoga

Campbell opens Part IV with Jung's statement: "The problem of healing occupying the minds of the East for two thousand years... methods and doctrines developed... put our attempts in the shade." He argues that the yogic mapping of consciousness — particularly the Kundalini system of seven centres — is a more refined map of inner development than any available in the West.

The central insight of Kundalini psychology, as Campbell reads it: **consciousness is not located at one fixed level**. Most people operate from one or two chakras as their primary motivational centre. The practice of yoga (and the equivalent Western practices) is the gradual freeing of consciousness from its fixation at one level, allowing it to move upward — to discover that what seemed the whole of reality was only one level's perspective.

## The Seven Chakras as Modes of Motivation

### Chakra 1: Muladhara — The Dragon of Pure Materialism

**Traditional attribute**: earth, four petals, elephant Airavata, Brahma, Kundalini coiled  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: The motivational mode of pure materialism — the consciousness fixated on physical security, accumulation, and survival. The "dragon" that guards this level is the lethargy of the creature that hoards and cannot let go. Campbell writes: "At the level of this Kundalini, one is a dragon. Dragons know their propensity to hoard and guard things... they make no use of what they hang on to."

The task at this level is not to destroy the dragon but to break its grip — to release the hoarded energy (the "jewel" and the "maid," the creative life) so it can ascend. This parallels Myss's description of the first chakra as tribal consciousness: survival, loyalty, the group's definition of reality.

### Chakra 2: Svadhishthana — The Freudian Level

**Traditional attribute**: water, six petals, makara, Vishnu/Rakini, crescent moon  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: "The aim of life is sex." This is the level that Freud's psychology describes — libido as the fundamental motivation. All mythology, religion, and art is interpreted as sexuality in disguise; all rituals as fertility magic. Campbell notes that this interpretation is not wrong at this level — it correctly describes what motivates the consciousness that is fixed here. The problem is fixation: "to recognize the force and importance of this centre and let its energies pass, to be transformed into the aims of higher centres."

The connection to Myss: the second chakra governs relationships, sexuality, creativity, and money — the second sacred truth is "Honour one another." A person primarily motivated by second-chakra concerns interprets all relationships in terms of power dynamics, sexual exchange, and creative ownership.

### Chakra 3: Manipura — The Adlerian Level

**Traditional attribute**: fire, ten petals, ram, Shiva as lord of ash-smeared ascetics, Lakini  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: "The aim of life is power." This is the level that Alfred Adler's psychology describes — the will to power as the fundamental motivation; all behaviour as compensation for inferiority. The warrior, the builder, the conqueror, the achiever: these are the third-chakra modes. The imagery at this chakra is flames, the sun, destruction and creation by fire — Shiva smeared with funeral ash presides here.

The three lower chakras (1–3) are described collectively as "the modes of man living in the world, turned toward the world." Their joys and sorrows, their achievements and losses, are the whole of life for the consciousness fixed at these levels. Most of human history — most political, economic, and social life — operates at these three levels.

The connection to Myss: the third chakra is personal power, self-esteem, and individuation — the third sacred truth is "Honour oneself." The shadow at this level is the external reference that makes self-worth dependent on others' recognition rather than internal authority.

### Chakra 4: Anahata — La Vita Nuova, the New Life

**Traditional attribute**: air, twelve petals, antelope, Shiva in boon-bestowing form, Kakini, the Wish Fulfilling Tree  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: The heart chakra is the transition from the world-consciousness of the lower three to the spirit-consciousness of the upper three. Campbell quotes Dante's opening of *La Vita Nuova*: "The moment he first beheld Beatrice, the spirit of life which dwells in the chamber of the heart began to tremble so violently... the spirit of perception which dwells in the chamber of the spirits said: 'Behold your bliss.'"

At this level, the motivating force is no longer materialism, sex, or power but love — not romantic love (which is a fourth-chakra experience seen through second-chakra eyes) but the love that is not generated by two things striking together but arises spontaneously as the "sound not made by two things striking." This is the *anahata* — "not struck" — sound: the *om* that Campbell describes as the seed sound heard in the space of the heart.

The Wish Fulfilling Tree appears at this level: when consciousness reaches the heart chakra, it discovers that the things it was striving for in the lower three — security, pleasure, achievement — are already given, already available in the present moment without striving. "Things come of themselves. Give up the struggle for achievement, for love, for power, for good — and rest in peace."

Campbell reads Dante's entire journey through the *Paradiso* as a chakra ascent, with the rose of Paradise corresponding to the lotus of Sahasrara.

### Chakra 5: Vishuddha — The Razor's Edge

**Traditional attribute**: space/ether, sixteen petals, white elephant, Shiva Ardhanarishvara (half-woman Lord), Sakini  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: The fifth chakra is the "gateway to the Great Liberation" — but it is the most dangerous level of the ascent. Campbell calls it "the razor's edge" and supports this with the Katha Upanishad's warning: "The path [to the Self] is sharpened like a razor; it is difficult to traverse — poets declare — and hard to walk upon."

At this level the seeker encounters the Guardian figures — the threshold guardians of all mythological traditions: the Clashing Rocks of the Odyssey (which Odysseus must pass while his sailors' ears are stopped against the Sirens' song), the armed guardians of the Buddhist temple gate, the cherubim with flaming sword at Eden's gate. The Sirens represent the pull back to the lower three levels — the charm of the projected world.

What makes Vishuddha the razor's edge: it is the point at which consciousness must choose between the familiar world (however painful) and the genuinely unknown that lies above. To proceed requires releasing the last claim on the lower-level identity.

### Chakra 6: Ajna — Vision of Form (*Savikalpa Samadhi*)

**Traditional attribute**: two petals, Hakini, the lingam of lightning, the seed syllable *om*  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: At Ajna, the meditator achieves the state Ramakrishna described as *savikalpa samadhi* — conditioned rapture, in which the divine is encountered as Form. The divine still has attributes, still appears as a being, a light, a figure. Campbell quotes Ramakrishna: "When the mind reaches this plane, one witnesses revelations day and night. One sees God. One hears His voice. One gets into communion with the Indwelling Spirit by talking with Him... yet the mind has not reached the formless state."

This is the level of the mystic's vision — Blake's vision of God in the engravings of Job, Dante's vision of the three circles of the Trinity at the end of the *Paradiso* before the final dissolution, the Christian mystic's encounter with the divine as person. The uraeus serpent that appears on pharaoh's crown at the brow is the sixth-chakra symbol in Egyptian iconography.

Campbell's remarkable insight: the ecstasy of the sixth chakra is still not the final state precisely because it remains conditioned — there is still a subject (the meditator) experiencing an object (the divine form). The seventh chakra requires the dissolution of even this duality.

### Chakra 7: Sahasrara — Dissolution into Ground (*Nirvikalpa Samadhi*)

**Traditional attribute**: the thousand-petalled lotus above the head, shedding rays of the moon; the formless ground of all chakras  
**Campbell's psychological reading**: At Sahasrara, the distinction between subject (meditator) and object (the divine) dissolves. What remains is not a state that can be described in terms of consciousness apprehending something, because there is no longer a separate apprehending consciousness. This is what Meister Eckhart described as the point at which God and soul "are one thing, not merely joined together."

Campbell quotes Eckhart: "The soul that loves God has ceased to exist. She loves God as he is: a not-God, a not-Spirit, a not-Person, a not-Image. More than this, she loves him as he is: a sheer pure limpid One, alien from all duality."

The image at this level is the **Diamond Body** mandala — the mandala that appears in *The Mythic Image* (Figure 349) showing the state of one who has emerged from total absorption in the absolute. The central vajra (thunderbolt/diamond) represents the union of masculine and feminine; the world illusions have vanished; the energy gathered at the centre; the four gates of the courtyard show life energy streaming inward rather than outward toward objects.

Dante's final *Paradiso* passage describes this precisely — the moment before the final vision when "fantasy failed" and "my will and desire were rolled, like a wheel that is moved evenly, by the Love that moves the sun and the other stars."

## Ramakrishna's Account

Campbell's most vivid source for the lived experience of Kundalini ascent is Sri Ramakrishna (1836–1886), the Bengali saint of the Kali temple at Dakshineswar. Ramakrishna described five modes of the Spiritual Current's movement through the chakras:

> "The Spiritual Current rises along the spine, crawling like an ant... The soul swims in the ocean of ecstasy like a fish... There is a current like a monkey... It hops like a bird from branch to branch... [It moves] like a snake, going zigzag; and as it reaches the head, I go into samadhi."

Ramakrishna also described his own first full Kundalini experience in detail — the Current entering the Sushumna nerve, the drooping lotuses rising and opening as it touched them, the touch of the sixteen-petalled lotus at the throat, the two-petalled lotus at the brow, the final opening of the thousand-petalled lotus at the head, and the state of samadhi that followed.

Campbell offers this not as mystical testimony to be believed but as phenomenological evidence for the chakra system's reality as an experiential map.

## The Western Art Reading

Campbell's most original contribution is his demonstration that Western art, philosophy, and mysticism encode the same seven stages when read through the chakra lens:

| Chakra | Western parallel |
|--------|-----------------|
| 1–3 (lower) | The world of political, economic, and social life; ordinary consciousness; the fallen world of Eve/Adam |
| 4 (Heart) | Dante's *La Vita Nuova*; the moment of conversion in Christian mysticism; Beatrice; the troubadour tradition |
| 5 (Throat) | The razor's edge passages in Dante; the Sirens episode in Homer; the mystical desert of Christian tradition |
| 6 (Ajna) | Dante's vision of the Trinity; Blake's vision of the divine chariot; Meister Eckhart's sermons on the soul's encounter with God as Person |
| 7 (Sahasrara) | Dante's final vision (fantasy fails; will and Love are one); Eckhart's "Godhead beyond God"; the mystic death; the Diamond Body mandala |

Blake's image of Job and God (Figure 6 in *The Mythic Image*) — where God is shown with cloven hoofs and wrapped in serpent coils — is, in Campbell's reading, a third-chakra image: the divine as power and law (the ego's projection of divinity). Blake's entire Job series can be read as a chakra ascent from this third-chakra god-image to the final image of Job and his family restored in the dance of life.

## The AUM Analysis

Campbell provides a detailed analysis of the syllable **AUM** as the sonic map of the three states of consciousness and their ground:

- **A**: Waking consciousness — the world of named, separated objects; logic A ≠ not-A; subject apprehending objects; corresponds to chakras 1–3
- **U**: Dream consciousness — subject and object not yet separate; the dreamer creates the dream; gods and mythology; corresponds to chakras 4–5
- **M**: Deep dreamless sleep — the mass of undivided awareness; consciousness without an object; all determinations dissolved; corresponds to chakra 6
- **Silence after**: The fourth state (*turiya*) — the ground from which all three states arise and to which they return; neither being nor non-being; Sahasrara; Nirguna Brahman

Every word, Campbell argues, is a fragment of AUM — a momentary crystallisation of the one sound into a name and form. The meditation practice of intoning AUM is the practice of recognising all words and all things as particles of the one Form dissolving back into its source.

## Connection to Jung's Psychology

Campbell quotes Jung's observation that a woman patient's mandala painting showed a central spiral that she herself described as a snake coiling around a stone — which she had no conscious knowledge was the classical image of Kundalini at Muladhara. A second patient's mandala showed pointing forms indicating an awakening Kundalini. Both patients had arrived at the same symbols independently, without knowledge of the Tantric tradition.

This is Campbell's evidence for Jung's hypothesis: the chakra map describes a real psychological structure that operates independently of cultural transmission. The symbols arise spontaneously in the unconscious because they correspond to genuine stages in the development of consciousness — stages that the psyche traverses whether or not it has been taught the map.

## Significance for the Wiki's Core Framework

This reading by Campbell is the deepest scholarly ground for the chakra-as-energy-medicine system that Myss uses clinically:

1. **The biography-is-biology thesis** becomes, in Campbell's terms: the level at which consciousness is primarily fixed determines which area of the body is under the greatest biographical pressure. Third-chakra fixation creates third-chakra pathology; fourth-chakra deprivation creates heart and lung pathology. The energy pattern precedes and generates the biological expression.

2. **Symbolic sight** is precisely the capacity to read which chakra level a presenting image, symptom, or story is speaking from — and what the deeper pattern it carries might require for its resolution.

3. **The healing journey** from this perspective is an ascent — not from lower to higher in any moral sense, but from fixation at one level's partial reality to the wider awareness available at the next level. Campbell's language for this is not medical but it maps directly onto what Myss calls "biography becoming biology and then becoming spirit."

## Critique

**From mainstream psychology**: The claim that the chakra system maps universal psychological development stages is not supported by empirical research. The supposed parallels between Western mystical experience (Dante, Blake, Eckhart) and Kundalini stages are interpretive — imposed by Campbell's reading rather than inherent in either tradition.

**From the Tantric tradition**: Campbell's psychological reading, while sophisticated, transposes the Tantric chakra system from a ritual and liberation context into a therapeutic-developmental context. Johari's traditional reading makes clear that the chakras are not stages of psychological maturity but levels of cosmic existence from which Kundalini must be *liberated* — the goal is not psychological health but cosmic union. The therapeutic transposition is useful but it is a transposition.

**From comparative religion**: The identification of Dante's *Paradiso* with a Sahasrara experience, or Blake's Job plates with a third-chakra perspective, involves high-level interpretation that the original sources do not authorise. Both traditions had their own very different frameworks within which their imagery was generated.
