# Christ as a Symbol of the Self

**Summary**: Jung's reading of Christ as the historically dominant Western symbol of psychic totality — and of the excluded Antichrist as the shadow-complement that Christian doctrine's exclusion of darkness from the God-image inevitably generated; framed astrologically through the two-thousand-year astrological Age of Pisces.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1968). *Aion: Researches into the phenomenology of the self* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 9, Part 2). Princeton University Press.](../sources/aion.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

## Christ as an Empirical Symbol of the Self

Jung's claim is deliberately narrow and psychological, not theological: he is not asserting that Christ *is* God, or that Christianity is metaphysically true, but that the historical figure of Christ, as elaborated in Christian doctrine, exhibits the same structural features that Jung elsewhere identifies as characteristic of [Self](the-self.md) symbolism — totality, uniqueness, birth from a virgin (a symbol of psychic rather than biological origin), death and resurrection, and identification with the "inner man" (the Pauline *homo coelestis*, the "man according to the spirit" as opposed to the man of flesh).

Key patristic texts Jung marshals: Tertullian's and Origen's readings of Genesis's "image of God" (*imago Dei*) as located in the rational soul rather than the body; Augustine's doctrine that the *imago Dei*, though damaged by the Fall, is restored through Christ, whose totality (*homo totus* — "the whole man," body and soul) makes him the paradigm of the reformed, reintegrated human image. In Jung's reading, this is patristic theology independently arriving at the same structural insight analytical psychology reaches empirically through dream and mandala material: **the goal-image of psychological wholeness takes the symbolic form of a unique, totalizing human/divine figure.**

## Christ's Structural Incompleteness: The Excluded Shadow

Jung's central complication: for a symbol to represent *total* wholeness, it must in principle contain both light and dark — "the symbol of the self [the Self] is not necessarily a totality symbol... [but] in this case an antinomian one, since it embraces both halves." Christ, however, is explicitly defined by the Church as **sine macula peccati** — "without spot of sin" — and by the doctrine of [*privatio boni*](privatio-boni.md), evil is denied any substantial reality that could be included in the first place. The Christ-image, however psychologically powerful, is therefore a symbol of wholeness that structurally *excludes* the shadow.

Jung's argument is that this exclusion does not make the shadow disappear from the collective psyche — it merely displaces it onto a **complementary figure**: the Antichrist.

## The Antichrist as the Return of the Excluded Shadow

Jung reads the New Testament's own anticipation of an Antichrist (1 John 2:22, 2 Thessalonians 2:3-9, Revelation's Apocalypse material) not as a peripheral eschatological detail but as **doctrinally necessary** — the psychological consequence of building a totality-symbol that excludes darkness. "The insistence on the light must, sooner or later, call up the answer of the dark side" — a formulation identical in structure to his shadow-projection argument (see [Shadow](shadow.md) and [Privatio Boni](privatio-boni.md)): whatever is refused conscious integration returns as autonomous, externalized, and dangerous.

Jung traces this enantiodromia (the "running of one extreme into its opposite," a term borrowed from Heraclitus) across the entire Christian era: the vertical, heaven-directed spirituality of the early Church and the Gothic era gradually gives way, from the Renaissance onward, to an increasingly matter-directed, earth-conquering civilization — the voyages of discovery, the Enlightenment, the French Revolution, and the "dechristianization" and "material destruction" of the twentieth century's "World War[s]," which Jung reads as history's own large-scale re-enactment of the excluded Antichrist complex. Writing in 1951, his explicit contemporary examples of this collective shadow eruption are the Nazi and Stalinist regimes — see the fuller discussion at [Privatio Boni § Jung's Critique](privatio-boni.md).

## The Age of Pisces: Astrology as Symbolic Synchronicity

Jung frames this Christ/Antichrist polarity through the astrological symbolism of the fish. Christianity adopted the fish (Ichthys — an acrostic for "Jesus Christ, Son of God, Saviour") as an early identifying symbol at almost exactly the historical moment (calculated variously to around 7 BC–AD 1, depending on the precession model used) that the vernal equinox point entered the zodiacal sign of Pisces, the Fishes — a sign classically depicted as **two fish moving in opposite directions**, joined at a "commissure" or band of stars.

Jung's argument is explicitly **not** that astrology caused this symbolism, nor even that the ancient world was fully conscious of the precession of the equinoxes (a genuine astronomical phenomenon, discovered by Hipparchus). His claim is [synchronistic](synchronicity.md): the archetypal content (Christ and his shadow-complement) and the astronomical/symbolic frame (two fishes, one light and one dark, moving in opposed directions) arose together, as a **meaningful coincidence** rather than a causal relationship — the same acausal-connecting-principle logic Jung develops independently in his synchronicity work with physicist Wolfgang Pauli. He extends the same logic forward, speculating (with considerable historical hedging and multiple competing precession calculations) that the coming Age of Aquarius (the "Water Carrier") will bring its own, still-unknown symbolic transformation.

Jung surveys an enormous range of supporting material: medieval Jewish and Arabic astrological commentary linking the conjunctions of Jupiter and Saturn to the founding of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam (Abarbanel, Albumasar); the apocalyptic expectations of Joachim of Fiore (d. 1202), whose prophesied "Age of the Holy Spirit" Jung reads as an unconscious anticipation of the same symbolic transition; and Nostradamus's sixteenth-century prophecies. **Note**: none of this chronological-astrological material is treated as established by mainstream historiography or astronomy — see the Critique section on [Aion](../sources/aion.md).

## Relation to Myss's Three Ages of Power

Jung's astrological-aeon framework is the direct historical and conceptual precedent for Myss's much simpler [Three Ages of Power](three-ages-of-power.md) schema (Aries = Tribal power, Pisces = Individual power, Aquarius = Symbolic power) in *Why People Don't Heal*. Myss's version strips out nearly all of Jung's technical astrological apparatus and the Christ/Antichrist theology, retaining only the underlying structure: each astrological age corresponds to a dominant *mode* of consciousness and power that supersedes, without fully replacing, the ones before it. Where Jung's aeon-symbolism is primarily diagnostic (explaining the shape of Western religious and political history), Myss's is primarily prescriptive (identifying which "age" of power a given health challenge calls for).

## Relation to the Gnostic Anthropos Material

Jung connects the Christ-as-Self material directly to the Gnostic Anthropos (Primordial Man) speculation catalogued in [Gnostic Quaternios and the Structure of the Self](gnostic-quaternios.md) — both are attempts, in different symbolic vocabularies, to give the Self archetype a personified, totalizing form. The Naassene identification of Christ with the fallen and redeemed Adamas (Man) and the alchemical identification of Christ with the lapis (philosophers' stone) — already treated in [The Self § Symbols of the Self](the-self.md) — are, for Jung, structurally continuous attempts across thirteen centuries to articulate the same underlying archetypal content.

## Critique

**Christological reductionism**: Critics both theological and secular have objected that reading Christ purely as a psychological symbol of the Self, however carefully bracketed as a non-metaphysical claim, functions in practice to reduce a tradition's central truth-claim to an instance of a universal psychic pattern — the same objection raised against Jung's broader Self/God-image equation (see [The Self § Critique](the-self.md)).

**Historicity of the fish-symbolism dating**: Jung's own dating of the Pisces-Christianity correlation shifts across the chapter depending on which precession calculation and which historical source he is following — he acknowledges multiple, mutually inconsistent estimates (from Muenter, Gerhardt, and his own calculations) without settling on one. This uncertainty undercuts the astrological argument's evidentiary weight, though not necessarily Jung's narrower psychological claim about Christ/Antichrist as an archetypal polarity.

**Note**: mainstream New Testament scholarship treats the historical development of Antichrist expectation as explicable through intra-Jewish and early-Christian apocalyptic literary tradition (Daniel, the Qumran material, 2 Thessalonians, Revelation) without reference to astrological synchronicity; Jung's reading supplements rather than displaces this historical-critical account.
