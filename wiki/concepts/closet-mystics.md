# Closet Mystics

**Summary**: Borysenko's term for the large majority of Americans who have had genuine mystical experiences — being "bathed in light," contact with deceased loved ones, NDEs, ESP — but who keep them secret out of fear of ridicule; documented in Andrew Greeley's surveys and Gallup data; understood as evidence that spirituality (direct experience) has outpaced institutional religion (doctrine) in the contemporary West.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1990). *Guilt is the teacher, love is the lesson*. Warner Books.](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md)
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

## The Phenomenon

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) encountered a pattern repeatedly in her Mind/Body Clinic workshops and public lectures: when she asked participants about their inner spiritual lives, people who had said nothing — who presented as sceptical professionals or secular patients — would approach her afterward and quietly describe profound mystical experiences they had never shared with anyone. A vision of a deceased loved one. A moment of "bathing in light" during surgery. A sense of expanded consciousness in nature. A contact at the moment of death.

The fear of sharing these experiences was consistent: ridicule, pathologising, loss of credibility. A psychiatrist who had had a vision of Mary found herself unable to speak about it in professional circles for fear of being diagnosed with lobe epilepsy or psychosis.

Borysenko's term: "a nation of closet mystics."

## The Survey Data

Sociologist [Andrew Greeley](../thinkers/andrew-greeley.md) (University of Chicago, National Opinions Research Council) surveyed Americans in 1973 and again in the mid-1980s. The findings:

- **35%** of Americans in the 1980s reported having experienced "a force that seemed to lift them out of themselves"
- **5%** reported being "bathed in light" — an experience structurally comparable to the apostle Paul's encounter on the road to Damascus (Acts 9)
- **Two-thirds** of adults reported experiences of ESP
- **Half** of adults reported having had contact with a deceased loved one
- The incidence of these experiences had *increased* between 1973 and the 1980s across all categories

Gallup polls confirmed and extended the data:
- **5%** of Americans in 1981 reported having had a near-death experience (NDE) — a figure of 12–15 million people
- **95%** reported believing in God or a Universal Spirit
- **78 million Americans** did not belong to a church or temple by the mid-1980s, up from 61 million in 1978 — the same period in which mystical experiences were increasing

Greeley's crucial finding: people who reported these experiences tended toward above-average mental health, education, and intelligence — not toward psychopathology, credulity, or social instability. Daniel Goleman's assessment (Institute of Noetic Sciences): "We think the evidence points to a nation of closet mystics — it's time to come out of the closet."

## Why They Stay in the Closet

The silence around mystical experience reflects several converging pressures:

**Medical/psychiatric stigma**: The standard psychiatric framework pathologises visual experiences (hallucinations), altered states of consciousness (psychosis), and claims of contact with non-physical beings (delusion). A person with these frameworks cannot share a vision without fearing diagnosis.

**Scientific scepticism**: Western science's default materialist framework has no category for the experiences Greeley's data document. The experiences don't fit the framework; therefore they are treated as anomalies or errors.

**Religious guilt**: Institutional religion — especially the pessimistic strands Borysenko examines — has sometimes actively discouraged mystical experience outside officially sanctioned channels. The medieval church's persecution of mystics (and the 20th-century silencing of Matthew Fox) illustrates that religious institutions can suppress the very experiences they claim to facilitate.

**Social cost**: Being known as someone who has visions or believes in ESP risks professional ridicule, social marginalisation, and the loss of credibility in secular contexts.

## Spirituality vs. Religion

Borysenko cites Rachel Naomi Remen's distinction (important enough to be italicised): *"Religion is dogma — a set of beliefs; spirituality is a set of practices that give rise to experience."*

Walter Houston Clark (professor of psychology of religion, Andover-Newton Theological Seminary) spent decades studying this distinction and identified a pattern: organised religion functions like "a vaccination" — a small, controlled exposure to the sacred that protects against the real thing. Services, rituals, and doctrines can become substitutes for direct experience rather than pathways to it.

Borysenko's own trajectory illustrates the pattern. After a Jewish childhood with rich ritual experience (summer camp Shabbat services in the pine groves) but institutional synagogue experiences that felt empty, she eventually found her spiritual home in the intersection of traditions — Christian mysticism, Tibetan Buddhism, Sufism, and Kabbalah — united by their common thread: the Perennial Philosophy.

Aldous Huxley's Perennial Philosophy: across twenty-five centuries and all major world traditions, a consistent core teaching recurs — the purpose of human life is to realise one's connection to Divine consciousness; this realisation is the source of peace, wisdom, and compassion. Where institutional religion often obscures this teaching beneath doctrine, the closet mystics' experiences are direct encounters with it.

## The Three Eras of Medicine (Dossey) and Non-Local Consciousness

[Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md) provides the framework Borysenko uses to contextualise the mystical experience data within medicine:

- **Era I** (material medicine): consciousness is irrelevant; body is machine
- **Era II** (mind-body medicine): consciousness affects health; the mind's states alter the body's physiology
- **Era III** (non-local medicine): consciousness is not confined to the individual brain; it can affect others at a distance; prayer and intention have measurable effects

The closet mystics' experiences — shared consciousness, contact with the deceased, the sense of being held by a loving Presence — are consistent with Era III premises. Borysenko cites Cardiologist Randolph Byrd's controlled study (CCU patients whose names were given to prayer groups fared measurably better than controls) and the Transcendental Meditation crime-reduction studies (1% of the population meditating apparently reducing local crime statistics) as evidence that the Era III claims deserve investigation.

## Near-Death Experience and the Life Review

The 12–15 million Americans who (by 1981 Gallup estimate) had near-death experiences constitute a significant research population. Borysenko draws on Kenneth Ring's *Heading Toward Omega* and Raymond Moody's *The Light Beyond* to describe the consistent elements:

- Leaving the body and observing it from a distance
- Moving through a tunnel toward light
- Entering a state of overwhelming love
- A life review in which the experiencer re-experiences events *from the perspective of those they affected* — feeling what their actions caused others to feel
- Returning, sometimes unwillingly, to life — changed

The businessman who wept at a flower post-NDE; Marcie, who returned knowing "the point of life is love"; David, who encountered a ball of light in his doorway and was healed of his hip and knee pain — these are Borysenko's clinical cases of the phenomenon.

The NDE consistently produces:
- Loss of fear of death
- Increased compassion and concern for others
- Decreased interest in material achievement and status
- Increased interest in spiritual matters
- Lasting reduction in depression and anxiety

This is not the profile of psychotic episode or medication reaction. It is the profile of profound spiritual conversion — consistent with William James's "healthy-mindedness" transformation and Carl Jung's "conversion" as the cure for soul sickness.

## The Dark Night of the Soul

Not all spiritual paths involve luminous experiences. For many people (including the mystics of the Christian tradition — St. John of the Cross, whose term "dark night of the soul" Borysenko borrows), the journey passes through a period of dryness, depression, and the apparent abandonment of the Divine:

Rabbi Isaac Eizik Komarno (1806–1874) wrote in his diary of a prolonged period in which "dryness of enthusiasm faded, and months passed in which prayer and study became depressing... bitterness passed over my head a thousand times, the bitterness of death." Then, one day, "a light fell upon the house — the light of the Shekhinah resting. And for the first time in my life, I tasted the taste of light."

The dark night is not pathology but the necessary dissolution of the ego's grip before the Self can emerge. This is the psychological death that Borysenko identifies as preceding the rebirth — analogous to Snow White's sleep in the glass coffin (ego-death), from which she is awakened by the prince (compassion).

Borysenko distinguishes this from clinical depression requiring medical treatment; the dark night tends to have an object — the sense of God's absence — whereas clinical depression tends toward objectlessness. The clinical sign is whether the person retains a sense of meaning in the suffering, however painful.

## Grace

Grace is Borysenko's term for the divine energy that moves through the openings created by prayer, surrender, and willingness to change. It is not earned or deserved; it comes unsolicited, like the father running to meet the Prodigal Son "while he was yet a great way off."

The metaphor she uses: a seesaw. Personal effort (therapy, self-awareness, forgiveness practice) can move a person to 49% of the way toward change. The tipping point — the shift from one pattern to another — is grace's work. "You need to move only to 49% — the gift of God provides the rest."

Rachel Naomi Remen's formulation: watching the butter spreader tip on a dish, illustrating that the balance shift requires only the slightest additional leverage, and that leverage is not always personal effort.

The winds of grace, as the sage Ramakrishna taught, are always blowing. The practice is raising the sails.

## Connections to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Medical Intuition](medical-intuition.md)**: Borysenko's "closet mystics" with their intuitive, perceptual, and visionary experiences exist on the same spectrum as [Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s medical intuitive capacities and [Edgar Cayce](../thinkers/edgar-cayce.md)'s trance readings. All three represent the cultural suppression and clinical undervaluation of non-ordinary modes of knowing with potentially significant health and healing applications.

**[Prayer and Healing](prayer-and-healing.md)** (Shealy, Dossey): Byrd's CCU prayer study and the TM crime-reduction data cited by Borysenko are examples of the same research tradition that Dossey and Shealy document. The closet mystics' experiences are the individual experiential counterpart to this research: millions of people with direct knowledge of non-local consciousness who lack the framework to interpret or share it.

**[Spiritual Healing Traditions](spiritual-healing-traditions.md)** (Shealy): the cross-cultural survey of mystical and healing experience that Shealy documents in *Sacred Healing* describes, from an academic and historical perspective, what Borysenko is documenting sociologically: the universality of these experiences across cultures, traditions, and centuries.

## NDE Research Deepened: Fire in the Soul (Borysenko, 1993)

In [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md), the NDE material receives far richer treatment. The detailed account of **Donna Elsten** — who kept written notes of her two NDEs — provides the most fully documented single NDE in Borysenko's work. Donna describes the entire progression: leaving the body, the tunnel, entering the light, a total-acceptance love "not of the body," meeting the Supreme Being of Light face-to-face, a gifting of understanding, the experience of eternity and the irrelevance of physical death, the difficulty of returning, and the permanent transformation of her relationship to life.

**[Melvin Morse](../thinkers/melvin-morse.md)**'s pediatric NDE research (children resuscitated from cardiac arrest or coma) adds a dimension absent from Greeley's adult survey data: children report the same structural elements as adults — tunnel, light, beings — but without the cultural overlay that might shape adult accounts. The case of Terry (overdose, coma) is representative: a branching tunnel, an arch, and emergence into a light the child called "the light" — representing "love, peace, happiness, joy." None of Morse's child NDE survivors used drugs or alcohol as teenagers; they understood that substances "dim the light."

**Prayer efficacy** receives empirical grounding: cardiologist **Randolph Byrd**'s double-blind CCU study (400 heart attack patients, *Southern Medical Journal*, 1988) demonstrated that patients receiving distant intercessory prayer required fewer ventilators, had less pulmonary oedema, and needed fewer antibiotics than controls — neither patients nor staff knew who was prayed for. The **Spindrift Foundation**'s seed-germination research found that non-directed prayer ("Thy will be done") was more effective than directed prayer (specifying the desired outcome) in restoring vitality to salt-damaged seeds. Together these studies extend the "closet mystic" framework into the domain of controlled empirical research — suggesting that the non-local consciousness the mystics experience may have measurable effects in the physical world.

See also: [Near-Death Experiences](near-death-experiences.md) for fuller treatment of the NDE evidence.

## Critique

**Survey methodology**: Greeley's surveys rely on self-report and are vulnerable to social desirability effects (people reporting experiences they think the surveyor wants to hear or, conversely, underreporting to avoid appearing credulous). The question of what constitutes a "mystical experience" is not operationalised consistently across studies.

**Selection bias**: The people who shared their experiences with Borysenko in workshops were already attending her events — a self-selected population with some interest in the intersection of health and spirituality. The "closet mystic" pattern may be more common in this population than in the general population.

**Interpretation**: Greeley's data documents that people report these experiences; it does not confirm what the experiences are. A neuroscientific interpretation (temporal lobe activity, REM intrusion, hypoxia during surgery) and a mystical interpretation (genuine contact with transcendent reality) are both consistent with the survey data. Borysenko acknowledges this without resolving it, which is intellectually honest but leaves the core claim open.
