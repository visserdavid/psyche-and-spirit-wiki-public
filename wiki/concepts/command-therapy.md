# Command Therapy

**Summary**: A six-step visualisation and feeling-tone technique developed by W. Brugh Joy in 1973 — received in meditation — for supporting the body's healing of disease by combining deep relaxation, recall of wellbeing, present-moment feeling activation, and detailed mental imagery of recovery.

**Sources**:
- [Joy, W. B. (1979). *Joy's way: A map for the transformational journey — An introduction to the potentials for healing with body energies*. J. P. Tarcher.](../sources/joys-way.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Origins

Joy developed Command Therapy in spring 1973 through dialogue with what he calls his Inner Teacher — received in meditation as a six-step sequence for using imagery to support physical healing. He named it Command Therapy because the technique uses the mind to "command" the body's healing processes, consistent with the principle he attributes to Einstein's equation (E=mc²) that energy, consciousness, and matter exist in a convertible relationship: thought is energy, and energy can configure matter.

The technique draws on principles used in Christian Science, Religious Science, and Science of Mind — the metaphysical traditions teaching that mental imagery can alter physical conditions. Joy's version is distinguished by its clinical origins and its specific progression through feeling tone (not just visualisation) as the primary activating mechanism.

---

## The Six Steps

**Step One: Relax**
Enter a state of physical relaxation. Joy suggests imagery of butter melting in noonday sun. The key indicator is body sensation — feeling the tension leave — not the idea of relaxation. Studies cited by Joy show that anxiety is not a feeling-state produced by the psyche but a product of bodily tension; relaxing the body removes the substrate of anxiety.

**Step Two: Recapture wellbeing**
Retrieve from memory an experience of feeling healthy, vital, and alive. Use this memory as a template to flood the body with the sense of wellbeing — what Joy calls "feeling tone." The goal is not to think about the memory but to re-enter the feeling state it produced. Literature cited by Joy shows that depression, stress, and unhappiness weaken immune function (particularly NK cell activity and antibody production); the reverse — a sense of wellbeing — stimulates these mechanisms.

**Step Three: Make the feeling present**
Transpose the feeling tone from the memory into the present moment. Not "I remember feeling well" but "I feel well now." The memory is used only to guide awareness into a feeling state that is then activated in the present body. Joy argues this is the key step: taking advantage of the power of imagery to produce bodily responses through the autonomic nervous system and immune mechanisms.

**Step Four: Know the healing is occurring**
Trust the knowing that the healing process is underway — specifically in the area of disease or dysfunction. Focus the feeling of wellbeing directly on the affected area or areas. Joy distinguishes this from thinking about healing ("don't think, feel") and from directing the immune system specifically (which the conscious mind does not know how to do). The instruction is simply to feel wellbeing in the area and trust the body's inherent healing intelligence.

**Step Five: Visualise improvement**
See and feel the disease diminishing and healthy tissue replacing it. The imagery does not need to be anatomically precise — the body knows its own anatomy. Joy suggests talking to the body, explaining what is needed, and then allowing the imagery to arise of tissue healing and disease resolving. For cancer patients using Command Therapy alongside chemotherapy or radiation, Joy instructs them to visualise the medical treatment as effective, to direct it mentally toward the diseased area, and to command the body to accept the treatment without side effects.

**Step Six: Visualise normal activity**
See yourself fully recovered and engaged in normal life — walking, running, swimming, playing, working. Use sensory detail: see the faces of friends and family, feel the body moving, experience the emotions of recovery. Joy emphasises concluding with a felt sense of wholeness, harmony, and connection.

## Practice Schedule

Joy specifies three sessions per day:
- On waking (morning)
- Midday
- Before sleep

Each session should last approximately one to three minutes. Joy cautions against fatigue from over-practising — the technique should remain energising, not draining. He recommends starting with all six steps but notes that as the body responds and wellbeing increases, the morning session may become a single brief reinforcement.

## Mechanism

Joy frames the mechanism in terms of the mind-body-immune axis established by contemporary psychoneuroimmunology:

- **Immune suppression via stress**: prolonged worry, depression, and tension reduce NK cell activity, lymphocyte proliferation, and antibody production. Command Therapy addresses this by replacing the stress state with a felt sense of wellbeing.
- **BCG analogy**: Joy notes that one cancer treatment (BCG stimulation) works by activating the immune system generally to attack cancer cells. He proposes that Command Therapy may activate the same mechanisms through a mind-body pathway.
- **Side-effect reduction**: He reports that cancer patients who used Command Therapy alongside chemotherapy and radiation often required shorter treatment courses and reported fewer side effects — which he attributes to the body being commanded to accept rather than resist treatment.

These claims are presented as clinical observations, not controlled trials.

## Relationship to Other Healing Visualisation Approaches

Command Therapy belongs to a cluster of mind-body healing visualisation techniques that emerged in the 1970s:

- **Simonton method**: [O. Carl Simonton](../thinkers/o-carl-simonton.md)'s imagery technique (described in *Getting Well Again*, 1978) uses visualisation of immune cells attacking cancer cells — a more aggressive, combat-oriented imagery framework. Joy's approach is more permissive: the body is trusted to know what it needs; the practitioner provides feeling tone and intent, not battle imagery.
- **Brennan's healing**: [Barbara Ann Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md)'s healing techniques operate on the auric field from outside the patient, whereas Command Therapy is a self-directed internal technique.

Both the Simonton method and Command Therapy pre-date the formal development of psychoneuroimmunology as a field (Ader and Cohen, 1975), yet align with PNI's central finding that immune function is responsive to psychological states.

---

## Critique

**Evidence base**: Command Therapy was never subjected to controlled clinical trials. Joy's reports of tumour stabilisation and regression, and reduced chemotherapy requirements, are anecdotal case observations from an uncontrolled clinical setting. The "99% success rate" in training participants refers to feeling body energy fields, not to therapeutic outcomes from Command Therapy.

**The side-effect claim**: The assertion that patients can command their bodies to accept chemotherapy without side effects is not supported by clinical evidence and should not be adopted as a substitute for evidence-based antiemetic and supportive care protocols.

**Positive-thinking risk**: As with all visualisation-based healing approaches, Command Therapy risks being read as implying that illness persistence is a failure of visualisation effort — a route to patient self-blame. Joy explicitly addresses this by situating Command Therapy within the Transformational Process framework: if the underlying soul-level pattern is not changing, no technique will produce cure.

**Note**: Mainstream oncology does not include Command Therapy or equivalent mind-directed visualisation techniques as primary treatments for cancer or other serious illness. The technique may have value as a complementary support for wellbeing and coping, but should not be substituted for evidence-based medical treatment.
