# Communitas

**Summary**: Victor Turner's term for the intense, direct, egalitarian bond that arises among people who pass through [liminality](liminality.md) together — set against "structure" (society's normal, differentiated, role-bound organization) as its complementary dialectical partner, not its opposite or replacement.

**Sources**:
- [Turner, V. (1969). *The ritual process: Structure and anti-structure*. Aldine Publishing.](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-14

## Structure and Anti-Structure

Turner's central claim in *[The Ritual Process](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)* is that society is not a single fixed thing but a **process** that continually alternates between two modalities:

- **Structure**: society viewed as a differentiated, hierarchical system of politico-legal-economic positions, with many types of evaluation separating people in terms of "more" or "less."
- **Communitas** (Turner deliberately avoids the vaguer word "community," reserving it for the modality of relationship rather than any concrete group): society viewed as an unstructured or minimally structured community of equals — often, quite literally, the fellowship that forms among people confined together in [liminality](liminality.md).

Turner borrows Martin Buber's language of the I-Thou encounter to describe communitas: a relationship between concrete, historical individuals, not between role-players occupying positions in a system. He illustrates the idea with the Taoist image of the cartwheel — the spokes (structure) are useless without the empty hub (communitas) around which they turn; neither element is dispensable, and neither is more "real" than the other.

**No society can exist purely on one register.** Exaggeration of structure alone produces rigidity and oppression; exaggeration of communitas alone produces "the anarchic type of movement, or the ecstatic type of individual religious experience," and both tend toward eventual re-structuring, since even the most anti-structural movement requires organization, resources, and authority to sustain itself over time. Turner frames the relationship as a genuine dialectic: people pass out of structure into communitas and back into a structure now revitalized by the experience of communitas.

## Three Modalities of Communitas

Turner distinguishes:

1. **Spontaneous** (or existential) **communitas** — the direct, unplanned, "happening" quality of a moment of felt togetherness (Turner's example: 1960s hippie gatherings, "happenings," rock concerts).
2. **Normative communitas** — spontaneous communitas organized into a perduring social system, once the group has to mobilize resources and structure roles to sustain itself over time.
3. **Ideological communitas** — a set of theoretical formulations or utopian models of what a society based on communitas *would* look like (Turner's literary example: Gonzalo's egalitarian commonwealth speech in Shakespeare's *The Tempest*).

## Historical Case Studies

Turner traces the same structure-communitas dialectic through two extended historical examples:

- **The Franciscan poverty movement**: St. Francis of Assisi's radical embrace of poverty and nakedness — modeled explicitly on Christ's own homelessness — is read by Turner as an attempt to institutionalize permanent liminality (Francis conceived his friars as perpetual "liminars," between-and-betwixt any fixed place in the world). Within a century of Francis's death the order split into **Conventuals**, who accommodated structure and property (*dominium*) to sustain a growing, learned religious order, and **Spirituals**, who insisted on Francis's original *usus pauper* ("poor use") and were eventually suppressed by the Inquisition — a textbook case, in Turner's account, of communitas being reabsorbed by structure.
- **The Sahajiya-Vaishnava devotional movement** (Bengal, 14th–17th centuries), centered on Chaitanya's ecstatic Krishna-devotion: love between Krishna and the cowherdess Radha, structurally "illicit" (Radha is *parakiya*, another's wife), is elevated as the highest form of devotion precisely *because* it stands outside the sanctioned structure of marriage and caste — a direct parallel, Turner argues, to St. Francis's Lady Poverty as love that dissolves rather than confirms worldly bonds. The movement, too, eventually split between an "orthodox" branch reabsorbed into caste Hinduism and antinomian successors (the wandering Baul musicians, whom Turner connects to 20th-century folk revival figures like Bob Dylan).

## Communitas Beyond Ritual: Millenarian and Countercultural Movements

Turner extends communitas to social movements more broadly: prophetic and millenarian movements recruiting the "structurally inferior and marginal," and — writing in 1969 — contemporary hippie communities, which he reads as a modern, voluntary attempt to manufacture the conditions of communitas (through drugs, rock music, and the deliberate abandonment of status-bound dress, work, and possessions) in a society that no longer provides an institutionalized liminal phase for its members. He is careful to note that hippie communitas is *existential*, not tribal-obligatory: no elder or communal structure holds or authorizes it, distinguishing it from Ndembu liminality's socially sanctioned frame.

## Critique

**Idealization of communitas.** Turner's own anthropological successors have criticized *The Ritual Process* for painting communitas in exclusively positive terms — as egalitarian fellowship, love, and freedom from structure's "meanness, theft, adultery, and witchcraft." Critics note that real liminal or anti-structural periods (carnival, revolution, mob violence, war) can just as easily produce scapegoating, coercion, and violence rather than fellowship; Turner's own Ndembu material (the reviling of the chief-elect, described in [Liminality](liminality.md)) already shows that "communitas" can shade into something closer to licensed cruelty.

**Utopian generalization.** As with liminality, critics have questioned whether concepts drawn from small-scale kin-based societies (Ndembu, Tallensi) and a handful of historical religious movements (Franciscans, Sahajiyas) can bear the weight of a universal theory of "society" as such — particularly Turner's suggestion that industrialized, large-scale societies retain only vestigial, weakened traces of communitas compared to traditional ritual life.

**Connection to Jungian individuation.** Turner himself draws a passing parallel between the Sahajiya ritual's symbolic union of male and female and the Jungian idea that "union with the Great Mother archetype... precedes wholeness [and] individuation" — a genuine, if brief, point of contact with the wiki's [Individuation](individuation.md) material, though Turner's frame is social (communitas as a property of *groups*) where Jung's is intrapsychic (individuation as a property of the *single* psyche's relationship to its own contents). [James Hillman](../thinkers/james-hillman.md)'s critique of Jung's Self as unifying "monotheism" (see [Polytheistic Psychology](polytheistic-psychology.md)) offers an interesting further parallel: Turner's structure/communitas dialectic, like Hillman's structure/anti-structure of the psyche, refuses to grant either pole final authority over the other.
