# Sol, Luna, and the Alchemical Coniunctio

**Summary**: Jung's reading of the alchemical "chemical wedding" — Sol and Luna, King and Queen, Adam and Eve — as a symbolic map of the union of consciousness and the unconscious; the coniunctio's characteristic sequence of attraction, wounding, death, and rebirth models the psychological cost and reward of integrating a repressed opposite.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1970). *Mysterium coniunctionis: An inquiry into the separation and synthesis of psychic opposites in alchemy* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 14). Princeton University Press.](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md)

## The Coniunctio as Structure

**Coniunctio** (Latin: conjunction, union) is alchemy's term for the culminating operation of the *opus* — the joining of two previously separated and opposed substances into a single, transformed third thing. Jung treats it as alchemy's central symbol because it recurs at every level of the tradition simultaneously:

- **Chemical**: the literal combination of two reagents (sulphur and mercury, fixed and volatile)
- **Astrological**: the conjunction of Sun and Moon
- **Mythological/personified**: the marriage of King and Queen, or of Sol and Luna as divine consorts, or of Adam and Eve
- **Psychological** (Jung's addition): the union of consciousness (ego) with the unconscious — ultimately, the union of ego with the [Self](the-self.md)

Because these levels were never distinguished by the alchemists themselves, a single image — the "chemical wedding" — carries all four meanings at once. Jung's method throughout [*Mysterium Coniunctionis*](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md) is to take that indistinction seriously: rather than translating alchemical imagery into modern psychological language and discarding the original, he treats the alchemical text itself as already containing the psychological insight, expressed in the only vocabulary its authors had.

## Sol and Luna

**Sol** (the Sun) represents, in Jung's reading, the masculine, active, conscious principle — light, gold, the king, spirit. **Luna** (the Moon) represents the feminine, receptive, unconscious principle — silver, the queen, soul, changeability. The pairing is not simply "consciousness vs. unconscious" in the abstract; alchemical texts consistently give Luna a **dangerous, wounding character**: she is likened to a poisonous serpent or a rabid dog whose bite kills Sol, echoed in the recurring image of the sun "eclipsed," "slain," or "consumed" in the embrace of the moon.

Jung reads this wounding motif as psychologically exact: when a one-sidedly conscious, "solar" attitude is finally forced into genuine relationship with the unconscious it has excluded, the encounter is not comfortable integration but something closer to a **death** — the ego's previous, simplified self-image cannot survive contact with what it has repressed. This is the same insight Jung develops independently in his treatment of the [Shadow](shadow.md) (the shadow's ethical complexity, per von Franz's Moses/Khidr example) and in [Individuation](individuation.md)'s account of Phase 2 ("Encounter with the Shadow") — but here it receives its fullest symbolic elaboration through the Sol/Luna wounding-and-death sequence, and its resolution: **only after this "death" does a "child" (the union's offspring) appear**, representing the newly synthesized state.

### Luna and the Church (Ecclesia as Luna)

Jung traces a sustained parallel between alchemical Luna and the Christian image of the **Church as Moon** (drawing on Hugo Rahner's *Mysterium Lunae* scholarship): the Church, like the moon, generates no light of its own but reflects Christ's (Sol's); it also waxes and wanes, its historical fortunes rising and falling, just as Luna is periodically "darkened" or "eclipsed." Jung treats this not as coincidence but as evidence that Christian dogma and alchemical symbolism were independently working out the same underlying archetypal problem — the relationship between a stable, luminous principle (Christ/Sol/consciousness) and a changeable, receptive one (Church/Luna/soul) — at different levels of doctrinal sophistication.

## Rex and Regina (King and Queen)

The **Rex** (King) and **Regina** (Queen) personify the same opposition in explicitly political and social terms. Key motifs Jung traces:

- **The King's death and dismemberment**: alchemical texts repeatedly depict the King dying, being cut into pieces, and being "cooked" or dissolved before he can be reborn in renewed, "regenerated" form (often via the *Cantilena* of the 15th-century English alchemist George Ripley). Jung reads the King's death as the necessary collapse of an inflated, one-sidedly rational ego-consciousness that has lost touch with its own unconscious sources of renewal.
- **The King's "dark side"**: alongside the King's association with gold, spirit, and legitimate authority, alchemical texts also give him a shadow aspect — tyranny, sterility, the "old king" who must die precisely because he can no longer generate anything new. This maps directly onto [Individuation](individuation.md)'s account of persona-identification: a "royal" (inflated, identified-with-the-collective-role) ego must be dethroned before genuine development can resume.
- **The King as Anthropos**: at his most positive, the King is identified with the alchemical **Anthropos** — the primordial, total Man — connecting Rex/Regina directly to the Adam material below and to [The Self](the-self.md).

## Adam and Eve, and the Anthropos

Jung's chapter on Adam treats him as the alchemical **Anthropos**: an originally androgynous, undivided totality-being who only later differentiates into Adam and Eve, male and female, consciousness and its opposite. This is structurally identical to the [Child Archetype](child-archetype.md)'s "hermaphroditism" (the Divine Child "precedes the differentiation of opposites... its wholeness is a symbol of what the adult must recover through integration") — the same claim, made twice, once about the beginning of a human life and once about the beginning of the cosmos. Both point to the same underlying archetype: totality is prior to differentiation, and psychological maturity is not the avoidance of opposites but their conscious re-union after having been genuinely differentiated. This is the core logic of [The Self](the-self.md) as both origin and goal of individuation.

## The Psychological Interpretation: Transference as Living Coniunctio

Jung explicitly connects the coniunctio symbolism to clinical psychotherapy, citing his own earlier study *The Psychology of the Transference* (1946) as the direct clinical analogue: the phenomenon of transference in analysis — where unconscious contents of the patient are projected onto the analyst, and vice versa — reproduces the same structural pattern as the alchemical "chemical wedding" between adept and matter. The alchemist's absorbed, often obsessive relationship to his substance, Jung argues, was itself an early, unrecognized form of the transference relationship — projection of unconscious psychic content onto an external object (whether analyst or retort) as a necessary stage before that content can be consciously reclaimed.

## Relevance to This Wiki

### Extends the Sacred Contracts / Archetypal Wheel Symbolism

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s [Sacred Contracts](sacred-contracts.md) system, and the wider wiki's treatment of paired or complementary energetic principles (masculine/feminine chakra polarities, the [Shekhinah](shekhinah.md)/Tif'eret sacred marriage in the [Zohar](../sources/zohar-book-of-enlightenment.md)), share the coniunctio's basic logic: healing or wholeness is figured as the reunion of a separated pair, not the elimination of one side in favour of the other. The Kabbalistic Shekhinah-in-exile awaiting reunion with Tif'eret and the alchemical Luna awaiting reunion with Sol are, in Jung's own account, historically and structurally cognate images — Jung cites Kabbalistic material (Malchuth as Luna and widow) directly alongside alchemical sources in his argument.

### The Wounding Precedes the Healing

The Sol/Luna wounding motif gives a precise symbolic vocabulary to a claim already implicit across this wiki's illness literature: that confrontation with a previously excluded or denied part of the self (whether Jung's shadow, Myss's [woundology](woundology.md), or the "dark night" motifs in [Fire in the Soul](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)) is not incidental to healing but its necessary first stage. The alchemical sequence — attraction, wounding, death, rebirth — is a more granular map of the same arc [Individuation](individuation.md) describes as "Encounter with the Shadow."

## Critique

**Symbolic overreach**: As with the wider critique of *Mysterium Coniunctionis* (see the [source page](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md)), Jung's reading of Sol/Luna, Rex/Regina, and Adam/Eve material as *encoded psychology* rather than genuine chemical, astrological, or theological content in its own right is an interpretive commitment that cannot be independently verified. Historians of alchemy have argued this systematically discounts what practitioners themselves believed they were doing.

**Gendered essentialism**: The mapping of masculine=consciousness/solar and feminine=unconscious/lunar reproduces the same mid-twentieth-century gender binary already flagged as a limitation in [Anima and Animus](anima-animus.md) and in [Marie-Louise von Franz](../thinkers/marie-louise-von-franz.md)'s work. Contemporary Jungian scholarship treats these associations as historically contingent products of the source material rather than universal psychological law.
