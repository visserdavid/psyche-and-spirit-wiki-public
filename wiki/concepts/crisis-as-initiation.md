# Crisis as Initiation

**Summary**: Borysenko's central reframe in *Fire in the Soul* — that personal catastrophes (illness, loss, addiction, trauma, depression) are not punishments or failures but rites of passage analogous to the initiation rites documented by anthropologist Victor Turner; drawing on Turner's liminality, Joseph Campbell's night sea journey, Viktor Frankl's logotherapy, Stanislav Grof's perinatal stages, and shamanic soul-retrieval traditions.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## The Core Reframe

The dominant cultural framework for understanding crisis — illness, bankruptcy, divorce, addiction, depression, trauma — is pathological: something has gone wrong and must be fixed. The person is broken; the goal is restoration to the prior state.

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) proposes a different frame, grounded in anthropology, depth psychology, and the shamanic traditions of cultures worldwide: **crisis is initiation**. The dark night of the soul is not an interruption of the journey; it is the journey. What looks like failure or punishment from within the crisis is, from a larger perspective, a rite of passage — the soul's passage from one identity to a larger one.

This reframe does not minimise the pain of crisis. Borysenko is explicit: what she describes as "the night sea journey" includes states of genuine terror, disorientation, and despair. The reframe changes what the suffering *means*, not what it *feels like*. And that change in meaning — Viktor Frankl demonstrated from Auschwitz — is the pivotal variable in whether a person survives or is destroyed.

## Victor Turner: Liminality and Rites of Passage

Anthropologist [Victor Turner](../thinkers/victor-turner.md) studied the Ndembu people of Zambia and wrote extensively on how traditional cultures handle life transitions. His model of the rite of passage consists of three phases:

1. **Separation**: the initiate is removed from their previous role, identity, and social position. The vestiges of the old self must be stripped away.

2. **Liminality**: the threshold state — literally, "being on the threshold." The initiate is *mwadi* — a Ndembu word meaning "one who is between." Turner describes this as a state of invisibility, wandering, and formlessness: the initiate is neither what they were nor what they will become. It is a womb-state, a chrysalis. The defining quality is that normal definitions do not apply: the initiate exists outside the social categories that normally provide identity.

3. **Reincorporation**: the return to society with a new identity, new knowledge, and new authority derived from having survived the liminal threshold.

Turner's crucial observation: the liminal state strips the initiate of everything that provided false security — social role, accumulated possessions, ego identity — leaving only what is irreducibly real. This stripping is not punishment; it is the prerequisite for the larger identity that follows.

**The Western absence of this framework**: contemporary psychiatric and medical culture has no equivalent category. The person in crisis is "disordered" — a diagnostic label that reinforces the pathology frame. The psychiatrist, therapist, or physician operating without the initiation framework tends to work *against* the process, seeking to restore the prior state rather than holding the liminal space until the new identity can emerge.

Borysenko's twelve-step recovery programs as a corrective: the language of "bottoming out" and "surrendering to a Higher Power" is the language of liminality. The sponsor who has been through the same darkness becomes the Turner-style "liminal elder" who can hold the space of the mwadi without panic.

## Campbell's Night Sea Journey

[Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md) describes the hero's journey structure that recurs across all world mythologies. The central phase — which he calls the "night sea journey" — involves the hero descending into darkness (the belly of the whale, the underworld, the forest of trials) and encountering the forces of the unconscious before re-emerging transformed.

In *The Power of Myth*, Campbell identifies Jonah in the whale's belly as the archetypal night sea journey: the whale represents "the power of life locked in the unconscious." To be swallowed is to encounter the full force of what has been avoided. The resurrection that follows is not a return to the prior state but the emergence of a genuinely different person — one who has, as Campbell says, "found their bliss" in the place of deepest fear.

Borysenko's childhood neurosis (age ten) maps directly: the ten months of OCD, hallucinations of headhunters and snakes, compulsive rituals — a descent into the "water world of the unconscious." The poem she wrote during the worst of it, "Light," was a communication from a level of consciousness that survived intact through the ordeal. Her emergence from the neurosis without medication or conventional therapy was, she recognises in retrospect, the primary initiation that made her work as a healer possible.

## Viktor Frankl: Meaning as the Survival Variable

[Viktor Frankl](../thinkers/viktor-frankl.md) survived four Nazi concentration camps (Auschwitz, Dachau, and others) and founded logotherapy — a psychotherapy whose premise is that the primary human drive is not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler) but *meaning*.

From the camps, Frankl observed that the variable predicting survival was not physical health, social status, or prior psychological resilience, but the presence or absence of a *reason to live* — some meaning, purpose, relationship, or task the person oriented toward. Those who lost their sense of meaning — who ceased to believe in a future, a task, a person who needed them — deteriorated rapidly. Those who found or maintained meaning endured conditions that destroyed others.

Logotherapy's insight: suffering that cannot be eliminated can still be transformed if the person can find a meaning in it. This is not rationalization; it is the fundamental psychological act of *orientation*. The initiation frame provides exactly this: a meaning-context for suffering. The person who understands themselves as being initiated — not punished, not broken, but being forged — has access to a different set of psychological resources than the person who understands themselves as a random victim.

Frankl's dictum: "Those who have a *why* to live can bear almost any *how*."

Borysenko links Frankl directly to [Abraham Maslow](../thinkers/abraham-maslow.md)'s concept of peak experiences and self-actualisation: Maslow spoke of the "metapathology" — the suffering caused by a thwarted need for meaning, distinct from neurotic suffering (anxiety, guilt) and physical suffering (pain). Jack, a patient in his forties who had everything — money, family, stability — and yet felt "I'm not living" was suffering the metapathology: the meaning drive was blocked. He needed not therapy but initiation into a larger purpose.

## Stanislav Grof: The Birth-Rebirth Stages

[Stanislav Grof](../thinkers/stanislav-grof.md) was selected by the Sandoz pharmaceutical company in the 1950s to investigate the therapeutic applications of LSD-25. Over a decade of research, he discovered that under careful therapeutic conditions, participants consistently re-experienced what he called "perinatal matrices" — states of consciousness organised around the stages of biological birth, which also corresponded to death-and-rebirth motifs in world mythology, shamanic ritual, and near-death experience.

**Four stages of the perinatal process:**

1. **Stage 1: Cosmic Engulfment** — onset characterised by overwhelming anxiety, imagery of being sucked into a vortex or swallowed by a beast (Jonah and the whale). Corresponds to the moment before labour begins.

2. **Stage 2: No Exit / Hell** — claustrophobic constriction; no exit is visible; the person feels trapped, victimised, hopeless. Corresponds to contractions before the cervix opens. Grof's research subjects described this as "hell."

3. **Stage 3: Death-Rebirth Struggle** — the cervix opens; propulsion through the birth canal; cataclysmic imagery (wars, disasters, forces of nature). Agony and ecstasy coexist. The critical difference from Stage 2: *there is now hope*. The suffering is recognised as purposeful.

4. **Stage 4: Death and Rebirth** — expulsion; complete ego death; blinding light; liberation; what Grof's subjects described as experiences of salvation, moksha, samadhi. The prior self is gone; a new one has emerged.

Borysenko applies these stages to the experiences she has observed in therapy, dark nights of the soul, addictions, and trauma recovery. The person who knows they are in Stage 2 — not permanently trapped, but in the narrowest part of the passage — can endure what they otherwise might not.

**Ka-Tzetnik 135633** (Yehiel De Nur): A survivor of Auschwitz who could not testify at the Eichmann trial without collapsing, and who lived thirty years with untreated PTSD. Five sessions of LSD therapy with Professor Jan Bastiaans at Leiden allowed him to relive the trauma at cellular depth, encounter the divine name (*shivitti* — "I set the Lord before me") superimposed on the Auschwitz horizon, and ultimately pass through a Grof Stage 4 experience — his mother's soul rising as a phoenix from the crematorium flames, the S.S. officer and the prisoner both recognised as co-participants in a divine mystery beyond human comprehension. The therapy healed the soul split that had kept him from writing in the first person (his books had been published under his prisoner number, with himself referred to in the third person).

**Holotropic Breathwork**: Grof and his wife Christina Grof developed this as a non-pharmacological method for accessing the same states — controlled rapid breathing induces altered consciousness in which the perinatal process can be engaged therapeutically. It is now practised internationally and is used in spiritual emergency cases.

## Shamanic Soul Retrieval

Shamanic traditions in cultures worldwide share a common diagnosis for certain forms of illness and psychological distress: *soul loss*. Trauma, grief, abuse, or extreme fear can cause part of the soul to leave the body — to remain "stuck" in the space and time of the traumatic event, unable to move forward.

The shaman's role: to enter the imaginal realm (Henry Corbin's term — see [Past-Life Therapy](past-life-therapy.md)) on behalf of the patient, locate the lost soul fragment, and retrieve it through ritual, chant, and intention.

Borysenko describes a 1985 earthquake in Mexico after which conventional medicine and therapy failed to help PTSD survivors; a female healer-housewife who diagnosed soul loss and worked with shamanic methods (including ice-water shocks to reawaken emotion and then careful holding) achieved recoveries that doctors could not. The method worked by helping the person relive the trauma enough to retrieve the frozen emotional state — analogous to what Bastiaans achieved with Ka-Tzetnik through LSD.

The parallel to modern trauma therapy: trauma specialists (following Judith Herman and Peter Levine) have arrived at similar conclusions through completely different routes — the body stores the trauma and must be engaged somatically; cognitive reframing alone is insufficient. The shamanic understanding of soul loss and retrieval is a pre-scientific articulation of the same clinical observation.

## The Inner Self Helper

Research on multiple personality disorder (MPD) in the 1970s and 80s uncovered a phenomenon that [Willis Harman](../thinkers/willis-harman.md) observed corresponds exactly to descriptions of the Self in Indian philosophical texts. Psychiatrist Ralph Allison discovered that within MPD systems — where the personality is fragmented into multiple alters by severe childhood trauma — there was invariably one alter with unique properties:

- No fear, guilt, hatred, anger, or depression
- Access to the full history of the system
- A quality Allison called "conduit for God's love"
- Described by Allison as having existed before birth and as continuing to exist after death

Allison called this the **Inner Self Helper**. The Inner Self Helper corresponds directly to what Jung called the Self, what Assagioli called the Higher Self in psychosynthesis, what the Upanishads describe as the *Atman* — the irreducible, unconditional core of consciousness that survives all the fragmentation the personality can undergo.

Borysenko's significance: the Inner Self Helper demonstrates that even under conditions of extreme trauma — the most radical fragmentation the personality can experience — the Self remains intact. The night sea journey never threatens the Self; it only threatens the ego's claim to be the whole person.

## Critique

**The initiation frame and severe trauma**: For survivors of severe trauma (torture, genocide, childhood abuse), the suggestion that their experience was an "initiation" can feel like minimisation or even victim-blaming. The Ka-Tzetnik material is handled with care, but the framework risks being applied too quickly to experiences that require primarily witness, validation, and safety — not reframing.

**Empirical status**: Grof's birth-stage model is clinically observed but not established through controlled research. The claim that the birth experience leaves memories that can be therapeutically accessed is biologically plausible (given what is now known about implicit and procedural memory) but not demonstrated in ways that mainstream psychiatry accepts.

**Cultural specificity**: Turner's model of liminality is derived from traditional societies with clear communal structures and elder-held rites of passage. Contemporary Western individuals undergoing crisis often lack the elder support, communal container, and cultural framework that make rites of passage transformative rather than simply chaotic. The initiation frame may work best when the "tribe" shows up to hold the space.

**Religious claims**: The Inner Self Helper research shades from clinical observation into spiritual claim (the Helper says it "continues after death"). Borysenko acknowledges this threshold and does not fully resolve it.
