# Deep Memory Process

**Summary**: Roger Woolger's three-level past-life regression therapy — integrating Jungian active imagination, Gestalt body awareness, Reichian energy work, and Moreno's psychodrama — progressing from cathartic release of stored emotion (Stage 1) through archetypal shadow integration (Stage 2) to integral mystical acceptance from the witness Self (Stage 3); explicitly modelled on Jung's individuation process.

**Sources**:
- [Woolger, R. J. (1987). *Other lives, other selves: A Jungian psychotherapist discovers past lives*. Doubleday.](../sources/other-lives-other-selves.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Overview

[Woolger](../thinkers/roger-woolger.md) developed the Deep Memory Process (DMP) — initially called "past-life therapy" — over a decade of clinical practice in Vermont, workshop teaching at Esalen, the Omega Institute, and CITP, and ongoing collaboration with Stanislav Grof's transpersonal work. It is a psychotherapy, not a spiritual practice: its aim is the resolution of clinical symptoms, not the demonstration of reincarnation or the acquisition of spiritual status.

The method synthesises:
- **Jungian active imagination**: following autonomous images without directing or interfering
- **Hypnotic trance induction**: reaching altered states through body focus, eyes-closed attention, and guided imagery
- **Gestalt therapy**: body awareness, felt sense, staying with the present-moment experience
- **Reichian body-armouring theory**: reading body symptoms as stored emotional history; permitting physical release
- **Moreno's psychodrama**: role reversal, witness stance, dramatic enactment of the past-life scene

The crucial distinction from guided fantasy: in guided fantasy, images transform (a tree becomes a staircase, a peasant becomes Sir Lancelot). In DMP, the client is instructed to *stay with the story* — to follow the narrative where it goes, including through death. The therapist asks questions about sensory detail (what are you wearing? what does the ground feel like?) to deepen embodiment of the past-life personality.

## Three Levels

Woolger describes therapy as proceeding through three levels, using a Zen Buddhist koan as his organising metaphor: "First mountains are mountains. Then mountains are not mountains. Then mountains are mountains again."

### Level 1 — Realistic/Cathartic Stage

*Mountains are mountains.*

At this level, the past-life story is taken at face value as a real event deserving full emotional engagement. The therapist's role is to facilitate catharsis — the full experiencing and expression of the emotions, sensations, and thoughts embedded in the story, especially those from the death moment.

The key clinical target: *dying thoughts*. Woolger's finding: the most therapeutically potent content is not the dramatic events of the life but the final thoughts and feelings at death. A soldier dying thinking *I'm a failure — I'll never give up* creates a samskara that drives a current-life compulsion to never surrender, even to the point of self-destruction.

Catharsis at this level typically produces physical responses: shaking, breathing changes, tears, sometimes involuntary vocalisations. These correspond to what yoga calls *kriyas* — energy releases as stored patterns in the subtle body discharge.

**Limitations**: Some clients get stuck at this level — reliving the same scenes repeatedly without integrating the meaning, or using past-life stories as an escape from present-life responsibility. This is the signal to move to Level 2.

### Level 2 — Symbolic/Archetypal Stage

*Mountains are not mountains.*

At this level, the therapist guides the client to de-literalise the story — to find its symbolic meaning and, crucially, to engage the *shadow dimension*. Woolger's key observation: every victim role carries a corresponding perpetrator polarity. The tortured becomes the torturer; the abandoned becomes the abandoner; the oppressed becomes the oppressor. This polarity is not a moral judgement but a psychological law.

The technique: after completing the victim experience, ask the client to reverse roles and experience the life from the perspective of the persecutor. The nurse who relived being disembowelled by a feudal lord is then guided to be the lord — and discovers in that role the unmet needs for power, recognition, and territory that drove the violence. The shadow figure reveals its own wounds.

This is Jungian shadow integration extended to the multi-lifetime psyche. The goal is not condoning but *owning*: recognising that the torturer, the abandoner, the killer are also parts of the soul's history, not external opponents.

Moreno's psychodrama technique — appointing substitutes for the dramatis personae, speaking from the "witness point" above the drama — is particularly useful here. The witness point is what Jung calls the "transcendent function": the observing consciousness that stands between two opposing elements and enables their reconciliation.

### Level 3 — Integral/Mystical Stage

*Mountains are mountains again.*

At this level, the pattern is seen and accepted as one's karma — not with resignation but with the quality Woolger, following [Dürckheim](../thinkers/karlfried-graf-durckheim.md), calls "the dignity to dare." The beheadings, the tortures, the abandonments are not mistakes to be erased but configurations of the soul's learning — part of the drama of consciousness playing through human lives.

Woolger quotes Dürckheim's *The Way of Transformation*:

> "The aim of practice is not to develop an attitude which allows a man to acquire a state of harmony and peace wherein nothing can disturb him. On the contrary, practice should teach him to let himself be assaulted, perturbed, moved, insulted, broken and battered — that is to say, it should enable him to dare letting go of his hankering after harmony... and to venture into the zones of annihilation and contact with the Divine Being."

At Stage 3, the soul recognises itself as both the slayer and the slain, the oppressor and the liberating force. The Buddhist *bodhisattva* who has gone beyond the drama of opposites and returns to the marketplace is Woolger's image for this stage. The Self — Jung's archetype of wholeness — holds both poles without being destroyed by either.

The Zen image of mountains: ordinary consciousness sees mountains as solid fixed things (Stage 1). Insight reveals them as impermanent, constructed (Stage 2). Mature wisdom sees them again as mountains — but now with the lightness of one who knows what they are made of (Stage 3).

## The Lotus Wheel: Multi-Layered Complex Structure

Woolger maps the layered structure of a single complex using a lotus diagram. One core emotional theme — say, abandonment — organises:
- Present-life manifestations (current patterns and fears)
- Childhood events that activated the complex
- Birth trauma layer (Grof's perinatal matrices — the birth canal as the first abandonment)
- Past-life root samskara (the originating wound)

All layers are thematically unified. Therapy can enter at any layer; full resolution often requires reaching the deepest layer. This structure parallels Grof's COEX (condensed experience) systems.

## The Death Experience in Therapy

Woolger makes the dying moment central. Unlike most therapies, DMP routinely guides clients through the full experience of death in the past life — the physical sensations, the dying thoughts, the moment of departure from the body, and sometimes the after-death state (floating free, meeting other souls, reviewing the life).

This is not morbid. Woolger's finding: the *post-death* state is often the most healing part of the session. After processing the trauma of the life and death, clients frequently experience a state of peace, clarity, or meeting with figures of love that provides the corrective emotional experience. The Buddhist teaching that there is peace available to the dying mind — if old patterns are released — finds clinical confirmation here.

## Connections to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Individuation](individuation.md)** (Jung): Woolger's three-level model is explicitly a multi-lifetime reformulation of individuation. Stage 2's shadow integration and Stage 3's Self-identification mirror the arc of individuation in Jungian depth psychology.

**[Crisis as Initiation](crisis-as-initiation.md)** (Borysenko): Both frameworks use the descent into darkness as a necessary path — the past-life regression goes into the underground of the soul's history; the initiatory crisis goes into the underworld of present suffering. Both require the ego to surrender its familiar sense of reality.

**[Past-Life Therapy](past-life-therapy.md)**: This concept page provides the detailed theoretical and clinical framework for the broader survey page on past-life therapy.

**[Five Stages of Dying](five-stages-of-dying.md)** (Kübler-Ross): Woolger's wheel-of-death chapter parallels Kübler-Ross's five stages, but reversed in clinical use — the therapist guides the client *into* the death experience rather than preparing them for a future death.

**[Bardos](bardos.md)** (Sogyal Rinpoche): The Tibetan teaching that dying thoughts determine the next incarnation is the metaphysical framework that most closely mirrors Woolger's clinical finding that dying thoughts create the samskara. The bardo of becoming is the intermediate space in which the samskara seeks its next embodiment.

**[Centering Prayer](centering-prayer.md)** and [Ground Luminosity](ground-luminosity.md): Stage 3 of Woolger's process — the witness Self holding both poles — is structurally equivalent to the contemplative state in which one identifies with awareness rather than its contents; to Keating's sacred word as pointer; and to Sogyal Rinpoche's recognition of Rigpa during life as preparation for recognition of the Clear Light at death.

## Critique

**Against spiritual bypassing**: Woolger explicitly cautions against using past-life stories to avoid present-life responsibility. "The past-life framework is a means to relieving symptoms and accessing deeper layers of the complex — it is not an end, and not a replacement for present-life psychological work."

**The suggestibility problem**: The method requires a degree of trance; the therapist's presence shapes content. Woolger acknowledges this and mitigates it by staying phenomenologically neutral rather than confirming or directing the content of regression.

**Who benefits**: Woolger identifies contraindications — psychotic clients, and those who use past-life fantasies to inflate the ego ("I was Cleopatra"). The method is most effective with clients who have persistent somatic symptoms, phobias, or relational patterns that conventional therapy has repeatedly touched but not resolved.
