## Overview

DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone) is an adrenal steroid hormone — the most abundant steroid in the human body — that declines significantly after age 30. [Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) proposes it as the most clinically accessible marker of what energy medicine traditions call *chi*, *qi*, or life force.

The hypothesis: DHEA levels reflect the body's overall vitality reserve. High DHEA correlates with health and resilience; low DHEA correlates with chronic stress, illness, and biological aging. This makes DHEA a bridge between bioelectric and biochemical frameworks for energy.

## DHEA as "Chemical Battery"

Shealy was first drawn to DHEA during a 1992 research trip to Ukraine, where he encountered quantum physics-based medicine (Microwave Resonance Therapy). He returned intrigued by reports that DHEA declines with age and that this decline reflects exhausted adaptive capacity.

His hypothesis: DHEA is the "chemical battery" of the body — a reserve of life energy that can be measured in blood. When the battery runs low (from chronic stress, poor nutrition, electromagnetic overload — see [Electromagnetic Dysthymia](electromagnetic-dysthymia.md)), the person is vulnerable to illness.

## DHEA and Women's Hormones

Shealy observed that women typically lack progesterone at menopause — and that progesterone is a precursor to DHEA. He tested the hypothesis that progesterone replacement would enhance DHEA production. In a small trial of seven men, DHEA increased 30–100% (mean 60%) following treatment. This finding suggests that hormone deficiency patterns can be cascading — one deficiency leading to another.

## DHEA and Healing

In Shealy's experiments with healer Ostad Hadi Parvarandeh:
- Parvarandeh applied hands-on healing to twelve individuals over three days.
- In two individuals, DHEA rose.
- In eight, DHEA decreased by 50–75%.
- In two, no change occurred.

The decrease puzzled Shealy initially but he proposed that DHEA was being mobilised and consumed in the healing process — put to work rather than simply elevated. One patient with prostate cancer (elevated PSA) showed a 65% drop in PSA alongside the DHEA drop, suggesting active therapeutic engagement rather than depletion.

## The Sacred Water Experiment

Shealy also tested DHEA in the context of "Sacred Water" — a material discovered by Jim Carter (described as appearing oily but mixing with water) that Shealy believed had energetic properties. Fifty students used two ounces per day in bath water. After eight days:
- Two students showed 40–60% DHEA increases.
- Most showed lowered DHEA, increased energy, and reduction in allergy symptoms.
- Average weight loss of seven pounds in eight days was reported.

These findings are anecdotal and have not been independently replicated.

## Normal Ranges and EMD Diagnosis

Shealy uses DHEA as a diagnostic marker for [Electromagnetic Dysthymia](electromagnetic-dysthymia.md):
- Normal DHEA: ~715 µg/dL in men, ~510 µg/dL in women.
- EMD diagnostic threshold: below 180 µg/dL in men, below 130 µg/dL in women.
- Most patients with EMD have levels 50% below the mean; many have levels near zero.

A dramatic 50% change in DHEA is not explained by normal diurnal variation (which is approximately 15%); seasonal variation (25%); or the maximum expected day-to-day variation (not exceeding 100%). This is the basis for Shealy's claim that healing interventions — or stressors — can produce real physiological changes in DHEA.

## Critique

DHEA has been studied extensively in mainstream endocrinology and gerontology. The findings are more complex than Shealy's "chemical battery" metaphor suggests:

- DHEA does decline with age and is associated with health markers in observational studies, but DHEA supplementation trials have not shown consistent health benefits in clinical populations.
- The specific claim that DHEA measures "life force" or *qi* has no scientific support — it is a conceptual mapping, not an established correspondence.
- Shealy's Sacred Water and healer experiments are not published in peer-reviewed literature and have not been independently replicated.
- DHEA supplementation carries risks (androgenic effects in women, potential promotion of hormone-sensitive cancers) and is not recommended by mainstream endocrinology without specific clinical indications.

**Note**: DHEA is a real hormone with well-documented age-related decline and genuine associations with health outcomes. Whether it constitutes a measurable proxy for life energy — and whether healers can alter it at will — remains scientifically unestablished.
