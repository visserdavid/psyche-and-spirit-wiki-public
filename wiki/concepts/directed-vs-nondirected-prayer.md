# Directed vs. Non-directed Prayer

**Summary**: Dossey's distinction between petition prayer that specifies a desired outcome (directed) and prayer that surrenders outcome to the Absolute (non-directed / "Thy will be done") — illuminated by the Spindrift experiments, which consistently found non-directed prayer more effective on biological systems, and by Jung's introvert/extrovert typology applied to spiritual practice.

**Sources**:
- [Dossey, L. (1993). *Healing words: The power of prayer and the practice of medicine*. HarperSanFrancisco.](../sources/healing-words.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## The Distinction

[Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md) distinguishes two fundamental prayer strategies, drawing on both experimental evidence and contemplative theology:

**Directed prayer**: A specific outcome is held in mind. "May the cancer disappear." "May the infection resolve." "Lord, heal my sister's heart." The pray-er specifies what should happen and directs the prayer at that outcome. Common in Western petitionary and intercessory prayer traditions; common in guided imagery and visualisation approaches to healing.

**Non-directed prayer**: No specific outcome is held. The attitude is "Thy will be done" — "May the highest good prevail." The pray-er does not tell the universe what to do; they align with the Absolute and open to whatever resolution is best, even if that resolution is not cure, not recovery, not what the pray-er would have chosen. The Cloud of Unknowing, Meister Eckhart, and Thomas Keating all articulate versions of this approach.

The distinction maps roughly onto *petition/intercession* (directed) vs. *contemplation/prayerfulness* (non-directed), though Dossey is careful to note that both are genuine forms of prayer and neither invalidates the other.

## The Spindrift Experiments

The most striking experimental evidence for the distinction comes from the **Spindrift organization** (John and Bruce Klingbeil, Salem, Oregon), which conducted a decade of controlled laboratory experiments testing prayer effects on biological systems.

**Mold plate experiment**: Mold growing on a rice agar plate was divided by a string into a control side (A) and a prayed-for side (B). The mold was first stressed by an alcohol wash. Results: directed prayer (pray for growth on side B) produced no significant growth change. Non-directed prayer (no goal; "whatever is best for the mold") produced consistent concentric growth rings on side B — the stressed mold recovered to a statistically significant degree above control.

**Seed germination experiments**: Batches of seeds were over-hydrated or under-hydrated — both conditions retarding germination. A non-directed prayer practitioner did not know which batch was which. Results: under-hydrated seeds (which needed to gain water) gained water; over-hydrated seeds (which needed to lose water) lost water. The prayer steered each batch toward its biological norm without the practitioner specifying what that norm was. Non-directed prayer appeared to "read" the organism's own healing requirement and facilitate it — rather than imposing the healer's concept of what should happen.

**Key finding**: Non-directed prayer outperformed directed prayer in repeated trials — typically by two to four times. The Spindrift researchers interpreted this as showing that an organism's own healing intelligence (what Lewis Thomas called its "superintelligence") is better at knowing what the organism needs than the conscious, outcome-specifying mind of the healer.

**Important caveat**: Spindrift studies were never published in peer-reviewed journals and did not undergo independent external replication before the Klingbeils' deaths. The findings are suggestive and internally consistent but not confirmed by the usual scientific standards.

## Personality and Prayer Style

Non-directed prayer is not inherently superior to directed prayer for all people. [Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md) draws on Carl Jung's introvert/extrovert typology to explain why people naturally gravitate toward different prayer strategies:

**Extroverts** (Jung's action-oriented, outward-directed type): naturally pray through directed approaches — specific petitions, affirmations, outcome visualisations. They find non-directed prayer formless and passive. Contemplative "just sitting" feels like giving up.

**Introverts** (contemplative, inward-directed type): naturally drawn to non-directed approaches — wordless presence, surrender, "be still and know." Forced to pray in directed, extrovert styles, they may experience "prayer neurosis" and become prayer dropouts.

Augustine Baker (17th-century Benedictine contemplative), writing four centuries before Jung, already distinguished these two types: "souls that have not the propensity to interior ways … [find God through] sensible images," while others "have the propensity to interior ways … [and] must give place to exercises of the spirit." Neither path is superior; different souls require different approaches.

Herbert Benson (Harvard) showed empirically that the same relaxation response — and presumably the same prayer effects — can be elicited using directed phrases (Catholic: "Hail Mary"; Jewish: "Shalom"; Protestant: "Our Father who art in heaven") as with non-directed TM mantras. The conclusion: what matters is the depth of engagement, not the strategy. Dictating one approach disenfranchises practitioners of the other.

## Surrender as Active Practice

Non-directed prayer is frequently misunderstood as passive or as abandoning agency. Dossey and Janet Quinn (nurse theorist, University of Colorado) both insist that surrender is an active practice — not resignation, but a minute-by-minute choosing to align with something larger than the ego's preferences.

Quinn, working with AIDS patients: "Surrendering to AIDS is empowering — it is not giving in or refusing to take action. It requires doing. Surrender cannot be done once; it is required minute by minute…. The peacefulness that comes from surrender, rather than the despair that comes from giving up, is qualitatively different."

The non-directed approach does not require eliminating imagery or emotions. Dossey describes allowing images, feelings, and sensations to arise without attaching to them as goals or outcomes — similar to the river-of-presence metaphor in Thomas Keating's [Centering Prayer](centering-prayer.md): "resist no thought, hang on to no thought, react emotionally to no thought." The attention rests on the sacred word (Keating) or on the sense of the Absolute's presence, not on constructing a desired outcome.

## Connections to Contemplative Traditions

Non-directed prayer corresponds to the Christian apophatic (via negativa) tradition — the path to God by negation of all concepts, images, and desires. Dossey traces a continuous thread:

- **Meister Eckhart** (14th century): "That person who perceives God and attaches some name to God, that is not God."
- **Cloud of Unknowing** (anonymous, 14th century): directs the reader to suppress all concepts about God and reach toward the divine darkness with "a naked intent of the will."
- **Thomas Keating**: "Silence is the language God speaks; everything else is a bad translation." See [Centering Prayer](centering-prayer.md).
- **Taoism (Chuang Tzu)**: the concept of *wu wei* — effortless action, non-doing. The archer hits the mark by not striving; the artist achieves perfection by allowing.
- **Hindu non-directed**: Patanjali's *samadhi* is the dissolution of the observer's separate will into pure awareness.

The Spindrift finding that non-directed prayer follows the organism's own wisdom rather than the healer's specification parallels the Taoist insight that the Way works by aligning with nature's intelligence rather than imposing human intention on it.

## Practical Implications

Dossey draws practical guidance for both patients and physicians:

- Neither directed nor non-directed prayer should be presented as the "correct" method. Personality differences are real and should be honoured.
- When a patient asks "How should I pray?", the honest answer — which the Byrd CCU study preserved by giving intercessors no instructions — is that there is no single correct method.
- Non-directed prayer is particularly suited to situations where the healer cannot know what the organism needs (which is usually the case). "Thy will be done" removes the healer's ego as an obstacle to the organism's healing intelligence.
- Directed prayer may be more effective when highly specific changes are wanted (e.g., Jeanne Achterberg and G. Frank Lawlis showed that more specific and vivid imagery correlates with better tumour response — though this was imagery, not prayer per se).

## Critique

- The Spindrift findings are the primary empirical foundation for preferring non-directed prayer, but they have not been independently replicated in peer-reviewed form. Without replication, the finding should be treated as suggestive, not confirmed.
- The claim that "the organism knows best" (implicit in non-directed prayer outperforming directed) is consistent with systems biology and homeostasis, but has not been experimentally disentangled from other variables.
- The introvert/extrovert typology is a simplification — Jung's own four-function model is more complex, and real people do not fall neatly into two categories.
- Non-directed prayer's surrender dynamic may make psychological harm more likely if it is used as a reason not to seek treatment, or if "accepting God's will" is conflated with helpless resignation.
