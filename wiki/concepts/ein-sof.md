# Ein Sof

**Summary**: The Kabbalistic name for the utterly transcendent, infinite aspect of God — "Without End" — that is beyond all attributes, description, and comprehension; the unknowable ground from which the ten sefirot emanate, and whose ultimate nature is identical with the final goal of spiritual ascent.

**Sources**:
- [Matt, D. C. (Ed. & Trans.). (1983). *Zohar: The book of enlightenment* (Classics of Western Spirituality). Paulist Press.](../sources/zohar-book-of-enlightenment.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Overview

*Ein Sof* (אֵין סוֹף, "Without End" or "Infinite") is the Kabbalistic term for God's absolute nature — the aspect of divinity that precedes, transcends, and underlies all manifestation. The Zohar rarely names Ein Sof directly; a 13th-century kabbalist noted that "Ein Sof is not hinted at in Torah, Prophets, Writings, or the words of the Rabbis." Ein Sof cannot be thought, spoken, or grasped. It has no attributes, no personal qualities, and no relationship with creation.

The sefirot — the ten qualities of the Divine — are not Ein Sof but its emanations, the way the Infinite makes itself knowable and present. They are related to Ein Sof "as flame joined to coal" (Zohar 3:70a): the flame is not the coal, yet it cannot exist apart from it.

---

## The Problem Ein Sof Solves

Kabbalah navigated a paradox in Jewish theology:
- On one hand, God is utterly transcendent, beyond all human comprehension (the Maimonidean inheritance from Neoplatonism and Aristotelianism)
- On the other hand, God is intimately personal — responds to prayer, loves Israel, experiences pain at the exile of the Shekhinah, is affected by human sin and righteousness

Ein Sof accounts for the transcendent dimension; the sefirot account for the personal and relational dimension. They are one reality, not two — but the distinction allows the Zohar to affirm both.

Critics (including the kabbalist Abraham Abulafia) charged that positing ten sefirot alongside a transcendent Ein Sof verged on polytheism. The Zohar's response: Ein Sof and the sefirot form an absolute unity — their multiplicity is perspectival, not ontological.

## Keter: The Interface

The highest sefirah, Keter (Crown), functions as the interface between Ein Sof and the world of manifestation. Keter is sometimes called *Ayin* (Nothingness) — not because it lacks being, but because all being is annihilated in comparison with its simplicity. No differentiation, individuality, or opposition exists at Keter. From Keter, the seed-point of Hokhmah (Wisdom) emerges and the cascade of creation begins.

The mystic who ascends the sefirot through meditation reaches Binah (Understanding/the Divine Mother), rests there in contemplation, and ultimately is absorbed into Hokhmah and then Keter — where thought itself dissolves into the Infinite. "Meditation is sucking not knowing" (Isaac the Blind, c. 1160–1235).

## Cross-Tradition Parallels

Ein Sof occupies the same structural position in the Kabbalistic system as several key concepts in other wiki sources:

| Tradition | Equivalent | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Kabbalah (Zohar) | Ein Sof | Infinite, beyond all attributes, known only through sefirot |
| Tibetan Buddhism | Rigpa / Dharmakaya | Primordial awareness; clear light of reality; known through recognition |
| Tibetan Buddhism | Ground Luminosity | Clear Light at death; the fundamental nature that was always present |
| ACIM | God | Utterly beyond form; known only through the Holy Spirit |
| Jung | The Self (archetype) | The unknowable totality of psyche; known only through symbols |
| Brennan | Core Star | Divine essence at the deepest dimension of being; undamaged by biography |
| Myss | Divine Potential | The soul's inherent capacity for its highest expression |
| Vedanta | Brahman / Atman | The absolute ground of being, identical with the individual soul |

The convergence is striking: across traditions, the deepest level of reality is:
1. Infinite, without attributes, beyond comprehension
2. Intimately present — in fact, identical with the individual soul at its core
3. Known through mediated forms (sefirot, Rigpa, the Holy Spirit, symbols, the haric level)
4. The goal of spiritual practice is recognition/union with this ground

## Relation to the Holographic Paradigm

David Bohm's [implicate order](implicate-order.md) — the undivided wholeness from which all explicit phenomena are unfolded — functions as a secular parallel to Ein Sof. Bohm explicitly refused to equate them, but the structural parallel is close: both are the infinite ground from which the visible world emerges through a process of unfolding/emanation, both are beyond direct human grasp, and both imply that multiplicity is secondary and unity is primary.

Willis Harman's [M-3 metaphysics](m-1-m-2-m-3-metaphysics.md) (consciousness primary) arrives at a similar conclusion through philosophical analysis.

## Critique

**The apophatic limit**: Ein Sof is a radically apophatic (negative) concept — it can only be defined by what it is not. This places it beyond all empirical investigation. The Zohar's claim that the sefirot are its manifestations cannot be verified; the system is internally coherent but cannot be externally tested.

**The unity problem**: Despite the Zohar's insistence that Ein Sof and the sefirot form an absolute unity, the system has been criticised (by Abulafia and by later Jewish rationalists) for functionally positing a plurality within the Godhead. The Lurianic elaboration of Ein Sof (through the concept of tzimtzum — divine self-contraction to make space for creation) deepens rather than resolves this tension.

**Syncretic use**: When Ein Sof is mapped onto Rigpa, Core Star, or Brahman, the distinctively Jewish relational and covenantal dimension of the Kabbalistic system is typically lost. The Zohar's Ein Sof is not simply an impersonal ground of being; it is intimately bound up with Israel's historical covenant, the exile of the Shekhinah, and the obligation of tikkun through Torah practice.
