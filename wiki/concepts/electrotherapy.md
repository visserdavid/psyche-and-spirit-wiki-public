## Overview

Electrotherapy — the therapeutic application of electrical current to the body — has a history of nearly two thousand years, from Roman use of torpedo ray shocks for gout to contemporary TENS and neuromodulation devices. [Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) regards electrotherapy as the practical expression of [bioelectromagnetism](bioelectromagnetism.md) — the means by which the body's bioelectric circuits can be activated, rebalanced, and restored.

The Flexner Report of 1910 — commissioned by the American Medical Association — is presented by Shealy as deliberately suppressing electrotherapy in favour of drug-based medicine, throwing away decades of promising research in the process.

## TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)

Shealy's entry into electrotherapy came through his work on the Dorsal Column Stimulator (DCS) — a surgically implanted device he introduced in April 1967 for cancer pain. The DCS worked in 75% of patients but carried surgical risks.

From 1967, Shealy argued that surface skin stimulation could achieve the same effect non-invasively — and was 1,000 times safer. Through collaborations with engineer Norman Hagfors (formerly of Medtronic), the surface TENS device was developed. Today an estimated 100,000 TENS units are sold annually with projected revenues of $100 million. Shealy considers standard TENS devices insufficiently powerful and advocates for his own redesigned Shealy TENS device, which uses frequencies matching those of the historic Electreat device (patented 1919 by naturopath C. W. Kent) and of GigaTENS.

**Clinical results**: TENS relieves pain in approximately 50% of chronic pain patients. Shealy's variant claims 85% improvement in chronic pain through its combined frequencies.

## Cranial Electrical Stimulation (CES) / Liss Device

In 1975, engineer Saul Liss developed the CES device (renamed the Liss Body Stimulator). Shealy's experiences with it:
- Placing one electrode on the head at 10:30 pm, he experimented for an hour and went to sleep at 11:30 pm — waking at 2:30 am with dramatically increased energy and alertness.
- Application at 8:00 am for 45 minutes raised one associate's blood serotonin level five times above the upper normal limit.
- A patient with sixteen years of depression was relieved in a single hour of stimulation.

Clinical findings from Shealy's practice:
- 80% of chronic pain patients normalised serotonin output after CES treatment.
- Combined with photostimulation, music, and education, CES relieved depression in 85% of patients.
- CES relieved jet lag and improved sleep.
- When applied in an acupuncture pattern, CES raised DHEA levels.

## GigaTENS / Microwave Resonance Therapy

During a December 1992 visit to Kiev, Ukraine, Shealy encountered Microwave Resonance Therapy (MRT) — a device using gigahertz (billion-cycle) frequencies:
- Human DNA resonates at 52–78 gigahertz.
- Animal DNA resonates at ~47 gigahertz.
- Plant DNA resonates at ~42 gigahertz.

Ukrainian physicians reported treating 200,000 patients with MRT, claiming:
- 50% of drug addicts cured.
- 92% of alcoholics successfully treated.
- 80% of arthritis patients improved.
- Effective for angina, diabetes, osteomyelitis, and bone infections.
- No complications reported.

Working with Saul Liss and Ukrainian physicists, Shealy developed the GigaTENS device to apply these frequencies in clinical settings. His own clinical results:
- 70% pain relief in arthritis patients where drug therapy failed.
- 80% improvement in neuropathy patients.
- 50% improvement in patients with pain and depression.

Shealy notes that C. W. Kent's original Electreat device (1919) emitted a range of frequencies that apparently included giga frequencies — the first GigaTENS was marketed over a century ago without the technology to explain why it worked.

## Electroacupuncture

Shealy began combining electrical stimulation with acupuncture needles in 1967 (calling the technique PENS — Percutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation) before he knew Chinese practitioners had done the same. He studied with Dr. Felix Mann in England in 1973.

Key findings of electroacupuncture:
- 1 Hz stimulation raises beta-endorphin levels (explaining pain relief).
- Higher frequencies relieve pain without raising endorphins (different mechanism).
- Acupuncture restores fertility in two-thirds of otherwise infertile males — no conventional treatment achieves comparable results.
- Specific circuits (e.g., Tchong Mo) improve PMS symptoms, restore fertility, and restore DHEA.

## Critique

The core application of electrical stimulation to pain management is well-established and widely practised. FDA-approved PEMF (pulsed electromagnetic fields) for bone fracture healing is a proven application. TENS for pain relief is supported by evidence (though of modest effect size in systematic reviews).

The more ambitious claims — that GigaTENS cures addiction, that CES reliably reverses depression, that electroacupuncture restores fertility — are based on clinical observations and Ukrainian reports that have not been subjected to rigorous controlled trials in Western research settings.

The history of electrotherapy includes many overclaimed devices and fraudulent products; the suppression of the field by the Flexner Report also suppressed careful clinical research. The challenge today is distinguishing genuine therapeutic signals from the long history of electrotherapy excess.

**Note**: Mainstream medicine accepts TENS for pain management (with moderate evidence), implanted electrical stimulation for specific neurological conditions, and PEMF for bone healing. The broader claims for GigaTENS, CES, and electroacupuncture for systemic disease require stronger evidence before clinical adoption.
