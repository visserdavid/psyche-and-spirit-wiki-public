# The Emotional Plague

**Summary**: Reich's late concept of a socially transmitted, character-rooted "biopathy" — moralizing envy, sadistic persecution of natural vitality, and rationalized cruelty directed at anyone whose life-energy or genital expression threatens the armored majority's own repression — which he saw as the psychological engine behind mass phenomena like fascism and the Inquisition.

**Sources**:
- [Reich, W. (1949). *Character analysis* (T. P. Wolfe, Trans.; 3rd, enlarged ed.). Orgone Institute Press. (Original work published 1933)](../sources/character-analysis.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-18

## The Concept

Reich is explicit that "emotional plague" carries no moralizing connotation of conscious malice or degeneracy — it names a **chronic biopathy**, structurally continuous with the individual [character neuroses](character-armor.md) described elsewhere in the book, but manifesting specifically in *social* behavior: sadistic moralizing, envious surveillance of others' vitality, and reflexive persecution of anything — sexuality, spontaneity, joy — that threatens the plague-afflicted person's own precarious character armor.

Reich distinguishes plague reactions from ordinary neurotic symptoms by their **motivational structure**: a neurotic symptom is experienced by the sufferer as foreign and distressing (ego-dystonic); a plague reaction is thoroughly rationalized and experienced as righteous, necessary, even virtuous — the person acts *against* someone else's happiness or freedom while sincerely believing themselves to be acting on legitimate, high-minded grounds (morality, order, loyalty, purity). The tell, in his account, is that the stated reason for the action and its real motive never coincide, and that the reaction is not curable by rational counter-argument (only genuine analytic/therapeutic work on the underlying armor changes it) — much as one cannot "argue" a phobia away.

He treats it as **epidemic** rather than hereditary — transmitted not through biology but through mass-scale suppression of children's natural (in Reich's framework, genital and emotional) self-regulation from the first days of life, reproduced generation after generation through education and family structure. He points to periodic historical "pandemics" of the plague at civilizational scale: the medieval Inquisition and, writing in the 1940s, contemporary fascism.

## Relationship to Reich's Broader Theory

This concept sits downstream of [character armor](character-armor.md) and [orgastic potency](orgastic-potency.md): Reich's claim is that a population whose members are systematically prevented from achieving orgastic potency and genuine emotional self-regulation develops, at scale, a shared reservoir of chronically bound rage and envy that seeks socially sanctioned outlets — moral crusades, scapegoating, totalitarian movements — rather than remaining a purely private, individual neurosis.

## Relationship to Other Wiki Material

Reich's social-diagnostic move — reading mass political pathology as the aggregate expression of individual character structure formed under a repressive social/familial order — parallels [Erich Fromm's](../thinkers/erich-fromm.md) analysis of Nazi character structure and [individual and group narcissism](individual-and-group-narcissism.md) in *[The Heart of Man](../sources/heart-of-man.md)*, and, more distantly, [M. Scott Peck's](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md) treatment of group evil (the My Lai material) in *[People of the Lie](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md)*. All three thinkers reject the idea that mass atrocity requires a special category of "evil person," instead locating its roots in ordinary, widely distributed character pathology reinforced by social structure — though Reich's specific etiology (sexual repression and orgastic impotence as the causal engine) is narrower and more sexually deterministic than either Fromm's or Peck's accounts.

## Critique

**As social theory**: Reich's underlying sex-economic thesis — that sexual repression is *the* root cause of authoritarian and fascist psychology — reflects his particular (and, by 1933, politically fraught) synthesis of Marxism and psychoanalysis, and is generally regarded by historians of psychology and political theory as one-dimensional; mainstream accounts of fascism's psychological appeal (drawing on social psychology, group dynamics, and historical-institutional analysis) treat sexual repression as at most one contributing factor among many, not the master variable.

**As clinical description**: the core observation — that rationalized cruelty toward others' vitality or difference is often driven by envy and displaced self-repression rather than by the stated moral justification — remains a recognizable and still-cited insight in depth psychology, echoed in Fromm's and Peck's later, less narrowly sexual formulations.
