# Entropy, Laziness, and Grace

**Summary**: Peck's argument that a force he names grace — evidenced clinically in dreams, serendipity, and unexplained survival — actively supports human psychological and spiritual growth against a countervailing force he calls entropy, whose psychological expression is laziness: Peck's candidate for the functional meaning of original sin, and the seed of his later clinical definition of evil.

**Sources**:
- [Peck, M. S. (1978). *The road less traveled: A new psychology of love, traditional values, and spiritual growth*. Simon & Schuster.](../sources/the-road-less-traveled.md)

## The Clinical Evidence for Grace

Peck builds his case for grace from a series of clinical and personal observations that he argues resist explanation by known causal mechanism:

- **Unexplained survival and "accident-resistance"**: patients (and Peck himself) who narrowly avoid disaster in ways existing concepts like disease "resistance" or survival instinct do not adequately explain, since instinct cannot account for interactions between a person and inanimate objects (a car's mechanical failure, a chance decision to brake before seeing a hazard)
- **Dreams and slips of the tongue** as unbidden, often unwelcome messages from the unconscious — Peck reads these, following [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) rather than Freud, as fundamentally oriented toward the dreamer's health rather than as disguised expressions of repressed, antisocial desire
- **[Synchronicity](synchronicity.md)** — Peck cites Jung's scarab-beetle case directly, alongside dream-telepathy experiments (Ullman and Krippner) and his own anecdotal experience of an improbably timed loan of a book that resolved a specific writing problem
- **Symptoms as forced notice**: psychiatric symptoms (his patient "Betsy"'s agoraphobic anxiety attacks, precipitated by an unacknowledged fear of freedom after leaving college) are read not as illness in themselves but as a message that a person's stalled growth requires attention — illness, in this account, precedes and is revealed by the symptom, not created by it

He calls the common thread **serendipity**: "the gift of finding valuable or agreeable things not sought for," and argues that some people take advantage of such gifts and others do not — the difference being a large part of what determines whether psychotherapy, or life generally, succeeds.

### The Myth of Orestes

Peck uses the Greek myth of Orestes — pursued by the Furies after killing his mother in vengeance for his father's murder, and eventually transformed by his acceptance of responsibility into the Eumenides, "bearers of grace" — as his central illustration: psychiatric symptoms are Furies until the sufferer accepts responsibility for their condition, at which point the same energy becomes available as a resource for healing.

## Entropy and Laziness

Peck borrows the second law of thermodynamics as a metaphor for psychological life: physical systems tend toward disorganization (**entropy**) unless energy is expended against that tendency, and evolution itself — the drive toward ever greater organizational complexity — runs counter to this universal current. He proposes that **laziness** is the psychological form entropy takes in human beings: the reluctance to extend the boundaries of thought and responsibility that constitutes love and growth.

Laziness, in this account, is Peck's reading of the Adam and Eve story: the sin was not disobedience but the failure to do the harder thing — questioning God directly rather than accepting the serpent's account without challenge. He generalizes this to a claim that **all people harbor laziness to some degree**, that it operates covertly (disguised as legitimate busyness, disguised rationalizations, or "cop-outs" from therapy), and that the majority of psychotherapy patients who drop out do so from fear of the work involved rather than from any failure of the method.

## The Problem of Evil (Preview)

In a brief section anticipating *[People of the Lie](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md)* by five years, Peck offers four conclusions about evil:

1. **Evil is real** — not a figment of the mind trying to explain suffering, but a phenomenon in which people or institutions respond to goodness with destructive hostility, motivated by an unwillingness to face their own unworthiness
2. **Evil is laziness carried to its most active extreme**: where mere laziness is a failure to love, evil is the active use of power to protect one's self-image by crushing whatever threatens it
3. **Evil is a possible, not inevitable, stage of evolution** — the tension between entropy (laziness/evil) and the force of love/growth exists in every person, resolved differently case by case
4. **Evil is not itself a discrete force** — it is a name for entropy manifesting through human will

This is markedly softer and more universal than the diagnostic category Peck develops later: see [Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)](human-evil.md) for the full clinical elaboration (malignant narcissism, scapegoating, the revulsion response) and the explicit comparison between the two statements.

## Evolution, Consciousness, and Godhood

Peck extends the argument theologically: if grace is a force that pushes individuals and the species toward greater organization against entropy, and if that force cannot be located inside or outside the individual without paradox, the most economical hypothesis — he argues, while disclaiming any wish to prove it — is a loving God who wants human consciousness to evolve toward union with the divine ("we are born that we might become Godhood," in his phrasing). He credits Jung's image of consciousness as a bud growing from an unconscious rhizome (from *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*) as his closest available analogy for how the individual, unconscious, and God relate.

## Resistance to and Welcoming of Grace

Peck argues most people resist grace precisely because accepting it (accepting responsibility, accepting the call to greater power and its accompanying aloneness) is itself effortful — the same laziness that resists ordinary discipline resists grace. He closes by redefining serendipity as **the capacity to recognize and use gifts of grace already being given**, arguing this capacity — not a fixed quota of grace unequally distributed — is what varies between people who transcend difficult beginnings and those who do not.

## Critique

**Unfalsifiability**: as with Jung's [synchronicity](synchronicity.md), Peck's argument for grace rests on anecdote and cannot be tested; alternative explanations (survivorship bias, selective memory for near-misses, ordinary probability in large numbers of daily events) account for the same phenomena without requiring an external force.

**Theological ambition exceeding clinical evidence**: Peck moves from clinical anecdotes about dreams and lucky escapes to a substantive theological claim (a loving God actively directing human evolution) that few readers of the clinical material would find compelled by it alone. He acknowledges this gap himself, framing the theology as hypothesis rather than demonstration.

**Precursor to a harder claim**: the "evil as extreme laziness" formulation here is considerably more forgiving and universal than the categorical clinical diagnosis of evil Peck proposes in *People of the Lie* — where evil becomes a property of a narrower class of people who consistently refuse self-examination, rather than a potential in everyone. Readers of both books should note Peck did not reconcile the two framings explicitly; see [Human Evil](human-evil.md) for the tension.

**Note**: Mainstream psychiatry does not use "grace" or "entropy" as clinical categories; both are Peck's own theological and physical metaphors imported into psychotherapy, not terms with standardized clinical meaning.
