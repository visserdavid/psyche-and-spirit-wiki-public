# Eros and the Will to Power: Freud, Adler, and Jung

**Summary**: Jung's early triangulation of his own analytical psychology against Freud's sexual/Eros theory of neurosis and Adler's will-to-power theory — arguing both are correct but partial, each a valid description from within one attitude-type (extraverted/object-oriented for Freud, introverted/subject-oriented for Adler) rather than a universal law.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1966). *Two essays on analytical psychology* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 7). Princeton University Press.](../sources/two-essays-on-analytical-psychology.md)

## The Two Theories

By the early 1900s, [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) had two established, mutually exclusive clinical theories of neurosis available to him from within the psychoanalytic movement he had joined:

**Freud's Eros theory**: [Sigmund Freud](../thinkers/sigmund-freud.md) reads neurosis as a conflict between civilized restraint and repressed instinct — centrally sexual instinct, though Freud broadened the term "libido" over his career. Dreams are disguised wish-fulfilments; the symptom encodes a repressed desire the conscious mind cannot admit. Freud's own late qualification — introducing a death instinct (Thanatos) opposed to Eros — is, in Jung's reading, itself an admission that a single-instinct theory could not hold.

**Adler's will-to-power theory**: [Alfred Adler](../thinkers/alfred-adler.md), a former Freud collaborator who broke from psychoanalysis, reframes the same clinical material as a "masculine protest" — neurosis as strategy, however costly and self-defeating, for securing power, superiority, or safety against a felt sense of inferiority. Symptoms (Jung's illustrative case: a woman's asthma and night terrors) are read not as repressed wishes but as unconscious devices for dominating a household or relationship — securing devotion, sympathy, and control through illness itself.

## Jung's Case Studies

*Two Essays* stages the contrast through parallel readings of similar case material — most memorably a woman patient whose asthma and anxiety attacks began after her father's death and resurfaced around a flirtation with an Italian stranger. Read through Freud's Eros theory, her symptoms encode a repressed erotic attachment to her father, redirected. Read through Adler's power theory, the same symptoms are a bid for domestic control once her earlier means of securing her father's exclusive attention (through illness) failed and needed a substitute target (her husband). Jung's conclusion: **both readings fit the same material**, and neither is simply wrong.

## Jung's Resolution: Attitude-Type, Not Instinct

Jung's own move is not to propose a third instinct but to relocate the disagreement. Freud, he argues, is characteristically **extraverted** — his theoretical attention runs toward the object (the desired person, the external world) as the site where libido gets invested and frustrated. Adler is characteristically **introverted** — his attention runs toward the subject's own security and position, treating the object as instrumental to the subject's self-assertion. Each theorist has, in effect, generalized his own typical orientation into a universal law of neurosis.

This typological argument is the wiki's earliest primary-source statement (1917, considerably predating the 1921 *Psychological Types* and the exposition in [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md)) of [introversion and extraversion](four-psychological-functions.md) as basic attitude-types — developed here specifically as a diagnostic tool for adjudicating between rival schools of psychotherapy, not yet as the full four-function typology.

## Jung's Own Position

Jung does not claim his synthesis escapes typology altogether — he is candid that his own view of the unconscious as a self-regulating, [compensatory](compensation.md) system is itself a particular (if type-transcending) construction. His argument is narrower: neurosis is neither Eros alone nor power alone but a **conflict of opposites** specific to the individual case, and the task of therapy is not to reduce the symptom to one drive but to read it as [compensation](compensation.md) — a purposive correction the psyche is attempting, which requires attending to what the *specific* one-sidedness in this *specific* patient's conscious attitude has been.

## Relevance to the Wiki

This triangulation gives context to why the wiki's Jung material (archetypes, individuation, the collective unconscious) developed independently of, rather than as an extension of, Freudian and Adlerian frameworks: Jung's founding move was to treat both existing schools as valid-but-partial rather than to choose between them or synthesize their content directly. It also supplies the clearest primary-source statement of why Jung's system needed **types** at all — the theory of introversion/extraversion did not arise from abstract personality research but from the very practical problem of two brilliant clinicians examining the same patients and reaching opposite, equally well-evidenced conclusions.

## Critique

**A convenient self-positioning**: Locating Freud and Adler's disagreement in their respective attitude-types lets Jung avoid directly adjudicating either predecessor's substantive clinical claims — a move that is elegant but also conveniently exempts Jung's own theory from the same relativizing treatment (Jung does not ask what his *own* attitude-type predisposes him to see or miss in the same way he asks it of Freud and Adler).

**Note**: mainstream clinical psychology does not treat Freud's drive theory, Adler's individual psychology, or Jung's typological resolution as empirically adjudicated frameworks in the modern evidence-based sense; the debate belongs to the history of psychodynamic theory rather than to a settled question in contemporary psychiatry.
