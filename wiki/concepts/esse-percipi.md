# Esse Percipi (To Be Is to Be Perceived)

**Summary**: Hillman's principle — borrowed from George Berkeley — that the soul's image needs to be *seen* by a witness (mentor, teacher, friend, lover) in order to fully come into being; perception bestows being, and mentoring is the act of perceiving the calling in another person.

**Sources**:
- [Hillman, J. (1996). *The soul's code: In search of character and calling*. Random House.](../sources/souls-code.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## The Principle

*Esse percipi* — "to be is to be perceived" — is George Berkeley's philosophical proposition that existence depends on perception: things exist because they are perceived, ultimately by God. Hillman appropriates this for psychology: the soul's image, the [acorn](acorn-theory.md), requires a witness to fully manifest. Being seen is not merely pleasant; it is ontologically necessary for the calling to come into the world.

The implication: mentoring is not teaching. It is the act of *perceiving* another person's unique image with sufficient clarity that the person can recognise themselves in that perception. The mentor does not create the calling; they create the conditions under which it becomes visible.

## The Cases

Hillman assembles a series of biographical cases in which a mentor's *seeing* produced a decisive turn in the development of an eminent life:

**Washington and Hamilton (1777)**: George Washington, at forty-five, recognised something in the twenty-two-year-old Alexander Hamilton — "frail artillery officer" — that no inventory could have identified. Within months Hamilton became Washington's principal presidential aide and Commander-in-Chief's spokesman. The basis was not assessment; it was perception.

**Roosevelt and Lyndon Johnson (1937)**: Franklin Roosevelt, meeting the twenty-eight-year-old congressman, told aides: "That kid Lyndon Johnson will be a Southern President one day." His adviser was puzzled. Roosevelt had perceived a quality — the ability to communicate on the level of implication, not just words — that only the trained eye of a seasoned perceiver could detect.

**Darwin and Henslow (1831)**: Schoolmaster John Henslow saw, in twenty-two-year-old Charles Darwin (beetle-hunter, clergy candidate, member of the Glutton Club), a potential naturalist suited for a voyage of exploration. He persuaded the reluctant family and the Admiralty. Without Henslow's perception, Darwin does not board the Beagle.

**Miss Shank and Elia Kazan (c. 1930)**: Kazan writes of a grade teacher, Miss Anna B. Shank, who one morning stood him at the desk "and the light from the window fell on his features" — she saw something. Twenty-five years later, having become one of America's great stage and film directors, he wrote her a letter.

**Catherine Wood and Truman Capote (c. 1937)**: Capote — who failed algebra, French, and Spanish, threw tantrums, wandered school hallways with a pencil and paper — was completely seen by one English teacher, Miss Catherine Wood, who "came to his class year after year" to see him, who "invited him to dinner, read his stories, catered to his class." She saw the writer before the writer had written anything.

**Camara and Manolete**: The manager José Flores Camara, watching Manolete in a ring, "saw the boy becoming." He saw beyond the technical incompetence to the quality of courage — a fearlessness that was not performed but constitutive. He signed the boy and began the transformation.

## What the Mentor Sees

Hillman argues that what the mentor perceives is not the person's current competence but the *image* — the pattern that the person is moving toward. This requires:

- **Holiness of the heart's affections** (Keats): the capacity to be moved by what is genuinely present in another, not by what is projected, desired, or conventionally expected
- **Truth of imagination**: seeing what is real in another by imagining it fully, not by measuring or categorizing
- **The eye of the heart**: a mode of perception that is neither purely cognitive nor emotional but imaginal — it reads the gestalt, the as-yet-inarticulate form

The mentor does not need to explain what they see. Camara watching Manolete was perceiving before any analysis was possible. The perception itself is the gift.

## Esse Percipi and Therapy

Hillman makes a pointed critique of psychotherapy in this chapter. Therapy, he argues, promotes the illusion that insight — *seeing oneself* — is the curative factor. But the Oedipal model of therapy is about self-blinding (Oedipus putting out his own eyes), not about being seen by another. The therapeutic emphasis on the patient's own self-examination, introspection, and mirror-gazing misses the ontological function of being *witnessed* by another.

The therapist who truly sees the patient's image — not their pathology, not their history, not their diagnosis — performs a different function than the one who helps the patient understand themselves. The latter is insight; the former is presence.

## Esse Percipi and Love

Hillman extends the principle to falling in love. To fall in love is to be seen by another — to have one's image perceived, even if not understood, and to perceive another's image in return. The "holiness of the heart's affections" opens the eye. Love is not primarily about the other's qualities; it is about the mutual perception of images, the simultaneous seeing-and-being-seen.

The mentor relationship and the love relationship share this structure: in both, the call to be seen is answered, and the being seen enables the becoming.

## Critique

**The witness asymmetry**: Hillman's examples are overwhelmingly of famous people being seen by less-famous ones. What the mentor gains from the seeing is rarely examined. The transactional dimension — perception as a form of power and selection — is not fully addressed.

**The limits of perception**: Not all seeing is enabling. Mentors can also project, idealize, or colonize the person they see. The history of art, music, and academia includes many cases where a mentor's perception was more about the mentor's image of a protégé than the protégé's own calling.

**The ordinary life**: Hillman's cases are all exceptional. What does esse percipi mean for people whose image is quiet — who are not going to become Truman Capote — but who nonetheless need to be seen? The principle may apply universally, but the evidence is all from the extraordinary.
