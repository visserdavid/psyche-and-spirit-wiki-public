# Exceptional Cancer Patients

**Summary**: The group therapy programme founded by Bernie Siegel in New Haven, Connecticut in 1978; a framework that identifies three patient types (those who wish to die, compliant actors, and survivors), describes the psychological profile of long-term cancer survivors, and provides the practical tools — imagery, drawing, meditation, the four questions — by which patients develop a survivor personality.

**Sources**:
- [Siegel, B. S. (1986). *Love, medicine and miracles: Lessons learned about self-healing from a surgeon's experience with exceptional patients*. Harper & Row.](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## The Programme

ECaP (Exceptional Cancer Patients) was founded by [Bernie Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md) with his wife Bobbie Siegel and a nurse in 1978, after Siegel attended a workshop run by O. Carl Simonton and Stephanie Matthews-Simonton on psychological factors in cancer. Siegel sent 100 letters to his cancer patients inviting them to group therapy; twelve showed up. Over the years of working with them, he observed consistent patterns that distinguished patients who outlived their prognoses.

ECaP is structured as a two-hour weekly group therapy programme. It is not a replacement for medical treatment but a supplement designed to help patients:

- Develop authentic emotional expression rather than performance or suppression
- Understand the four diagnostic questions that reveal will to live
- Use imagery, drawing, meditation, and visualisation as tools for communicating with the body
- Build a support network of people who encourage growth rather than reinforce illness
- Develop the characteristics of the survivor personality
- Find or restore meaning in life independent of the illness

No psychiatric referral is required; no fee is charged beyond what is needed to defray costs. The programme does not promote positive thinking — it promotes authentic selfhood.

## Three Types of Cancer Patient

Siegel's fundamental clinical observation, made through ECaP, is that cancer patients distribute into three distinct types:

### Type 1: Those Who Welcome Death (15–20%)

These patients consciously or unconsciously welcome the cancer as an escape from an unbearable life. They show no signs of stress at diagnosis. When a doctor asks "What troubles you?" they say "Nothing." Family members may report that the patient has said they want to die. These patients resist treatment and may appear peaceful because they have already mentally departed.

Siegel does not condemn this response — he recognises that some people genuinely feel they have no reason to live, and that illness functions for them as permission to exit. His goal with these patients is not to overcome their death wish but to help them discover what would make life worth living, and to give them agency: the right to refuse treatment at ninety is the same right one should have exercised at thirty.

### Type 2: Compliant Actors (60–70%)

The majority of patients are "auditioning" — they perform the role of the good patient, doing what they think the doctor wants, taking their medications, showing up for appointments, but never questioning, never investing their own conviction, never truly committing to living. These patients survive or don't according to the statistics because they provide nothing to tip the balance. Siegel calls them actors because their compliance is performance rather than engagement.

This group is the most challenging therapeutically because they appear cooperative. The therapeutic goal is to help them drop the performance, express authentic emotion (including anger and fear), and take genuine responsibility for their choices.

### Type 3: Survivors (15–20%)

The exceptional patients — those who outlive their prognoses, who form genuine therapeutic relationships with their doctors, who demand information, who question authority, who change their lives, and who engage love as a biological force. These patients are often described by their physicians as "difficult." [Leonard Derogatis](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)'s 1979 study of 35 breast cancer long-term survivors found that the survivors were rated difficult by their physicians: they expressed anger, fear, and distress, and maintained their individuality rather than conforming to the patient role.

Siegel argues that the physician's label "difficult patient" is one of medicine's most consequential confusions: the patients who will live are the ones who refuse to perform wellness and who insist on genuine partnership.

## The Survivor Personality

Siegel draws on the work of psychologist Al Siebert, who studied survivor personalities beginning with Korean War paratrooper veterans in 1953. Siebert found that survivors share a structure of character rather than any specific trait:

**Complexity and the union of opposites**: Survivors do not fit neat categories. They are simultaneously serious and playful, ambitious and relaxed, caring and self-centred. This complexity gives them flexibility — a range of resources to draw on in crisis.

**Self-motivated growth**: Survivors display:
- Aimless playfulness for its own sake (the child's mode)
- The ability to become absorbed in an activity and lose track of time
- Genuine curiosity
- Willingness to make mistakes and laugh at themselves
- Receptivity to criticism without defensiveness
- Active imagination, daydreams, inner conversations

**Higher-order functioning** (Siebert's "synergy" level beyond Maslow's self-actualisation):
- Empathy including for opponents
- Pattern recognition in organisations and systems
- Intuition used as information (not impulse)
- Accurate timing in action and speech
- Nonconformity: choosing to abide by laws when it serves others, not out of fear
- Ability to absorb experiences without being destroyed — the gift of serendipity, turning what others call misfortune into opportunity

**Self-respect and creativity**: The survivor expresses authentic identity rather than performing a role. Quentin Crisp's journey from "retired waif" to celebrated eccentric is Siegel's example: health follows self-acceptance, not conformity.

**Independence and assertiveness**: The survivor refuses to submit to institutional or relational passivity. Siegel's "bad patient" list — questions every test, refuses without explanation, demands dignity — is the survivor's survival toolkit.

The survivor personality is not a fixed type but a developmental achievement. ECaP's goal is to help patients grow into it.

## The Four Questions

Siegel's four diagnostic questions are tools for assessing and revealing the will to live, for surfacing unconscious material, and for guiding the therapeutic conversation. They are asked not rhetorically but seriously, and the patient's answers — including the answers they cannot give — are the starting point for the work.

### 1. Do you want to live to be 100?

This question is not about age preference but about whether the patient has a reason to continue. Siegel consistently found that only 15–20% of audiences spontaneously raise their hands when he asks who wants to live to 100 — the same percentage as the survivor group in ECaP. Gerontologist Ken Dychtwald found that when asked "When do you want to die?", most people gave ages between 60 and 65, assuming life after that point would be characterised by dependence, pain, and meaninglessness. The question opens the conversation about what would make the future worth inhabiting.

### 2. What happened in the year or two before your illness?

This question uses the Holmes-Rahe stress scale framework — life events (divorce, retirement, bereavement, children leaving, career reversal) that preceded the diagnosis. It identifies the stress-predisposing factors discussed in the biography-is-biology literature. But its deeper purpose is to help the patient see the connection between what they lost and what their body did — not to create guilt but to give them agency: if their biography contributed to the illness, their new choices can contribute to the recovery.

### 3. What does your illness mean to you?

This question surfaces the patient's operative belief about their situation. For many patients, cancer means death — and that meaning, held unconsciously, becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. For others, cancer means release (Type 1), or failure (Type 2), or challenge (Type 3). The patient who answers "Cancer means a challenge I can address" is already in a different biological situation from the patient who answers "Cancer means I'm going to die in six months."

Jennifer's case: admitted to a hospice programme, expected to die within six months, kept living because she was committed to watching the seasons change. She became a "hospice dropout" and joined ECaP. Her illness meant: "Something I can navigate if I stay present to what I love."

### 4. Why do you need your illness?

This is the most confrontational question — and the most therapeutically powerful. Illness gives patients permission to do things their lives had denied them: to say no to unwanted demands, to take time off, to receive love and attention, to retire from a despised career, to have an excuse for setting limits on a destructive relationship.

The question is not a statement of blame ("You made yourself sick") but an invitation to identify what needs the illness is meeting — so that those needs can be met through other means. If love and attention come only through being sick, the patient has an unconscious reason to remain sick. If the patient can find other routes to those same needs, the illness loses its function.

Siegel gives the example of Gladys, a patient who manipulated her family through her symptoms for fifty years. His goal was not to shame her but to help her find direct ways to connect. The illness had been her only language for "I need love."

## Imagery, Drawing, and the Unconscious

ECaP uses visual tools to bypass the defences of language. Patients are asked to draw three images:
1. A picture of their disease, their treatment, and their immune cells
2. A landscape scene
3. A picture of their home and family

These drawings are interpreted in group sessions with guidance from the therapist. Susan Bach's 30-year research on patients' drawings (published in *Psychosomatische Medizin*, 1980) documented how patients' pictures revealed diagnoses, prognoses, and hidden conflicts that neither patient nor physician had consciously acknowledged.

[Jeanne Achterberg](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md) and [G. Frank Lawlis](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md) extended this into quantified research: in a study of 126 cancer patients, imagery assessment (evaluating the imagery's vividness, activity level, and symbolic content) predicted subsequent disease state more accurately than any blood chemistry marker. In a study of 200 patients, Achterberg achieved 95% accuracy predicting which patients would die within two months vs. enter remission.

Siegel uses drawings in clinical assessment, before surgery (to understand the patient's beliefs about the procedure), and in ECaP sessions (to surface conflicts and unconscious attitudes toward treatment). Dreams are interpreted alongside drawings as a second window into the body's knowledge.

## ECaP in the Context of Other Programmes

ECaP emerged from [O. Carl Simonton](../thinkers/o-carl-simonton.md) and Stephanie Matthews-Simonton's work at the Cancer Counseling and Research Center, documented in *Getting Well Again* (1978). Siegel's contribution was to move the Simonton method from a relatively structured imagery protocol toward a more open therapeutic relationship centred on authentic emotional expression, love, and survivor personality development.

The Simonton group therapy programme (using imagery, meditation, and psychological techniques) produced double the average survival time in 159 patients compared to the statistical norm; 19% of those patients saw their cancers regress and 22% stabilise. Siegel viewed ECaP as extending this finding by adding the emotional and relational dimensions that the imagery-focused model did not fully address.

A Yale study by George Gellert and Hal Morgenstern that compared ECaP members with a non-ECaP group found ECaP members showed longer survival time over the follow-up period; Morgenstern noted that the patient self-selection process might skew results, but concluded the difference "deserves observation and follow-up planning."

Kenneth Pelletier studied patients who recovered against the odds and found five characteristics:
1. Profound change in attitude: prayer, meditation, or contemplative practice
2. Profound changes in relationships — people now placed on an equal footing
3. Alterations in diet — food taken seriously as nourishment rather than habit
4. A sense of responsibility for all aspects of life
5. A feeling that recovery was not a gift or a remission but something they had participated in earning

## Critique

**The evidence problem**: ECaP's outcomes rest primarily on Siegel's clinical narrative, the Simonton results, and the Gellert-Morgenstern study — all of which involve self-selected, motivated patients. Controlled trials of psychological interventions in oncology show mixed results. A widely cited 1989 Stanford study by David Spiegel found that group therapy extended survival in metastatic breast cancer patients by an average of 18 months; but subsequent attempts to replicate this finding showed variable results, and the mechanism remains debated.

**The self-blame problem**: Siegel is aware that the ECaP model can be read as blaming patients for their cancers — if attitude causes illness, then a bad attitude caused your cancer. He explicitly rejects this reading: the cancer personality develops in response to childhood conditions the patient did not choose; the goal is not guilt but agency. Critics argue that this distinction is hard to maintain in the cultural reception of the work, where many patients do experience shame and self-blame when their cancer progresses despite their best efforts.

**The survivor personality claim**: Al Siebert's work on survivor personalities was based on interview research rather than controlled prospective studies. The specific traits he identified have not been validated as cancer survival predictors in peer-reviewed research. Siegel presents them as clinically confirmed through ECaP experience rather than as experimental findings.

**Note**: Mainstream oncology does not prescribe psychological therapy as a cancer treatment, though it does support psychological interventions for quality of life, management of anxiety and depression, and treatment adherence. The specific survival claims made for ECaP-type programmes — that attitude change extends survival — remain contested in the research literature.
