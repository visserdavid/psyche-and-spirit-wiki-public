# Fight-or-Flight Response

**Summary**: Walter Cannon's term for the body's emergency activation system — the evolutionarily ancient response that prepares the organism for physical combat or escape by mobilising maximum energy; the foundational problem in Herbert Benson's research, because modern life repeatedly activates it without permitting physical discharge, producing hypertension and stress-related disease.

**Sources**:
- [Benson, H., & Klipper, M. Z. (1975). *The relaxation response*. William Morrow.](../sources/the-relaxation-response.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Discovery

[Walter Cannon](../thinkers/walter-cannon.md) (1871–1945), Professor of Physiology at Harvard Medical School, named and described the fight-or-flight response in the early twentieth century. Observing that animals confronted with threat underwent a suite of coordinated physiological changes, he recognised these as a unified emergency response — ancient, conserved across species, and activated by the sympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system.

The response is not a conscious act. It is controlled by the hypothalamus — the brain's autonomic command centre — and operates outside voluntary awareness. When the hypothalamus reads an emergency, it triggers the sympathetic nervous system and the adrenal glands, which release epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine (noradrenaline) into the bloodstream.

## Physiology

When the fight-or-flight response is activated:

| System | Change | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Blood pressure | Increases | Drives blood faster to muscles |
| Heart rate | Increases | Pumps more blood per minute |
| Breathing rate | Increases | Delivers more oxygen |
| Body metabolism | Increases | Burns fuel faster for energy |
| Blood flow | Redirects massively to skeletal muscles (arms and legs) | Prepares for running or fighting |
| Digestion | Suspended | Non-essential in an emergency |
| Blood lactate | Increases (muscular metabolism) | Correlates with anxiety states |

The response is integrated and automatic: all changes occur together, controlled as a unified programme by the hypothalamus.

## The Evolutionary Logic

The fight-or-flight response evolved as a survival mechanism. Cannon's cat arching its back, pupils dilated, facing a dog — or a human ancestor sprinting from a predator — was experiencing the response as it was designed: a brief, intense mobilisation immediately discharged through physical action (running or fighting). The response then subsides.

The central problem Herbert Benson identified is that modern human life repeatedly activates the response in situations that provide neither the discharge nor the resolution:
- A job threatened by a supervisor cannot be resolved by fighting the supervisor
- A mortgage crisis cannot be resolved by running away
- A traffic jam, a family conflict, a looming deadline — each triggers the physiological pattern; none can be resolved through the physical actions the response prepares

When the response is elicited repeatedly and chronically without physical discharge, its effects accumulate. Blood pressure remains elevated. Stress hormones remain elevated. The body lives in a continuous state of costly preparation for an emergency that never resolves.

## The Modern Epidemic: Hypertension

[Herbert Benson](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md) proposed chronic fight-or-flight activation as the primary driver of essential hypertension — the 90–95 percent of high blood pressure cases with no identifiable anatomical cause. In *The Relaxation Response* (1975), he assembled evidence:

**J. Brod's experiment** (Czechoslovakia): subjects were given a mental arithmetic task (subtract 17 from 1,194 repeatedly, against a metronome, with observers present and making comments). The result was the full integrated fight-or-flight response: elevated blood pressure, increased heart rate, increased muscle blood flow. Mental stress — not physical threat — produced a complete emergency activation.

**Population studies**:
- Puerto Rican migrants moving to urban areas: blood pressure correlated with degree of Westernisation and urban adjustment demands
- African Zulu tribe: BP rose with migration to cities
- Fijian Islanders: BP tracked degree of Westernisation
- Texas City explosion (1947): elevated blood pressures in surrounding patients in the days following the blast
- University of Michigan (E. Harburg): blacks in high-stress Detroit ghetto neighbourhoods had higher BP than class-matched blacks in less-stressed areas

**Animal model**: Folkow and Rubinstein (University of Göteborg) implanted electrodes in rat hypothalami to stimulate the fight-or-flight area electrically. Rats receiving repeated stimulation developed persistent, chronic hypertension. Controls maintained normal blood pressure. This demonstrated that repeated hypothalamic activation alone — without dietary, renal, or genetic factors — can produce sustained elevation of blood pressure.

## The Holmes-Rahe Scale

Thomas H. Holmes and Richard H. Rahe (University of Washington Medical School) developed the **Social Readjustment Rating Scale** to quantify the stress of life-change events — measuring not only negative but positive changes, because all change requires adjustment and adjustment is the proximate cause of fight-or-flight activation.

Highest-rated events (Life Change Units):
- Death of spouse: 100
- Divorce: 73
- Separation: 65
- Imprisonment: 63
- Death of close family member: 63
- Personal injury or illness: 53
- Marriage: 50

Key finding: Holmes and Rahe documented that widows and widowers die at ten times the normal rate in the year following their spouse's death; divorced persons have five times the illness rate of married counterparts. The point is not that bad feelings are dangerous — it is that the physiological activation required to adapt to any major change, if sustained, produces cumulative biological cost.

## The Neural Mechanism

The fight-or-flight response operates through the **sympathetic nervous system** — one branch of the autonomic nervous system (which controls functions outside conscious will: heart rate, blood pressure, breathing, digestion). The complementary branch is the **parasympathetic system**, which governs rest, repair, and restoration.

When the hypothalamus activates the sympathetic system:
1. The adrenal medulla releases **epinephrine** (adrenaline) and **norepinephrine** (noradrenaline)
2. These hormones trigger the cardiovascular, respiratory, and metabolic changes above
3. Blood lactate rises as skeletal muscles burn fuel rapidly
4. The immune system is partially suppressed (non-essential in an emergency)

Walter R. Hess (Nobel Prize, Physiology/Medicine, 1949) demonstrated in cat experiments that the hypothalamus controls both the sympathetic (ergotropic, activating) state and its complement — the parasympathetic (trophotropic, restorative) state. These two states are mutually inhibitory: activating one suppresses the other. This is the biological foundation of the [relaxation response](relaxation-response.md) as fight-or-flight's counterpart.

## Relation to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Hans Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome](../thinkers/hans-selye.md)**: Selye's three stages of stress response (alarm, adaptation, exhaustion) describe the same phenomenon from an endocrinological perspective. The alarm reaction is fight-or-flight; the exhaustion stage is what happens when it cannot be resolved. Benson and Selye are describing the same biological emergency from different angles.

**[Biography Is Biology](biography-is-biology.md)**: [Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s thesis that life history is encoded in the body requires a biological mechanism. The fight-or-flight response is that mechanism: each unresolved conflict, each chronic fear, each sustained resentment maintains a low-level activation of the sympathetic system, depleting energy from the organs corresponding to that conflict over time.

**[Psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md)**: The fight-or-flight response suppresses immune function (via cortisol and adrenalin) as a short-term adaptation — energy for immediate survival at the expense of long-term maintenance. Chronic activation therefore chronically suppresses immunity. This is the PNI mechanism linking stress to illness.

**[Guilt and Soul Sickness](guilt-and-soul-sickness.md)**: [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) identifies chronic shame as a generator of continuous, low-level fight-or-flight activation — what she calls "soul sickness." The shame-based personality lives in the same physiological state as an organism permanently poised for threat.

**[The Relaxation Response](relaxation-response.md)**: Benson's proposed solution — the trophotropic counter-response that reverses every parameter the fight-or-flight response raises, accessible through meditation, focused breathing, repetitive prayer, or any technique with the four structural elements.

## Critique

**Oversimplification**: The fight-or-flight model is a useful heuristic but oversimplifies the stress response. Modern stress physiology recognises that responses vary enormously by stressor type (acute vs. chronic, controllable vs. uncontrollable, social vs. physical), individual appraisal (Lazarus and Folkman's cognitive appraisal model), and context. Some researchers add a "freeze" response (immobility under inescapable threat) and a "tend-and-befriend" response (social affiliation, especially in females — Shelley Taylor) as distinct patterns.

**The cortisol specificity problem**: Epinephrine and cortisol are both stress hormones, but they have different time courses and effects. Cannon's fight-or-flight model was based on epinephrine (immediate, brief); Selye's GAS was based on cortisol (sustained). The integration of these two systems into a single "fight-or-flight" framework can obscure important distinctions.

**Note**: Mainstream physiology and medicine fully endorse the fight-or-flight response as a real, well-characterised biological system. Its link to hypertension and stress-related disease is among the best-established findings in psychosomatic medicine.
