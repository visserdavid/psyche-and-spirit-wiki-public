# Five Stages of Dying

**Summary**: Kübler-Ross's empirically observed five-stage model of the emotional response to terminal diagnosis — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance — with hope as a constant undercurrent; the foundational framework of modern death studies and the direct precursor of Brennan's seven-stage healing model.

**Sources**:

## Overview

From interviews with over 200 terminally ill patients at the University of Chicago Billings Hospital between 1965 and 1969, Elisabeth [Kübler-Ross](../thinkers/elisabeth-kubler-ross.md) identified a recurring pattern of emotional responses to the news of impending death. She named five stages through which most patients moved, in some form and in some order, as they came to terms with dying.

Kübler-Ross was emphatic on several points:
- The stages are *not* strictly sequential — patients move back and forth between them
- Stages can coexist — a patient may be simultaneously in denial and in anger
- Individual variation is enormous — some patients skip stages entirely; some spend weeks in a stage that another passes through in an afternoon
- The model is descriptive, not prescriptive — it maps territory, not a required path
- **Hope** persists across all five stages as a constant undercurrent and is the last thing patients relinquish

## The Five Stages

### Stage 1: Denial and Isolation

The first response to terminal diagnosis is almost universally *"No, not me."* Denial serves as a necessary buffer — a psychological shock absorber that gives the person time to mobilise their defences and prepare for what cannot yet be fully confronted.

Denial takes various forms: requests for second opinions, conviction that the X-rays were mixed up, shopping for doctors who will contradict the diagnosis. Of Kübler-Ross's 200+ patients, nearly all passed through some period of denial, but only three maintained it to the end.

The key clinical point: denial belongs to *the patient's* schedule, not the caregiver's comfort or the physician's communication preference. When staff or family rush patients out of denial — usually to relieve their own anxiety — they serve themselves, not the patient. Denial is replaced, at the patient's pace, by partial acceptance.

### Stage 2: Anger

When denial can no longer be maintained, it is replaced by feelings of rage, resentment, and envy. *"Why me? Why not the eighty-two-year-old man across the hall who is of no use to anyone?"*

The anger is typically **displaced** — projected onto whoever is available: physicians ("they don't know what they're doing"), nurses (blamed for every discomfort), visiting family, or God. Kübler-Ross's extended cases show that this displacement is not capricious: it is the howl of a person who has been stripped of every form of control and meaningful future.

The worst staff responses are avoidance, shortening visits, and defensiveness — all of which confirm the patient's sense of abandonment and entrench the anger. The most effective response is dignified acknowledgment: *"You have every reason to be angry."*

The case of Mr. O., a controlling businessman with Hodgkin disease, illustrates the constructive management of second-stage anger: by consistently reframing choices as belonging to the patient ("Bet you'll decide to start eating"), his care team restored his sense of control and allowed his anger to subside naturally.

### Stage 3: Bargaining

Less discussed and shorter-lived than the other stages, bargaining is the stage of conditional acceptance and hidden deals. Patients make private negotiations: *"If I can see my son's wedding, I'll accept the rest."* *"If I dedicate my life to God, perhaps He will grant me more time."*

Kübler-Ross's paradigm case is an opera singer with a jaw malignancy who could no longer perform but asked to do so "just once more." She performed — and then bargained again. None of Kübler-Ross's patients kept their bargains; the pattern repeated like a child's "Just one more story."

Many bargains involve guilt (promises of religious devotion, of corrected behaviour) and are kept secret, shared only with the hospital chaplain. The chaplain's role is to hear these without judgement and help patients release the guilt that enforces the bargain — guilt that, if unaddressed, can intensify depression.

### Stage 4: Depression

When the physical toll of illness makes denial impossible and the losses accumulate, the patient enters depression. Kübler-Ross distinguishes **two types**:

**Reactive depression** — grief about losses already suffered: the woman who no longer feels like a woman after a mastectomy; the breadwinner who can no longer work; the mother whose children are in others' care. This responds to active intervention: reassurance, problem-solving, addressing practical concerns.

**Preparatory depression** — grief about impending loss: the anticipatory mourning of everything and everyone that must be released. This is a *necessary process*, not a pathology to be corrected. Attempts to cheer the patient at this stage interfere with the inner work of preparation. Kübler-Ross's guidance is unambiguous:

> "The patient who is allowed to express his sorrow will find a final acceptance much easier. It is a tool to prepare for the impending loss of all the love objects."

The appropriate response to preparatory depression is *not* cheerfulness but presence — sitting in silence, holding a hand, communicating without words that the person is not alone.

### Stage 5: Acceptance

Acceptance is not happiness or resignation. Kübler-Ross describes it as *"almost devoid of feelings"* — a time of quiet contemplation, of inner readiness. The struggle is over. The patient who has worked through the preceding stages reaches a point of waiting that carries a quality of completion and dignity.

The circle of interest narrows: the patient may prefer silence, brief visits, and the presence of one trusted person over stimulation or visitors. Communication becomes more physical — a touch, a gesture — than verbal.

**Acceptance is not resignation** (passive despair) and not **depression** (stage 4's preparatory grief). It has an active, earned quality — something the patient has arrived at, not merely collapsed into.

Family members and staff often find acceptance the hardest stage to witness, because they interpret the patient's detachment as rejection or "giving up." The case of Mrs. W. — who had reached acceptance and wanted to be left to die in peace but was subjected to unwanted surgery because her husband and surgical team could not accept her acceptance — is Kübler-Ross's most painful illustration of the harm that can result from forcing stage 5 patients back into earlier stages.

### Hope: The Constant Thread

Hope runs as an undercurrent through all five stages. Nearly all of Kübler-Ross's patients, even those in final acceptance, maintained some residue of hope — for an experimental drug, a remission, a miracle. "I think they never totally give up the possibility that there is a slight chance that something might work."

When a patient stops expressing hope, Kübler-Ross found it was often a sign that death was imminent within 24 hours. The physician's role is to be honest about prognosis without extinguishing hope:

> "It is the physician's task not to take away hope, but to mean by hope what the patient needs it to mean."

## Extension: Brennan's Seven-Stage Healing Model

[Barbara Ann Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md) in *Light Emerging* (1993) explicitly builds on Kübler-Ross's five stages to describe the patient's emotional journey through *healing* (not only dying). She retains all five stages and adds two further stages:

- **Stage 6: Rebirth** — the emergence of new life; disorientation as old identity dissolves; access to the Higher Self
- **Stage 7: Creation** — active building of a new life aligned with the soul's purpose

The resulting model, documented in [Seven Stages of Healing](seven-stages-of-healing.md), applies the arc of the dying process to the healing process — arguing that genuine healing, like dying, requires a passage through denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance before new life can emerge.

## Connections Within the Wiki

**Crisis as initiation**: The five stages are one of the richest empirical maps of [Crisis as Initiation](crisis-as-initiation.md) from the inside — how the psyche actually moves through the descent before transformation. Victor Turner's liminal zone corresponds closely to stages 1–4; acceptance is the threshold.

**Healing vs. curing**: A patient in acceptance — at peace with dying — is one of the clearest illustrations of [Healing vs. Curing](healing-vs-curing.md): wholeness and peace achieved independently of any physical recovery.

**The psychology of illness**: The five stages provide the most clinically grounded account of the emotional phenomenology of serious illness — the precursor of the entire psycho-oncology field. See [The Psychology of Illness](the-psychology-of-illness.md).

**Near-death experiences**: The life review documented by Ring, Morse, and other NDE researchers resonates with the review of one's life that acceptance tends to prompt — and with Kübler-Ross's own later conviction (following this book) that death is a transition rather than an ending.

**Forgiveness**: Borysenko's [six-step forgiveness process](forgiveness.md) can be understood as a structured traversal of stages 2 through 5 in a non-terminal context — anger acknowledged, bargaining dissolved, grief completed, acceptance reached.

**Dehumanised dying and modern medicine**: Kübler-Ross's critique of hospital dying directly parallels Dossey's critique of Era I and II medicine. See [Meaning and Health](meaning-and-health.md) and [Two Paradigms of Medicine](two-paradigms-of-medicine.md).

## Critique

**Not empirically validated as universal or sequential**: Subsequent research has consistently failed to confirm that the five stages occur universally, in order, or as distinct categories. Wortman and Silver's research (1989) found that many bereaved people do not reach "acceptance" and that this does not indicate a failure to grieve. The stages are best understood as a useful heuristic, not an empirical law.

**Sample limitations**: All patients were drawn from a single university hospital in 1960s Chicago. Cultural, religious, and socioeconomic variation was not systematically studied.

**Prescriptive misuse**: The model has been widely misapplied as a *normative* sequence — patients (and professionals) sometimes judge themselves or others against it, concluding that grief "out of order" or without reaching "acceptance" represents a failure. This is contrary to Kübler-Ross's intent and has caused harm.

**Scope creep**: The five stages were developed specifically from patients facing their own *death*. Their extension to all forms of grief and loss, though widely practised, involves assumptions about equivalence that have not been tested.

Note: The mainstream scientific consensus in grief psychology (e.g., continuing bonds theory, dual-process model — Stroebe & Schut, 1999) regards stage models as descriptively useful but empirically unsupported as universal sequences. Kübler-Ross's model remains clinically influential precisely because its descriptive richness captures what many dying patients report, even if it cannot be validated as a universal law.

## Tibetan Buddhist Parallel

[Sogyal Rinpoche](../thinkers/sogyal-rinpoche.md) in *[The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying](../sources/tibetan-book-of-living-and-dying.md)* explicitly acknowledges Kübler-Ross's work as a Western counterpart to the Tibetan teaching on the dying process. The parallel:

- Kübler-Ross's five stages describe the *emotional-psychological* process of the personality coming to terms with death during the [bardo of dying](bardos.md)
- The Tibetan teaching describes the *energetic dissolution* process during the same period — earth, water, fire, air, and space dissolving in sequence

Both point toward the same destination: a quality of openness and readiness that enables recognition of what lies beyond ordinary consciousness. Kübler-Ross's Acceptance (Stage 5) is the personality's equivalent of the meditative preparation that allows recognition of [Ground Luminosity](ground-luminosity.md) at the moment of death.
