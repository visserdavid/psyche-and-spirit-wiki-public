# Five Stages of Sacred Contract

**Summary**: Myss's model of how a Sacred Contract unfolds across a lifetime — five stages (Contact, Heeding the Call, Renaming, Assignments, Surrender) drawn from the biographies of Abraham, Jesus, Muhammad, the Buddha, and contemporary spiritual teachers.

**Sources**:
- [Myss, C. (2001). *Sacred contracts: Awakening your divine potential*. Harmony Books.](../sources/sacred-contracts.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

Myss argues that every person's Sacred Contract unfolds in a recognisable pattern of stages. She derives this model by reading across the biographies of the founders of the world's major spiritual traditions — Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, and Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha) — and finding common structural features. She also illustrates the stages with contemporary figures: Ram Dass, Hilda Charlton, and Bernie Glassman.

The stages are not linear in the sense of happening once and being complete. They recur across a lifetime, at different scales and in different domains of life. Each major life transition or calling may initiate a new cycle of the five stages.

## Stage 1: Contact

The first breakthrough of Divine awareness into ordinary consciousness. This may take any form: a dream, a vision, a chance encounter, a coincidence, a crisis, a book, a voice, an overwhelming feeling of being addressed or redirected.

**Biblical/historical examples**: Yahweh's first call to Abram to leave Ur; Jesus's baptism by John in the River Jordan; Muhammad's visions in the cave of Hira; the Buddha's encounter with old age, illness, and death outside the palace walls; Ram Dass's moment of inexplicable recognition when his guru Neem Karoli Baba named the manner and time of his mother's death.

**Key feature**: The contact is not invited or sought in the moment it arrives. It interrupts ordinary consciousness and cannot be easily rationalised away, though the ego often tries.

## Stage 2: Heeding the Call

Acting on the awareness received in the contact. This stage involves a first concrete response — not necessarily a complete commitment, but a movement toward the direction indicated. It typically involves leaving something behind: a former identity, a location, a role, a set of expectations.

**Examples**: Abraham's departure from Haran with his family; Jesus entering the desert for forty days after his baptism; Muhammad beginning to convey revelations to his circle; Ram Dass returning to the United States from India transformed and beginning to lecture and teach.

**Key feature**: The call must be *acted upon*. Awareness without action does not advance the Contract. Ram Dass phrases this as: "After enlightenment, chop wood, carry water" — the ordinary continues, but from within a reoriented frame.

## Stage 3: Renaming

A shift in identity corresponding to a new stage of the Contract. The soul that has passed through contact and initial heeding is no longer the same as before. In many traditions this shift is marked by a change of name or by an initiation ceremony.

**Examples**: Abram becomes Abraham; Jacob becomes Israel; Jesus is identified as the Messiah (the Anointed One); Muhammad receives his mission and the epithet "al-Amin" (the Trustworthy) becomes central to his identity; Siddhartha Gautama becomes the Buddha; Richard Alpert becomes Ram Dass.

**Key feature**: The renaming does not need to be literal. It represents the moment when the ego's former self-narrative gives way to a soul-level identity. For most people, this is not a public ceremony but an internal recognition: "I am no longer who I was; I am becoming who I am meant to be."

## Stage 4: Assignments

The accumulation of tasks, relationships, challenges, and opportunities that constitute the active content of the Contract. These assignments are not always chosen consciously — they are the events and encounters that arise precisely when they are needed for growth, often disguised as obstacles, crises, or encounters with "tyrants."

**Examples**: Abraham's test with Isaac; Jesus's ministry and his encounters with opponents; Muhammad's battles and reforms; the Buddha's forty-nine years of teaching across India.

**Key feature**: Assignments test the qualities developed in earlier stages. They demand courage, faith, endurance, and surrender. They often arrive as challenges to the very qualities the Contract is designed to develop — a teacher whose students revolt, a healer who becomes ill, an advocate whose cause fails.

## Stage 5: Surrender

The deepest stage — releasing the will to control the outcome of the Contract and trusting the Divine to direct the course of events. This is not passive resignation but active trust: continuing to act while releasing attachment to specific results.

**Examples**: Jesus's prayer in Gethsemane ("Not my will, but thine be done"); the Buddha's acceptance of nirvana while continuing to teach; Muhammad's surrender — *islam* means "surrender" — to the ongoing course of revelation; Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac.

**Key feature**: Surrender is not a once-for-all event. It recurs at every level of the Contract. Each new assignment brings a new frontier of control to release. The soul that has fully surrendered is not passive but paradoxically most fully itself — acting entirely from Divine direction rather than ego preference.

## The Stages in Contemporary Life

Myss illustrates the stages with people from her workshops. Liza, paralysed in an accident, came to see the accident as "the boat I was given to row with" — a Contact moment that required Heeding (accepting the limitation), Renaming (reorienting her identity around service rather than athletic ability), and continuing Assignments (working with children's organisations).

Sharon the journalist encountered Contact through a chance encounter with a police volunteer (Bill) and Heeded by shifting her reporting focus to people motivated by service — eventually marrying Bill as a confirmation of the subcontract her soul had registered.

## Relationship to the Mystic Tradition

Myss explicitly connects these stages to Evelyn Underhill's five stages of mystical development (as described in *Mysticism*, 1911): Awakening, Purification, Illumination, the Dark Night of the Soul, and Union. She notes that the stages are not identical but structurally parallel, suggesting a universal pattern of spiritual maturation.

Saint Teresa of Ávila's *Interior Castle* (interior rooms of the soul as stages of development) and Saint John of the Cross's *Dark Night of the Soul* are referenced as Western Christian expressions of the same structural progression.

## Critique

**From within the framework**: The model fits the biographies of religious founders more cleanly than ordinary lives. The lives of Abraham, Jesus, Muhammad, and the Buddha are literary and theological constructions as much as historical accounts, and Myss's reading of them is selective. The pattern of Contact-Heeding-Renaming-Assignments-Surrender is versatile enough to be applied to almost any life story in retrospect.

**Psychological perspective**: The five stages parallel models of psychological development (ego development theory, transformational learning theory). The value of the framework is that it provides a meaning structure for life transitions that many people find orienting. It does not, however, make testable predictions about when or how the stages will appear.

**Historical/scholarly perspective**: Myss's comparative reading of world religious founders is compressed and selective. Scholars of Islam, Buddhism, or biblical studies would note significant omissions and interpretive liberties. This is consistent with Myss's project — she is constructing a spiritual framework, not a scholarly comparison — but readers should be aware that the biographical portraits are schematic.
