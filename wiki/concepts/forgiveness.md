# Forgiveness

**Summary**: In Borysenko's framework, forgiveness is neither condoning nor forgetting — it is the systematic release of the ego's need to occupy the victim position and judge the offender, opening the way for the flow of grace; practised through six steps for forgiving oneself and six parallel steps for forgiving others, it is the culminating act of the hero's journey and the flower of psychospiritual growth.

## The Concept

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) defines forgiveness as "a process and an attitude." As a *process*, it converts the suffering created by mistakes and hurts into occasions for growth. As an *attitude*, it constitutes the stance of non-judgmental compassion from which the other and the self can be seen whole — not through the lens of victim and aggressor, debtor and creditor, but as co-learners.

Forgiveness does not mean:
- Condoning harmful behaviour
- Forgetting what happened
- Restoring a damaging relationship
- Pretending pain was not real
- Becoming a pushover (Borysenko: forgiveness is not "Pollyannaism")

Forgiveness does mean:
- Releasing the ego's grip on the victim role
- Seeing the person who harmed you as acting from their own pain and conditioning
- Moving from the position of "one who was wronged" to "one who is growing"
- Freeing the *forgiver* — who is most harmed by holding unforgiveness

The paradox: unforgiveness imprisons the person holding it, not the person toward whom it is directed. Milton's thirty years of alcoholism had injured many people; the guilt and resentment locked in his body were manifesting as angina. The forgiveness work was, medically, a physiological intervention.

## Compassion as the Ground

[Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md)'s reading of the Grail legend provides the framework. In Wolfram von Eschenbach's *Parzival*, the hero Parsifal reaches the Grail Castle and sees the Grail King wounded and suffering — but fails to ask "What ails thee?" This failure condemns him to years of further wandering. When he finally returns and asks the question — the simple act of compassionate attention — the King is healed and Parsifal achieves the Grail.

Campbell's interpretation: the Grail is "the fulfillment of the highest potentialities of human consciousness." It is attained not through achievement, power, or piety but through *compassion* — the willingness to enter another's suffering, to forget one's own ego-agenda, and to ask "What ails thee?"

Compassion (from Latin *cum-pati*, "to suffer with") is the capacity to leave one's own sphere of concern and enter the life of another. Buddhism articulates this as the *bodhisattva* ideal — the soul who has achieved liberation but chooses to remain in the cycle of suffering to help others. Jesus, in Paul's Epistle to the Philippians, "took the form of a servant" — the same act of compassionate descent.

Forgiveness is compassion applied: the willingness to see the person who hurt you as also suffering, also acting from fear and conditioning, also deserving of the love that is the universe's ground.

## P.L. Travers's Tale

Borysenko opens the forgiveness chapter with a literary image from Parabola Magazine: P.L. Travers (author of Mary Poppins) writes a tale of a woman on pilgrimage through an enchanted forest. She has been seeking something she cannot name or see. At a moment of decision, a lady in blue blocks her path and reaches to remove her veil — revealing a face the pilgrim recognises with the words "Forgive me." The lady's robe underneath is "tucked, puckered, and stained with living." The pilgrim, seeing this, opens her arms. The tale ends: an angel paused, witnessing the embrace. "Two became three! The Third One reconciles what has not been seen or known — the mercy to forgive."

The Third One — the one who is called into being by the act of forgiveness — is grace. Forgiveness does not merely repair the relationship; it opens a new dimension of being.

## Six Steps for Forgiving Oneself

1. **Take responsibility** — acknowledge the action without denial, rationalisation, or hiding. The addict who admits the addiction; the person who confesses the lie. This is the foundational move: "I did this."

2. **Confession** — disclose the nature of the wrong to God, to a trusted person, or to a support group. James Pennebaker's research: confession releases the metabolic cost of holding secrets. Jung: "Secrets hold emotion — unconfessed, they cut us off from the community of human beings." The sacrament of confession exists across traditions precisely because the relief of disclosure is real.

3. **Look at your good points** — fight the depression that follows exposure of the shadow. Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (*Restore My Soul*): "It is a duty to search for one's good points — always. Even in the worst person, there is something good." The guilt spiral that follows confession can become its own trap; finding the point of goodness interrupts the downward spiral.

4. **Make amends** where possible — without causing additional harm. The shoplifter mails the money. The person who wounded a friend in anger writes a note or makes a call. If the direct approach would cause more damage (showing up at a former partner's door years later), the amends can be made in meditation — imagining the conversation, offering apology, requesting forgiveness, extending forgiveness in return.

5. **Ask God for help** — prayer not as rote petition but as the living cry of the soul in distress. King David's psalms model this: the full range of emotion (grief, rage, pleading, gratitude, joy) turned toward God. The act of surrender — "I cannot do this alone; I need help" — creates the opening through which grace enters.

6. **Reflect: what have I learned?** — the final step converts the experience from wound to wisdom. Carlos Castaneda's framework: "petty tyrants" (difficult circumstances and people) are appointed teachers. Every mistake, when processed through the forgiveness steps, becomes instruction. The lesson learned is what forgiveness actually delivers.

## Six Steps for Forgiving Others

These parallel the self-forgiveness steps:

1. **Recognise what you are holding** — acknowledge that you are harbouring anger, resentment, or hurt toward a specific person. Not holding someone accountable is not forgiveness; it is denial. George (ulcers, estranged from his daughter) had to first admit he was holding the estrangement before anything else could shift.

2. **Confess the story to a neutral listener** — a friend, therapist, or clergy member who can hear without reinforcing the resentment. The listener's role is not to validate the hurt (which keeps one stuck as victim) but to bear witness with compassion. Seeking therapy is "a sign of strength, not weakness."

3. **Look for the good points in the person who hurt you** — this does not mean excusing what they did; it means recovering a fuller picture of who they are. George, remembering Rachel's specific qualities as a daughter and the love he once felt for her, began to feel the grief under the anger — and grief can move.

4. **Consider what action, if any, needs to be taken** — forgiveness is an internal state, but sometimes it requires an external action: a call, a letter, a meeting. Expressed feeling should precede action; anger vented in the moment of deciding to act produces words that cannot be unsaid.

5. **Ask God for help** — the willingness to forgive is itself the opening; the actual release often comes only through grace. The Buddhist *metta* (loving-kindness) practice: visualising the person who hurt you surrounded in light and sending them loving-kindness — beginning with the easiest (beloved friends) and extending gradually to the most difficult (the "enemy").

6. **Reflect: what has this person taught you?** — the petty tyrant as teacher (Castaneda). Don Juan placed Castaneda in years of apprenticeship to a tyrannical foreman precisely because what the foreman provoked — the need for patience, groundedness, and inner stability — was exactly what the apprentice needed to develop. Forgiveness does not require approving the tyrant's methods; it requires recognising the gift they carried.

## Forgiveness and the Body

Borysenko's clinical data and Pennebaker's research converge: unforgiveness is a physiological burden. Holding resentment activates the fight-or-flight response — chronically, in proportion to the intensity of the held grudge. The cortisol and adrenalin of unresolved anger suppress immune function, tighten the cardiovascular system, and contribute to the stress-related illness load.

Milton's angina (chronic guilt about thirty years of alcoholism's consequences); George's bleeding ulcers (held resentment at his estranged daughter) — both cases showed physiological improvement as forgiveness work proceeded.

The mechanism: resentment is a form of the same mental-to-physical pathway documented in [psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md). Anger rehearsal activates cortisol as surely as physical threat. The immune suppression is real; the recovery through forgiveness is physiologically plausible.

## Relationships as Forgiveness Teachers

Chapter Nine of the book treats intimate relationships as the primary school of forgiveness. Partners are mirrors: the qualities that most infuriate in the other are frequently disowned qualities of the self (shadow projection). The anger David directed at Sandy for her emotional "weakness" reflected his own disowned emotional need; Sandy's compliance with David's criticisms reflected her disowned assertion.

The David and Sandy case: a surgeon's Logos-dominant personality and a nurse's Eros-dominant personality produced years of escalating blame until crisis (Sandy's affair) forced both into the forgiveness process. Resolution came through:
- Recognising childhood origins of each reaction
- Seeing the partner's charge as a childhood echo, not a current reality
- Learning to bridge communication styles (aspect differences)
- Reaching the "51 percent point" — the grace-assisted tipping point where compassion replaced blame

"Petty tyrants" in relationships — whether partner, parent, or colleague — are, in Castaneda's formulation, the teachers one's life has assigned. Forgiveness is not the end of the relationship but the condition under which real relationship becomes possible.

## The Life Review

Borysenko draws on near-death experience (NDE) research ([Kenneth Ring](../thinkers/rachel-naomi-remen.md), Raymond Moody's *The Light Beyond*) to frame forgiveness in the largest temporal context. NDE accounts consistently describe a "life review" in which the person re-experiences the emotional impact of their actions on others — not from the outside but *from the inside of the person they affected*. This is not judgment by an external God but the direct experiential knowing of what love promoted and what fear obstructed.

The businessman who returned from near-death experience and wept at a flower — "I am now part of one living universe; to hurt any living thing is to hurt myself" — illustrates the shift that NDE research documents: the dissolution of the victim/aggressor frame in the experience of underlying unity.

Heaven and hell, in this reading, are not places but states of mind: the parable of the feast where both rooms have long spoons — in one, people feed each other; in the other, they starve trying to feed themselves. Compassion is the distinction.

## The Aramaic Depth: Fire in the Soul (Borysenko, 1993)

[*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md) deepens the forgiveness framework in several dimensions:

**The Aramaic Lord's Prayer**: Neil Douglas-Klotz's *Prayers of the Cosmos* (1990) translates the Lord's Prayer from Jesus's original Aramaic — a language far richer and more embodied than the Greek through which it was first transmitted to the West. The petition typically rendered "Forgive us our debts as we forgive our debtors" becomes, in the Aramaic *washboqlan*: "Loose the cords of mistakes binding us, releasing the strands we hold of others' guilt" — an image of *untying* rather than absolution. The Aramaic conveys karma as literal cord binding soul to unresolved action, and forgiveness as the cutting of those cords.

**Anger as self-imprisonment**: Borysenko demonstrates this kinesiologically with teenagers in a Boston school, including gang members. When a volunteer accesses the feeling of anger, arm strength collapses under two-finger pressure. When they access a loving memory, strength returns. The demonstration makes physiologically vivid what philosophy states abstractly: anger is not power but its loss. "The anger you hold against someone else holds you."

**The Circle of Women and karma-ending**: The seasonal healing circles Borysenko co-hosts provide the communal container in which forgiveness becomes possible — not as individual psychological effort but as collective ritual. The equinox meditation through the "City of Lights" and the Hall of Lives (where the fear/hurt pattern is displayed and its roots across apparent lifetimes become visible) represents forgiveness operating at the scale of karma rather than personal grievance. Resolving the pattern dissolves the compulsion to recreate it.

**Robin Casarjian**: Therapist and author of *Forgiveness: A Bold Choice for a Peaceful Heart* (1992), founder of the Lionheart Foundation (forgiveness workshops in prisons), Casarjian is Borysenko's closest collaborator in the forgiveness work. Borysenko describes the "Karma Ending" dream she had during a workshop they co-led: attacking energy that reproduced every time it was resisted, but that lost its power entirely when recognised and not engaged. The dream's teaching: what is not resisted cannot persist.

**Forgiveness and past lives**: The medieval England regression (an apparent lifetime in which Borysenko's husband Morgan killed her father, and she was condemned as a witch) reveals the root of a pattern of fear-of-betrayal that present-life analysis could not reach. Understanding the "lifetime" context — the soul's need to play both victim and betrayer across lifetimes in order to transcend the polarity — allowed forgiveness of actors she had not consciously known she was holding. The Nag Hammadi poem *Thunder Perfect Mind* provides the framework: the soul must hold and transcend both sides of every duality, not get stuck identifying with one (betrayer or betrayed) against the other.

## Connections to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Healing vs. Curing](healing-vs-curing.md)**: Sam's story (from *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind*) demonstrates forgiveness as the path to healing in the absence of curing. Sam achieved wholeness — completion, peace, genuine presence — through a year of forgiveness work that included the very relationship with the illness that was killing him. He did not survive; he healed.

**[Spirit Retrieval](spirit-retrieval.md)** (Myss): Myss's practice of calling energy back from wounds, resentments, and the past is the energy-field equivalent of Borysenko's forgiveness steps. Both describe the same movement: releasing the grip of biographical injury so that energy becomes available for present life and healing.

**[Woundology](woundology.md)** (Myss): Forgiveness is the antidote to woundology. The woundologist uses the wound as currency; forgiveness releases its hold. Both Myss and Borysenko identify unforgiveness as a primary obstacle to healing — from different angles (social/economic for Myss, psychological/spiritual for Borysenko).

**[Attitude and Health](attitude-and-health.md)** (Shealy): Shealy's data on forgiveness and health align with Borysenko's: forgiveness and serenity are the strongest attitude predictors of health; resentment and anger are among the most health-depleting. Different frameworks, same clinical observation.

## Critique

**The burden on the harmed**: Forgiveness frameworks can be weaponised — used to pressure victims of serious harm to "forgive and move on" before processing the harm or seeking redress. Borysenko explicitly addresses this: forgiveness is *not* about the perpetrator's comfort or about avoiding accountability. George did not need to reconcile with Rachel before forgiving her; Milton's amends did not require him to accept his alcoholic behaviour as acceptable. But the critique remains: in contexts of serious abuse or violence, forgiveness frameworks require careful handling.

**The timeline question**: Borysenko presents forgiveness as achievable through the six-step process within a defined clinical program. Grief researchers (Kübler-Ross, Worden) suggest that forgiveness — particularly after severe injury — may take years or decades and has no guaranteed arrival. The six steps may better describe a *direction* than a *destination*.

**The spiritual premise**: The forgiveness steps embed a specific spiritual framework (prayer, God, grace). For non-theists, Steps 5 and 6 may require translation. Borysenko acknowledges this implicitly by using the language of "Higher Power" interchangeably with God, but the framework remains grounded in theistic spirituality.

## Hay's Contribution: Forgiveness as Cancer Prevention

[Louise Hay](../thinkers/louise-hay.md) grounds forgiveness in a more starkly causal claim than Borysenko: **releasing resentment will dissolve even cancer**. Resentment held long enough, Hay argues, eats at the body and produces cancer — it is not merely a contributing factor but the primary mechanism. By the same logic, forgiving those we resent removes the primary driver of the illness.

Hay quotes *A Course in Miracles* directly: "All disease comes from a state of unforgiveness." She adds her own formulation: "Whoever you most need to forgive is the person you NEED TO LET GO OF." Forgiveness is not a moral achievement — it is physiological self-preservation.

**The Emmet Fox exercise**: Hay's primary forgiveness technique is drawn from Emmet Fox (New Thought minister and author of *The Sermon on the Mount*, 1934). The practice:
1. Sit comfortably, close the eyes, and relax.
2. Imagine the person to be forgiven sitting on a stage in a darkened theatre.
3. Visualise only good things happening to this person — success, love, laughter.
4. See them smiling and at peace.
5. Hold the image for several minutes, then let it fade.

Hay adds a second step: the person leaves the stage and you step onto it. The same good things happen to you. "The exercise dissolves the clouds of resentment most of us carry." She recommends doing it once a day and measuring the change over a week or month.

**Forgiveness of self**: The most important target is oneself. Hay's mirror work (see [Mirror Work](mirror-work.md)) is essentially self-forgiveness practice: looking into one's own eyes and saying "I love you" replaces the internalised critic's verdict. Most clients find forgiving themselves harder than forgiving others — a finding Borysenko also identifies.

**Limits of Hay's account**: Unlike Borysenko, Hay offers no psychological process model for forgiveness — no stages, no therapeutic scaffolding, no acknowledgement that the exercise may need to be repeated over months or years. The Emmet Fox technique is presented as reliably effective, which may understate its difficulty for people carrying serious harm. [(Hay, 1984)](../sources/you-can-heal-your-life.md)

## ACIM's Radical Counterpart

[*A Course in Miracles*](../sources/a-course-in-miracles.md) offers the most metaphysically thoroughgoing version of forgiveness in the literature: true forgiveness recognises that no sin occurred because the world of apparent wrongdoing is a dream. Where Borysenko's six-step process works *through* the grievance, ACIM's forgiveness dissolves the ontological status of the grievance itself. Both frameworks converge on the same recognition — that the forgiver is freed, not merely the forgiven — but ACIM takes that recognition to its logical extreme. See [Forgiveness (ACIM)](forgiveness-acim.md) for a detailed comparison.
