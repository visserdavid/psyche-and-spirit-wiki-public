# Formative Psychology

**Summary**: Stanley Keleman's clinical approach to somatic education, working with the body's pulsatory organization rather than analysis or ideal posturing — aiming to restore the range of biological aliveness that chronic stress has narrowed.

## Overview

Formative Psychology is [Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md)'s name for his clinical and educational approach — the practical application of the framework laid out in *Emotional Anatomy*. The name reflects its central concern: the ongoing, living process by which the body *forms* itself, and the ways in which that forming can be consciously engaged and reorganized.

The approach is distinct from:
- **Psychoanalysis**: it does not centre on interpretation of the past or insight into unconscious content.
- **Relaxation techniques**: relaxing muscles does not reorganize the structural patterns underlying distress.
- **Ideal posturing**: imposing a correct posture on a person's habitual form does not produce lasting change and may make things worse (e.g., breathing exercises inappropriate to swollen structures, reposturing inappropriate to collapsed structures).

## Core Principle: Working with Pulsation

The foundation of Formative Psychology is the recognition that [pulsatory](pulsation.md) organization — the body's capacity to expand and contract across a full range — is the ground of health. Distress patterns narrow this range: rigid structures can expand but not yield; collapsed structures can yield but not expand; swollen structures diffuse without containing. Education means restoring the range.

The approach works by:
1. **Identifying the structural pattern** (which of the [somatic distress patterns](somatic-distress-patterns.md) is present, and in which layers).
2. **Engaging the person's own impulses** — not imposing change but inviting the organism's pulsatory intelligence to reorganize itself.
3. **Working with the continuum** — small, graduated movements that explore the space between current structure and expanded possibility, rather than dramatic cathartic interventions.
4. **Connecting soma and soul**: Keleman speaks of linking "head, heart, soma and soul" — the aim is not just structural change but the reconnection of physical process with feeling, thought, and meaning.

## The HOw Process: The Practical Methodology

*Embodying Experience* (1987) extends Formative Psychology from theory to practice through the five-step **[HOw Process](how-process.md)**. The steps are:

1. **What is my somatic image?** — recognize the current stance, posture, or pattern
2. **How do I organize/maintain it?** — identify the specific muscles and *intensify* the pattern deliberately (not for catharsis, but for knowledge)
3. **How do I stop?** — release the pattern incrementally, accordion-like, bit by bit
4. **What happens when I stop?** — sustain the somatic pause; allow suppressed impulses and feelings to surface without immediately filling the gap
5. **What do I do with what I have learned?** — form a new response and practice it

The key counter-intuitive insight from EE is that **inhibition is the foundation of change**, not an obstacle to it. The brain is primarily an organ of delay and differentiation — its capacity to stop, slow, and redirect impulses is what makes new learning possible. The HOw process is essentially an education in voluntary inhibition: Steps Two through Four train the practitioner to interrupt the automatic perpetuation of a somatic pattern and remain in the gap long enough for reorganization to begin.

## Somagrams

A second major contribution of *Embodying Experience* is the **[somagram](somagrams.md)** — a drawing of one's somatic image, representing the felt interior of one's bodily organization. Somagrams are not anatomically accurate drawings but somatic projections: they show areas of tension, constriction, deadening, impulse, and relational pattern. Used in conjunction with the HOw process, somagrams make visible what verbal language obscures.

## The Dialogue of Compartments

Somatic education, in Keleman's view, requires restoring dialogue between the body's compartments — between brain stem, thalamus, and cortex; between head pouch, chest pouch, and abdominal pouch; between inner layers and outer layers. Distress fragments this dialogue: trauma "congeals in one layer," leaving others frozen or overactivated. Health restores the conversation.

## Context: Formative Psychology at the Center for Energetic Studies

Keleman has developed this work over more than thirty years at the Center for Energetic Studies in Berkeley, California. The clinical education series (Patterns of Distress, Bonding, Love: A Somatic View) represents the application of the principles across specific relational and developmental domains.

## Connection to the Wiki's Energy Medicine Tradition

Formative Psychology shares structural concerns with [energy medicine](energy-anatomy.md) (the importance of the body's organizational field) and [medical intuition](medical-intuition.md) (reading the body's form as a text of biographical experience), but differs fundamentally in method. Keleman works with physically observable pulsation and structure; Myss and Shealy work with the subtle energy field. The healing aims overlap (restoration of aliveness, addressing biographical encoding in the body) while the means differ radically.

Formative Psychology's emphasis on gradual reorganization — not dramatic catharsis, not ideal form — resonates with the distinction [Myss draws between curing and healing](power-and-healing.md): both frameworks hold that surface-level intervention (symptom removal, relaxation) is insufficient without addressing the deeper structural or energetic pattern.

## Critique

Formative Psychology has not been the subject of controlled clinical trials. Its effectiveness is supported by clinical case reports and the testimony of practitioners and clients, but not by the randomized controlled evidence that mainstream medicine requires. The framework's complexity (identifying pattern combinations across layers and pouches) makes it difficult to operationalize for research. Like many body-centered approaches, it occupies a productive but scientifically unvalidated space between mainstream psychology and somatic medicine.
