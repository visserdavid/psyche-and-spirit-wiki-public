# Four Psychological Functions

**Summary**: Jung's model of four modes of psychic orientation — thinking, feeling, intuition, and sensation — combined with the introvert/extravert attitudes; the inferior function is the gateway to the unconscious, and the goal of individuation includes developing a more conscious relationship with it.

## Overview

The introversion/extraversion distinction first appears in this wiki's Jung material in [*Two Essays on Analytical Psychology*](../sources/two-essays-on-analytical-psychology.md) (1917), where Jung develops it not as personality typology in the abstract but as a diagnostic tool for explaining why [Freud and Adler](eros-and-will-to-power.md) reached opposite clinical conclusions from similar case material — Freud's theory characteristically extraverted (object-oriented), Adler's characteristically introverted (subject-oriented). See [Eros and the Will to Power](eros-and-will-to-power.md) for the full argument. The four-function model (thinking, feeling, sensation, intuition) crossed with this attitude-pair was only systematized later, in *Psychological Types* (1921).

In [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md), [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) introduces the four psychological functions as the modes through which human consciousness orients itself to experience. Every individual develops a primary reliance on one or two functions, while the complementary functions remain less developed — and the most neglected function (the **inferior function**) remains most deeply connected to the unconscious.

## The Four Functions

Jung organises the four functions into two pairs of opposites:

**Rational functions** (evaluative, involving judgment):
- **Thinking**: Comprehends experience through concepts and logical connections; asks "What does this mean?" and "How does this work?"
- **Feeling**: Evaluates experience through values and relational tone; asks "What is this worth?" and "Do I want this or not?" (Note: "feeling" in Jung does not mean emotion — it is a rational function of value-evaluation, distinct from affect)

**Irrational functions** (receptive, involving perception without judgment):
- **Intuition**: Perceives through hunches, possibilities, and the sense of meaning beyond what is immediately given; knows without knowing how it knows
- **Sensation**: Perceives through the senses in the immediate present; attends to facts, concrete details, and the physical reality of what is there

## The Two Attitudes

The functions operate within two orientations:
- **Extraversion**: Libido (psychic energy) primarily oriented outward, toward the object and the world
- **Introversion**: Libido primarily oriented inward, toward the subject's own inner world

The combination of dominant function and attitude produces eight basic psychological types — the foundation of subsequent personality typology systems, including the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI).

## The Superior and Inferior Function

No individual develops all four functions equally. The **superior function** is the most conscious and most reliably available — the person's primary mode of engagement with experience. The **auxiliary function** supports the superior. The **inferior function** — the function most opposite to the superior — remains the most undeveloped and most deeply connected to the unconscious.

The inferior function is significant for individuation:
- Because it is the least conscious, it is the most autonomous — it acts without the ego's direction
- It is the primary gateway through which the unconscious intrudes into conscious life (as compulsions, obsessions, blind spots, and involuntary reactions)
- It is also the function through which the unconscious communicates most directly — the inferior function is the door between the ego and the deeper psyche

A thinking type (superior function: thinking) will typically have feeling as the inferior function. Their emotional life will be relatively undeveloped, prone to archaic or violent expression when it does emerge, and capable of great depth precisely because it has not been domesticated by the ego's control.

## Relevance to [Individuation](individuation.md)

The goal of individuation is not to eliminate the inferior function but to develop a more conscious relationship with it — to expand the range of the personality by integrating what has been neglected. This integration is often uncomfortable: the inferior function typically emerges first in its raw, unmodified, even primitive form before it can be worked with.

In the case of "Henry" from [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) (recorded by [Jolande Jacobi](../thinkers/jolande-jacobi.md)), Henry's superior function is thinking (engineering, rationality, control) and his inferior function is feeling (relatedness, emotional commitment, the willingness to love). His entire individuation arc in the case study is the gradual development of feeling function — the willingness to remain with a woman, to commit, to accept the vulnerability of genuine relationship.

## Connections Across the Wiki

- [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s [chakra system](chakra-system.md) correlates with the four functions: thinking → fifth chakra (communication, logic); feeling → fourth chakra (love, relationship); sensation → first-second chakra (body, survival, desire); intuition → sixth-seventh chakra (inner knowing, spiritual perception)
- The inferior function as gateway to the unconscious parallels the shadow as the first autonomous figure encountered in individuation — both are "what has been least developed and most excluded"

## Note: MBTI and Type Reductionism

Jung's typology was systematised into the **Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI)**, which has become one of the most widely used personality instruments in business and education. Psychologists note that MBTI has limited test-retest reliability (people score differently on re-testing) and that its categorical (type rather than trait) structure oversimplifies Jung's original continuous model. Jung's typology was meant as a heuristic for understanding the unconscious dynamics of the inferior function — not as a fixed identity label.
