# Four Survival Archetypes

**Summary**: The four universal archetypes that Myss argues are present in every human psyche — Child, Victim, Prostitute, and Saboteur — representing the core challenges around dependency, power, faith, and self-sabotage that must be worked with to fulfil one's Sacred Contract.

**Sources**:
- [Myss, C. (2001). *Sacred contracts: Awakening your divine potential*. Harmony Books.](../sources/sacred-contracts.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

Unlike the eight personal archetypes (which differ between individuals), the four survival archetypes are shared by all human beings. Myss calls them "the four legs of the table on which the Sacred Contract rests." They represent the fundamental vulnerabilities and challenges through which personal power is developed — or surrendered. Each archetype has both a shadow expression (the pattern of disempowerment) and a light expression (the guardian function that supports growth).

The four archetypes function primarily through the lower three chakras — the energy centres governing survival, sexuality/creativity, and self-esteem — and they are the first layer to examine when working with the [Archetypal Wheel](archetypal-wheel.md).

## 1. The Child: Guardian of Innocence

### Core issue
Dependency versus responsibility. The Child archetype governs the deep patterns established in childhood: the perceptions of safety, nurturing, and loyalty formed before adulthood.

### Aspects
Myss identifies several variants: the Wounded Child, the Abandoned/Orphan Child, the Magical/Innocent Child, the Eternal Child (Puer Aeternus), the Dependent Child, the Divine Child. Each represents a different relationship to the need for care and the transition into adult responsibility.

### Shadow
The Wounded Child that refuses to grow; entrenching childhood wounds as reasons for current limitation. The Dependent Child that expects others to provide what it did not receive.

### Guardian function (light side)
When integrated, the Child archetype preserves wonder, openness to guidance, and the capacity to ask for help. It reconnects the adult to imagination and innocence without being captured by them.

### Example (Myss)
Ollie, who was forced into adult responsibilities at age six after his mother left, spent adulthood re-enacting this abandonment — taking extra days off work to "play" the childhood he never had — until the pattern was recognized and worked with.

## 2. The Victim: Guardian of Self-Esteem

### Core issue
Empowerment versus disempowerment. The Victim archetype manifests whenever power is surrendered to someone or something outside the self, or when injustice is endured without response.

### Shadow
Using victimhood as social currency or as a reason not to take responsibility. Staying in a situation that perpetuates powerlessness because it provides sympathy, avoidance, or familiar identity. (See [Woundology](woundology.md) for the broader cultural pattern.)

### Guardian function (light side)
When integrated, the Victim archetype becomes a highly sensitive detector of genuine injustice, and a motivator to establish boundaries and develop self-respect. Tim, in Myss's example, learned that protecting himself from being cheated was not aggression but an act of self-esteem.

## 3. The Prostitute: Guardian of Faith

### Core issue
Integrity versus compromise for security. The Prostitute archetype activates whenever survival is felt to be threatened and one is tempted to sell one's values, integrity, morals, or spirit for material security, approval, or safety.

### Shadow
Remaining in marriages, jobs, or situations that violate one's values because of financial fear. Performing actions that compromise the soul in exchange for security. Belinda, in Myss's account, realised she had been filtering every opinion through the question "What will this cost me in terms of office politics?"

### Guardian function (light side)
When integrated, the Prostitute becomes a fierce guardian of integrity and faith — an alert to moments when the soul is being asked to betray itself. It strengthens the conviction that the Divine will provide, making compromise unnecessary.

## 4. The Saboteur: Guardian of Choice

### Core issue
Fear of change versus courage to grow. The Saboteur archetype governs the patterns of self-undermining that arise when opportunities present themselves — creating disruption, self-doubt, or avoidance precisely when movement forward is most available.

### Shadow
Unconscious fear of empowerment. Patterns that abort dreams before they can be tested. The shadow Saboteur convinces the psyche that change is too dangerous and keeps life safely small.

### Guardian function (light side)
When integrated, the Saboteur becomes a sharp-eyed advisor — an internal voice that warns when genuine danger exists versus when fear is manufacturing obstacles. It supports the development of discernment: knowing when caution is wisdom and when it is avoidance.

## Working with the Four Survival Archetypes

Myss suggests a systematic process for identifying how each archetype operates:

1. **Recognition**: Notice when the archetype is active — the feeling of victimisation, the impulse to compromise, the impulse to abort an opportunity, the pull toward dependency.
2. **Interview the archetype**: In journalling, address the archetype directly; ask what it needs, what it is protecting, what it is teaching.
3. **Identify the guardian function**: Ask how the shadow pattern can be transformed into a source of strength.
4. **Apply to the chakra system**: Each archetype connects to specific chakra levels — understanding which energy centre is engaged helps reveal the depth and origin of the pattern. See [Chakra System](chakra-system.md).

## Relationship to the Archetypal Wheel

When the four survival archetypes are placed on the [Archetypal Wheel](archetypal-wheel.md), they reveal which domains of life are most burdened by survival fears and which offer the greatest opportunities for liberation. The house in which each archetype lands provides context for how the core issue manifests.

## Critique

**From within the literature**: The four archetypes overlap considerably with each other and with other psychological frameworks. The Victim is not far from the learned helplessness described in mainstream cognitive psychology; the Saboteur resembles the inner critic of inner-child therapy.

**Mainstream psychological perspective**: The names Victim and Prostitute are charged and potentially stigmatising. Myss is aware of this and argues they are used precisely because their connotations make the patterns visible — the discomfort with the name is itself diagnostic. However, critics note that using pathologising language can reinforce shame rather than facilitate healing.

**Empirical status**: The claim that these four archetypes are universal is asserted rather than demonstrated. Cross-cultural psychology offers no equivalent taxonomy, though related patterns (dependency, victimhood, compromise, self-sabotage) are widely recognised in clinical literature.
