# Fragmentation and Wholeness

**Summary**: David Bohm's argument that the habit of treating convenient divisions of thought — nations, professions, disciplines, the separate ego — as if they were divisions in reality itself is the single generative mechanism behind ecological destruction, social conflict, and individual psychological confusion; motivates his experimental "rheomode," a proposed verb-centred grammar meant to stop language from manufacturing the illusion of separate, static things.

**Sources**:
- [Bohm, D. (1980). *Wholeness and the implicate order*. Routledge & Kegan Paul.](../sources/wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

## The Diagnosis

Bohm opens *Wholeness and the Implicate Order* not with physics but with a social and psychological argument. He observes that modern life is fragmented at every scale: knowledge is divided into specialities that no longer communicate; nations, religions, and professions each regard themselves as self-existent wholes in conflict with others; the individual mind is split into "conflicting compartments" of desire, ambition, and loyalty — a condition he notes we already recognise, in its extreme form, as neurosis.

His central claim is that this fragmentation is not a description of how reality actually is, but the consequence of a *habit of thought*: humans develop theories and distinctions for practical convenience — dividing a field into different crops, a society into professions — and then forget that the division was a tool of thought, coming to experience the world as if it were *actually* constituted of separate, self-existent fragments. Once this happens, Bohm argues, the fragmenting habit becomes self-confirming: people act according to the divided world-view, produce evidence of division through their own actions (conflict, ecological damage, social breakdown), and take that evidence as proof that the divisions were real all along.

He traces the word "health" to the Old English *hale* (whole) and its cognate the Hebrew *shalem*, arguing that human language itself has long registered wholeness as a condition to be maintained, and fragmentation as a kind of sickness.

## Theories as "Forms of Insight," Not Descriptions of Reality

Bohm's proposed corrective is not to declare fragmentation false and wholeness the truth, but to change the status we grant theories themselves. He argues that scientific theories — Newtonian gravitation, relativity, quantum theory, evolutionary biology — are best understood not as final descriptions of reality but as "forms of insight": ways of seeing that illuminate a domain of experience without being mistaken for the whole of reality. The danger is not having theories, but forgetting that they are theories — "unending confirmation of the limitations of modes of thinking" that mistake the map for the territory.

This distinguishes his position from naive scientific realism ("the theory just describes what's there") and from relativism ("theories are arbitrary"): a theory can be a genuine, hard-won insight into a real domain of experience while still being a partial, provisional way of looking rather than a photograph of an unconditioned reality.

## The Rheomode: An Experiment in Language

Because Bohm holds that the subject-verb-object structure of English (and most Western languages) grammatically encodes the assumption that the world consists of separate, static "things" acted upon by other separate things, he proposes an experimental alternative grammar he calls the **rheomode** (from Greek *rheo*, "to flow").

The rheomode builds new words from verb roots rather than treating nouns as fundamental: for example, from the root *levate* ("to lift attention"), Bohm derives *re-levate* (attention that fits its context; "relevant"), *irre-levate* (attention that doesn't fit; "irrelevant"), and the noun form *levation* (the general activity of lifting attention) — with the crucial feature that the verb-root, and therefore the sense of ongoing activity, remains primary; the noun is a derived, secondary abstraction rather than the name of a static thing. He performs the same construction for perceiving (*vidate*), dividing (*dividate*), and ordering (*ordinate*).

The rheomode never became a working language — it remains, by Bohm's own account, an experiment intended to demonstrate a point about how grammar shapes thought rather than a workable replacement for English. Its philosophical purpose is to make vivid the claim that treating the world as composed of static objects related by verbs (rather than as continuous processes provisionally described by nouns) is a specific, contingent feature of how Western languages carved up experience — not a linguistic necessity, still less a fact about reality.

## Relation to the Implicate Order

The fragmentation diagnosis is the psychological and social counterpart to the physics Bohm develops later in the same book: see [Implicate Order](implicate-order.md). Where the implicate order argues that physical reality is an undivided "holomovement" from which apparently separate objects are abstracted, the fragmentation chapter argues that the same mistake — treating an abstraction as a self-existent separate thing — is the root of humanity's social and ecological crises. Bohm treats these as two expressions of one underlying error, not as separate arguments that happen to share an author.

## Relationship to This Wiki

**[Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) and individuation**: Bohm's argument that psychological fragmentation ("conflicting compartments" of desire and ambition) is a mild, universal form of the same disorder that produces social crisis at scale parallels Jung's account of the unintegrated [shadow](shadow.md) — both frame psychological wholeness as a developmental achievement against a natural tendency toward splitting, though Jung's framework is clinical and archetypal where Bohm's is physical and linguistic.

**[Myss's biography-is-biology](biography-is-biology.md) and the [addictive system](addictive-system.md)**: Anne Wilson Schaef's argument (via Northrup) that patriarchal culture functions as an "addictive system" sustained by denial parallels Bohm's account of self-confirming fragmentation: both describe a social pattern that perpetuates itself precisely because participants mistake the produced condition (addiction; division) for an unchangeable fact about reality.

**[Krishnamurti](../thinkers/j-krishnamurti.md)**: [Bohm's](../thinkers/david-bohm.md) dialogues with the philosopher J. Krishnamurti, referenced in this book's first footnote, directly shaped the fragmentation diagnosis; Krishnamurti's teaching that the psychological "self" is itself a fragmenting construct is a close parallel Bohm draws on explicitly.

## Critique

**A philosophical claim, not an empirical one**: Bohm asserts that fragmentary thought *causes* ecological and social crisis, but this is argued by analogy with physics and by appeal to introspection, not established through sociological or psychological research. The wiki has no independent empirical literature (comparable to, say, the PNI research supporting [meaning-and-health](meaning-and-health.md)) that tests this causal claim directly.

**The rheomode's limited reach**: The proposed grammar had no uptake in linguistics, philosophy of language, or cognitive science; it functions in the secondary literature (and in this wiki) purely as an illustration of Bohm's argument, not as a validated alternative to ordinary language.

**Note**: Bohm's claim that particular grammatical structures (subject-verb-object syntax) constrain or determine thought echoes the much-debated linguistic relativity ("Sapir-Whorf") hypothesis in linguistics, which mainstream linguistics treats as at most weakly supported — language may subtly influence habitual patterns of attention, but strong claims that grammar determines what can be thought are not accepted as established science.
