# General Adaptation Syndrome

**Summary**: Hans Selye's model of the body's triphasic response to any sustained demand — alarm reaction, resistance, exhaustion — mediated by the pituitary-adrenal-cortical axis; the foundational physiological concept underlying the mind-body medicine literature's link between chronic stress and disease.

**Sources**:
- [Selye, H. (1956). *The stress of life*. McGraw-Hill.](../sources/the-stress-of-life.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-22

---

## The Three Stages

Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (G.A.S.) describes a stereotyped bodily response that appears regardless of the specific demand ("stressor") that provokes it — cold, infection, injury, exertion, or emotional shock all trigger the same underlying mechanism, even though the mechanism's *causation* is nonspecific while its *form* is highly specific (the same organs — adrenal cortex, thymus, gastrointestinal tract — respond every time):

1. **Alarm reaction**: the immediate response — adrenal cortex enlargement, thymus and lymphatic tissue shrinkage, gastrointestinal ulceration, adrenaline and corticoid release. Adaptive in the short term; the organism mobilizes defenses.
2. **Stage of resistance (adaptation)**: the alarm signs recede as the body adapts to the continuing stressor. This adaptation has a cost — resistance to the specific stressor rises, but resistance to other, previously tolerable stressors falls (Selye's principle that adaptation is "always a spatial concentration of effort").
3. **Stage of exhaustion**: if the stressor persists long enough, adaptation fails, the alarm-stage signs reappear, and — unlike the initial alarm reaction — the organism can no longer recover. This is where Selye locates the onset of chronic disease.

## Operational Definition

Selye defines stress precisely as "a state manifested by a specific syndrome which consists of all the nonspecifically induced changes within a biologic system." He is emphatic about what stress is *not*: not nervous tension (it occurs in denervated limbs and cell cultures), not simply the adrenaline discharge of acute emergency, not a deviation from homeostasis per se (ordinary perception and muscle contraction also deviate from resting state), and not identical to the alarm reaction alone (which is only the syndrome's first phase). The concept only became scientifically usable once "stress" (the state) was distinguished from "stressor" (the agent) — a terminological fight Selye had to win against considerable resistance, including a formal debate at the Collège de France over how to render the word in French, before "le stress," "der Stress," and "lo stress" entered the language of medicine worldwide.

## Diseases of Adaptation

Selye's most consequential clinical claim is that a wide range of chronic conditions — hypertension, cardiovascular and renal disease, gastrointestinal ulcers, inflammatory and rheumatic diseases, and some nervous and mental disorders — are not caused directly by external pathogens but by the G.A.S. mechanism itself malfunctioning: excess or insufficient adrenal-cortical hormone output, or a stress response sustained past the point where the body can recover. Disease, in this framing, is partly the body's own defense mechanism becoming maladaptive rather than solely an assault by an outside agent.

## Adaptation Energy

Selye also proposed — more speculatively, and by his own acknowledgment less rigorously supported — that organisms possess a finite, non-renewable store of "adaptation energy" that is depleted over a lifetime of stress responses, offering this as a partial biological theory of aging. This concept never received an identified physiological substrate and is generally regarded as suggestive metaphor rather than established mechanism.

## Influence on the Wiki's Mind-Body Medicine Literature

G.A.S. is the physiological backbone underneath several later frameworks in this wiki:

- [Herbert Benson's](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md) [relaxation response](relaxation-response.md) is explicitly framed as the physiological counterpart to G.A.S.'s alarm/exhaustion pathway — see [The Relaxation Response](../sources/the-relaxation-response.md).
- [Norman Shealy's](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) [Electromagnetic Dysthymia](electromagnetic-dysthymia.md) treats Selye's exhaustion stage as the mechanism underlying a modern syndrome of cumulative environmental, chemical, and electromagnetic stress overload.
- [Biography Is Biology](biography-is-biology.md): Selye's G.A.S. provides the endocrinological mechanism underlying Myss's and others' claim that chronic emotional patterns become physical illness — Selye supplies the disease process, while energy-medicine and psychoneuroimmunological accounts supply the pathway from *meaning* to *stress trigger*.
- [Somatic Distress Patterns](somatic-distress-patterns.md): Keleman's body-shape typology can be read as the structural, long-term residue of chronic G.A.S. activation, though Keleman arrives at his typology independently through body-centered clinical observation rather than endocrinology.
- [Norman Cousins's](../thinkers/norman-cousins.md) *[Anatomy of an Illness](../sources/anatomy-of-an-illness.md)* (1979) takes G.A.S. as its explicit starting point: Cousins read *The Stress of Life* roughly a decade before his own illness and reasoned that if chronic negative emotion produces the negative chemical changes Selye documented, positive emotion (laughter, hope, will to live) might produce positive ones — see [Laughter and Healing](laughter-and-healing.md).

## Critique

The core finding — a real, hormonally mediated, triphasic stress response whose chronic activation damages tissue — is well established and underpins psychoneuroimmunology (see [Psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md)). The claim of full **non-specificity**, however, has been substantially revised: later stress research (descending from Bruce McEwen's concept of allostatic load) shows the physiological response varies by stressor type, cognitive appraisal, social context, and individual biology rather than being a single undifferentiated cascade (needing verification against primary allostatic-load sources, not independently confirmed here). "Stress" as a construct has also become broad enough that almost any adverse condition can be labeled a stressor, which risks explanatory vagueness when used to account for a specific disease in a specific patient.
