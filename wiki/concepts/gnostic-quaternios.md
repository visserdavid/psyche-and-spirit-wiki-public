# Gnostic Quaternios and the Structure of the Self

**Summary**: Jung's catalogue of Gnostic, Naassene, and comparative-religious symbols of psychic totality — the self-knowing "point," the Anthropos (Primordial Man), and a series of interlocking fourfold relational diagrams (quaternios) built from biblical and Gnostic figures — culminating in the Moses/Shadow/Paradise/Lapis quaternio structure that directly anticipates the alchemical schema of *Mysterium Coniunctionis*.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1968). *Aion: Researches into the phenomenology of the self* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 9, Part 2). Princeton University Press.](../sources/aion.md)

## The Method: Amplification Across Traditions

Jung's procedure in *Aion*'s final chapters is **amplification** — assembling a wide array of historically unconnected symbolic material (2nd-century Gnostic texts via Hippolytus's heresiological compendium *Elenchos*, Meister Eckhart's sermons, Vedic Upanishads, Plotinus, medieval alchemy) and showing that it converges, independently, on the same small set of structural images for psychic wholeness: **the point, the circle, the quaternity (fourfold structure), and the Anthropos (personified totality)**. The convergence, for Jung, is evidence that these are not culturally transmitted ideas but spontaneous products of a common psychic structure — the [collective unconscious](collective-unconscious.md) expressing the [Self](the-self.md) archetype through independently invented symbolism.

## The Naassene Material: Anthropos, the Point, and the Living Water

The **Naassenes** (a 2nd-century Gnostic sect, named for the Hebrew *nachash*, "serpent") receive the most extended treatment. Their teaching, preserved by the heresiologist Hippolytus, describes:

- **The Original Man (Anthropos/Adamas)** as the source of all souls — "Man is the beginning and end of the whole" — divided into three natures (spiritual, psychic, material) corresponding to Trinity-like emanations.
- **Christ as Logos and living water**, flowing from the Original Man through the "four sources" (identified with the four rivers of Paradise — Pishon, Gihon, Tigris, Euphrates — and, in the Naassene reading, with the four senses/faculties of the human body).
- **The self-knowing point**: the Gnostic teacher Monoïmos's letter urges the seeker to "seek [the point] from thyself, and learn who it is that takes possession of everything in thee... [thou wilt] find the starting-point... and the point from which thy exodus... [comes]." This "point become a monad" — indivisible, containing all things in potentia — is Jung's clearest Gnostic parallel to the [mandala](mandala.md)'s single centre-point and to Eastern *atman*/*brahman* teaching (the Kena and Brihadaranyaka Upanishads, which Jung quotes directly as an independent parallel).

Jung's own summary judgment: "One is willy-nilly reminded... of the Indian [atman]" — the material demonstrates, for Jung, a genuine cross-cultural convergence rather than historical borrowing.

## The Quaternio Structures

Jung's most original contribution in this material is a series of explicit **quaternio diagrams** — four-term relational structures, modeled on the Naassene "Moses Quaternio" (Moses/Zipporah as husband/wife; Jethro/Miriam as the outer, animus/anima-like pair), which Jung extends into a linked set covering the full range from spirit to matter:

1. **The Anthropos Quaternio**: Adam–Eve / Man–Woman, at the "spiritual" pole, representing the world of archetypal totality.
2. **The Shadow Quaternio**: Adam–Eve / the Ethiopian Woman (Moses's second wife, Numbers 12:1)–Jethro, extending the marriage-quaternio downward into its shadow aspect — the excluded, "heathen" and racially-othered figures that the biblical text itself marginalizes. Jung reads this explicitly as the schema's structural admission of a **shadow dimension within the marriage quaternio itself**, not merely alongside it.
3. **The Paradise Quaternio**: the four rivers of Eden (Pishon, Gihon, Hiddekel/Tigris, Euphrates) surrounding the serpent — the natural, vegetative pole, where the tree and serpent (Mercurius) stand for the *prima materia* out of which the work of individuation begins.
4. **The Lapis Quaternio**: earth, water, fire, air arranged around the lapis (philosophers' stone) at the centre — the fully material pole, anticipating [*Mysterium Coniunctionis*](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md)'s extended alchemical treatment of the same image (see [The Self § The Self as Adam/Anthropos](the-self.md)).

Taken together, these four quaternios form a single descending series — from pure spirit (Anthropos) through the human, shadow-inflected pair (Shadow Quaternio), through the vegetative-instinctual (Paradise Quaternio), to pure matter (Lapis Quaternio) — which Jung reads as a complete symbolic map of the individuation process understood as **integration across the entire spectrum from spirit to body**, not merely a purely intrapsychic event. This is the direct conceptual seed of the far more elaborate coniunctio schema in *Mysterium Coniunctionis* — see [Sol, Luna, and the Alchemical Coniunctio](coniunctio.md).

## The Marriage Quaternio and Relational Psychology

The underlying template for all these quaternios is what Jung elsewhere (in "The Psychology of the Transference") calls the **marriage quaternio** — husband/wife plus each partner's anima/animus-figure, forming a four-cornered structure that Jung uses clinically to analyze projection dynamics in intimate relationships. *Aion*'s biblical elaboration (Moses–Zipporah–Miriam–Jethro) gives this abstract clinical schema a concrete historical genealogy, and explicitly incorporates the **incest motif** (sibling and near-kin pairings recur across the schema's variants) as a symbolic marker of psychic *identity* rather than literal desire — the closeness of the paired figures signals that anima/animus and shadow figures are, structurally, parts of a single psyche rather than genuinely external others.

## Point, Circle, and City: Convergent Symbols of Centre

Beyond the quaternio structures, Jung surveys a wider family of centre-symbols that recur across traditions independently of the quaternio schema specifically:

- **The point/monad** (Monoïmos, alchemical *punctum solis*, John Dee's *Monas Hieroglyphica*)
- **The circle and sphere** (Plotinus's image of the soul moving not in a line but "in circles about... the centre from which the circle proceeds")
- **The city, temple, and vessel** as architectural expressions of the same centring principle — extending the wiki's existing material on mandala-as-city-plan (see [The Self § Self in Art](the-self.md), citing Aniela Jaffé's chapter in [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) on Rome, Washington D.C., and Angkor Wat)

## Christ, Anthropos, and the Continuity of the Quaternio Material

Jung reads the Naassene identification of Christ with the "stone cut from the mountain without hands" (Daniel 2:34, already assimilated by the Naassenes to Adamas/Anthropos) as continuous with the alchemical identification of Christ with the lapis, and with his own broader argument in [Christ as a Symbol of the Self](christ-as-symbol-of-the-self.md) that the historical Christ-image functions as a totality-symbol structurally parallel to — though theologically distinct from — the Gnostic Anthropos. The **Interrogationes Maiores Mariae** fragment (quoted from Epiphanius), describing a visionary hieros gamos between Christ and Mary on a mountain, is read by Jung as a symbolic dramatization of the same underlying process the alchemists later stage as the coniunctio of Sol and Luna (see [Sol, Luna, and the Alchemical Coniunctio](coniunctio.md)).

## Relation to *Mysterium Coniunctionis*

*Aion*'s quaternio material (1951) is the direct conceptual precursor to the far more extensive alchemical elaboration Jung publishes five years later in [*Mysterium Coniunctionis*](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md) (1955–56). Where *Aion* draws its quaternios primarily from Gnostic and biblical sources, *Mysterium Coniunctionis* develops the same underlying logic — fourfold structures resolving into unity — through the full alchemical corpus (Sol/Luna, Rex/Regina, the elements, Dorn's unio mentalis schema). Read together, the two books show the same archetypal content (the Self expressing itself through quaternary structure) worked out across two different historical symbol-systems that Jung treats as independently arriving at structurally identical solutions.

## Critique

**Interpretive latitude**: Jung's construction of the specific quaternios (which biblical or Gnostic figures belong at which "corner") involves considerable interpretive selection — the biblical text itself does not present Miriam, Jethro, and Zipporah as a deliberate structural unit, and Jung's grouping reflects his own theoretical needs as much as anything demonstrably present in the source material. This is characteristic of Jung's amplification method generally (see the parallel critique on [collected-works-9-1.md](../sources/collected-works-9-1.md)) — it is compelling as a demonstration of *possible* symbolic convergence, not as historically rigorous textual scholarship.

**Reliance on Hippolytus**: Much of the Naassene material survives only through Hippolytus's polemical heresiology (*Elenchos*, written to refute Gnostic teaching), a hostile secondary source whose accuracy in representing the sects it attacks is disputed among scholars of Gnosticism. Jung treats Hippolytus's reports largely at face value as primary evidence of Naassene symbolism.

**Note**: mainstream historical scholarship on Gnosticism (informed since Jung's era by the 1945 Nag Hammadi library, largely unavailable to Jung when writing *Aion*) treats the diversity of 2nd–3rd century Gnostic movements as considerably less unified than Jung's amplification method implies; his cross-sect and cross-tradition parallels are stronger as psychological pattern-finding than as claims about a coherent historical Gnostic theology.
