# Grace and Charism

**Summary**: In Myss's framework, grace is a Divine energy that descends to support the fulfilment of one's Sacred Contract — strengthening resolve, providing direction, and enabling healing — while charism (from the Greek for "gift") is the unique endowment through which grace expresses itself in an individual's particular purpose.

**Sources**:
- [Myss, C. (2001). *Sacred contracts: Awakening your divine potential*. Harmony Books.](../sources/sacred-contracts.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Grace

### Definition
Myss describes grace as a form of energy analogous to prana (Sanskrit), ch'i (Chinese), or the Christian pneuma (spirit). It is the Divine force that descends in response to need, prayer, or alignment — not as reward for merit but as support for growth and the fulfilment of the Sacred Contract.

Grace is, in her framing, a practical force: it provides courage in the face of fear, stamina when endurance fails, direction when the path is unclear, and healing when illness or crisis has depleted personal resources. The masters of the world's spiritual traditions — Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, the Buddha — all received it. So do ordinary people at turning points.

### Forms of grace
Myss identifies several expressions of grace in lived experience:

- **Epiphany**: A moment of illumination in which the soul recognises its connection to the Divine and its purpose becomes clarified. This can be sudden or gradual.
- **Coincidence and synchronicity**: Events that seem unlikely yet occur precisely when needed — the right book, the right person, the right opportunity — which Myss reads as grace manifesting through the material world.
- **Angelic intervention**: In multiple cultural traditions (Christian, Islamic, Jewish, Buddhist), divine messengers deliver guidance at critical moments. Myss frames these as expressions of the same grace operating through different cultural vocabularies.
- **Near-death experiences**: Many NDE accounts describe a sense of being told to return because work is unfinished — an experience Myss interprets as direct confirmation of the Sacred Contract.

### How grace is invited
Myss argues that grace cannot be earned but can be *invited* through:
- Prayer and meditation (creating the inner silence in which grace can be received)
- Acts of service and compassion (aligning one's energy with the Contract's direction)
- Willingness to surrender control (the fifth stage of the Sacred Contract — see [Five Stages](five-stages-of-sacred-contract.md))
- Acknowledging and working with the shadow archetypes (removing the blockages that prevent grace from flowing through)

## Charism

### Definition
The word *charism* (from the Greek *charisma*, meaning gift of grace) originates in Christian theology, where it denotes a specific gift given by God as evidence of divine presence and calling — the capacity to heal, to prophesy, to teach, to lead.

Myss extends the concept beyond its theological context: a charism is the unique energetic quality or gift through which grace expresses itself in a person's specific Sacred Contract. Where grace is the universal force, charism is its particular form in a given individual.

### Charism as identity
A person's charism is not merely a skill or talent but the signature energy of their soul — the quality others recognise as distinctively them when they are most fully themselves. It manifests when a person is aligned with their purpose and is often felt as effortless: the teacher in full flow, the healer whose touch seems to carry something beyond technique, the artist whose work opens something in the viewer.

### Examples
- **Cindy's epiphany in a bookstore**: Sitting alone, she felt an inexplicable infusion of energy and meaning that reorganised her relationship to her life — a charism event that left her oriented rather than adrift.
- **Saint Teresa of Ávila, Ignatius Loyola, Sri Ramakrishna**: Mystics who described the inflow of divine energy that permanently reoriented their lives — Myss reads these as charism events.
- **Ram Dass**: His encounter with Neem Karoli Baba is described as a moment of charism recognition — the guru's knowledge of his mother's death dissolved Ram Dass's intellectual defences and opened him to the possibility of grace.

## Relationship to the Sacred Contract

Grace and charism are the Divine side of the Sacred Contract's bilateral structure. The human soul agrees to a lifetime of learning and service; the Divine guarantees the *support* needed to fulfil those terms — not through removing obstacles, but through providing the inner resources to meet them. As Myss puts it: "The Contract holds the quality of the project. We warrant the task."

## Convergence with Peck's Clinical Account of Grace

Peck arrives independently at a similar concept in *[The Road Less Traveled](../sources/the-road-less-traveled.md)*: an unearned, growth-supporting force evidenced by serendipity and synchronicity. Peck grounds his account almost entirely in psychiatric case material — dreams, slips of the tongue, symptom formation, and unexplained survival — rather than the devotional and archetypal vocabulary Myss uses, and he pairs grace with a countervailing force he calls entropy (whose psychological expression is laziness). The two accounts reinforce each other on the central claim (grace is real, unearned, and evidenced through synchronicity) while differing sharply in register and evidentiary base. See [Entropy, Laziness, and Grace](entropy-and-grace.md) for the full comparison.

## Critique

**Theological background**: The concept of grace has a rich and contested history in Christianity (Augustinian, Calvinist, Arminian, and Catholic traditions disagree significantly about its nature, conditions, and recipients). Myss's use of the concept is ecumenical and draws primarily on lived experience rather than doctrinal tradition. This broadens its accessibility but risks losing theological precision.

**Empirical status**: Grace as a form of energy descending from a Divine source cannot be measured or verified empirically. The experiences Myss describes — epiphany, synchronicity, renewed direction after prayer — are real psychological events, but their cause is a matter of interpretation. Secular explanations (flow states, the unconscious, coincidence) account for the same phenomena without requiring a divine agent.

**Eastern parallels**: Myss aligns grace with prana and ch'i, but these concepts function differently in their home traditions. Prana is the life force that animates all living beings; ch'i is the vital energy of the cosmos. Neither is traditionally understood as a personal divine gift in response to prayer in the way Christian grace is. The synthesis is suggestive but philosophically compressed.
