# Group Evil and MyLai

**Summary**: M. Scott Peck's analysis of how individual evil is amplified in group settings through specialisation, fragmentation of conscience, group narcissism, and dependency on leaders — using the 1968 MyLai massacre as the primary case study, and tracing responsibility through the individual soldier, Task Force Barker, the US military, and American society as a whole.

**Sources**:
- [Peck, M. S. (1983). *People of the lie: The hope for healing human evil*. Simon & Schuster.](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Overview

Peck argues that groups routinely behave in ways that no individual member would sanction alone — not because groups are intrinsically evil, but because the conditions of group membership systematically undermine the individual mechanisms that normally prevent evil: self-examination, the tolerance of guilt, the exercise of independent judgment, and the maintenance of empathy for those outside the group.

The MyLai massacre of March 16, 1968 — in which elements of Task Force Barker killed five to six hundred unarmed Vietnamese civilians — serves as Peck's central case. He analyses it at five escalating levels of responsibility.

## The Five Levels of Responsibility at MyLai

### 1. Individual Stress and Regression
Soldiers under sustained combat stress undergo measurable psychological regression. The capacity for nuanced moral judgment is compromised; emotional sensitivity is blunted as a survival mechanism (Robert Jay Lifton's "numbing"). Task Force Barker had been in the field for weeks with no successful enemy engagements, taking casualties from mines and booby traps with no visible enemy to fight. The group was primed for violence. The killing of unarmed villagers was not the action of psychopaths; it was the action of stressed, regressed, numbed soldiers whose normal moral functioning had been degraded by circumstances.

### 2. Group Dynamics
Groups amplify the tendency toward regression through several mechanisms:

**Dependency on leaders**: When individuals join groups, they tend to cede independent judgment to designated leaders. The military institutionalises this dependency (obedience as discipline; soldiers are explicitly not supposed to think). Charlie Company moved into MyLai following orders from Lieutenant Calley; the majority followed without mutiny because the structure of military group membership made independent resistance extraordinarily difficult.

**Fragmentation of conscience**: In specialised groups, conscience is distributed: each person is responsible only for their piece. The napalm manufacturer, the transportation officer, the pilot, the soldier — each can point to someone else's decision. No one person holds the whole moral weight. The result is that the sum of individual consciences can be zero: collective action for which no one feels individually responsible.

**Group narcissism**: Groups develop pride in themselves as units. Unit cohesion is built partly through the hatred of the out-group. Task Force Barker's soldiers, like American forces generally in Vietnam, experienced the Vietnamese as "gooks" — a racial dehumanisation that removed them from the category of beings deserving moral consideration. Group narcissism works through the same mechanism as individual narcissism: refusing to acknowledge fault, projecting blame outward, scapegoating the Other.

### 3. The Composition of Task Force Barker
Task Force Barker was not a cross-section of American society but a self-selected and system-selected group: individuals who could not avoid the draft, career soldiers who needed the war for status and promotion, and young men like "Larry" (Peck's composite) who had been offered the choice of military service or incarceration. The military was functioning as a dumping ground for those society wanted removed — and then using these people to perform the violence society wanted done but did not want to see.

### 4. The US Military
The military in 1967–1968 was a specialised institution undergoing a crisis of institutional narcissism. Its entire raison d'être was military victory; Vietnam was demonstrating that victory was not achievable. The institution's narcissism (pride, esprit de corps, the mythology of American invincibility) was under assault. Peck argues that institutions under narcissistic injury tend to escalate destructiveness — the Vietnam-era body-count culture, the escalating atrocities, and the systematic lying of the cover-up all reflect the military as an institution enacting the same pattern as an individual narcissist defending self-image.

The use of technology (bombing, defoliants, napalm) had also progressively insulated soldiers from the experience of killing — which Peck argues deadened moral sensitivity further. Killing by machine is psychologically different from face-to-face killing; the technology interposed a layer of unreality between action and consequence.

### 5. American Society
At the broadest level, MyLai was possible because American society had delegated the war to specialists and stopped paying attention. The Johnson administration's Gulf of Tonkin fraud (the false pretext for escalation) was tolerated because most Americans did not want to examine what was being done in their name. This is Peck's most challenging argument: the civilian population shares moral responsibility for the actions of its military through the mechanism of **willed ignorance** — the laziness and narcissism of a society that outsources its violence to professionals and looks away.

The decision to escalate was driven by the administration's narcissistic inability to acknowledge failure: by 1967, evidence was abundant that the war was unwinnable, but admitting this would have destroyed the self-image of the administration and the nation. As with an individual evil person, the evidence of harm was suppressed by projecting blame outward and lying to maintain appearances.

## The Ladder of Collective Responsibility

Peck's model implies a "ladder" of collective responsibility:
- Individual soldier (direct perpetrator)
- Immediate officers (order-givers)
- Task Force Barker (group dynamics)
- US military (institutional culture)
- American society and government (delegation and wilful ignorance)

Each rung is necessary for the atrocity; removing any rung would have prevented it. Collective responsibility does not eliminate individual responsibility — Calley was rightly convicted — but individual responsibility cannot account for the phenomenon without the collective analysis.

## Laziness and Narcissism as Root Causes

Peck identifies two root causes operating at every level: **laziness** and **narcissism**.

**Laziness**: The failure to do the work required to know what is being done in one's name, to examine the attitudes and policies one has inherited, to resist the inertia of received positions. Most Americans in 1968 had not asked basic questions about the history of Vietnam, the nature of the conflict, or the morality of the war. Laziness is not passive; it is an active avoidance of the work of self-examination.

**Narcissism**: The refusal to tolerate evidence that challenges one's self-image — as a nation, as a military, as a policy-maker. The arrogance that assumes American power must prevail; the pride that cannot admit failure; the rage that emerges when the self-image is threatened. Peck explicitly links individual narcissism (the root of personal evil) to collective narcissism (the root of national evil).

## Prevention

Peck's prescriptions for preventing group evil are structural:

1. **Citizen service**: Mandatory civic participation (a service corps, a citizen draft) prevents the insulation of specialised groups from accountability. When everyone's children are potentially soldiers, society cannot delegate violence to specialists and look away.

2. **Institutional submission**: Corporations, military institutions, and governments need mechanisms of submission to values beyond their own interest — not just profit or national power, but truth and life. Peck sees the reintegration of religious/ethical values with scientific and political life as essential to this.

3. **Education in the nature of evil**: Peck calls for schools and churches to teach the mechanisms of group evil — laziness, narcissism, scapegoating, specialisation — so that individuals develop the vocabulary and the habit of self-examination that prevents complicity.

Peck's group evil analysis resonates with several threads in the broader wiki:

The [shadow](shadow.md) concept's **collective dimension** — the shadow of cultures and nations projected outward onto scapegoated groups — is precisely what Peck is describing at the national level. Jung used the Holocaust as his primary example; Peck uses Vietnam.

Peck's MyLai analysis charts the distance between where humanity is and where Ring's thesis claims it is headed.

Peck reaches the same conclusion through a secular-clinical route.

## Critique

**The causal chain**: Peck's analysis is compelling as narrative but lacks empirical specificity. The claim that "laziness and narcissism" caused MyLai is true at a certain level of abstraction but doesn't engage with the specific historical, political, and structural factors that shaped the war.

**Collective moral responsibility**: The ascription of moral responsibility to an entire society for the actions of its government and military is philosophically contested. Citizens of a democracy bear some responsibility for collective decisions, but the degree and mechanism are unclear.

**The Vietnam exceptionalism question**: Peck focuses on MyLai as exceptional, but he also asks whether it was exceptional at all — whether comparable atrocities occurred in other wars (Philippine Insurrection, Korea, World War II) that have simply not been documented. He calls for research that was never conducted.
