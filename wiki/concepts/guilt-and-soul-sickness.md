# Guilt and Soul Sickness

**Summary**: William James's term "soul sickness" names the chronic shame-rooted condition that Joan Borysenko identifies as the deepest source of stress-related illness — distinguished from healthy guilt (conscience) by its identity-level character, its twenty-one behavioural expressions, and its requirement for a spiritual (not merely psychological) remedy.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1990). *Guilt is the teacher, love is the lesson*. Warner Books.](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## The Concept

"Soul sickness" is [William James](../thinkers/william-james.md)'s term from *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) for a syndrome he observed in patients of the New Thought movement: a state of chronic psychological contraction characterised by perfectionism, anxiety, compulsive helpfulness, inability to say no, self-blame, and the habitual belief that one is at fault for what goes wrong.

[Carl Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) used nearly the same description when he wrote to Bill W. (co-founder of Alcoholics Anonymous) that alcoholism is *spiritus contra spiritum* — a craving for Spirit disguised as a craving for alcohol — and that it requires not psychotherapy but "conversion": a fundamental shift in one's relationship to the universe. Jung was describing the same condition James named.

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) synthesises these observations with her psychoneuroimmunological framework: soul sickness is not merely a spiritual problem but a physiological one. The shame-based personality generates chronic activation of the fight-or-flight response — producing cortisol and adrenalin, suppressing immune function, and contributing to the 75% of physician visits she attributes to stress-related conditions. The soul sickness of unresolved guilt is, in her framing, a primary mechanism of [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md).

## Healthy Guilt vs. Unhealthy Shame

Borysenko distinguishes two phenomena that share the name "guilt":

**Healthy guilt** is the feedback loop of conscience:
- Arises in response to a *specific act* that violates one's values
- Is proportionate to the act
- Motivates responsibility — confession, amends, learning
- Is a *passing* emotional state that deepens character and compassion
- Example: the student caught cheating who genuinely reflects on what drove the lapse and resolves to change

**Unhealthy shame** is identity-level:
- Is not about what one *has done* but about what one *is*
- Does not attach to specific acts; it suffuses the entire self
- Generates personality organised around self-protection rather than self-expression
- Is chronic and pervasive — "I am a mistake" rather than "I made a mistake"
- John Bradshaw (*Healing the Shame That Binds You*) calls this "shame identity" or "toxic shame"
- [Gershen Kaufman](../thinkers/gershen-kaufman.md) (*Shame: The Power of Caring*) distinguishes shame as a *passing emotion* (the blush of exposure — valuable survival information) from shame as *identity* (an enduring conviction of worthlessness)

The move from guilt to shame to soul sickness occurs when a child's shaming experiences — broken [interpersonal bridges](inner-child.md), parental withdrawal of love, cultural or religious messages of unworthiness — accumulate until shame becomes the organising principle of the self.

## Twenty-One Expressions of Unhealthy Guilt

Borysenko compiled these from years of clinical work and workshops:

1. **Overcommitment**: never enough time; can't say no; believe worth must be earned through achievement
2. **Chronic worry**: catastrophising, awfulizing about worst-case scenarios
3. **Compulsive helping**: saviour complex, helper disease; reaching for others to avoid one's own pain
4. **Chronic apologising**: pre-emptive self-blame for imagined offenses
5. **Waking in anxiety**: middle-of-the-night rumination about what has gone wrong
6. **Self-blame**: reflexive attribution of all difficulties to personal flaw
7. **Worrying what others think**: rehearsing conversations, obsessing over how one appeared
8. **Fear of people's anger**: hypersensitivity to others' displeasure; reading anger into neutral events
9. **Feeling like a fraud**: fear of being "found out"; the impostor syndrome
10. **Not saying what you think**: self-censorship to avoid conflict or disapproval
11. **Never having time**: energy exhausted by helping, worrying, and over-functioning; none left for self
12. **Comparing oneself to others**: competitive anxiety; envy
13. **Living by "musts" and "shoulds"**: internal parent/critic dictating through obligation rather than choice
14. **Difficulty accepting criticism**: reasonable feedback experienced as attack
15. **Perfectionism**: 90% not good enough; the law of probability defied
16. **Worrying about others**: energy invested in others' problems as substitute for facing one's own
17. **Difficulty receiving help**: "gifted giver, blocked receiver"; dams the flow of love
18. **Difficulty accepting compliments**: invalidating praise through catalogue of disqualifications
19. **Worrying about punishment for sins**: pessimistic frame — illness and misfortune as evidence of unworthiness
20. **Hypochondria about illness**: body-focused awfulizing; attention routed from emotional pain to physical symptoms
21. **Inability to say no**: fear that refusal means rejection and abandonment

These expressions are understood as coping strategies born of shame identity — attempts to earn love through performance, to avoid exposure, or to numb the underlying pain.

## Carl Jung's Parallel

In 1961, Jung wrote a letter to Bill W. stating that alcoholism is seated so deep in the spirit that medicine and psychology cannot reach it; what is required is "conversion" — a *metanoia*, a change of heart/mind at the level of the soul's orientation. Bill W.'s insight led to the Twelve Steps, which Jung would recognise as precisely the "equivalent of a religious conversion" he described as necessary.

Borysenko reads this letter as confirmation that soul sickness — the condition she is diagnosing in guilt, perfectionism, and shame — requires an Era III response (Dossey's framework): not drugs (Era I), not stress management (Era II alone), but a fundamental reconnection to the Source of one's being.

## Soul Sickness and the Body

The physiology Borysenko documented in [*Minding the Body, Mending the Mind*](../sources/minding-the-body.md) applies directly:

- Chronic shame generates chronic fight-or-flight activation (cortisol, adrenalin)
- Cortisol suppresses T-lymphocyte activity and natural killer cell function ([psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md))
- The immune suppression is proportional to the duration and intensity of the shame-driven stress
- The body cannot distinguish imagined threat (rumination about past shame) from real threat

Result: soul sickness is not merely psychological suffering but an ongoing immunological event — which is why stress-related illness (headaches, bowel disorders, infections, hypertension) clusters among people with shame-based personalities.

This is the specific mechanism by which the guilt/shame dimension of [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md) operates. Where [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) describes depleted chakra energy as the precursor to illness and [Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md) describes somatic distortion, Borysenko provides the endocrine and immune pathway.

## Three Spheres of Healing

Borysenko organises healing from soul sickness across three spheres:

**Psychological** (Part One of the book): Understanding the childhood origins in broken bridges, shame, and the formation of the shadow bag; [inner child](inner-child.md) work; developing the ability to listen to and comfort the wounded parts of the self.

**Spiritual** (Part Two): Reconnecting with the Self that underlies the personality; opening to mystical experience; distinguishing [spiritual optimism](spiritual-optimism.md) from guilt-inducing religious pessimism; finding grace.

**Relational** (Part Three): [Forgiveness](forgiveness.md) as the systematic release of the ego's judgment; relationships as mirrors; compassion as the culminating capacity.

## Critique

**Diagnostic precision**: The category of "soul sickness" encompasses what contemporary psychology might distinguish as generalised anxiety disorder, shame-based personality structure, codependency, and perfectionism. Conflating these under one construct may obscure treatment-specific differences. However, Borysenko's synthesis also reveals what these conditions share — the shame-based identity that psychology can name but finds difficult to cure.

**The spiritual remedy**: Borysenko argues that behavioural and cognitive approaches are insufficient because they address the Level II symptoms without reaching the Level III cause — the disconnection from Spirit. This claim is not empirically testable in the usual sense. Her clinical observations (including the recovery histories of numerous workshop participants) are suggestive but not controlled.

**The guilt-creates-illness claim**: The specific proposition that shame-based personality directly causes illness is consistent with PNI research on stress-induced immune suppression but not proven in the stronger form (that guilt causes specific diseases). The mechanism is real; the specificity of outcomes is not established.

**Note**: Mainstream psychology recognises shame as a clinically significant and difficult-to-treat condition. The addition of a spiritual framework (reconnection to Source) is not within the standard cognitive-behavioural or psychodynamic treatment repertoire, but there is growing evidence that spiritual/existential interventions have measurable health benefits (e.g., Fredrickson's research on positive emotions; work on religious coping and health outcomes).
