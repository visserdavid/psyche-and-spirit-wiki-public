# Healing vs. Curing

**Summary**: The distinction between curing (removal of disease) and healing (restoration of wholeness, peace, and connection to one's deepest nature) — illustrated by Sam's story in Borysenko (1987) and implicit in Myss's and Shealy's frameworks; healing can occur even in the presence of terminal illness and imminent death.

## The Distinction

**Curing** is the removal or remission of disease — the goal of conventional medicine. A cure is an outcome measurable in biological terms: the tumour is gone, the infection cleared, the organ repaired.

**Healing** is the restoration of wholeness — the return to a state of peace, connection, and alignment with one's deepest nature. Healing is a relationship between the person and their life, not between the physician and the disease. It can occur with or without curing, and curing can occur without healing.

The distinction matters clinically because:
- A person may be "cured" (disease removed) but remain fragmented — alienated from meaning, disconnected from love, unchanged in the attitudes and patterns that contributed to illness
- A person may die of their disease but be genuinely healed — at peace, connected to loved ones, no longer trapped by fear, resentment, or incompleteness
- Most importantly: the practices that support healing (forgiveness, mindfulness, completion of unfinished business, reconnection to purpose and love) are not contingent on the prognosis

## Sam's Story

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s clearest illustration of this distinction is Sam — a physician in his thirties who was dying of AIDS in 1983. Borysenko was called as a consultant to teach him meditation for peace of mind. She worked with him for a year.

Sam was not cured. He died of AIDS-related complications in the spring. But in the course of that year he:
- Learned to meditate and access the [relaxation response](relaxation-response.md), reducing his fear enough to be present
- Completed unfinished business with family and friends — saying what needed to be said
- Achieved forgiveness — of himself and of others who had hurt him
- Reconnected to love as the meaning of his life, releasing his fixation on accomplishments and possessions
- Led, from his hospital bed on his final day, a spontaneous group meditation in which each person present spoke from the heart

His family later cast in gold the yantra (a geometric symbol of the Self) that Borysenko had given him to use as a meditation focus. The yantra — a square containing a circle containing a cross — is described as a symbol of the totality of the Self; it appeared across cultures and in Jungian symbolism (as the mandala). Sam's dying, in this frame, was a healing.

Borysenko: *"Sam's healing reminds me that healing doesn't mean curing — it means the restoration of the person to their own wholeness."*

## The Distinction in Myss and Shealy

The healing/curing distinction appears throughout the wiki, though not always under this name:

**Myss's framework** in [*Anatomy of the Spirit*](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md): Healing requires the conscious engagement of the patient in the work of releasing the energy pattern that gave rise to illness. This engagement may produce physical remission; it may not. But it always produces a change in the person's relationship to their life — a move toward authenticity, self-respect, and alignment with their deeper purpose. The cases of Julie (who died unchanged) and Joanna (whose cancer went into remission after she reclaimed her authority) in *The Creation of Health* illustrate that the same disease pattern, met with different quality of engagement, produces different outcomes — but the healing work is valuable independent of the physical result.

**Myss's Three Principles**: The third principle of energy medicine in Myss's framework — "All help is self-help" — implies this distinction: no healer can cure the patient, but any patient can heal themselves, regardless of what happens to the disease.

**[Shealy's Sacred Healing](../sources/sacred-healing.md)**: Shealy's documentation of spiritually gifted healers who sometimes produce measurable physical remissions, but whose primary gift is described as a transformation in the patient's experience of themselves and their illness — a peace that precedes and sometimes accompanies physical improvement.

**[Power and Healing](power-and-healing.md)**: Myss's distinction between personal power (the capacity to make choices that honour the self) and physical health is the same distinction differently stated. Personal power can be developed even in a deteriorating body; it is the precondition of healing regardless of curing.

## Forgiveness and the Near-Death Evidence

Borysenko cites Kenneth Ring's near-death experience research (*Heading Toward Omega*, 1984/1985) as evidence for the priority of love and connection in the structure of a life. Survivors of near-death experiences consistently report that the life review they experienced centred entirely on love — not on accomplishments, possessions, or status:

> "My life passed before my eyes — not as a caricature of events but as every emotion I felt and every emotion I caused others to feel. The basis of the review was not what I had achieved but how much love I had shared and expressed."

This reframes the meaning of life around precisely the domain in which healing — as distinct from curing — operates. The practices of forgiveness, completion of unfinished business, and reconnection to love are not merely techniques for stress reduction; they address what the near-death evidence suggests is the fundamental question of a life.

## Relation to Jungian Individuation

[Jung's individuation](individuation.md) provides a parallel frame: individuation — the process of becoming who one essentially is — is not prevented by physical illness, ageing, or dying. The individuating person does not require physical health to complete the psychological and spiritual work of becoming whole. Hillman's [acorn theory](acorn-theory.md) similarly holds that the soul's calling can be fulfilled through dying well, not only through achieving external goals.

## Critique

**The risk of romanticising death**: The healing/curing distinction can be misused to suggest that people who die with "good attitudes" are healing while those who die with fear and resistance are failing. This imposes a further burden on the dying and is not what Borysenko or Myss intend. Healing is a possibility, not a requirement; dying frightened and angry is a legitimate human response, not a failure of healing.

**The placebo and attitude effect on survival**: Some research suggests that psychological states affect survival time (Siegel's exceptional patients; the Greer breast cancer attitude study). This suggests that healing may sometimes contribute to curing, not only serve as its alternative. The relationship between the two is not cleanly separable.

**Medical ethics**: If healing can occur without curing, and healing practices are available to dying patients, there is an argument for including them in palliative and end-of-life care. This is increasingly accepted in integrative oncology and hospice contexts. The distinction is clinically and ethically significant, not merely philosophical.
