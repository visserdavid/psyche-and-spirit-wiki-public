# Health as Wholeness

**Summary**: The philosophical argument, developed by Andrew Weil from etymological and cross-cultural evidence, that health, holiness, and wholeness share a single root and that medicine cannot define or promote health without understanding it as a dynamic state of balance and integration — not the mere absence of disease.

**Sources**:
- [Weil, A. (1983). *Health and healing: Understanding conventional and alternative medicine*. Houghton Mifflin.](../sources/health-and-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## The Etymological Argument

[Andrew Weil](../thinkers/andrew-weil.md) opens his account of health by tracing the word to its root. In Proto-Indo-European, *kailo* — whole, uninjured, of good augury — is the single ancestor of:

- **Health** (Old English *hǣlth*)
- **Holy** (Old English *hālig*)
- **Whole** (Old English *hāl*)
- **Hale** (Old English *hāl*, as in "hale and hearty")
- **Heal** (Old English *hǣlan*)

The convergence is not coincidence. In pre-modern cultures, medicine, religion, and magic were unified — the healer, the priest, and the wise person were the same figure, addressing the same question. Health was understood as wholeness; holiness was understood as the state of being whole; healing was understood as the restoration of a wholeness that had been disrupted.

Allopathic medicine severed these connections when it decided to become a natural science in the nineteenth century. In doing so, it gained precision about particular disease mechanisms and lost the conceptual framework for understanding health.

The practical consequence: modern medicine can define diseases in exhaustive detail but cannot define health. The absence of measurable disease is not the same as the presence of health — Weil's symptom diary shows that a clearly healthy person experiences dozens of minor fluctuations in any week that would meet clinical criteria for disturbance.

By contrast:
- **Cure** (from Latin *cura*: care, management) refers to the management of a pathological state
- **Medicine** (from an Indo-European root meaning "to measure") refers to the establishment of measurable order

Weil argues: medicine has focused on measuring and managing disease while abandoning the question of what health is.

## Two Properties of Wholeness

Wholeness as Weil defines it has two properties:

**Completeness**: all components of the organism are present and integrated — physical, psychological, social, spiritual. A person missing any of these dimensions is not wholly healthy even if their body shows no pathology.

**Balance**: the components are in dynamic equilibrium with each other and with the environment. Balance is not stasis; it is the capacity for continuous self-correction in the face of changing conditions.

The distinction between stasis and dynamic equilibrium is clinically important:
- The goal of health is not zero symptoms — zero symptoms is either a fantasy or a dead body
- The goal is the resilience to move through disturbances and return to equilibrium
- A person who never gets ill may be less healthy than one who gets ill and recovers quickly — the first may lack the capacity for the adaptive immune response the second exercises

## Cross-Cultural Expressions

Weil finds the same intuition across cultures:

**Black Elk's hoop**: The Oglala Sioux medicine man Black Elk described his people's power as arising from their circular relationship with the land and each other — the hoop of the nation. When the U.S. government required them to live in square houses on reservations and ended their circular encampment patterns, the hoop was broken and their power departed with it. The circle as the symbol of wholeness, the square as the symbol of alienation and fragmentation.

**The caduceus**: Hermes's staff — two serpents winding around a central axis, crowned with wings — is the ancient Western symbol of healing. Weil reads it as the integration of opposites: the two snakes are expansion and limitation, light and dark, masculine and feminine, the solar and the chthonic. The wings represent transcendence that is achieved not by eliminating one pole but by holding both in balance around a central axis. Saturn (limitation, the Grim Reaper) is exalted in Libra (balance): restriction is not the enemy of health but a necessary component of equilibrium.

**Chinese medicine**: The Taoist concept of health as the balance of yin and yang forces, expressed through the five-element system and the flow of *qi* through the twelve organ networks, is a sophisticated elaboration of the same intuition — health is dynamic balance, illness is a disruption of balance, treatment is the restoration of balance.

## Health as Dynamic Equilibrium

Weil's practical model: health is not a resting state but an active process. The body is constantly receiving challenges — pathogens, injuries, toxins, psychological stresses — and constantly generating compensatory responses. The "healthy body" is one whose response to any given challenge is proportionate, effective, and self-limiting.

This has clinical implications:

1. **Symptoms are often healing responses, not diseases**: Fever enhances immune function and should not be routinely suppressed. Inflammation at a wound site is the first phase of repair. Nausea and vomiting expel toxins. Treating the symptom as the disease delays healing.

2. **The goal of treatment is to support healing, not replace it**: Medicine's job is to remove obstacles to the body's own healing — not to give the body something it lacks. Wound healing is the paradigm: clean the wound, remove dead tissue, hold the edges together. No substance known accelerates healing; many delay it by irritating tissue.

3. **Resilience, not perfection**: The aim of health practice is not to eliminate all illness but to maximise the organism's capacity for self-correction. A person whose infections resolve quickly, whose wounds heal cleanly, and whose psychological disturbances reach resolution is healthier than one who avoids all exposures but collapses when one penetrates.

## Relationship to Other Wiki Concepts

**[Biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md)**: Myss's framework is a specific application of the wholeness model — the biography (psychological, relational, spiritual life) is not separate from the biology but is expressed through it. Incompleteness in the biography (unhealed grief, suppressed anger, deferred purpose) appears as physiological incompleteness (organ dysfunction, immune dysregulation).

**[Two Paradigms of Medicine](two-paradigms-of-medicine.md)**: Weil's health-as-wholeness model is the philosophical foundation he believes holistic medicine should build on. His critique of holistic medicine's "fantasy of sweetness and light" is a critique of applying the language of wholeness without the conceptual rigour.

**[Innate Healing System](innate-healing-system.md)**: Weil's three-component healing system (reaction, regeneration, adaptation) is the operational description of how the body maintains dynamic equilibrium.

**[Healing vs. Curing](healing-vs-curing.md)**: The wholeness model reframes the medical outcome question: curing restores the body to a prior state; healing restores the person to wholeness (which may or may not include the body's full physical recovery).

**[Northrup's women's wisdom](../thinkers/christiane-northrup.md)**: Northrup's concept of women's health as requiring balance between inner and outer worlds, between body and psyche, parallels Weil's dynamic equilibrium model with a specific application to female physiology and life cycles.

## Critique

**Mainstream medicine's objection**: Without operational measures, "wholeness" cannot function as a clinical concept. How does one assess whether a patient is "whole"? Weil's etymological argument establishes a philosophical framework, not a clinical protocol. Mainstream medicine argues that measurable, disease-centred definitions are more useful precisely because they are measurable.

**Internal tension**: Weil's nine principles define health partly in terms of the capacity for healing (innate ability, susceptibility factors) — but these are functional measures that fall back on specific organ systems and physiological processes, not directly on wholeness. The philosophical framework and the clinical framework are not fully integrated even within *Health and Healing*.

**Note**: The etymological argument is sound as intellectual history but does not constitute evidence for the clinical superiority of wholeness-based medicine. The claim that wholeness models produce better outcomes than disease models is an empirical question that Weil acknowledges remains incompletely answered.
