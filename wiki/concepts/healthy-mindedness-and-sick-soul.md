# Healthy-Mindedness and the Sick Soul

**Summary**: William James's foundational typology of religious temperament in *The Varieties of Religious Experience* — the "once-born" healthy-minded soul who systematically ignores evil, against the "twice-born" sick soul who must first confront and be dismantled by it before a deeper, more durable happiness becomes possible through conversion.

**Sources**:
- [James, W. (1902). *The varieties of religious experience: A study in human nature*. Longmans, Green.](../sources/the-varieties-of-religious-experience.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-20

---

## Healthy-Mindedness

James describes "the religion of healthy-mindedness" as the temperament, congenital in some people, that responds to existence with systematic optimism — a constitutional inability or unwillingness to dwell on evil. Its purest expression is the "once-born" character (his examples include Walt Whitman and, in a more theologically developed form, the Christian Science and New Thought "mind-cure" movements): people for whom sin, guilt, and suffering are treated as errors of perception to be corrected rather than realities to be reckoned with.

James is respectful of this temperament's genuine therapeutic power — mind-cure practitioners, he notes, achieved documented healings of chronic nervous conditions through suggestion, relaxation, and a deliberate turning of attention away from morbid preoccupation — but he treats it as a *partial* solution, one that works only for people whose capacity for evil and suffering is genuinely shallow. Where the pain threshold is higher, healthy-mindedness collapses.

## The Sick Soul

Opposed to healthy-mindedness is "the sick soul" — James's term (later picked up in the wiki via [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s "soul sickness," see [Guilt and Soul Sickness](guilt-and-soul-sickness.md)) for the temperament that cannot look away from evil, loss, and the "vale of tears" quality of existence. Its most severe form is what James calls **anhedonia** — a pathological incapacity for pleasure — and the sense, which he documents at length from Tolstoy's and Bunyan's autobiographical accounts (and, thinly disguised as a "French correspondent," from his own 1869 breakdown), that the "evil facts" of existence are not incidental but "a genuine portion of reality," and that no cheerful reinterpretation can dissolve them.

James's crucial claim: the sick soul's vision is not merely a personal pathology to be cured back to health-mindedness — it is frequently the *truer* report of the human condition, and the pattern of recovery available to it (religious conversion, not mere mood repair) reaches a wider, more durable happiness than healthy-mindedness ever attains, because it has passed through, rather than around, the evil it registers.

## Once-Born and Twice-Born

James borrows Francis W. Newman's terms: the **once-born** need no deliverance because they never registered a fall requiring one; the **twice-born** must die to an old self before a new one can be born. This is explicitly a psychological claim about **two entirely different developmental routes to religious happiness**, not merely a difference of doctrine — James insists the difference "as great as that between passivity and activity, the defensive and the aggressive mood," comparing the "frosty" acceptance of the Stoic Marcus Aurelius (a resigned, once-born-adjacent submission to the cosmic order) with the passionate self-surrender of Christian mystical writers (the *Theologia Germanica*, the *Imitation of Christ*) — the same formal move (accepting one's place in the universe) executed in emotionally opposite registers.

## The Divided Self and Conversion

Between healthy-mindedness and full sick-soul dissolution, James describes "the divided self" — the heterogeneous, internally conflicted personality — and its **unification**, gradual or sudden, which he treats at length under **conversion** (Lectures IX–X). Conversion for James is a real psychological event, not merely a change of belief: a shift in the habitual center of a person's personal energy, arriving (he argues, following Myers and Janet) through the **subliminal self** — a threshold of consciousness wider than ordinary waking awareness. Whether the "self surrender" that precipitates conversion is triggered suddenly (Stephen Bradley's case) or gradually, James treats the value of the conversion exactly as he treats every other religious phenomenon: **"not by its process but by its fruits."**

## Cross-Wiki Connections

### Symbolic Death and Rebirth
James's twice-born structure — dissolution of an old self, followed by rebirth into a wider, transformed identity — is a psychological-experiential parallel to the death/rebirth pattern [Mircea Eliade](../thinkers/mircea-eliade.md) documents ritually across initiatory cultures (see [Symbolic Death and Rebirth](symbolic-death-and-rebirth.md)) and that [Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md) documents mythologically in the hero's Belly of the Whale (see [Hero Myth](hero-myth.md)). James arrived at this pattern independently, from clinical/biographical case material rather than comparative religion, roughly contemporary with Eliade's and Campbell's mid-twentieth-century sources — three separate routes converging on the same underlying structure.

### Crisis as Initiation
Joan Borysenko's reframe of personal catastrophe as initiation (see [Crisis as Initiation](crisis-as-initiation.md)) explicitly credits James's account of soul sickness and its cure through conversion as a direct precursor — James supplies the individual-psychology register that Borysenko later integrates with Turner's anthropology, Campbell's mythology, and Grof's perinatal-stage research.

### Individuation
The twice-born pattern — an old, partial self dissolving so a larger, more integrated one can emerge — anticipates [Carl Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s [individuation](individuation.md) process, though James's frame is explicitly religious/conversion-based rather than Jung's developmental-psychological one. Jung read James closely; James's notion of a "wider self" through which saving experiences arrive is a recognizable precursor to Jung's [the Self](the-self.md) as the psychic totality from which healing proceeds.

## Critique

**Selection bias**: James's testimony collection (much of it via Edwin Starbuck's questionnaires and Protestant devotional literature) has been criticized as reflecting a Protestant, individualist, culturally narrow sample of "religious genius," systematically underrepresenting institutional, communal, and non-Christian religious experience.

**The value of pathology**: James's own defense against the charge that he over-values psychopathology in religious genius is pragmatic rather than empirical — he argues that whatever a state's origin, its worth must be judged by fruits alone, but does not fully resolve how to weigh "immediate luminousness" against "philosophical reasonableness" when the two conflict (his own example: "the difference between Philip drunk and Philip sober").

**Note (needs verification / mainstream contrast)**: Modern clinical psychiatry would treat several of James's core cases (visionary states, anhedonia, what he calls "querulous melancholy") within diagnostic categories he explicitly refuses to adopt — his insistence that pathological origin does not settle spiritual value is a philosophical position, not a finding accepted by contemporary psychiatric science, which generally does treat such states as requiring clinical intervention regardless of any "fruits" they might also produce.
