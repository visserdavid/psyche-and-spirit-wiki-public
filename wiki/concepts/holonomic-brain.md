## The Problem: Where Is Memory?

In the 1920s, Canadian neurosurgeon Wilder Penfield mapped memory localisation in epileptic patients' brains by electrically stimulating the temporal lobes — reliably triggering vivid detailed autobiographical recall. He concluded that every experience was recorded somewhere specific in the brain.

But neuropsychologist Karl Lashley spent thirty years training rats in mazes, then removing portions of their cortex in search of the specific tissue containing the maze-running memory. No matter how much cortex he removed — up to 90% — the memories persisted, though with degraded quality. He could not find the engram. He wrote despairingly: "Learning just is not possible, yet in spite of it all, learning sometimes does occur."

Karl Pribram, who worked with Lashley in the 1940s and witnessed these results, began seeking a mechanism for distributed memory storage.

## The Holographic Solution

In the mid-1960s, Pribram read a *Scientific American* article on holography and immediately recognised the solution:

In a hologram:
- Information is stored as an interference pattern distributed across the entire plate
- Any piece of the plate contains the full image (though with less resolution)
- Destroying half the plate does not remove half the image — it reduces resolution uniformly

If the brain stored information as distributed interference patterns across large swaths of neural tissue, then partial destruction would reduce quality without eliminating specific content. This matched Lashley's data precisely.

## The Fourier Mathematics Confirmation

The holographic model required that the brain process information using wave-form mathematics. The confirmation came in 1979 when neurophysiologists Russell and Karen DeValois at Berkeley discovered that visual cortex cells respond not to patterns (lines, edges, shapes) but to the **Fourier transforms** of those patterns — the wave-form representations of images.

Dennis Gabor had invented holography using Fourier transforms — the same mathematical operation. The brain, it turned out, was using the identical mathematics. As Pribram put it: the brain decodes reality by performing Fourier analysis on incoming neural signals, converting pattern information into frequency-domain wave forms and back again.

This was confirmed across senses: Hermann von Helmholtz had already shown that the ear is a frequency analyser (cochlear resonance); research showed smell, taste, and touch are all frequency-based; Bernstein's 1930s work on movement showed that motor patterns are encoded as Fourier wave forms (confirmed by painting dots on dancers and filming them against plain backgrounds).

## Properties Explained by the Holonomic Model

**Vast memory storage**: John von Neumann calculated the human brain stores approximately 2.8 × 10²⁰ bits over a lifetime. Holographic frequency encoding can store this in roughly one square inch of neural tissue — fifty Bibles' worth of information per square inch.

**Associative memory**: In holography, two encoded objects can be linked so that illuminating one conjures the other. This models Proust's madeleine — a sensory fragment that reconstructs an entire scene.

**Invariant recognition**: Interference holography allows recognition of an object across different viewing angles and even after years of change. The brain's holographic processing explains recognising a face not seen in twenty years.

**Phantom limb pain**: The brain has holographically encoded the absent limb in its interference patterns; the phantom is the persistence of that encoding even after the physical limb is removed.

**Photographic memory**: Individuals with eidetic memory may have access to larger regions of the distributed holographic storage — more of the plate illuminated.

**Learned skill transfer**: Motor patterns encoded as Fourier wave forms can be read out through different body parts — explaining why a person can write their name in the air with their elbow.

## Pribram's Synthesis with Bohm

Bohm cites Pribram's work directly in *[Wholeness and the Implicate Order](../sources/wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)* (Chapter 7): he treats Pribram's evidence that memory is not stored in single cells but distributed holographically across brain tissue as neuroscientific support for extending the implicate order to consciousness. This is the primary-source version of a connection the wiki previously had only via Talbot's popularisation.

When Pribram encountered Bohm's [implicate order](implicate-order.md), the two frameworks merged:

- Pribram's brain is a hologram that converts frequency-domain signals into the experience of objects
- Bohm's universe is a holomovement whose explicate-order surface is the frequency domain that the brain processes

Together they propose: **the brain is a hologram within a holographic universe**. Beneath the apparently solid physical world is a sea of interfering frequencies (implicate order); the brain performs Fourier transforms on these frequencies to generate the experienced world of objects. Reality as experienced is the brain's holographic projection from this deeper frequency domain.

As Pribram stated: "If you didn't have that lens, the world would be in a blur of interference patterns. You would be in the frequency domain. You'd be in the state that mystics describe."

## Implications for Healing

The holonomic brain model underlies [Brennan's](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md) [holographic healing model](holographic-healing-model.md) in three ways:

1. **Mind creates body**: If the body is a holographic projection of consciousness frequencies, then shifts in consciousness (beliefs, intentions, energy field patterns) can produce physical changes — the biological basis of the entire biography-is-biology framework.

2. **High Sense Perception**: Individuals who can perceive the human energy field may be accessing the frequency domain more directly — perceiving the interference patterns before they are projected into the object-world. Valerie Hunt's bioelectric measurements at UCLA and Shafica Karagulla's clinical HSP documentation support this.

3. **Distributed healing**: Because each piece of a hologram contains the whole, healing can be initiated from any point in the system — one organ, one chakra, one energy layer — and the whole responds.

## Critique

**Analogy vs. mechanism**: The holonomic brain model uses the mathematics of holography to explain distributed memory but does not provide a specific biological mechanism (which neurons encode which wave-fronts; how neural interference patterns are produced and reconstructed). It is a mathematical analogy at a higher level than a cellular theory.

**Mainstream acceptance**: Pribram's Fourier analysis of visual cortex responses is accepted neuroscience. The full holographic interpretation — that the brain is literally a hologram — is considered speculative by most neuroscientists.

**Note**: Mainstream neuroscience accepts distributed memory storage and Fourier processing in sensory systems. The holographic interpretation is a minority framework.
