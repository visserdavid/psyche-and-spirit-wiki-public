# HOw Process (Five Steps)

**Summary**: Stanley Keleman's five-step somatic methodology for engaging, intensifying, disrupting, and reorganizing one's habitual body patterns — the practical core of Formative Psychology, presented in *Embodying Experience* (1987).

**Sources**:
- [Keleman, S. (1987). *Embodying experience: Forming a personal life*. Center Press.](../sources/embodying-experience.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

The HOw process is Keleman's answer to a central problem in somatic therapy: insight does not produce change. Knowing that one has a rigid, collapsed, or swollen structure — even understanding its biographical origins — does not dissolve it. What produces change is working directly with the muscle contractions and pulsatory patterns that organize one's existence. The HOw process provides a repeatable five-step structure for doing this.

"HOw" is Keleman's term for the foundational question of somatic self-inquiry: *how does one organize oneself?* The five steps together constitute a methodology for moving from automatic somatic organization to conscious, reformable somatic organization.

## The Five Steps

**Step One: What is my image or situation?**
Identify the current somatic image — the posture, gesture, tension pattern, or bodily story that organizes the moment. This is surface recognition. The question is not "why?" but "what am I doing, bodily, right now?" One might notice: chest held up, neck braced, belly sucked in, shoulders pulled forward. The image includes the story associated with the posture.

**Step Two: How do I organize or maintain this image?**
Identify the specific muscle patterns that generate the image and deliberately *intensify* them. If the pattern is neck-stiffening, increase the neck tension a little. If it is chest-holding, tighten the chest further. This is not catharsis — it is reconnaissance. By voluntarily amplifying the contraction, one gains explicit knowledge of the body's organizational strategy: which muscles are involved, which layers are engaged, what feeling the pattern produces. Awareness through doing, not through introspection.

**Step Three: How do I stop doing this?**
This is the accordion step — the disorganization phase. Having intensified the pattern in Step Two, now release it *incrementally*. Not a sudden letting go, but a gradual loosening: loosen a bit, notice what happens, loosen a bit more. Keleman is insistent that relaxation from the beginning is ineffective; one must first intensify (Step Two) and then release bit by bit (Step Three). This incremental disorganization interrupts the automatic perpetuation of the pattern without shocking the organism into a new defensive response.

**Step Four: What happens when I stop?**
Remain in the disorganized state — the somatic pause, the hiatus of the interrupted pattern. This is the incubation stage. Neither action nor passive waiting: an open, receptive attention to what emerges from the released territory. At this stage, suppressed impulses, memories, images, and feelings arise spontaneously. Keleman distinguishes this from insight (cognitive) or catharsis (emotional release): it is somatic gestation — the organism reorganizing itself from the inside. The quality of attention required is similar to what he calls "waiting for the visitor" — neither forcing nor preventing.

**Step Five: What do I do with what I have learned?**
Take what emerged in Step Four and form a deliberate response. This step requires choosing — returning to the old pattern, incorporating the new material, or practicing a reformed behaviour. The key word is *practice*: the new somatic organization does not install itself automatically. Step Five must be repeated across multiple sessions and daily life situations until the reorganized pattern has the same somatic fluency as the original.

## The Accordion Principle

Steps Two and Three embody what Keleman calls the accordion principle: the body, like an accordion, organizes through the building and releasing of pressure. Health is not low tension but the full range of the continuum — from maximum expansion to maximum contraction and back. The HOw process engages this accordion action deliberately: Step Two builds pressure (intensification); Step Three releases it incrementally (disorganization); Step Four is the silence between two notes; Step Five is the new phrase formed.

## Application to Daily Life

The five steps can be applied to any somatic situation:

- **Problem-solving**: What is my somatic stance toward this problem? How do I organize it muscularly? How do I disorganize that stance and wait for a different response?
- **Conflict resolution**: Internal conflict (two impulses competing) and external conflict (relational tension) both have somatic signatures; the HOw process addresses both at the structural level.
- **Interpersonal contact**: The patterns of connection and separation, closeness and distance, are organized somatically. Steps One through Three map these patterns; Steps Four and Five create space for new relational forms.

## Inhibition as Learning Tool

A central insight of the HOw process is that **inhibition is a competency**, not a problem. The ability to stop — to interrupt an automatic pattern, sustain the pause, and wait for something new — is the somatic foundation of all learning. The brain is, in Keleman's account, primarily an organ of inhibition: its most sophisticated function is delaying and redirecting impulses, not simply firing them.

This means that the HOw process, far from being a relaxation technique, is actually an education in controlled stopping. Steps Two through Four are different forms of inhibition: intensify and hold (Step Two), release incrementally (Step Three), sustain the gap without filling it prematurely (Step Four). The practitioner who masters these steps develops a somatic capacity for self-regulation that operates at a level more fundamental than cognition or willpower.

## Motor Memory

The HOw process works directly with **motor memory** — the encoding of biographical experience in muscle patterns. Every significant experience leaves a muscular trace: the contraction patterns associated with fear, humiliation, longing, and loss are stored as repeatable somatic configurations. Cognitive recall of these events is secondary; the primary memory is muscular.

By intensifying these muscle patterns (Step Two) and then systematically disorganizing them (Step Three), the HOw process accesses motor memory directly — not through narrative but through the body's own re-enactment. This is why Keleman argues that somatic reorganization can produce change in biographical patterns that insight-based therapy cannot reach: it works at the level where the memory is stored.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks in the Wiki

- **Myss's [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md)**: Both frameworks hold that biographical experience becomes bodily organization. Keleman's HOw process is the clinical tool for unwinding this organization; Myss offers no comparable practical technique — her approach is primarily symbolic, energetic, and spiritual.

- **Jung's [individuation](individuation.md)**: Individuation in Jung's sense is a lifelong process of bringing unconscious material into conscious relationship. The HOw process operates at the somatic level of what Jung called the shadow encounter: making the automatic visible, the involuntary voluntary. But where Jung's process works through dreams, symbols, and active imagination, Keleman's works through the body's own pulsatory patterns.

- **Hillman's [acorn theory](acorn-theory.md)**: Hillman argues that the daimon shapes the biography through compulsion and obsession. Keleman would say the same organizing force is visible in somatic pattern: the compulsion to brace, to collapse, to stiffen is the body's enacted story. The HOw process does not eliminate this story but reorganizes it — makes it usable rather than automatic.

## Critique

**Strengths**: The five-step structure is explicit, repeatable, and teachable. The emphasis on incremental intensification before release is clinically sophisticated — it distinguishes Keleman from both cathartic approaches (which seek immediate release) and relaxation approaches (which bypass the pattern entirely). The connection between Steps Four and Five — incubation leading to conscious choice — is psychologically sound.

**Limitations**: The methodology requires extensive training and ongoing supervision to apply safely. The book's compressed, telegraphic prose makes it difficult to extract a reliable training manual from the text alone. Self-application, without a trained practitioner present, risks fragmentary or counterproductive engagement with the Steps.

**Mainstream psychological parallel**: The HOw process shares structural features with Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing (titration, pendulation between activation and settling) and with Pat Ogden's Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (working with somatic impulses and truncated movements). These approaches have developed since Keleman's work and have more clinical research support. The overlaps suggest that Keleman's insights are genuine; the methodology would benefit from the kind of systematic evaluation these newer approaches have received.

**Note**: mainstream cognitive-behavioural psychology would question whether body-level interventions are sufficient for changing habitual patterns, arguing that cognitive restructuring is also required. The somatic tradition's counter-argument — that cognitive insight without somatic reorganization produces "knowing without being able to change" — is supported by clinical experience but not yet by controlled trials.
