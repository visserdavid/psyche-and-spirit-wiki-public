# Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)

**Summary**: M. Scott Peck's proposal that human evil is a diagnosable personality disorder characterised by malignant narcissism, the refusal to tolerate self-knowledge, compulsive lying, and scapegoating — the clinical and spiritual obverse of the healing frameworks developed by Myss, Borysenko, and the rest of the wiki's literature.

## Definition

In Peck's framework, evil is defined functionally as **the use of power to destroy the growth of others in order to defend and preserve the integrity of one's own self-image**. This definition has several implications:

1. **Evil is relational**: it requires a victim — someone whose spirit is diminished by contact with the evil person
2. **Evil is defensive**: it arises not from strength but from the terror of self-examination
3. **Evil is enacted in the name of righteousness**: the evil person consistently believes themselves to be acting correctly, even virtuously
4. **Evil is a pattern, not an act**: a single harmful action does not constitute evil; the diagnostic criterion is a *consistent pattern* over time

This definition distinguishes evil from:
- **Sin**: All humans sin (miss the mark); evil is not defined by the magnitude of sins but by the refusal to acknowledge them
- **Psychopathy/sociopathy**: Psychopaths lack conscience; evil people *have* conscience but consistently override it
- **Mental illness**: Most mentally ill people are not evil; most evil people appear mentally healthy (competent, functional, respected)

## Precursor: Evil as Extreme Laziness

Five years before *People of the Lie*, Peck previewed the problem of evil in a brief section of *[The Road Less Traveled](../sources/the-road-less-traveled.md)*, where he defined evil as ordinary [laziness](entropy-and-grace.md) — his candidate for the psychological meaning of original sin — carried to its most active extreme: the exercise of power to crush whatever threatens one's self-image, rather than mere failure to extend it. That earlier formulation was softer and more universal, treating evil as a potential present in everyone in some degree, resolved differently case by case. *People of the Lie* hardens this into the categorical clinical diagnosis described below, applying only to a narrower class of people who consistently refuse self-examination. Peck does not explicitly reconcile the two statements. See [Entropy, Laziness, and Grace](entropy-and-grace.md) for the earlier account.

## The Core Mechanism: Narcissism

Peck places **malignant narcissism** at the root of evil. This is not ordinary self-interest but an absolute demand that the self-image of goodness and perfection be maintained at all costs. When reality threatens this image — through the feedback of conscience, the reactions of others, or the evidence of harm caused — the narcissistic response is to destroy the evidence rather than update the self-image.

The mechanism proceeds:

1. **The threat**: Evidence of imperfection, failure, or harm caused presents itself (conscience, a child's distress, another person's judgment)
2. **The intolerable pain**: The narcissistic personality cannot bear the discomfort of self-criticism — experiencing it as equivalent to annihilation
3. **The response**: Project the fault outward (scapegoat the child, rationalise the harm, reframe the evidence as an attack)
4. **The lie**: Construct a narrative in which the self is blameless and the victim is responsible
5. **The hardening**: Each cycle deepens the pattern; the capacity for self-awareness diminishes; the lies become more elaborate and more automatic

Peck draws on [Erich Fromm](../thinkers/erich-fromm.md)'s *[The Heart of Man](../sources/heart-of-man.md)* (1964) for the concept of malignant narcissism and on [Martin Buber](../thinkers/martin-buber.md)'s *Good and Evil* (1953) for the image of evil as a compulsive **insistence on self-affirmation** regardless of evidence. See [Fromm's Three-Strand Account](#fromms-three-strand-account-necrophilia-narcissism-symbiosis) below for how Peck's single-mechanism narcissism account relates to Fromm's broader three-strand "syndrome of decay."

## Fromm's Three-Strand Account: Necrophilia, Narcissism, Symbiosis

Fromm's own framework, which Peck draws from, is broader than the narcissism-centred mechanism above. Fromm identifies **three** converging strands in his "syndrome of decay" — [necrophilia](necrophilia-and-biophilia.md) (the love of death, decay, and the mechanical), [malignant narcissism](individual-and-group-narcissism.md) (individual and group), and [incestuous-symbiotic fixation](incestuous-symbiotic-fixation.md) (regressive fusion with mother, clan, nation, or race) — arguing that the most destructive personalities (his central case is Hitler) show all three reinforcing one another, rather than narcissism operating alone.

Peck's clinical diagnosis absorbs only the narcissism strand into his own definition of evil, without building necrophilia or incestuous-symbiotic fixation into his diagnostic criteria. This is a genuine narrowing: Peck's evil is a single-mechanism account (the refusal to tolerate the pain of self-examination) while Fromm's is a three-mechanism convergence account. The two are compatible rather than contradictory — Peck can be read as elaborating the narcissism strand of Fromm's broader syndrome in much greater clinical and theological detail — but Peck does not explain why he leaves necrophilia and symbiotic fixation out of his own diagnostic picture, even though he cites Fromm approvingly elsewhere (for instance, linking necrophilia to the desire "to control, fossilise, and prevent the living variety of others").

All three thinkers now in the wiki's evil cluster — [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) (*[privatio boni](privatio-boni.md)*), Fromm (necrophilia/narcissism/symbiosis), and Peck (clinical narcissism-centred evil) — agree evil is psychologically and clinically real, not a mere absence or illusion, but locate its generative mechanism differently: Jung in the failure to integrate the [shadow](shadow.md); Fromm in a three-strand character syndrome; Peck in the single mechanism of narcissistic self-image defense. None of the three offers a controlled empirical test that would adjudicate between the accounts.

## A Fourth Account: Martin's Literal, External Evil

[Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md)'s *[Hostage to the Devil](../sources/hostage-to-the-devil.md)* (1976) — Peck's own acknowledged second teacher on evil, alongside Fromm — adds a genuinely different kind of account to this cluster, not merely a different mechanism. Where Jung, Fromm, and Peck all locate evil's origin *within* the human personality (however severely disordered), Martin's possession framework locates it in a wholly external, non-human, intelligent malevolent agency that requires — but does not originate in — the possessed person's consent. See [Martin's Stages of Possession](possession-process-martin.md) for the detailed four-stage entry model and six-stage exorcism-ritual sequence.

This is a difference in kind rather than degree: Martin's entity has independent will, memory, and strategic intent, standing at the furthest possible remove from Jung's *privatio boni* critique (evil as no mere absence but still a structural feature of the psyche) and from Fromm's and Peck's character-syndrome accounts (evil as a pattern of choices and defenses within an ordinary human personality). Peck's own possession chapters (see [Possession and Exorcism (Peck)](possession-and-exorcism.md)) attempt a partial synthesis — proposing that ordinary evil and possession sit on the same spectrum — but Martin does not extend the same courtesy to secular or psychological accounts; his framework treats the reduction of possession to a "psyche" phenomenon as itself one of the entity's characteristic deceptions.

**Note**: mainstream psychiatry, clinical psychology, and academic religious studies do not treat literal possession by a discrete external entity as an evidence-based diagnostic category. The clinical phenomena Martin and Peck describe (dramatic apparent personality change, altered voice, self-injury, extreme distress) are addressed in the mainstream literature under dissociative identity disorder, psychotic illness, and culturally-shaped dissociative/conversion syndromes — see [Martin's Stages of Possession § Critique](possession-process-martin.md#critique) for the fuller discussion.

## Scapegoating as the Defining Behaviour

The most clinically visible expression of evil is **scapegoating**: attributing one's own unacknowledged faults, failures, and darkness to others — especially children, partners, and subordinates who are in no position to resist.

Bobby's parents (from *People of the Lie*) gave their son his older brother's suicide weapon for Christmas. When confronted, they rationalised: they couldn't afford better; boys like guns; it wasn't the same calibre. They believed these rationalisations. Their scapegoating was not cynical — it was structural. They could not see their own destructiveness because seeing it would have destroyed their self-image as caring parents.

Scapegoating has two functions:
- **Preservative**: relocating the sense of fault to where the narcissist cannot be blamed
- **Destructive**: the scapegoat (typically a child) internalises the fault as self-blame, developing the shame, worthlessness, and self-destruction that the perpetrator is projecting

## Evil and Sin

Peck offers a careful distinction between sin and evil. Both involve wrongdoing; the difference is in the relationship to that wrongdoing:

- **Sinners** fail, acknowledge failure (at least to themselves), feel guilt or shame, and potentially change
- **Evil people** fail, deny failure absolutely, project the failure onto others, and feel no guilt because they have redefined their behaviour as righteous

This parallels [A Course in Miracles](../sources/a-course-in-miracles.md)'s distinction between the ego's "seek but do not find" strategy and genuine self-examination — though ACIM dissolves the moral distinction between sin and error, which Peck emphatically does not. See [Ego vs. Holy Spirit](ego-vs-holy-spirit.md).

The sense of sin — the capacity to say "I have done wrong" — is, in Peck's framework, a **health-protective** quality. The person who can bear the pain of self-knowledge is protected from the slide into evil.

## The Diagnostic Signal: Revulsion

In therapeutic encounters with evil patients, Peck reports a characteristic response that he identifies as diagnostically significant: **revulsion** — not sadness, not pity, not anger, but a gut-level desire to escape the presence. He argues this is not countertransference (which can be worked through) but an evolved protective signal: "Something in us knows when we are in the presence of evil and wants to get away."

This revulsion is distinct from the contempt or disgust triggered by other forms of psychopathology. Peck argues it should be named and used — both as a diagnostic indicator and as a self-protective instinct that prevents the clinician from being drawn into the patient's destructive field.

## Evil and Illness

Peck argues extensively for classifying evil as an illness — a personality disorder requiring clinical study, compassionate treatment, and scientific investigation. His arguments:

**For the illness designation**:
- Evil is a disorder that prevents individuals from fulfilling their human potential
- Recognising it as illness obligates the healing professions to study and treat it
- It extends compassion rather than mere condemnation

**Against common objections**:
- "Evil people don't suffer" — they do suffer (lives of terror and pretence), but their narcissism prohibits acknowledging it
- "We can't call something a disease that doesn't have a treatment" — many diseases lack cures; naming them as diseases enables the search for treatment
- "Labelling someone evil is a moral judgment, not a medical one" — yes, but medicine cannot avoid moral judgments in all circumstances; the alternative (not recognising evil as a clinical reality) leaves its victims without a diagnostic framework

**The dangers Peck acknowledges**: Moral judgments carry the risk of misuse, projection of one's own shadow onto patients, and the weaponisation of clinical authority. The solution is rigorous methodology and the discipline of self-examination — not the avoidance of the concept.

## Evil and the Shadow

Peck's framework converges strongly with Jung's concept of the [shadow](shadow.md). Jung stated that much evil results from the "failure to meet the shadow" — the refusal to acknowledge one's own darkness, which then gets projected outward onto others. Peck's evil people are, in precisely this sense, people who have never met their shadow: every encounter with evidence of their darkness is met with a projection (scapegoating) rather than a confrontation (shadow integration).

Where Jung sees shadow integration as the path to wholeness and individuation, Peck sees its consistent avoidance as the path to evil. The clinical implication is the same: the shadow must be acknowledged, named, and integrated — or it will be enacted outward in destruction.

Jung's fullest theoretical statement on the reality of evil is his critique, in [*Aion*](../sources/aion.md), of the Christian doctrine of [*privatio boni*](privatio-boni.md) — the claim that evil has no substance of its own, only a privation of good. Jung and Peck converge on treating evil as psychologically and clinically real rather than a mere absence to be explained away, but diverge in scope: Jung frames the shadow (including its evil aspect) as a universal developmental task everyone carries, while Peck proposes a categorical clinical diagnosis applying only to a narrower class of people who consistently refuse it. See [Privatio Boni § Tension with M. Scott Peck's Clinical Psychology of Evil](privatio-boni.md) for the fuller comparison.

Jung noted that the Germans' failure to recognise their collective shadow led directly to the Holocaust — the ultimate collective scapegoating. Peck reaches the same conclusion through his MyLai analysis. See [Group Evil and MyLai](group-evil.md).

## Connections to the Wiki's Healing Literature

This concept is the structural opposite of the wiki's central thesis. Where [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md) holds that unacknowledged biographical patterns produce illness *in the body*, Peck's psychology of evil holds that unacknowledged biographical patterns produce destruction *in others*. Both are expressions of the refusal to bring the contents of the unconscious (the shadow, the wound, the suppressed truth) into the light.

Myss's [woundology](woundology.md) describes a milder version of the same dynamic: people who use their wounds as identity, resist healing, and drain those around them — not through malice but through the investment of their life-force in the wound rather than growth. Evil, in Peck's framework, is woundology taken to its extreme: the wound becomes a weapon directed against others.

The Myss/Shealy [psychology of illness](the-psychology-of-illness.md) asks: what biographical patterns lead a person to fall ill? Peck's complementary question is: what biographical patterns lead a person to harm others systematically while remaining physically healthy?

The answer in both frameworks is the same: the refusal of self-knowledge.

## Critique

**The boundary problem**: Who qualifies as "evil" versus "severely narcissistic" or "socially destructive"? Peck's criteria — scapegoating, lying, maintaining appearances, harming others — apply with varying intensity across a wide human spectrum. Drawing the diagnostic line is inherently a judgment call, and carries real risks of misapplication.

**Empirical basis**: The concept is built on a small number of clinical cases plus theological reflection. It lacks the epidemiological grounding that would distinguish it from a compelling narrative.

**The agency question**: Peck acknowledges but does not fully resolve the question of how much choice is involved. If evil is a disease (as he proposes), the degree of individual responsibility is diminished. If it is a series of freely made choices, the disease model seems inappropriate. He affirms both — freedom and disease — while acknowledging the tension.

**Note**: Mainstream psychiatry does not recognise "evil" as a diagnostic category. Narcissistic personality disorder (NPD) and antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) capture some of the features Peck describes, but neither maps cleanly onto his definition. Peck's proposal remains outside the DSM framework.
