# Insults to Form

**Summary**: Keleman's term for the stresses, shocks, emotional injuries, and developmental challenges that alter the body's pulsatory patterns and progressively shape its physical form.

**Sources**:
- [Keleman, S. (1985). *Emotional anatomy: The structure of experience*. Center Press.](../sources/emotional-anatomy.md)
- [Keleman, S. (1987). *Embodying experience: Forming a personal life*. Center Press.](../sources/embodying-experience.md)

## Overview

Every life involves insults — Keleman's deliberately broad term for any event or condition that disrupts the body's natural pulsatory organization. Insults include acute shocks (terror, physical trauma, sudden loss), chronic stresses (sustained relational hostility, deprivation, demand for emotional suppression), and developmental injuries (the particular ways in which childhood environments fail to support the child's emerging form).

The body responds to insults through two primary reflexes — the **startle reflex** and the **stress response** — and through their chronic activation, these reflexes progressively reshape the body's structure.

## The Startle Reflex

The startle reflex is the organism's immediate response to an unexpected threat. The head draws back, the chest compresses, the abdomen pulls in, the limbs contract toward the core. It is a primitive protective response — the organism shrinks its surface and braces its interior.

In ordinary life, the startle response is brief and resolves. But when insults are overwhelming, repeated, or chronic, the startle pattern becomes habitual — the organism stays in partial startle, maintaining the braced, shortened configuration even in safety.

## The Stress Continuum

Keleman describes a **startle/stress continuum** with six characteristic positions organized around two poles:

**Overbound pole (expansion dominant)**:
1. Pulling back — the withholder; rigid and braced against approach
2. Pulling up — the dominator; elongated and stiffened against collapse
3. Compacted — squeezed and dense; resisting abandonment by becoming small

**Underbound pole (contraction dominant)**:
4. Deflated — losing form; falling into the space between expansion and collapse
5. Swollen — inflated and porous; merging outward to avoid containment
6. Collapsed — sinking and resigned; pulsation extinguished, form abandoned

Each position along the continuum represents a different ratio of the two survival strategies: **resistance** (becoming overbound) and **yielding** (becoming underbound). The organism oscillates along this continuum in response to different challenges; chronic insults fix it in one region.

## How Insults Shape Structure

Insults alter structure through a three-stage process:

1. **Acute response**: The organism responds with a startle or stress reflex — an immediate change in muscular tone, pulsatory speed, and postural organization.

2. **Habituation**: If the insult continues or is repeated, the response becomes habitual. The muscles involved maintain their emergency tonus even without a current threat. Diaphragms constrict; pouches lose their coordinated pulsation.

3. **Structural fixation**: Over time, the habitual response becomes anatomical. Tissue density, muscle length, connective tissue arrangement, and organ placement are altered. The body's form itself carries the record of the insults it has sustained.

This is the somatic parallel to [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s ["biography is biology"](biography-is-biology.md) — but at the level of visible, palpable tissue structure rather than the subtle energy field.

## Family Dynamics and Structural Formation

Keleman gives particular attention to the family origin of structural patterns:

- **Rigid structures** (withholder type) develop in families where independence is first encouraged and then met with disapproval or humiliation — the child learns to brace against the unpredictable withdrawal of support.
- **Rigid structures** (dominator type) arise where performance is demanded and tenderness shamed — the child elongates against the threat of collapse into vulnerability.
- **Swollen structures** develop in families with an emphasis on closeness and merger — the child inflates to belong, losing the ability to contain itself.
- **Collapsed structures** develop in families with abandonment, indifference, or deprivation — the child gives up trying to fill, and the organism's pulsation dims.

## Excitation, Shock, and Startle Fixation (*Embodying Experience*)

*Embodying Experience* adds detail on the mechanism by which acute shock creates structural fixation:

**Excitation as the carrier of shock**: Every insult arrives as an excitatory event — a sudden charge that the organism must process, absorb, or deflect. If the excitatory charge is too large or too sudden to metabolize, the organism goes into shock: pulsation speeds and then locks, the startle reflex fires fully and fails to complete, and the excitatory pattern becomes the new baseline.

**Two poles of the startle reflex** under prolonged insult:
1. **Overbound fixation** (bracing/compacting): the organism stiffens, raises its centre of gravity, compresses the abdomen, braces the arms and neck — preparing to strike or turn. This pole involves increased organization, contraction, and muscular pressure. If chronically sustained, this produces the rigid and dense/compact distress patterns.
2. **Underbound fixation** (withdrawal/collapse): after the overbound response fails to resolve the threat, the organism passes into the withdrawal phase — it narrows, loses form, its pulsation slows and diffuses. This pole involves decreased organization, elongation failure, and slackened muscular tone. If chronically sustained, this produces the swollen and collapsed distress patterns.

**The significance of incompletion**: Keleman argues (consistent with later somatic trauma theorists including Peter Levine) that the problem is not the startle response itself but its failure to complete. A startle reflex that fires, runs its course, and resolves leaves no structural trace. It is the *interrupted* response — the one that neither fully strikes nor fully withdraws — that becomes fixated in tissue and posture.

The HOw process addresses this directly by working with the incomplete action: Step Two intensifies the fixated pattern; Step Three disorganizes it incrementally; Step Four gives the incomplete response time and space to complete itself in a controlled, non-threatening way.

## Connection to Woundology

Keleman's insults-to-form parallels Myss's [woundology](woundology.md) — the pattern of using biographical wounds as the organising principle of identity. Where Myss's focus is on the psycho-spiritual dimension (the wound as social currency, as an excuse not to heal), Keleman's focus is on the somatic dimension: the wound as structural inscription, as a shape the body has learned to maintain. Both observations point to the same underlying truth that unresolved biographical experience becomes bodily organization — one from the energy-spiritual direction, the other from the somatic-anatomical direction.

## Critique

The claim that emotional insults produce specific anatomical changes (rather than psychological symptoms) is central to somatic therapy but contested in mainstream medicine. While stress is known to affect inflammation, cortisol levels, and immune function — and chronic tension is associated with musculoskeletal problems — the specific mappings Keleman describes (this family dynamic produces this structural pattern) have not been established in controlled research. The framework is clinically influential and intuitively coherent; its empirical basis remains limited.
