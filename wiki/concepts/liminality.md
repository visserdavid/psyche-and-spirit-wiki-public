# Liminality

**Summary**: Victor Turner's term for the "threshold" phase of a rite of passage — the state of being "betwixt and between," structurally invisible, stripped of status and property, and marked by symbols of death, gestation, and formlessness; the psychologically dangerous and transformative core of any ritual of transition.

**Sources**:
- [Turner, V. (1969). *The ritual process: Structure and anti-structure*. Aldine Publishing.](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-14

## From van Gennep to Turner

[Arnold van Gennep](../thinkers/arnold-van-gennep.md) (1909) identified that rites of passage universally comprise three phases: separation, margin (limen), and aggregation — see [The Rites of Passage Schema](rites-of-passage-schema.md) for the full account, now grounded in the primary source. [Victor Turner](../thinkers/victor-turner.md), in *[The Ritual Process](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)* (1969), takes the middle phase — which he renames **liminality** — as the object of sustained theoretical attention van Gennep himself did not give it.

Liminal *personae* ("threshold people") elude the normal network of classifications that locate people in cultural space: they are neither what they were nor what they will become. Turner catalogues the symbols societies use to represent this condition: death, the womb, invisibility, darkness, bisexuality, the wilderness, an eclipse of sun or moon. Liminal subjects are commonly disguised as monsters, stripped of clothing and property, addressed by a single genderless term, subjected to ordeals, and required to obey their instructors without complaint — while simultaneously developing intense comradeship and equality among themselves, since all distinctions of rank have been leveled.

## Ndembu examples

Turner draws his central illustrations from his own fieldwork:

- In the **Isoma** ritual (see [The Ritual Process](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)), the afflicted woman and her husband pass through a tunnel between two symbolic holes — one standing for death and witchcraft, the other for life and health — enacting liminal death-and-rebirth directly in ritual space.
- In the **installation of the Kanongesha** (Ndembu paramount chief), the chief-elect is stripped, dressed only in a ragged waist-cloth, addressed by the same term used for a common slave-wife, seated in a posture of shame, ritually insulted and reviled by the commoner Kafwana (who in ordinary life owes the chief deference), and forbidden to show any resentment. Only after this ordeal of humiliation is he invested with the sacred *lukanu* bracelet and enthroned. Turner reads this as revealing a general principle: **structural inferiors ritually wield power over the liminal superior**, because "liminality implies that the high could not be high unless the low existed" — the sacred humility of high office is manufactured, not natural.
- Circumcision lodges (**Mukanda**) and other initiation rites show the same pattern: novices are stripped of clothing and former names, addressed by a single collective term, subjected to ordeal, and instructed by a figure who functions as a surrogate father — a structure Turner explicitly compares to the *Rule of St. Benedict*'s monastic novitiate (obedience, poverty, the leveling of birth-rank among monks), arguing that liminality is institutionalized wherever a tradition builds permanent "outsider" roles: monks, pilgrims, and the "holy fool."

## The Table of Oppositions

Turner's most cited passage is a schematic table (adapting a device from Lévi-Strauss) contrasting liminality with the normal, structured status system: transition vs. state, totality vs. partiality, homogeneity vs. heterogeneity, equality vs. inequality, anonymity vs. named position, nakedness vs. clothing distinguishing rank, sexual continence vs. sexuality, humility vs. pride of position, sacredness vs. secularity, silence vs. speech, foolishness vs. sagacity, acceptance of pain and suffering vs. their avoidance.

## Status elevation vs. status reversal

Turner distinguishes two kinds of liminal ritual with opposite social direction:

- **Rituals of status elevation** (initiation, chieftainship installation): a subject or novice is permanently conveyed from a lower to a higher position, humbled during the liminal phase but never returning to their prior structural rank.
- **Rituals of status reversal**: society periodically inverts its normal hierarchy for a bounded, repeatable period — inferiors are ritually licensed to revile, mock, or dominate superiors, who must accept the reversal without retaliation. Turner's examples range from Halloween's masked, anonymous children (structurally likened to earth-demons and the dead, licensed to threaten propertied adults with "tricks") to the Christmas-Day British Army tradition of officers serving dinner to enlisted men, to the license of the medieval court jester and the drummers of the Barotse royal barge, who alone could rebuke the king. Unlike status elevation, status reversal changes no one's permanent rank; it functions as a release valve that, paradoxically, reaffirms the very structure it inverts.

## Critique

**Overgeneralization.** Turner acknowledges in his own 1977 preface that critics accused him of extending concepts derived from a limited set of small-scale, traditional-society rituals (chiefly Ndembu) to describe ritual and social process universally — a concern that recurs across the book's reception in anthropology. The three-phase schema and the liminal "properties table" impose a single template on an enormous diversity of practices; the specific cultural meaning of any one rite may not reduce cleanly to the universal pattern.

**Romanticization.** As with the wiki's existing discussion of [Initiation Rites](initiation-rites.md) (drawn from Joseph Henderson's Jungian account), Turner's description of liminal ordeal risks understating real physical harm, coercion, and danger. His own Tsonga circumcision example — boys "beaten like shepherds," deprived of sleep and water, subjected to finger-crushing — is presented as functional "stripping" toward a new status, but the line between transformative ordeal and simple abuse, especially where consent is not meaningfully available (as with child initiates), is one Turner does not dwell on.

**Cultural specificity of the social container.** [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s extension of liminality to modern Western personal crisis (see [Crisis as Initiation](crisis-as-initiation.md)) already registers, within this wiki, the central limitation of applying Turner's model outside the societies he studied: liminality in the Ndembu case is always held by a community — elders, instructors, a "tribe" that assigns meaning to the ordeal and guarantees reincorporation. Contemporary individuals in crisis typically lack any equivalent communal container, which may make unsupported "liminality" simply chaotic rather than transformative.
