# Love and Fear (ACIM)

**Summary**: *A Course in Miracles* asserts that there are only two emotions — love and fear — and that all negative feelings (anger, guilt, jealousy, grief, resentment) are forms of fear while all positive expressions are forms of love; the course of healing is the progressive replacement of fear with love through forgiveness.

**Sources**:
- Schucman, H. (1976). *A course in miracles*. Foundation for Inner Peace.

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## The Two-Emotion Framework

Chapter 13, Section 6 of the Text states plainly: "I have said you have but two emotions, love and fear." This is one of the Course's simplest and most far-reaching claims. All emotional experience, however varied in form, reduces to one of two underlying states:

- **Love**: The extension of God's nature — given, not exchanged; expansive, not diminishing; unconditional, not contingent. Love is the original state of the Son of God and the destination of the Course's curriculum.
- **Fear**: The ego's creation — a response to the belief in separation, guilt, and the anticipation of punishment. Fear takes many forms: anger, resentment, jealousy, grief, envy, shame, boredom, despair.

The Course states: "Fear is lack of love." It is not a positive force but an absence — or more precisely, a substitution: what the mind holds in place of love when it has chosen the ego's thought system.

## Love Is Not What the Ego Calls Love

The Course is careful to distinguish real love from what the ego calls love. Ego-love is always conditional and selective: it is directed at particular people who supply particular needs. This is the love of [special relationships](special-vs-holy-relationships.md) — love that necessarily excludes and therefore contains resentment.

Real love, in ACIM's framework, is the extension of the divine — it does not withhold, does not demand, and does not exclude. It is not a feeling of warmth toward specific individuals but the natural state that arises when fear is absent. The Workbook lesson "Love is not learned. It is simply recognized when illusions are gone" captures this: love is not an achievement but a recognition.

## All Attack Is Fear

The Course's claim that attack is always fear in disguise is one of its most practically useful teachings. When a person attacks — in any form, whether physically, verbally, or in thought — the Course says the attack is a "call for love." The attacker is afraid; the attack is the ego's expression of that fear.

This reinterpretation changes what is called for in response: not retaliation, not pity, but love — a response that addresses the fear rather than the form of the attack. The Course states: "The Holy Spirit interprets all communication as either love or a call for love."

This principle is closely related to the work of [Bernie Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md), who found that loving responses to patients — regardless of their behaviour — consistently produced better outcomes. [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s forgiveness work similarly reinterprets angry or hurtful behaviour as a sign of someone's pain rather than evidence of their character.

## Fear and the Body

The Course teaches that fear is the foundation of the body's existence: the body was made by the ego as a proof of separation, and fear is the emotional atmosphere in which separate selves live. Illness, in ACIM's framework, is fear made visible — the body expressing the mind's belief in its vulnerability and guilt.

This parallels [fight-or-flight response](fight-or-flight-response.md) in the mind-body literature: chronic fear activates the sympathetic nervous system, suppresses immunity, and generates the physiological conditions for disease. The Course agrees with the direction of this causal arrow (fear → illness) but locates the primary cause in the mind's metaphysical beliefs, not in the nervous system itself.

Note: mainstream medicine and [psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md) document the fear-illness relationship empirically, within a naturalistic framework. ACIM frames the same relationship metaphysically. Both converge on the clinical importance of reducing fear states.

## Love and Healing

The Course's position on healing follows directly from the two-emotion framework: healing is the restoration of love. Not physical restoration (that may or may not follow) but the mind's restoration to its natural state of love and extension.

"The holy instant is a time of love." When the mind is in a holy instant — fully present, ego temporarily suspended — it is in love, and from love healing flows naturally. This is why forgiveness is healing in ACIM: forgiveness removes fear (the mind's projection of guilt onto the brother), and in the absence of fear, love — and healing — are natural.

Siegel's finding that love serves as a measurable health variable (McClelland's IgA data, the Medalie-Goldbourt study) provides empirical grounding for what the Course asserts metaphysically: love heals. See [attitude and health](attitude-and-health.md).

## Comparison with Other Frameworks

**Borysenko**: In [*Minding the Body, Mending the Mind*](../sources/minding-the-body.md), Borysenko uses the two-emotion framework explicitly, citing the Course as a source: "There are really only two emotions — love and fear." She treats it as a practical organising principle for therapeutic work.

**Dossey**: [Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md)'s research on love as an immune mechanism ([*Healing Words*](../sources/healing-words.md)) — McClelland's Mother Teresa documentary, helper's high, IgA responses — provides empirical support for the physiological reality of love as a health variable. ACIM would say: of course — love is the only real emotion and the only real power.

**Myss**: [Myss's](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) framework of [woundology](woundology.md) — the use of wounds (i.e., fear-states) as social currency — describes the same dynamic from the perspective of energy depletion. Holding fear in the form of unresolved grievances depletes the [cellular energy bank](cellular-energy-bank.md). The Course's prescription — forgive, release fear, return to love — addresses the same depletion.

## Critique

The two-emotion framework is a simplification that some find useful and others find reductive:

- It may undervalue the complexity and validity of human emotional experience — grief, for example, is not obviously "just fear."
- Applied rigidly, it can feel invalidating: "you are only afraid" may not be what someone in acute grief needs to hear.
- The framework assumes a non-dual metaphysics in which love is the only real substance; this is a philosophical position, not a psychological finding.

The Course itself tends to apply the framework gently, not as a club: it acknowledges that fear is the real experience of the dreaming mind and does not ask for its denial, only for its eventual healing.
