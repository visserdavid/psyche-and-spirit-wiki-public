# Love as an Act of Will

**Summary**: Peck's redefinition of love as "the will to extend one's self for the purpose of nurturing one's own or another's spiritual growth" — deliberately excluding feeling, cathexis, dependency, and self-sacrifice, all of which he treats as related but distinct phenomena frequently mistaken for love.

**Sources**:
- [Peck, M. S. (1978). *The road less traveled: A new psychology of love, traditional values, and spiritual growth*. Simon & Schuster.](../sources/the-road-less-traveled.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

---

## Definition

Peck defines love as **"the will to extend one's self for the purpose of nurturing one's own or another's spiritual growth."** Four features of this definition matter to him:

1. Love is defined by its **purpose** (growth), not by its feeling-tone
2. Self-love and love of others are **not opposites** — genuine capacity for one requires the other
3. Love **requires effort** — extending the self's limits demands exertion, not just desire
4. Love is a matter of **choice**, not simply an emotion that arrives or departs unbidden — "love is as love does"

On this basis, Peck spends most of the section distinguishing love from several phenomena regularly confused with it.

## What Love Is Not

### Falling in Love
Peck argues that the experience of "falling in love" — the collapse of ego boundaries and the temporary sense of merger with another person — is not itself an act of love. He traces its origin to the infant's early, boundary-less fusion with the mother, arguing that romantic falling-in-love echoes and briefly re-creates that pre-differentiated state. Because it is involuntary, effortless, and not directed at the other person's growth (it typically seeks to end loneliness or satisfy a reproductive drive), it fails his definition on every count. It nonetheless serves an evolutionary function: it makes people available to begin the real, effortful work of loving once "the boundaries snap back into place" and the relationship's difficulties become visible.

### Cathexis
Cathexis — psychiatric shorthand for emotional investment or attachment — is necessary for love (one cannot love what one has not cathected) but insufficient: a person can cathect a hobby, a stock portfolio, or a pet without any commitment to that object's growth. Peck argues that mistaking cathexis for love explains many failed marriages and misdirected devotion (a parent who cathects a child as an extension of the parent's own ego, rather than loving the child's separate, developing identity — a pattern he calls **narcissism**).

### Dependency
Clinical dependency — an inability to tolerate being alone, organized around desperately needing to be cared for — is, in Peck's account, close to the opposite of love: parasitic rather than nurturing, contracting rather than growth-oriented. He traces its origin to inconsistent or absent parental love in childhood, producing adults who cling to relationships not to nurture the other's growth but to fill a persistent, unfillable emptiness.

### Self-Sacrifice
Ostensibly loving behavior — a minister's constant availability to his family, a woman's endurance of a mistreating husband — can, on inspection, be organized around maintaining the giver's self-image (as loving, as superior, as a martyr) rather than the recipient's actual growth. Peck's clinical case of masochistic "devotion" links this pattern to the covert pursuit of moral superiority and revenge, not love.

## Attention as the Chief Work of Love

Peck argues that since love is an act of will, it requires **work**, and that work takes the specific form of **attention** — most concretely, the discipline of genuinely listening. He gives extended clinical illustration of parents who fail (and succeed) at listening to children at different developmental stages, and argues that a lecturer's or therapist's felt sense of exhaustion after real listening is itself a marker that the discipline is being exercised rather than merely performed.

## Risk

Because extending the self's boundaries is inherently uncertain, genuine love requires risking loss, independence, commitment, and confrontation. Peck illustrates each with clinical case material: a withdrawn woman who avoids cathecting any living thing to avoid the risk of loss; a young man's abrupt departure from a prestigious boarding school as a first exercise of independent selfhood; a patient ("Rachel") whose inability to trust the durability of her therapist's commitment manifests as sexual frigidity, resolved only once she tests and confirms that commitment.

## Love and Psychotherapy

Peck extends the definition into clinical practice directly: he argues the effective agent in psychotherapy is the therapist's genuine, willed extension toward the patient's growth — a claim he distinguishes sharply from the classical psychoanalytic treatment of a patient's loving feelings toward the therapist as "transference" to be defused rather than a real relationship to be honored. He argues equally sharply against the therapist ever consummating erotic attraction to a patient, on the grounds that doing so would substitute the therapist's own need for the patient's growth — the same distinction he draws between genuine love and dependency or cathexis generally.

## Relationship to Discipline

Love, in Peck's schema, supplies the motivating energy that [discipline](discipline-four-tools.md) requires — without love (of self or others), a person has no reason to accept the difficulty of delayed gratification, responsibility, truth-telling, or balance. Conversely, love without discipline collapses into indulgence: Peck's account of parents who give children everything except structure argues that permissiveness is a failure of love, not an expression of it.

## Critique

**Definitional narrowness**: Peck's insistence that love is only will and never feeling has been criticized (including by later commentators on the book) as overcorrecting against a real target (the romantic myth of love-as-feeling) at the cost of excluding compassion, tenderness, and spontaneous care that most people would still recognize as loving.

**Clinical rather than empirical grounding**: As with the rest of the book, the framework rests on Peck's own case histories rather than controlled research; it is best read as clinical wisdom literature rather than a tested psychological theory.

**Parallel with mainstream attachment theory**: Peck's account of falling-in-love as regression to infantile boundarylessness anticipates, in impressionistic form, later empirically grounded work on adult attachment styles — but Peck does not engage that literature, which was still emerging when he wrote.
