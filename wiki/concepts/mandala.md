# Mandala

**Summary**: A circular image with a centre — spontaneously produced across cultures as a symbol of psychic wholeness and the Self; for Jung the primary visual expression of the individuation process; for Myss the structural precedent for the Archetypal Wheel.

## Definition

Mandala (Sanskrit: *maṇḍala*, circle) is the term Jung adopted for circular images with a defined centre that arise spontaneously across cultures, religions, and in the drawings of modern patients as a symbol of psychic wholeness.

The mandala is the primary visual symbol of [the Self](the-self.md) — the archetype of totality. When the psyche needs to organise itself under conditions of internal conflict or disintegration, the mandala arises as a spontaneous ordering structure.

> "I knew that in finding the mandala as an expression of the self I had attained what was for me the ultimate."
>
> — Jung, *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (referenced in [(Jung, 1969)](../sources/collected-works-9-1.md))

## The Quaternity

The most common mandala structure is the **quaternity** — fourfold division: the circle divided by a cross, or by four equal sections, or containing four figures, animals, or elements. This fourfold structure reflects the psyche's tendency to orient itself through opposites:

- The horizontal axis: consciousness and unconsciousness
- The vertical axis: spirit (above) and matter (below)

The quaternity does not represent four things but the *completion of the opposites* — the resolution of tension between pairs of contraries into a living whole. It appears across traditions: the Christian cross, the Buddhist wheel of life, the Hindu yantra, the Navajo sand painting, the alchemical squared circle.

## Functions of the Mandala

### Protective / Defensive

The mandala creates a **temenos** — a sacred bounded space that protects against disintegration. In its original use (Buddhist and Hindu tantric traditions), the mandala was drawn as a protective circle into which dangerous forces could be safely invoked and contained. Psychologically, the spontaneous drawing of a mandala in crisis serves the same function: it creates an inner boundary around the centre, preventing the fragmentation of consciousness.

### Expressive / Constructive

The mandala also **expresses** what the psyche is moving toward — the wholeness that is not yet achieved but already felt as potential. Patients in the process of individuation produce mandalas at turning points in the therapeutic work: the mandala appears when the psyche is on the cusp of integration.

## Mandala in World Traditions

Jung traces the mandala across:

- **Tibetan Buddhism**: The mandala as a visualisation tool for meditation; complex, iconographically precise; used in initiation and healing rituals
- **Hindu yantra**: Geometric mandala as a focus for worship and meditation
- **Christian iconography**: The halo, the rose window, the radiant glory, the Chi-Rho symbol
- **Navajo sand painting**: Created and then destroyed; the mandala as a healing ritual
- **Medieval alchemy**: The *rotundum* (the round) as the prima materia of transformation; the squared circle as the goal

The universality of the mandala is Jung's primary evidence that it arises from the [collective unconscious](collective-unconscious.md) rather than cultural transmission: the same structural form appears in traditions with no historical connection.

## Brother Klaus and the Wheel Vision

A central case in CW 9.1: Brother Nicholas of Flüe (15th-century Swiss mystic) received a terrifying vision of a face that he spent years assimilating. He eventually gave it form as a wheel-mandala painted on the wall of his cell (the Sachseln Trinity picture). Jung reads this as the mandala functioning as a psychic container: the overwhelming raw experience of the archetype could only be metabolised through the ordering structure of the circular symbol.

## Mandala as Process

Jung and his patients produced mandalas throughout the [individuation](individuation.md) process. The key observation: the mandalas change over time, reflecting the evolving state of inner integration. Early mandalas may be fragmented, asymmetrical, or dominated by a few elements; later mandalas tend toward greater harmony, symmetry, and completeness. The mandala is thus not only a symbol but a **mirror of the individuation process**.

In *A Study in the Process of Individuation* (CW 9.1), Jung traces ten paintings by "Miss X" over several years, showing how the progressive development of mandala imagery tracks the psychological integration work.

## The Archetypal Wheel as Mandala

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s [Archetypal Wheel](archetypal-wheel.md) in [*Sacred Contracts*](../sources/sacred-contracts.md) has an explicitly mandala-like structure: a circle divided into twelve houses, with the twelve archetypal companions arranged around a centre. Myss draws explicitly on this Jungian inheritance: the wheel is a map of the soul's contracted territory, and its mandala structure reflects the totality that the Sacred Contract encompasses.

The **eighth chakra** that Myss introduces above the crown — the seat of the Sacred Contract — corresponds structurally to the mandala's centre: the point of orientation around which the entire psychic life is organised.

## Mandala in Healing Practice

Contemporary applications of mandala drawing in therapeutic and medical contexts draw on Jung's observation that the spontaneous production of circular images promotes psychological stabilisation. Mandala-based art therapy is used in oncology, trauma treatment, and anxiety management — not as a direct physical treatment but as a means of accessing the psyche's self-organizing capacity.

## The Diamond Body Mandala (*The Mythic Image*)

[Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md) in [*The Mythic Image*](../sources/the-mythic-image.md) presents and analyses a specific Tantric mandala that appears at the culmination of the Kundalini ascent: the **Diamond Body mandala** (Figure 349 in *The Mythic Image*, a Tibetan vajrayana example). Campbell's description:

> "The mandala shows the state of one who has emerged from contemplation of the absolute state. The central dorje (diamond/thunderbolt) symbolizes the union of masculine and feminine. The world of illusions has vanished. The energy has gathered at the center. The four dorjes at the gates of the courtyard are meant to indicate that the life energy is streaming inwards rather than outwards toward objects."

This mandala of the Diamond Body corresponds to what Christian tradition calls the *corpus glorificationis* — the resurrection body; and what Tibetan Buddhism calls *rigpa* — the recognition of mind's nature. Campbell identifies this as the same state described by Dante in the final canto of the *Paradiso* ("my will and desire were rolled, like a wheel that is moved evenly, by the Love that moves the sun and the other stars") and by Meister Eckhart in his identification of the soul with the Godhead.

Campbell also cites Jung's observation that two women patients independently produced mandala drawings showing central spirals described as snakes coiling around a stone — the classical image of Kundalini at Muladhara — without any conscious knowledge of the Tantric tradition. This spontaneous production of Kundalini imagery in Western patients is Campbell's strongest evidence that the chakra map and the mandala converge on the same archetypal substrate.

## Critique

**The universality claim**: While mandala-like circular images appear widely across cultures, the claim that they all express the *same archetype* (the Self) imposes a Jungian interpretive framework on traditions that may understand their own circular symbols very differently. A Buddhist practitioner's relationship to a mandala is not the same as Jung's patient's spontaneous drawing.

**The clinical evidence**: That mandala drawing has therapeutic effects is supported by some clinical observation, but controlled research on mandala art therapy shows modest, inconsistent results. The dramatic claims about mandalas as Self-expressions are not validated by the clinical evidence base.

## Mandala in Art History (*Man and His Symbols*)

[Aniela Jaffé](../thinkers/aniela-jaffe.md)'s section in [(Jung et al., 1964)](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) extends the mandala analysis into the full history of art and architecture:

**Architecture as mandala**: Rome (*Roma quadrata*), Washington D.C., Angkor Wat, and the ideal city Palmanova (Italy, 1593) all follow mandala-based ground plans — circular or radially symmetrical, with a sacred or civic centre at the intersection point. Jaffé argues that city planning as mandala reflects the psyche's need to project its interior ordering structure onto the lived environment. [Karl Kerényi](../thinkers/karl-kerenyi.md), in his Prolegomena to [*Essays on a Science of Mythology*](../sources/essays-on-a-science-of-mythology.md), traces the same *Roma quadrata* material in greater depth, alongside comparative Etruscan and West African circle-and-square city-founding rites and a Tibetan Buddhist mandala parallel (via Heinrich Zimmer) — treating quadripartite city-founding as itself a *world-founding* mandala pattern that precedes the mandala's later clinical use as a symbol of the Self.

**Cathedral rose windows**: Medieval Gothic rose windows are mandalas in stone and glass — circular, centred, radiating. They function as containers of the Self image within the collective religious space.

**Modern art's unconscious mandala search**: The history of modern abstract art can be read, Jaffé argues, as an unconscious search for the mandala in a civilisation that has lost its shared centre. Kandinsky, Klee, Mondrian, and Pollock each approach the circle motif differently — but the circle returns persistently in their work as an autonomous symbol rather than a deliberate choice. This parallels what happens in the dreams of modern patients: the mandala arises spontaneously when the psyche needs to reorganise itself.

**UFOs**: Jung's essay on flying saucers (1958) proposed that the circular, luminous disc reports from the 1950s onward represent a collective mandala projection — the psyche of a disoriented civilisation projecting the wholeness symbol onto the sky.
