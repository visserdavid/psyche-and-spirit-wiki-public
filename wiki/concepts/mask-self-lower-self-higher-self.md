# Mask Self, Lower Self, and Higher Self

**Summary**: Brennan's tripartite model of the psyche, introduced in *Light Emerging* (1993) — the Higher Self (divine core), the Lower Self (the separated, pleasure-seeking part that acts from the illusion of disconnection), and the Mask Self (the protective facade that denies both wound and lower self while maintaining a socially acceptable front).

**Sources**:
- [Brennan, B. A. (1993). *Light emerging: The journey of personal healing*. Bantam Books.](../sources/light-emerging.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

---

## Overview

In Chapter 1 of *[Light Emerging](../sources/light-emerging.md)*, [Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md) introduces a three-part model of the psyche to explain why healing is not simply a matter of gaining insight or making better choices. The model identifies three aspects of the self operating simultaneously, each with a different quality of consciousness, intention, and relationship to the wound:

1. **Higher Self** (divine core, core being) — the source of wisdom, love, and creative energy; directly connected to the divine; expressed through the [core star](core-star.md)
2. **Lower Self** (separated self) — the part that believes in separation from the divine and others; acts from that belief; the source of "negative pleasure"
3. **Mask Self** — the protective facade; denies both the wound and the lower self; maintains a front of social acceptability; the source of most interpersonal confusion

All three are present in every person simultaneously. The balance among them shifts moment by moment, and which aspect is "speaking" determines the quality of one's actions and words.

## The Higher Self

The Higher Self is not the ego's idealized self-image but the divine essence — what Brennan elsewhere calls the [core star](core-star.md) and the "core being":

> "Your Higher Self is your creative self, your divine self, your God self. It is the source of your wisdom and love and courage. It is the fountainhead of your creations."

The Higher Self was never damaged by biographical experience. It is always present but increasingly obscured as the mask self develops in childhood. Its expressions in adult life are moments of authentic kindness, creative inspiration, love given without agenda, and courage in the face of fear — "the act of kindness, the expression of love, the moment of friendship that happens before you think."

At the energy level, the Higher Self corresponds to the core star and the higher auric levels (sixth and seventh layers).

## The Lower Self

The Lower Self is the part of the psyche that has accepted the illusion of separation from the divine and from others, and acts from this belief:

> "The lower self is the part that has forgotten. It is the part of your psyche that believes it is separated from the world and acts that way."

Critically, the Lower Self is not the same as the Mask Self — it does **not** deny what it is:

> "The lower self is not in denial. The lower self says 'I don't care' and means it. It is not pretending. It does not care about the world of separation. It enjoys its pleasure and wants what it wants."

The Lower Self's defining feature is **negative pleasure** — the direct experience of pleasure from releasing pent-up pain, even destructively. When a person "lashes out" and feels relief afterward, or indulges a craving despite knowing it harms them, or enjoys another's failure — this is the Lower Self's negative pleasure. The energy movement itself is pleasurable; the pleasure is real, not imagined. This is why changing lower-self patterns is not simply a matter of understanding.

At the energy level, the Lower Self corresponds to the second and third auric layers in their blocked, stagnant expressions — particularly the clouds of compressed "DOR" (deadly orgone energy) that Wilhelm Reich described.

## The Mask Self

The Mask Self is the protective facade built to manage the gap between the wound (which the psyche refuses to acknowledge) and the social world (which must be navigated):

> "The mask self is an attempt to be right. An attempt to express in such a way that the world will not reject you."

The Mask Self has three defining characteristics:

**1. Denial**: It denies the wound, the lower self, and the negative pleasure. From the mask's perspective, these do not exist — "It is all someone else's fault." This denial is structurally necessary; the mask would collapse if it admitted what it was covering.

**2. Blame and projection**: Since the mask cannot acknowledge its own wound or lower self, it externalises responsibility. Pain is always caused by others. This is not conscious manipulation; the mask genuinely does not perceive its own contribution.

**3. Proving and performing**: The mask's core project is to prove something — that it is good enough, loveable, intelligent, spiritual, or whatever quality the wound has called into question. The mask performs this proof continuously, at great energy cost. The energy spent on performance is unavailable for genuine creative expression.

> "The mask self puts on a certain face for the world — according to its beliefs about what will make the world accept it and what it feels the world says it should be."

## The Original Wound and the Three Selves

Brennan argues that the three-self structure arises from a universal developmental process:

1. **Core connection in early childhood**: Young children are relatively close to the core — they experience wonder, aliveness, and direct connection to life.
2. **Wounding experiences**: Inevitably, the child encounters pain, abandonment, shaming, loss. Connection to the core fades.
3. **The mask's development**: The psyche creates the mask to replace the lost core connection — but the mask can never succeed because it is a substitute, not the real thing. The effort to maintain the mask is exhausting and ultimately self-defeating.
4. **The lower self crystallises**: Alongside the mask, the part of the psyche that has truly accepted separation (and the negative pleasure of acting from separation) solidifies.

The result: the adult carries all three simultaneously, with the mask dominating social life, the lower self active in private or triggered moments, and the Higher Self appearing only in unguarded moments of authentic expression.

## The Role of Intention

Brennan locates the three selves in the [haric level](hara-level.md) as well as the auric level — each aspect of the psyche operates from a different underlying intention:

- **Higher Self**: intentions aligned with truth, love, and the life task
- **Lower Self**: intentions aligned with pleasure and avoidance of pain; honest about this
- **Mask Self**: intentions are double — a stated intention (appearing good) and a hidden intention (maintaining the illusion, avoiding the wound)

The quality of any interaction is determined by which self is speaking and which intention is active. The same words can carry very different intentions:

> "You can say the same words and mean three different things. Your higher self says 'I love you' and means exactly that. Your mask self says 'I love you' meaning I will never challenge your illusion of yourself. Your lower self says 'I love you' to the degree I will allow you to love me."

## Relationship to Illness

The mask self's chronic suppression of the wound — and of the energy that the wound contains — is a central contributor to illness:

> "The habitual defense system creates distortions in the energy field that cause disease."

The mask requires enormous energy to maintain. This energy is drawn from the [cellular energy bank](cellular-energy-bank.md) (Myss's parallel concept) and from the natural flow of the [core star](core-star.md) through the auric field. Over time, the mask's energy debt creates:
- Stagnations and compressions in the auric field ([energy blocks and defense systems](energy-blocks-defense-systems.md))
- Distortions that, if sustained, lead to physical illness in the body regions corresponding to the blocked chakras and auric layers

Healing, in Brennan's framework, involves progressively contacting the wound (bypassing the mask), integrating the lower self (acknowledging its existence and its negative pleasure without indulging it), and allowing the Higher Self to emerge.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

**Myss's woundology**: Myss's concept of [woundology](woundology.md) — using wound identity as social currency — corresponds closely to the mask self's strategy: the wound is simultaneously denied (the mask won't admit it) and used (the mask draws on it for manipulation). The mask's use of wound energy without acknowledging the wound is a precise description of woundology.

**Jung's shadow**: Jung's [shadow](shadow.md) encompasses both what Brennan calls the lower self (the explicitly destructive, pleasure-seeking impulses) and some of what the mask self denies. However, Brennan's model distinguishes the denied lower self (shadow in Jung's sense) from the active facade (the mask/persona), giving each a more specific operational description.

**Keleman's somatic patterns**: Keleman's [somatic distress patterns](somatic-distress-patterns.md) — the four body shapes (rigid, compact, swollen, collapsed) — encode the long-term effects of mask maintenance in the body's structure. The mask self's energy expenditure creates specific somatic organisations that Keleman maps at the pulsatory level.

**Hillman's critique of depth psychology**: Hillman would note that the "lower self" concept, like many depth psychology constructs, risks pathologising what may be a legitimate soul-level imperative expressed in an uncomfortable form.

## Critique

**Dualistic structure**: The model risks creating a hierarchy that denigrates the lower self and overvalues the higher — which Brennan herself acknowledges. The lower self is not purely negative; its directness and its capacity for negative pleasure contain a form of authenticity that the mask, for all its appearance of virtue, lacks.

**The mask self's function**: Psychoanalytic traditions would note that what Brennan calls the mask self is doing essential developmental and protective work. Defenses are not merely obstacles to healing; they are the psyche's best solution to an unbearable situation, and premature removal can be destabilising.

**Verification**: The three-self model is a clinical phenomenology, not a neuroscientific description. It is a map for working with energy field phenomena and behavioural patterns — its value is pragmatic rather than empirical.
