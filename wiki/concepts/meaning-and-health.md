# Meaning and Health

**Summary**: The claim, central to Larry Dossey's *Meaning & Medicine* (1991), that perceived meanings — how patients interpret events in their lives — are biologically active agents that enter the body, alter physiology, and can cause illness or catalyse healing; grounded in David Bohm's soma-significance theory and corroborated by psychoneuroimmunological research.

**Sources**:
- [Bohm, D. (1980). *Wholeness and the implicate order*. Routledge & Kegan Paul.](../sources/wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)
- [Dossey, L. (1991). *Meaning & medicine: A doctor's tales of breakthrough and healing*. Bantam Books.](../sources/meaning-and-medicine.md)
- [Siegel, B. S. (1986). *Love, medicine and miracles: Lessons learned about self-healing from a surgeon's experience with exceptional patients*. Harper & Row.](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

## The Central Claim

Dossey's thesis is that perceived meaning is a primary biological variable — as consequential for health outcomes as cholesterol level or blood pressure, and in many documented cases more consequential. "Meaning" is used in two related senses throughout the book:

1. **The significance a patient assigns to an event**: What does this illness mean? What does this job mean? What does this Monday morning mean? The interpretation determines the physiological response.
2. **The narrative context of a life**: The pattern of biographical experience (love, rejection, entrapment, purpose) that forms the substrate in which illness develops or health persists.

Both senses converge on the claim that the body is a **meaning meter** — registering the meanings attributed to events and responding to them biologically, regardless of whether those meanings are accurate or not.

## Bohm's Soma-Significance Theory

Dossey grounds the claim in David Bohm's theory of soma-significance, presented in a direct communication from Bohm:

- **Soma** (from the Greek for "body") refers to the physical, material aspect of experience.
- **Significance** (from the Latin *significans* — meaning, pointing toward) refers to the mental, conscious aspect.
- Bohm proposes that soma and significance are not two separate substances interacting (the classical mind-body problem) but two aspects of a unified whole, connected through **meaning** as a third, bridging entity.

The analogy Bohm uses: a magnet has two poles (north and south), but those poles are encompassed by a single magnetic field — neither the poles nor their apparent separation captures the whole. Similarly, mind, body, and meaning form a whole in which the apparent separation of mental and physical dissolves. Dossey presents this as a direct communication from Bohm rather than a quotation from his published work; it is consistent with, but distinct from, the "projections of a common ground" formulation Bohm develops in *[Wholeness and the Implicate Order](../sources/wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)* (see [Implicate Order](implicate-order.md)).

Implication: meaning changes soma directly, not through an intermediate causal step. When Frank (a patient with suspected heart attack) changes what his chest pain means — from "heart attack" to "muscle ache I can manage" — his heart rate changes measurably in real time. The meaning change is the physiological change; they are two descriptions of the same event.

Bohm's theory is complemented by Alfred North Whitehead's view (functionings of nature not divided from mind) and Gregory Bateson's (mind not confined to brains but a network of patterns across organism and environment).

## Key Evidence from *Meaning & Medicine*

### Black Monday Syndrome

Heart attacks cluster on Monday mornings at 9 a.m. This pattern is documented across 14 published studies (synthesised by Dr. James Muller, Harvard Medical School). Strokes show the same weekly and daily pattern. The 1972 Massachusetts study found that job dissatisfaction was the strongest predictor of first heart attack among men under 50 — stronger than cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking, or diabetes. Men experiencing no traditional risk factors still had heart attacks at this rate; some with all four risk factors did not.

Dossey argues the mechanism is the meaning of Monday: dread of returning to a meaningless or despised job activates the fight-or-flight cascade — including platelet aggregation, adrenaline surges, arterial constriction — in ways that precipitate cardiac events. The heart cannot distinguish between a physical threat and a meaning-laden one.

### The Giving-Up, Given-Up Complex

George Engel (University of Rochester) spent six years collecting 170 press reports of sudden death and analysed the psychological circumstances. The dominant context was the **giving-up, given-up complex** — the feeling of being trapped with no exit, neither able to fight nor flee. The distribution: 21% occurred following collapse or death of a close person; 20% during acute grief; 27% during danger or threat of injury; and 13% during reunion, triumph, or moments of intense positive emotion.

Engel's model was elaborated by Joel Dimsdale (Harvard Medical School): psychological stress → individual's interpretation → meaning assigned → coping failure → ANS activation → cardiac event. The key step is the interpretation — the meaning — not the event itself.

This directly parallels Cannon's [voodoo death](voodoo-death.md) research: the mechanism in both cases is the meaning assigned to the situation.

### Bereavment and Immune Suppression

Steven Schleifer (Mount Sinai) studied 15 men whose wives had breast cancer. In the year following the wife's death, the men's lymphocyte proliferation declined dramatically. The bereaved men's immune systems were not merely stressed — they were measuring the loss of meaning (the centrality of the partner's presence in their psychological world) and responding with a corresponding reduction in defensive capability. Australian researchers replicated this finding; Ohio State students with high loneliness scores had lowest NK cell activity.

### Job Satisfaction and Immunity

Ronald Glaser's studies at Ohio State: exam stress reduced NK cell activity in medical students. William Ruberman's study of 2,320 post-MI male patients: those with high life stress and social isolation (both correlated with low education and job dissatisfaction) had four times the mortality risk. These findings point to social meaning — belonging, competence, dignity — as immune-relevant variables.

### Health Self-Perception as Predictor

Ellen Idler (Rutgers) and Stanislav Kasl (Yale) synthesised five studies involving 23,000 people ages 19–94. Their finding: a person's self-reported opinion of their own health was a more powerful predictor of whether they would die in the next decade than any combination of physician exam findings, laboratory tests, or behavioural risk factors. People who said they were healthy — even when tests said otherwise — tended to live longer. The mechanism is unknown; possibilities include that self-perception captures information not captured by tests, or that positive self-meaning functions as a self-fulfilling belief.

### The Placebo Response as Meaning

Dossey treats the placebo effect not as a nuisance variable to be controlled but as the clearest demonstration of meaning's biological power. A patient died from anaphylactic shock after swallowing a sugar pill he believed contained penicillin (to which he was allergic). The meaning of the pill — not its chemistry — killed him. Dossey argues that calling this "just placebo" minimises what is actually a profound demonstration of meaning becoming matter.

Shepard Siegel's rat experiments: rats injected with heroin in their home cage developed tolerance; moved to a new cage and given the same dose, they died. Environment-as-meaning was part of the pharmacological mechanism.

## The Three Eras of Medicine as Context

Dossey's [Three Eras of Medicine](prayer-and-healing.md) framework provides the theoretical scaffolding:

- **Era I**: Mechanical/local medicine — the body as machine; meaning not a factor
- **Era II**: Mind-body medicine — mind affects body within the same person; the domain of PNI, biofeedback, placebo, and meaning-as-medicine
- **Era III**: Non-local medicine — consciousness not confined to individual brain/body; prayer, therapeutic touch, intuitive diagnosis at distance

The meaning-and-health thesis primarily operates in Era II, but the evidence for non-local healing (Byrd's prayer study, Myss's intuitive diagnosis) suggests it extends into Era III: meanings can operate at a distance, beyond the boundaries of an individual's awareness.

## Relation to Biography Is Biology

Dossey's framework is the closest published external corroboration of Myss's [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md) principle. Where Myss frames the encoding of biography in energetic terms (chakra depletion), Dossey frames it in clinical narrative and cites the psychoneuroimmunological research that provides the biochemical mechanism. Norman Cousins's phrase — "belief becomes biology" — appears in Dossey's discussion of [voodoo death](voodoo-death.md) and summarises the bridge between the two frameworks.

Both Myss and Dossey identify:
- Shame, helplessness, and entrapment as particularly destructive meanings
- Love, meaning, and connection as particularly protective ones
- The critical variable as not the event itself but the interpretation

Dossey adds the Black Monday data, the job dissatisfaction research, and the spontaneous remission cases — empirical anchors that support the general claim at a population level.

## The Body-as-Music Alternative to the Body-as-Machine

Dossey's most original philosophical contribution is the body-as-music metaphor, offered as an alternative to the body-as-machine metaphor that he traces to the Industrial Revolution and identifies as the source of modern medicine's difficulty incorporating meaning.

Susumu Ohno's DNA music: assigning musical notes to DNA nucleotide bases (cytosine = mi, adenine = sol, guanine = ti, thymine = do), Ohno transcribed organisms' genomes into musical compositions. Listeners unfamiliar with the source compared them to Bach, Brahms, and Chopin. Running the process in reverse, Ohno found that Chopin's Funeral March translates into the DNA of a cancer gene. Dossey draws the implication: the body contains melodies. Meaning — in this framework — is not something imposed on the body from outside but something the body itself is made of.

This metaphor dissolves the mind-body problem differently from Bohm's field analogy: if the body is music, then meaning is the score from which it plays — and changing the score changes what the body does. The Fishskin Boy, Michael the leukemia patient (who used a computer-as-body metaphor to sail through chemotherapy without side effects), and Stephen the truck driver (who visualised steel pipe flowing with blood and restored perfusion to his nearly-amputated foot) are all playing a version of this instrument.

## Critique

**The strongest version of the claim**: The epidemiological evidence for meaning as a health variable is substantial. Job dissatisfaction, social isolation, and perceived health status all predict mortality in large longitudinal studies. The PNI mechanisms by which psychological states affect immune function are well-established in mainstream science. Engel's sudden death research and the bereavement mortality data are robust. Dossey is not asserting anything the epidemiological literature does not support.

**The weaker claims**: Some of Dossey's evidence is anecdotal or from small studies not independently replicated: the Fishskin Boy case is a case report; the McClelland healer experiment was unpublished; the Siegel cancer finding was subsequently replicated and debated. The "body as music" section is speculative — Ohno's DNA/music work is intriguing but does not establish that musical structures in DNA causally affect health.

**The direction-of-causation problem**: Studies showing correlations between psychological states and health outcomes (positive or negative) do not establish that meaning causes illness or healing. People who are already ill may have more negative meanings; people who are already healthy may find more meaning. Longitudinal studies (Thomas, Vaillant, Alameda County) help but do not resolve this entirely.

**The body-as-music critique**: Metaphors carry implicit claims. Dossey is aware that the machine metaphor has harmful clinical consequences; the music metaphor may have its own distortions — romanticising illness, implying that the patient's "wrong music" caused their cancer, introducing a different route to blame.

**Note**: Mainstream medicine accepts the evidence that psychological states affect health outcomes, particularly for cardiovascular disease and immune function. The specific claim that meaning is a primary causal agent for most illness — independent of genetic, environmental, and microbial factors — is not the consensus position.

## Siegel's Contribution: The Will to Live as Meaning

[Bernie Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md) in [*Love, Medicine and Miracles*](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md) (1986) frames the will to live as a meaning variable — and argues that this variable can be assessed, strengthened, and made clinically actionable.

**The four questions as meaning assessment**: Siegel's four diagnostic questions (see [Exceptional Cancer Patients](exceptional-cancer-patients.md)) are instruments for revealing the patient's operative meaning of their illness and life:
- "Do you want to live to be 100?" — tests the positive meaning of the future
- "What happened in the year or two before your illness?" — recovers the lost meaning that preceded the illness
- "What does your illness mean to you?" — surfaces the meaning the patient attributes to the diagnosis
- "Why do you need your illness?" — reveals the meaning the illness is creating (permission to rest, to receive love, to exit a destructive situation)

These questions make Dossey's general claim — that meaning is the active biological variable — practically actionable in the consulting room.

**LeShan's loss of meaning connection**: [Lawrence LeShan](../thinkers/lawrence-leshan.md)'s three decades of research with cancer patients identified a specific meaning-collapse as the precipitating psychological event: the loss of the person's "life meaning connection" — the one relationship, career, or cause that made life feel real. When that connection broke, quiet desperation followed, and cancer appeared within months to years. The mechanism is what Dossey calls the "giving-up, given-up complex": hopelessness as a physiological instruction to lower biological defences. [(Siegel, 1986)](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

**Love as meaning-carrier**: Siegel's central claim is that love is the most powerful meaning that medicine can deploy. Norman Cousins's phrase — "belief becomes biology" — captures the general principle; Siegel's specific addition is that the belief most reliably associated with healing is love: love received, love expressed, love given unconditionally. McClelland's IgA research (Harvard) and Medalie and Goldbourt's Israeli angina study establish that experienced love predicts immune and cardiovascular health better than any standard clinical variable. Love is meaning made measurable.
