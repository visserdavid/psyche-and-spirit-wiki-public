## Overview

Medical intuition, as Myss defines and practises it, is the ability to read the human [energy field](energy-anatomy.md) to identify where illness is developing, what biographical stress patterns have contributed to it, and what the patient needs to understand in order to heal. It is distinct from psychic reading in that it is clinically applied — oriented toward health — and systematically compared against medical diagnosis.

Myss did not choose to develop this ability and did not receive training in it. It emerged spontaneously in autumn 1982 and became a sustained clinical skill through her collaboration with [C. Norman Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) from 1984 onwards.

## How Myss Practises Medical Intuition

Myss's standard method is **remote evaluation**: she is given only a patient's name and age, then allows impressions to arise in a state of focused but detached receptivity. She describes the process as follows:

- She takes no patient history and prefers no visual contact, which she finds interfering.
- Impressions arrive as images, symbols, sensations, and biographical information.
- She reads through the seven chakras in sequence, noting which energy centres show weakness or disruption.
- She reports both the physical location of energetic disruption (which she cross-references to specific organs) and the biographical/emotional context — what life experiences, attitudes, or relationship patterns are contributing to the depletion.

A key property of the process: the impressions carry no emotional charge for her. She describes it as the difference between looking at a stranger's photograph (no attachment) versus a family member's (charged with feeling). The absence of emotional connection is her indicator that the impression is authentic rather than projected.

## Development Through Collaboration

The collaboration with [Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) was the crucible in which Myss's skill matured. Shealy tested her by giving her patients with known diagnoses; her evaluations were compared against clinical findings. Over ten years she identified stress patterns in hundreds of patients that were verified by Shealy against medical records and patient self-report.

She also worked with [Christiane Northrup](../thinkers/christiane-northrup.md) from 1990, expanding the application of medical intuition into gynecology and women's health.

## Key Principles of the Skill

Myss articulates several operating principles for medical intuition:

1. **Stay with the first impression**: The first image that arises is the most reliable. The desire to check whether a person *wants* to hear a particular reading introduces distortion.

2. **Objectivity is essential**: The journalist's discipline of detachment — separating impression from interpretation — is the foundational skill. Impressions contaminated by personal investment (the need to prove one's ability, the need to give someone good news) lose accuracy.

3. **The body cooperates**: The body always wants health. Its energy signals are oriented toward promoting life. Medical intuition reads the body's own communications about what needs to change.

4. **Intuition can precede clinical detection**: Myss identifies the energy disruptions that precede illness at the stage where no clinical test would yet show abnormality. This makes the skill — in principle — preventive.

## Broader Ambition

Myss envisions medical intuitives as members of health care teams, working alongside physicians to identify the energetic and biographical roots of illness. She describes intuitives as a "bridge" between the world of conventional medicine (which measures physical pathology) and energy medicine (which reads the pre-illness field). Larry Dossey's concept of "Era III Medicine" — non-local healing that transcends the individual body — provides a theoretical context for this ambition.

## Dossey's External Validation: Era III Medicine

[Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md) provides the most explicit external validation of the Myss/Shealy collaboration in *Meaning & Medicine* [(1991)](../sources/meaning-and-medicine.md). Dossey cites *The Creation of Health* (Shealy & Myss, 1988) as the most striking current clinical evidence for Era III medicine — the non-local operation of consciousness.

Dossey's account: Myss worked from New Hampshire while Shealy saw patients in Missouri — a distance of approximately 1,200 miles. Myss was given only the patient's name and birthdate. Shealy compared her diagnoses against clinical findings and his own assessments. Dossey states that Shealy estimated Myss's accuracy at **93%**.

For Dossey, this is not merely a curiosity about one woman's unusual ability. It is evidence that:

1. **Consciousness does not require proximity to function diagnostically**: The 1,200-mile separation is not a limitation, which means the causal mechanism cannot be electromagnetic (signal strength falls off with distance) but must be non-local in the sense Dossey defines for Era III medicine.
2. **The body's information is accessible to awareness other than the patient's own**: This implies a shared informational field rather than strictly private biological states — consistent with the neuropeptide network's dispersal through the whole body and with Dossey's Bohmian framework (soma-significance operating at the level of the implicate order).
3. **Clinical integration is feasible**: Dossey's case for including medical intuition in clinical teams gains credibility precisely because it has been tested against medical records, not merely reported anecdotally.

This external citation from a physician not otherwise involved in the Myss/Shealy collaboration constitutes an independent second opinion on the collaboration's evidential value — and situates it within a broader theoretical framework (Era III medicine) that extends to prayer research, therapeutic touch, and EEG synchrony across distance. See [Prayer and Healing](prayer-and-healing.md).

## Critique

**Lack of rigorous validation**: While Shealy's clinical collaboration provides one form of validation, it does not constitute a controlled scientific study. No randomised trial has compared Myss's remote diagnostic accuracy against a control group using pre-specified, blinded, independently verified outcomes. The case studies reported are compelling but subject to selection bias, confirmation bias, and the Barnum effect (general statements that seem specific but apply broadly).

**The accuracy question**: Myss herself acknowledges that accuracy varies, and that interference (emotional investment, desire to prove the skill) reduces accuracy. This introduces an untestable quality: failed readings can always be attributed to interference rather than to the limits of the skill.

**Scientific status**: Medical intuition as described by Myss is not recognised as a clinical skill by mainstream medicine. No professional licensing body accredits medical intuitives, and the evidence base does not meet the standards required for integration into clinical practice.

**Ethical dimensions**: Evaluating a person's health — including identifying illness they may not yet know they have — carries serious ethical responsibilities. Myss acknowledges the gravity of this, describing her anguish at identifying cancer in patients and her careful approach to sharing difficult information.

## Lineage of Intuitive Diagnosticians (*Creation of Health*)

[*The Creation of Health*](../sources/creation-of-health.md) provides a survey of intuitive diagnosticians who preceded or paralleled Myss, situating her practice in a lineage:

- **[Edgar Cayce](../thinkers/edgar-cayce.md)**: The most prolific historical example — 15,000 trance health readings conducted at a distance, without clinical information, producing specific diagnoses and treatment recommendations. Shealy and Myss both cite Cayce as the foundational figure.
- **Robert Leichtman, M.D.**: A physician-intuitive whom Shealy studied alongside Myss for seven years as part of his effort to document and validate intuitive diagnostic capacity.
- **Edward Bach**: Developer of Bach Flower Remedies; diagnosed emotional states energetically and prescribed flower essences corresponding to them. His contribution is the systematic mapping of emotional states to physical health.
- **Torkom Saraydarian**: Philosopher and writer who analysed the energetic foundations of health from a Theosophical perspective.

What distinguishes Myss from these predecessors, in Shealy's framing, is the decade of systematic clinical verification — hundreds of patients, with her readings compared against medical diagnoses and patient histories.

## Shealy's Extension: Psychic Diagnosticians

In [*Sacred Healing*](../sources/sacred-healing.md), Shealy widens the frame beyond Myss. His A.R.E. contacts in 1972 led him to study a range of diagnosticians with claimed intuitive abilities. He specifically mentions studying the work of Dr. Robert Leichtman and [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) over seven years, with the ambition of establishing training programmes to authenticate individuals with intuitive diagnostic capabilities.

[Edgar Cayce](../thinkers/edgar-cayce.md)'s 15,000 trance health readings are presented as the most prolific historical example of medical intuition — operating at a distance, without clinical data, and producing specific diagnostic and treatment recommendations that many patients reported as accurate and effective.

Shealy's EEG experiments with healer Ostad Hadi Parvarandeh also bear on medical intuition: Parvarandeh could diagnose patients without seeing them (by voice, photograph, or name alone) and could identify diseases his patients' physicians had not yet detected — paralleling Myss's remote diagnostic capacity through a different cultural and religious framework.

## Joy's Contribution: Somatic Medical Intuition

[W. Brugh Joy](../thinkers/w-brugh-joy.md) in [*Joy's Way*](../sources/joys-way.md) (1979) represents a third mode of medical intuition alongside Myss (remote symbolic) and Brennan (close-range visual). Joy's approach is **somatic and tactile**: he detects energy fields through trained hand sensitivity rather than symbolic impression or visual clairvoyance.

Joy discovered his scanning ability through clinical practice — during medical examinations, not through formal intuitive training. This gives his account a distinctive quality: it is embedded in conventional medical procedure and therefore not readily dismissed as suggestion. His key clinical findings from body scanning:

- Diseases radiating from the location of the affected organ: hepatitis detectable over the liver, kidney disease behind the kidney, appendicitis over the appendix, heart disease over the chest
- Cancer creating a distinct field signature detectable at distance from the body surface — different in texture and configuration from the chakra fields
- Infection and cancer both producing elevated field signatures; Joy cannot distinguish between them
- Energy fields persisting in cadavers refrigerated for twenty-four hours after death

What distinguishes Joy from Myss and Brennan is the self-training claim: Joy asserts that approximately 99% of participants in his two-week conferences developed at least minimal hand-scanning ability. This suggests (within his framework) that somatic medical intuition is a trainable skill available to many people, not a rare natural gift.

The comparison across all three modes:

| Practitioner | Mode | Range | Content accessed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Myss | Remote symbolic/biographical | Name and age only | Chakra depletion, life patterns, organ location |
| Brennan | Visual HSP | In-person, close range | Seven-layer auric field structure, detailed anatomical mapping |
| Joy | Tactile somatic | Hand at 6 inches–6 feet | Field intensity at chakra locations, disease field anomalies |

## Achterberg's Contribution: Image CA as Laboratory Medical Intuition

[Jeanne Achterberg](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md) in [*Imagery in Healing*](../sources/imagery-in-healing.md) (1985) approaches the diagnostic intuition question from a laboratory research direction rather than a clinical phenomenological one. Her **Image CA tool** — which predicts cancer outcomes at 93% accuracy from patients' mental imagery alone — can be read as a research operationalisation of the same phenomenon Myss and Joy exemplify through direct perception.

The parallel structure:

| Mode | Myss (Medical Intuition) | Achterberg (Image CA) |
|---|---|---|
| What is read | The patient's energy field (remote, no contact) | The patient's own mental imagery (drawings and interview) |
| What is assessed | Biographical stress patterns, chakra depletion | Vividness, activity, and symbolic content of illness, immunity, and treatment imagery |
| Diagnostic claim | Identifies organ pathology and biographical roots | Predicts two-month outcomes (remission vs. death) |
| Accuracy claimed | ~93% (Shealy's estimate, Dossey's report) | 93% for remission/deterioration; 100% for death prediction |
| Evidence type | Clinical collaboration; not randomised | Clinical research; correlational |

The convergence at 93% is striking and has been noted: Myss's independently estimated accuracy and Achterberg's Image CA predictive accuracy are numerically identical. Whether this reflects a common underlying mechanism — both tapping into the body's information about its own trajectory — or is coincidental cannot be determined from the available data.

**The shamanic prototype**: Achterberg situates both the Image CA tool and Myss-style medical intuition within a single historical lineage: shamanic healing. The shaman's diagnostic journey — entering the SSC and reading the "spirit cause" of illness — is Type 2 (transpersonal) medical intuition; the Image CA is Type 1 (self-directed) intuition made quantifiable. The patient's own imagery is treated as a window into physiological information that precedes clinical detectability — exactly the claim Myss makes about her field readings.

See [Shamanic Healing](shamanic-healing.md) and [Mental Imagery and Healing](mental-imagery-healing.md).

## Brennan's Parallel: High Sense Perception

[Barbara Ann Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md) practices a closely related but distinct capacity she terms [High Sense Perception (HSP)](high-sense-perception.md). Both Myss and Brennan claim direct perceptual access to the Human Energy Field, but their practices differ significantly:

| Aspect | Myss (Medical Intuition) | Brennan (HSP) |
|---|---|---|
| Orientation | Diagnostic — reads the field to identify illness roots | Diagnostic AND therapeutic — reads and then restructures the field |
| Access mode | Remote evaluation; name and age only | In-person; hands-on or close proximity |
| Primary mode | Symbolic/biographical | Visual/anatomical (seven-layer model) |
| Spirit guidance | Not a feature | Central (Heyoan channels healing guidance) |
| Chakra pendulum | Not used | Used as diagnostic tool (clockwise/counterclockwise) |
| Scientific grounding | Neuropeptides (Pert), bioelectromagnetism | Hunt/Bruyere UCLA study; bioplasma; L-fields |

Both practitioners agree that the energy field's information precedes clinical illness, that accuracy improves with practice and decreases with emotional investment, and that the skill requires the observer's consciousness to be clear and free of personal agenda.

Brennan's additional contribution: the seven-chakra perceptual map showing which chakra governs which mode of HSP. This provides a structural account of why different intuitives "see" differently (via different chakras) and how perception can be developed systematically rather than only cultivated as a whole.

## Daskalos's Contribution: Diagnosis via Exomatic Projection

The Cypriot mystic [Daskalos](../thinkers/daskalos.md), as recorded by Kyriacos Markides in [*The Magus of Strovolos*](../sources/magus-of-strovolos.md) (1985), offers a fourth mode alongside Myss, Brennan, and Joy: diagnosis via **active projection** of the etheric body to the patient's location (what he calls [exomatosis](exomatosis.md)) or via touch/photograph while stationary. Unlike Myss's passive reception of symbolic impressions, Daskalos frames his method as consciously traveling to and examining the patient from multiple vantage points simultaneously — closer in structure to Brennan's in-person HSP than to Myss's remote symbolic reading, but claimed to work at a distance as Myss's does. As with the rest of Daskalos's material, these claims rest solely on first-person narrative recorded by a single participant-observer, with no independent instrumentation comparable to Shealy's EEG studies.
