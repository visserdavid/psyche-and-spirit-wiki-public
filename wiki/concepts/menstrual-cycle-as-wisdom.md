# Menstrual Cycle as Wisdom

**Summary**: Northrup's reframing of the menstrual cycle as a monthly information system — a cyclical movement between outward creativity (follicular/ovulatory phase) and inward knowing (luteal/premenstrual phase) — in which suppression of either phase produces PMS and other cycle disorders.

**Sources**:
- [Northrup, C. (1994). *Women's bodies, women's wisdom: Creating physical and emotional health and healing*. Bantam Books.](../sources/womens-bodies-womens-wisdom.md)

## Overview

[Christiane Northrup](../thinkers/christiane-northrup.md) argues in *Women's Bodies, Women's Wisdom* (1994) that the menstrual cycle is not merely a hormonal reproductive event but a **monthly information system** — a rhythm of alternating modes of consciousness that gives women access to different types of knowing at different phases of the cycle.

This reframing inverses conventional medicine's treatment of menstruation: rather than a process requiring management, suppression, or relief, the cycle is a guidance mechanism — one that generates PMS and other disorders when its informational function is ignored or suppressed.

## The Two Phases of the Cycle

### Follicular and Ovulatory Phase (Days 1–14): Outward Expansion

The first half of the cycle — from menstruation through ovulation — parallels the waxing moon. Northrup describes this as the phase of **outward expansion**: creativity, initiation, enthusiasm, and social engagement. Energy rises, ideas flow, and connection to others is easy.

Hormonally, the surge of FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) and LH (luteinizing hormone) that accompanies ovulation produces measurable increases in social receptivity and outward-facing energy. Studies document that women receive higher tips at ovulation, that their body language becomes more open and attractive, and that they are most receptive to new projects and relationships.

The parallel to consciousness: the follicular phase is when women are most visible, productive in socially recognised ways, and outwardly focused. Western culture — which values productivity, social performance, and outward-facing activity — privileges this half of the cycle.

### Luteal and Premenstrual Phase (Days 15–28): Inward Knowing

The second half of the cycle — from ovulation through menstruation — parallels the waning and dark moon. Northrup describes this as the phase of **inward knowing**: heightened access to the unconscious, increased sensitivity to what is not working in one's life, and a natural pull toward reflection, solitude, and evaluation.

The premenstrual phase is characterised by:
- **Reduced tolerance for what is incongruent**: What a woman can overlook at ovulation becomes intolerable premenstrually. The perceptual filter is thinner; the unconscious is closer to the surface.
- **Access to suppressed truth**: Dreams become more vivid and revealing; emotional responses to situations are exaggerated in ways that point toward what genuinely matters and what does not.
- **A pull toward stillness**: Northrup notes the cross-cultural pattern of women retreating together during menstruation — not because they are "unclean" but because this phase requires the support of quiet, inwardness, and shared female company.

## PMS as Suppressed Information

Northrup's central clinical argument about PMS: **PMS is not a hormonal malfunction but a communication failure**. When a woman has developed a pattern of ignoring the premenstrual phase's information — pushing through it, suppressing the emotions it surfaces, maintaining the same outward-facing productivity as the follicular phase — the body escalates its signals into physical symptoms.

The emotional content that PMS delivers — anxiety, irritability, tearfulness, rage — is not random noise. It is the accumulated truth of what is not being addressed in the woman's life, surfacing at the moment in the cycle when the veil between ordinary consciousness and deeper knowing is thinnest. When that content is acknowledged, named, and worked with, PMS symptoms typically diminish.

Practical implication: the first therapeutic question for a woman with PMS is not "what drug or supplement will manage the symptoms" but "what does the premenstrual phase consistently surface for you — and what is your ordinary relationship to that content?"

## Lunar Connection

Northrup cites studies showing that peak conception and ovulation rates cluster around the full moon, and that the greatest number of women begin bleeding at the new moon (dark moon). Women living without artificial light tended to synchronise their cycles to the lunar rhythm — bleeding with the new moon (the moon's resting phase) and ovulating with the full moon (the moon's peak expression).

The pattern mirrors the symbolic resonance: the premenstrual-menstrual phase (dark moon, inward knowing) corresponds to the moon's withdrawal; the ovulatory phase (full moon, outward expansion) corresponds to the moon's fullness.

Northrup interprets this as evidence that the menstrual cycle is not merely biological but cosmically embedded — the female body participates in a planetary rhythm that, in conditions of artificial light and over-stimulation, has been disrupted.

## "Days of Wisdom"

Northrup encourages women to track what arises in the premenstrual phase over several months. The recurring themes — the specific relationships, work situations, or unmet needs that consistently surface at this time — constitute a **personalised wisdom curriculum**: the soul's agenda, delivered monthly in the window when ordinary defences are lowered.

The woman who consistently becomes enraged at her job in the premenstrual phase and suppresses it with ibuprofen and caffeine is suppressing information about her job. The woman who cries over a relationship premenstrually and dismisses it as "just PMS" may be suppressing information about the relationship. When the information is taken seriously and acted upon, the cycle's delivery mechanism — the symptom — becomes unnecessary.

## Menstrual Cramps

Northrup attributes dysmenorrhoea (menstrual cramps) primarily to prostaglandin F2-alpha, produced in the uterine lining. But she connects the intensity of cramping to the woman's relationship with her body and with the cyclical rhythm: women who are most disconnected from the premenstrual inward phase, or who carry the most second-chakra tension (relational stress, suppressed creativity, shame around the body), tend to experience the most severe cramping.

Anti-inflammatory drugs (Advil, aspirin) work by blocking prostaglandin synthesis — effective in the short term, but addressing the mechanism rather than the pattern. Nutritional approaches (reducing animal fat and dairy, which increase prostaglandin production; increasing omega-3 fatty acids, which reduce it) address a contributing physiological factor. Addressing the energy anatomy of the second chakra addresses the deeper pattern.

## Menstrual Rites and Cultural Repair

Northrup connects the pathologisation of menstruation to the addictive system's treatment of women's cyclical nature as a problem. Cross-culturally, menstruation has been understood as a time of power — of intensified access to vision, dreaming, and the unseen. The modern cultural dismissal of this phase as "PMS" inverts its traditional valuation.

Northrup advocates for the creation of contemporary rites around menarche and menstruation — ways of initiating girls into respectful relationship with their cycles rather than shame, embarrassment, and management. She describes several practices used by patients and colleagues: keeping a moon journal for three months; celebrating the first period as a rite of passage; creating personal rituals that mark the transition from follicular to luteal phase.

## Relationship to Energy Anatomy

The menstrual cycle's dual-phase structure maps onto the second and first chakras:

- The **follicular/ovulatory phase** expresses the second chakra's creative and relational energy outwardly — new relationships, creative projects, engagement with the world.
- The **premenstrual/menstrual phase** returns energy to the first chakra for restoration and re-rooting — rest, reflection, the body's own restoration.

Disruption of either phase — failure to expand fully at ovulation, or failure to retreat and rest premenstrually — creates the energy pattern that accumulates as second-chakra disorders (fibroids, endometriosis, PMS, dysmenorrhoea, irregular periods).

## Critique

**The lunar connection is not well established**: The studies Northrup cites on lunar-menstrual synchrony are methodologically weak; the largest review studies have not confirmed a significant lunar effect on human menstrual timing.

**PMS as information vs. PMS as disorder**: The claim that PMS symptoms are always meaningful messages rather than physiological fluctuations that vary independently of psychological content is not supported by evidence. Hormonal and nutritional factors play documented roles in PMS severity that are not reducible to the information-suppression framework.

**The reframing can delay appropriate treatment**: Women who interpret persistent, severe PMS, dysmenorrhoea, or irregular cycles as "information to work with" may delay seeking investigation of underlying conditions (endometriosis, PCOS, fibroids) that warrant medical evaluation and treatment.

**Note**: Mainstream gynecology treats PMS as a condition with multiple contributing factors (hormonal, nutritional, psychological) and does not accept the information-suppression framework as the primary explanation for its symptoms.
