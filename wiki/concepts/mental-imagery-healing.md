# Mental Imagery and Healing

**Summary**: The field of research and practice examining how mental images — internal visual, sensory, and symbolic representations — influence physiological processes, with particular emphasis on the immune system, pain, and cancer outcomes; grounded in neurological findings that the brain cannot reliably distinguish between vividly imagined and actually performed experiences.

## The Core Finding: Images Are Physiologically Real

Edmund Jacobsen demonstrated in 1929 via electromyography (EMG) that imagining a physical movement produces measurable electrical activation in the muscle group responsible for that movement — even without visible motion. This finding, extensively replicated, establishes the foundational principle: mental imagery is not merely a subjective experience; it activates the same neural circuits as the corresponding sensory or motor event.

Clinical implications documented in the literature:
- Imagining eating a lemon produces salivation (measurable increase in saliva secretion)
- Athletes who perform mental rehearsal of complex movements show physiological changes in the relevant muscle groups
- Surgical patients who receive sensory imagery of procedures ("you will feel pressure and hear a scraping sound") require less pain medication than those given only information about the procedure
- Hypnotic analgesia produces measurable changes in cortical processing of pain signals (not merely report suppression)

[Jeanne Achterberg](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md) uses this foundation to argue that imagery is not a complementary technique added to medicine but the primary mechanism through which all mind-body communication occurs — the language the cortex uses to speak to the autonomic nervous system, immune system, and endocrine system.

## The Holographic Brain Model

Karl Pribram proposed (based on lesion studies, memory research, and analogy to holographic physics) that memory and sensory representation are stored *distributed* across the brain rather than in discrete locations. Evidence: specific brain lesions do not reliably eliminate specific memories; rather, memory capacity degrades proportionally with lesion size, not content-specifically. This holographic storage means each part of the brain contains enough information to reconstruct the whole representation.

Achterberg uses this model to explain how imagery changes physiology: an image activates a distributed representational pattern across the brain, which reverberates through the hypothalamus and autonomic nervous system to produce systemic physiological effects. The specificity of imagery — imagining the left hand versus the right hand, imagining white blood cells versus imagining relaxation in general — produces correspondingly specific physiological responses, consistent with the holographic view.

**Note**: Pribram's holographic brain model is an influential theoretical proposal, not established consensus neuroscience. Subsequent neuroimaging research has confirmed that memory storage is distributed (consistent with Pribram) but has also revealed significant regional specialisation (partly in tension with a strict holographic interpretation). The model is useful as a conceptual framework but should not be cited as confirmed neuroscience.

## Hemisphere Specialisation and Imagery

Achterberg proposes, drawing on split-brain research, that:
- The **right hemisphere** specialises in imagery storage, retrieval, and processing
- The **left hemisphere** processes language and sequential-analytic reasoning
- The **corpus callosum** mediates translation between these modalities

In this framework, imagery is the right hemisphere's primary mode of communication. Physiological and emotional states are first encoded as images (right hemisphere) before they can be translated into language (left hemisphere). When translation fails — as in alexithymia (the inability to name one's emotions) — the body may express in symptoms what cannot be expressed in words. This framework links imagery to [Biography Is Biology](biography-is-biology.md): the body speaks in images before it speaks in symptoms.

## Two Types of Imagery

Achterberg distinguishes two fundamentally different assumptions about the mechanism of healing imagery:

**Type 1 — Self-directed imagery**: The patient's own imagination affects their own body through physiological pathways. The mechanism is intra-personal: imagery activates neural circuits that cascade through the hypothalamic-pituitary-immune axis to produce immune, endocrine, and autonomic changes. Clinical applications include relaxation training, biofeedback, Simonton-style cancer visualisation, hypnotic analgesia, and pain management imagery.

**Type 2 — Transpersonal imagery**: The claim that healing intention or diagnostic information can be transmitted from one consciousness to another without physical mediation. This is the domain of shamanic healing, intercessory prayer, and distant diagnostic perception. Achterberg neither endorses nor dismisses this claim; she notes it cannot be explained by Type 1 mechanisms and requires a different evidentiary approach. She later conducted fMRI experiments (post-1985) showing correlated brain activations in pairs of healers and recipients separated by thousands of miles, suggesting possible empirical signals.

[Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s [medical intuition](medical-intuition.md) and the [Shamanic Healing](shamanic-healing.md) tradition both operate in Type 2 territory; most laboratory and clinical research addresses Type 1.

## The Image CA Tool

Developed by Achterberg and statistician G. Frank Lawlis from structured interviews and drawings with approximately 200 cancer patients at the Cancer Rehabilitation Clinic (Parkland Memorial Hospital, Dallas) and O. Carl Simonton's Cancer Counseling and Research Center (Fort Worth).

### Assessment Procedure

1. The patient undergoes a 20-minute guided relaxation induction
2. The patient is asked to describe (verbally and via drawing) three things: the cancer cells; the white blood cells and immune system; the treatment and what it is doing to both
3. Responses are scored on 14 dimensions

### The 14 Scoring Dimensions

**Cancer cell imagery**:
- Vividness (how clearly the cells are imagined)
- Activity (how active or passive)
- Strength (how powerful or threatening)

**Immune cell imagery**:
- Vividness
- Activity
- Strength

**Comparative dimensions**:
- Relative size of immune cells vs. cancer cells
- Relative number of immune cells vs. cancer cells

**Treatment imagery**:
- Perceived strength/effectiveness of treatment against the cancer
- Integration (does the treatment coherently target the specific cancer being imagined?)

**Overall imagery assessment**:
- Symbolism used (archetypal, natural, mechanical, abstract)
- Coherence and integration of all three imagery components
- Regularity of imagery practice
- Patient's own prognosis prediction

### Key Findings

- 93% accuracy predicting two-month outcomes (remission vs. deterioration vs. death) from imagery scores alone
- 100% accuracy identifying patients who would die within two months
- Imagery scores predicted outcomes independently of blood chemistry variables (current physiological state)
- Trestman's analysis found 12 significant correlations between Image CA dimensions and haematology/blood chemistry variables, including white blood cell count, lymphocyte counts, and complement levels
- Image CA has been applied to laryngectomy rehabilitation, mastectomy recovery, and other cancer populations, confirming predictive validity

### What the Imagery Reveals

The content of the imagery carries prognostic significance. Best-outcome imagery characteristics:
- Cancer cells are small, weak, confused, or fragmenting
- Immune cells are large, active, numerous, and purposeful
- Treatment is effective and specifically targeted
- The imagery tells a coherent story (all three components interact logically)

Imagery associated with poor prognosis:
- Cancer cells are depicted as overwhelmingly large, smart, or powerful
- Immune cells are passive (snow flakes, clouds, soft shapes)
- Treatment is ineffective or aimed at the wrong target
- The three components are disconnected

Specific symbolic forms: **Archetypal fighter imagery** (knights, warriors, Viking ships) and **predatory animal imagery** (sharks, bears, hawks) are associated with best immune response. However, forcing "killer" imagery against the patient's inclination is counter-productive; the image must arise authentically.

**Caution on causality**: The Image CA predicts outcomes; it does not prove that changing the imagery *causes* outcome improvement. Positive imagery may track improving immune function rather than cause it. Achterberg treats the tool as diagnostic, not merely therapeutic.

## Laboratory Research: Imagery Directly Controlling Immune Cells

**Smith and Schneider (Michigan State University, 1983)**: The most direct demonstration in the peer-reviewed literature that specific imagery directly controls specific immune cell behaviour. Subjects asked to imagine neutrophils leaving the blood vessel walls showed:
- Blood neutrophil count dropping from 8,200 to 6,400 per millilitre (p < 0.0001)
- 60% of circulating neutrophils exiting the bloodstream during the imagery session

In a follow-up experiment, subjects asked to image neutrophils adhering to vessel walls showed increased adherence (from 28% to 56%). The direction of the immune change followed the imagery direction.

**Hall hypnosis study (Pennsylvania State University)**: 20 male subjects asked under hypnosis to visualise white blood cells as "sharks attacking germs." T and B cell function improved significantly in subjects under 50 at one-week follow-up. Effect not sustained in older subjects (attributed to age-related immune-system constraints). Limitation: small N, no randomisation.

**Peavey biofeedback study (1982, award-winning)**: 16 stressed subjects in a biofeedback-assisted relaxation programme vs. 16 controls. Biofeedback subjects showed improved neutrophil phagocytic function (neutrophils more effectively engulfed bacteria in assay); controls did not. The effect was on *functional quality*, not cell count — suggesting imagery and relaxation improve immune performance rather than merely changing cell numbers.

## Clinical Applications

**Pain management**: Imagery + relaxation significantly outperformed relaxation alone and control conditions in Achterberg and Kenner's NIH-funded burn unit study (149 patients): imagery groups required less pain medication, less sedative medication, and less sleep medication. The combination of relaxation + imagery was optimal; adding biofeedback instrumentation added limited benefit beyond imagery alone.

**Childbirth preparation**: Dick Read (1942) and Lamaze built systematic imagery-relaxation programmes around the finding that fear induces uterine vasoconstriction, amplifying pain; relaxation imagery interrupts this cycle.

**Autogenic training** (Schultz and Luthe): Six standardised imagery exercises producing documented changes in muscle tension, skin temperature, blood sugar, blood pressure, hormone secretion, and brain waves. Used therapeutically for asthma, migraine, diabetes, and arthritis.

**Nursing** (Jean Johnson's "sensory information" research): Pre-procedural sensory imagery reduced hospital stays and analgesic use in cholecystectomy, cast-removal, and endoscopy patients.

**Cancer rehabilitation**: Image CA, Simonton visualisation, and follow-on programmes. Achterberg does not claim visualisation cures cancer; she argues it optimises the patient's immune environment, emotional state, and compliance with treatment.

**[W. Brugh Joy](../thinkers/w-brugh-joy.md)'s Command Therapy**: Direct imagery-based organ communication ("say to the cell walls, open"). Joy's approach operates by the same Type 1 mechanism (inner representation → physiological response) but uses direct address rather than symbolic visualisation. See [Command Therapy](command-therapy.md).

## The Endorphin Connection

Endorphins and enkephalins — endogenous opioid peptides discovered in 1973 — provide one biochemical pathway from imagery to immune function:

- Beta-endorphin enhances T cell proliferation (Gilman et al.)
- Enkephalins increase T cell percentages and increase their activity against cancer cells (Plotnikoff, 1982)
- Endorphins are released not only by pain and stress but also by positive emotional states: exercise, music (Goldstein, 1980 — "thrills" from music are naloxone-reversible, confirming endorphin involvement), sweet tastes, peak experiences, and imagery-induced states of joy or accomplishment

This creates a physiological pathway for the clinical observation (across Siegel, LeShan, Hay, and Achterberg) that emotional shift → immune function change. See [Psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md).

## Critique

**Small study sizes**: The Hall (N=20), Peavey (N=16), and Smith/Schneider (N=16) studies are preliminary. The Image CA research (N≈200) is more substantial but correlational, not randomised. Independent replication has been limited.

**Causal ambiguity**: Imagery may track physiological state rather than cause it. A patient who is recovering generates positive imagery; the imagery predicts rather than produces recovery. This is methodologically acknowledged by Achterberg but not resolved.

**Selection and motivation bias**: Patients who engage seriously with imagery practice may differ systematically from those who do not in ways that correlate with both better imagery scores and better outcomes.

**Holographic model status**: Pribram's holographic model is a theoretical framework, not established neuroscience. Its use as an explanatory basis should be held lightly.

**Note**: The fundamental findings — that imagery produces measurable physiological changes and that mental states alter immune function — are now well-established. The specific clinical applications (cancer prognosis prediction, neutrophil control, immune enhancement) remain more contested and require larger, randomised, controlled replication.
