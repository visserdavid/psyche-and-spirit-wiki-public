# Mindfulness

**Summary**: The practice of present-moment awareness without judgment — observing thoughts, emotions, and sensations as they arise without being swept away by them; the psychological counterpart to the relaxation response and the antidote to the mind's chronic awfulizing, conditioning, and suffering-generating patterns.

## Overview

Mindfulness — present-moment awareness without judgment — is one of the central clinical tools in [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s Mind/Body Program and the conceptual bridge between the meditation/breathing skills taught in early weeks and the emotional healing work of the later weeks.

Its roots are Buddhist: Thich Nhat Hanh's *Miracle of Mindfulness* (1976) and Jon Kabat-Zinn's Stress Reduction and Relaxation Program at the University of Massachusetts Medical School are cited as sources and inspirations. Borysenko integrates mindfulness into a clinical context, making it accessible to patients with no Buddhist background by framing it as a practical tool for reducing the suffering generated by the mind's habitual patterns.

Ram Dass / Richard Alpert is quoted in the book as a guide to mindfulness practice: "Put up signs around the house as reminders. The practice is just remembering what's happening right now."

## The Witness

The central metaphor is the **Witness** — a stable, non-reactive observational stance from which the mind's activity can be seen without identification. The Witness is not a spiritual abstraction but a trainable mode of attention: the capacity to notice "I am thinking this" rather than being the thought.

From the Witness position:
- Anxious thoughts can be observed without triggering the fight-or-flight response
- Painful emotions can be experienced without being amplified by resistance
- Awfulizing patterns can be identified before they generate physiological stress
- The body's wisdom can be heard beneath the noise of habitual mental commentary

Borysenko locates the Witness in what she calls the **Inner Self** — the stable, loving core of the person that underlies the conditioned personality. Accessing this core through meditation and breathing is, for her, the ground of genuine healing.

## Mindlessness and Conditioning

The opposite of mindfulness is conditioned automaticity — the mind running its established programs without observation. Borysenko draws on Pavlovian conditioning to explain how conditioned patterns become automatic health threats:

- A student who has become anxious at past exams will activate fight-or-flight at the sight of an exam room — regardless of the actual difficulty of the exam
- A patient who has experienced nausea during chemotherapy will begin to feel nauseous at the sight of the clinic — before receiving any treatment
- A person who has learned that expressing anger leads to rejection will suppress anger automatically, regardless of the current relationship

These patterns are not conscious choices; they are learned physiological responses. Mindfulness — specifically the capacity to observe the pattern arising — is the first step toward unhooking from it. "Awareness is the first step in breaking the pattern."

## Awfulizing and Mind Traps

Borysenko identifies **awfulizing** as the primary mechanism by which the mind generates unnecessary stress: catastrophising future events using fight-or-flight circuits that evolved for real physical threats. The mind can imagine a lemon vividly enough to cause real salivation; it can imagine a threatening conversation vividly enough to cause real cortisol release and immune suppression.

Mind traps are the habitual cognitive patterns that generate suffering:
- **Awfulizing**: "If X happens, it will be catastrophic and I cannot cope"
- **Control-need**: "I must control this situation or disaster will result"
- **Blame**: "My suffering is entirely caused by others; I am passive"
- **Denial**: "If I don't acknowledge this, it isn't happening" — which requires continuous energy to maintain and generates body tension

The Witness stance allows these patterns to be recognised. Recognition does not automatically dissolve them, but it opens the space for choice — the monkey who recognises that he is caught by the banana in the gourd can let go; the one who doesn't cannot.

## Mindfulness of Emotion

Chapter 7 of *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* extends mindfulness to the emotional domain. Borysenko presents emotions as teachers rather than problems:
- Emotions carry information about what matters to the person and what is being threatened
- Suppressing or overexpressing emotions both prevent the information from being received
- The mindful approach: feel the emotion, name it, take a breath and step into the Witness position, then choose a response

Two emotional categories:
- **Fear-based** (anger, resentment, guilt, grief): associated with bodily holding, muscle tension, fight-or-flight physiology
- **Love-based** (joy, humour, compassion, forgiveness): associated with relaxation, openness, parasympathetic dominance

Mindfulness of emotion means neither suppressing fear-based emotions nor being swept away by them — observing them with the same quality of non-judgmental attention that one brings to the breath in meditation.

## Mindfulness in the Mind/Body Program

In the clinic's ten-week program, mindfulness is introduced in week four as the bridge between the physiological skills (relaxation response, breathing, stretching) and the cognitive/emotional skills (reframing, forgiveness):

- The relaxation response creates the physiological state of parasympathetic calm in which mindful observation is possible
- Mindfulness, practised in meditation, trains the Witness capacity that makes cognitive reframing possible
- The Witness stance makes it possible to observe mind traps without being caught by them
- Mindful awareness of emotions makes it possible to feel them, complete them, and release them rather than storing them as chronic tension

## Relation to Other Wiki Frameworks

**Myss's [Symbolic Sight](symbolic-sight.md)**: Myss's practice of reading life events for their energetic and symbolic meaning requires a similar observational stance — stepping back from the literal and reactive to perceive the pattern. Symbolic sight is mindfulness applied to meaning.

**Brennan's [Mask Self](mask-self-lower-self-higher-self.md)**: The mask self's chronic suppression of wound and lower self requires continuous un-mindful automaticity. Mindfulness — noticing "I am performing this mask" — is a prerequisite for the mask beginning to soften.

**Hillman's [Acorn Theory](acorn-theory.md)**: Hillman argues that the soul's calling persists through the biographical noise. Mindfulness — the capacity to hear what is actually present beneath conditioned expectations — can be the means by which the calling is heard rather than silenced.

**[Woundology](woundology.md)**: Myss's woundology is the opposite of mindfulness applied to suffering: instead of observing the wound and moving through it, the woundologist uses the wound as currency — keeping it alive through repetition. Mindfulness of the wound pattern is the first step toward releasing it.

## Walking Meditation and Compassionate Action: Fire in the Soul (Borysenko, 1993)

In [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md), mindfulness extends from the sitting practice of the Mind/Body Clinic into walking meditation as a form of compassionate presence — and ultimately into compassionate action as the natural expression of mindful awareness.

Borysenko presents Thich Nhat Hanh's **walking meditation** from his *Guide to Walking Meditation* (1985) as the contemplative counterpart to the clinic's sitting practice:

> *Breathing in, I calm my body and mind.*
> *Breathing out, I smile.*
> *Dwelling in the present moment,*
> *I know this is the only moment.*

Each step is a return to the present; each step is an opportunity to leave an imprint of joy or worry on the earth. Thich Nhat Hanh: "Walk so that your footprints bear marks of joy and freedom, not marks of sorrow." The walker who sees the profusion of daffodils, notices the scent of earth, feels the sun — rather than running the income-tax commentary — is practising mindfulness as a bodily and relational act.

The practice of **walking meditation** is structured:
- Find a natural setting or simply a room
- Notice that the world contains wonders "even if you do not expect to find heaven here"
- Become the body walking — the pleasant rhythm of breathing, the movement of foot, shift of weight, placement
- Walk with "dignity, humility, and self-respect"
- Bring a smile — Thich Nhat Hanh's suggestion: imagine a figure who embodies these qualities and walk as they would walk
- Use the four-line breathing poem as a rhythm for the breath

The clinical bridge: walking meditation trains the same Witness capacity as sitting meditation, but in motion and in the world — making mindfulness portable, applicable to ordinary daily activity. Where the sitting practice develops the capacity to observe the mind from stillness, the walking practice develops the capacity to remain present in movement and encounter.

**Mindfulness as compassion**: Thich Nhat Hanh ends his walking meditation guide speaking of compassion as the natural fruit of presence. Walking a path mindfully, one begins to see the pain in others — not as a problem to manage but as the kinship that motivates service. Mindfulness does not produce detachment; it produces *engaged compassion* — the foundation of what Borysenko calls "converting suffering to service."

## Mindfulness and Grace: Thich Nhat Hanh's Poem

In [*Guilt Is the Teacher*](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md) [(Borysenko, 1990)](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md), mindfulness takes on an explicitly spiritual dimension — it becomes the practice of opening to grace. Borysenko presents Thich Nhat Hanh's breathing poem as the centrepiece of this dimension:

> *Breathing in, I calm my body and mind.*
> *Breathing out, I smile.*
> *Dwelling in the present moment,*
> *I know this is the only moment.*

The four lines address, respectively: calming the body and mind (the physiological work of the relaxation response), the smile (the parasympathetic relaxation of hundreds of facial muscles — "Buddhas and Bodhisattvas are always smiling"), dwelling in the present (releasing past and future), and the recognition that the present is the only moment of contact with reality.

Borysenko reads this poem as a technology of grace-opening: emptying the cup of concept so that the gift of the present moment — the love that is always already present — can be received. Meister Eckhart: "The most powerful prayer is thanksgiving." The poem is an act of thanksgiving that completes the circuit between creation and creator.

This connection between mindfulness and spiritual reception extends the Mind/Body Clinic's clinical use of mindfulness (Witness awareness as stress-reduction tool) into the broader framework of [spiritual optimism](spiritual-optimism.md): present-moment awareness is not merely a technique for reducing cortisol but the practice of contact with the Source of love that underlies the personality's noise.

## Critique

**Clinical evidence**: Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn partly from the same context (UMass, 1979), has an extensive evidence base for reducing anxiety, depression, and chronic pain. Borysenko's clinical program precedes or coincides with the formalisation of MBSR and shares its foundations.

**Secularisation concerns**: Buddhist teachers have raised questions about whether mindfulness divorced from its ethical and philosophical context (the Eightfold Path, the understanding of suffering's causes) retains its deepest effectiveness. Borysenko integrates mindfulness within a broader spiritual framework (including forgiveness, meaning, connection to the Inner Self) that arguably preserves more of its original context than purely technique-focused approaches.

**The awareness-change gap**: Mindfulness increases awareness of patterns; it does not automatically change them. Some patients become more skilled at observing their anxiety without relief from it. The cognitive restructuring and emotional completion work (Chapters 5–7 of the Mind/Body Program) are necessary complements to pure mindfulness practice.
