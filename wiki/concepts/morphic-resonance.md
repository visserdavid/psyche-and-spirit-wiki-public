# Morphic Resonance

**Summary**: Rupert Sheldrake's hypothesis that the form and behaviour of a system — from a crystal to an organism to a habit — is shaped not only by known physical law but by a cumulative, non-energetic resonance with all previous relevantly similar systems, unattenuated by distance or lapse of time; proposed as the mechanism of inheritance, instinct, and the propagation of newly learned behaviour across an entire species.

**Sources**:
- [Sheldrake, R. (1981). *A new science of life: The hypothesis of morphic resonance*. Blond & Briggs.](../sources/a-new-science-of-life.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-18

---

## The Core Hypothesis

Sheldrake starts from a problem he says mechanistic biology has never actually solved: DNA specifies which proteins a cell can make, but not the three-dimensional *form* a tissue, organ, or organism takes. A human arm and a human leg contain identical cell types and identical DNA, yet take different shapes; a fertilised egg develops epigenetically into structures nowhere pre-formed in it; damaged or partial embryos regulate — reaching the same normal end-form by a different route (Hans Driesch's classic 1890s experiments, in which isolated blastomeres from a two-celled sea-urchin embryo each grew into a complete, if small, sea urchin). Appeals to a "genetic programme," Sheldrake argues, quietly restore exactly the goal-directed, information-bearing language that mechanistic biology claims to have eliminated, without specifying any actual physical mechanism for it.

His alternative: **morphogenetic fields** — non-energetic, organising structures, hierarchically nested (a molecule's field is subordinate to the field of the crystal it forms part of; a cell's to the tissue's; a tissue's to the organ's; an organ's to the whole organism's) — that impose pattern on otherwise energetically indeterminate physical and chemical processes. A morphogenetic field does not supply energy; it "selects" among possibilities already permitted by physics and chemistry, the way an architectural plan selects a specific house shape from bricks and labour without itself containing any of the energy that builds the house.

The harder question is where a field's specific structure comes from. Sheldrake's proposed answer is **morphic resonance**: a system's form is progressively stabilised by the cumulative influence of all previous systems of sufficiently similar form, acting non-energetically across space and time, by a process analogous to (but not reducible to) resonance in physics — a tuning fork's sympathetic vibration, or radio reception. Unlike energetic resonance, morphic resonance is proposed to be unattenuated by distance ("as effective ten thousand miles as a yard") and cumulative — the millionth crystal of a given type resonates with the pooled influence of every previous crystal of that type, not merely the most recent one. Applied across the whole hierarchy of nature, morphic resonance is offered as a single mechanism spanning:

- **Crystallisation**: newly synthesised chemicals crystallise with difficulty the first few times and progressively more easily afterward — conventionally explained by physical seed contamination, but (Sheldrake argues) not fully accounted for by it.
- **Embryological development and regeneration**: the [Rites of Passage](rites-of-passage-schema.md)-like tripartite structure of morphogenesis — germ, canalised pathway ("chreode," borrowing Waddington's term), and stabilised final form — reinforced generation after generation.
- **Inheritance of form**: alongside genetic inheritance, organisms are proposed to inherit a species' morphogenetic fields via resonance with the accumulated form of past members — offering, Sheldrake argues, a non-Lamarckian mechanism by which environmentally or behaviourally acquired characteristics could still become progressively "hereditary" without any change to DNA.
- **Instinct and habit**: instincts are the most deeply and repeatedly reinforced chreodes (behavioural forms), habits the most individually specific; Sheldrake treats the difference between instinct and learned habit as one of degree, not kind.
- **Cross-population learning**: if morphic resonance is real, a behaviour mastered by one population should become progressively easier for genetically similar, physically unconnected populations to learn — his single most consequential and most contested prediction.

## The McDougall Rat Experiments

Sheldrake's central piece of supporting evidence is a real, decades-long controversy in experimental psychology. William McDougall, at Harvard, began in 1920 to test whether rats trained across many generations to escape a water-tank via a non-illuminated gangway would show a Lamarckian decline in errors — he expected, and disproved, direct transmission of the trained skill to the offspring of trained parents specifically. But across 32 generations and 15 years, *both* the deliberately trained line **and** a parallel untrained control line bred from the same original stock showed the same steadily falling average error rate (from roughly 165 errors in generation one to under 20 by later generations) — a result inexplicable by ordinary genetics or by Lamarckian inheritance, since the untrained rats had never performed the task at all.

Two attempted replications produced a genuinely mixed record. F. A. E. Crew's Edinburgh replication (18 generations) initially found no such trend — but Crew's rats started performing far better from generation one than McDougall's had (suggesting his stock was already carrying some prior influence) and suffered from severe unplanned inbreeding depression that likely masked any improvement. W. E. Agar's Melbourne replication (50 generations, 20 years) found the same improvement in both trained and untrained lines that McDougall had — including an unexplained drop in average errors in Agar's own untrained control stock between 1927 and 1932 that Agar could not account for by any conventional factor.

Sheldrake reads the combined record — no Lamarckian transmission specific to trained lineages, but a persistent decline shared across both trained and untrained lines within the same overall population — as consistent with morphic resonance and inconsistent with either standard genetic or Lamarckian explanations, while acknowledging the results are not conclusive and proposing a cleaner future test: train a large population at one location, then measure whether a small, geographically distant, never-trained population of the same strain learns the same task measurably faster than the first population originally did.

## Reception and Critique

*A New Science of Life* is explicitly framed by Sheldrake as scientific and falsifiable — he proposes specific, in-principle testable experiments (crystallisation timing under controlled seed-free conditions; the cross-population rat-learning design above) — a framing central to the dispute over how the book should be classified.

**The *Nature* "book for burning" episode**: the controversy reached its highest pitch when *Nature*'s editor John Maddox published an anonymous editorial (24 September 1981) titled "A book for burning?", calling the book "the best candidate for burning" in years and arguing that Sheldrake's proposed experiments were unfalsifiable in practice — any negative result could always be attributed to an unmeasured morphogenetic field, and no funding agency would ever support the research, so the theory could never actually be tested to anyone's satisfaction. Physicist Brian Josephson (Cambridge, Nobel laureate) wrote in Sheldrake's defence that the same objection — no account yet of the underlying mechanism, dismissed as "unscientific" on that basis — was once levelled at electricity and magnetism before their laws were known, and that Bohm's [implicate order](implicate-order.md) faced a structurally similar dismissal. Reviewer Lewis Wolpert (a leading developmental biologist) took the opposite position at length in the *Guardian*: that morphic resonance explains everything and therefore nothing, is not engaged with the actual experimental data of developmental biology (e.g. hydra regeneration, which he argues is well accounted for by known physico-chemical mechanisms), and exemplifies a public misunderstanding of how science actually advances — through laborious data-gathering and argument, not through romantic reversals of dismissed heretics being vindicated. Sheldrake himself, in a later BBC Radio 4 debate with Maddox, maintained that the theory's chief virtue was making a specific, falsifiable claim at all, where existing "genetic programme" language made none.

Mainstream biology's verdict has not shifted materially since 1981: morphic resonance remains classified as pseudoscience by the great majority of biologists and physicists, on the grounds that (1) no mechanism reconciling non-attenuated, non-energetic action across space and time with known physics has ever been proposed, even in outline; (2) the supporting evidence — the McDougall/Crew/Agar rat data foremost among it — is old, contested, and admits of alternative explanations (inbreeding effects, undocumented changes in animal-husbandry conditions across decades, statistical artefacts); and (3) the "hundredth monkey effect," which entered popular culture as an informal illustration of the same idea, has since been shown by researchers who checked the original Japanese macaque studies (most thoroughly by Ron Amundson, 1985) to be an unsupported, essentially fabricated anecdote with no basis in the underlying primatology. Sheldrake has continued an active, self-funded programme of research and public advocacy in the decades since (later work extends into telepathy and the "sense of being stared at"), without achieving acceptance within mainstream biology.

**Note**: This wiki does not adjudicate between Sheldrake's hypothesis and mainstream biology's rejection of it; both the claim and the critique above represent the strongest form of each side's case, per the wiki's balance-and-critique convention.

## Connections in the Wiki

**[Jung's collective unconscious](collective-unconscious.md)**: Sheldrake explicitly discusses Jung's collective unconscious in *A New Science of Life* as a psychological precedent for morphic resonance — both propose a form of inherited, trans-individual "memory" not encoded in any individual brain or genome. The parallel is structural but the domains differ: Jung's collective unconscious is a psychic/archetypal hypothesis about shared human imagery and instinct; Sheldrake's morphic fields are proposed to operate across all of nature, from crystals to organisms to behaviour, and are framed (contestedly) as a physical rather than purely psychological claim. Sheldrake goes so far as to suggest that if memories are not stored physically in the brain — a possibility he raises via K. S. Lashley's failure, after decades of research, to localise a single memory trace — then Jung's notion of an inherited collective unconscious "containing archetypal forms" could be reinterpreted as a special case of morphic resonance in nervous systems.

**[Implicate Order](implicate-order.md)**: Sheldrake discusses his hypothesis directly with David Bohm in a dialogue reproduced in the book's appendix. Bohm suggests morphogenetic fields can be understood as a "set of potentialities" whose realisation depends on "the extent of past history and the extent of creativity" — directly analogous to how the implicate order's enfolded potential unfolds into explicate form. Both hypotheses propose non-local, non-energetic organising structures underlying visible form, and both were received by mainstream physics/biology as suggestive but empirically unconfirmed philosophical extensions beyond what the data requires.

**[Omega Hypothesis](omega-hypothesis.md)**: Kenneth Ring adopts morphic resonance, via Peter Russell's *The Global Brain* (1983), as the proposed mechanism by which the transformation undergone by individual near-death experiencers could accumulate into a species-wide shift in consciousness — the layer of Ring's synthesis this wiki already flags as its most speculative link, now traceable to its primary source.

**[Seven-Layer Auric Field](seven-layer-auric-field.md)** and **[Hands of Light (source)](../sources/hands-of-light.md)**: Barbara Ann Brennan cites Sheldrake's morphogenetic fields as a scientific-sounding parallel to the Human Energy Field's organising function — both propose that biological form is shaped by a field-like structure beyond known physical anatomy. Brennan's HEF is at least partly grounded in claimed direct clairvoyant observation and some laboratory correlation studies (Hunt/Bruyere); Sheldrake's fields are, by his own design, meant to be indirectly testable through population-level statistical effects rather than direct perception.

**[Archetypes: Who Are You? (source)](../sources/archetypes-who-are-you.md)**: Myss cites Sheldrake in her Visionary-archetype chapter as an example of a credentialled scientist whose ideas exceed the boundaries mainstream science is comfortable with — illustrative of the Visionary's characteristic tension between genuine insight and premature or unfalsifiable claims (see [Archetypes: Who Are You? § The Visionary's Dark Shadow](../sources/archetypes-who-are-you.md)).
