# Mysticism's Four Marks

**Summary**: William James's phenomenology of mystical states in *The Varieties of Religious Experience* — ineffability, noetic quality, transiency, and passivity — offered as an empirical, cross-tradition description of what mystical experience actually consists of, independent of any particular theology.

**Sources**:
- [James, W. (1902). *The varieties of religious experience: A study in human nature*. Longmans, Green.](../sources/the-varieties-of-religious-experience.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-20

---

## The Four Marks

James proposes that any experience, regardless of its doctrinal or cultural setting, may be called mystical in his technical sense only if it exhibits these characteristics:

1. **Ineffability**: the experience "defies expression" — it cannot be adequately reported in words, and must, like an emotional state, be directly experienced to be understood. James likens this to trying to convey the quality of a feeling to someone who has never had it.

2. **Noetic quality**: although resembling states of feeling, mystical states carry for those who have them a sense that they are also states of *knowledge* — "illuminations, revelations, full of significance and importance" — a sense of insight into depths of truth unplumbed by the discursive intellect.

3. **Transiency**: mystical states cannot be sustained for long; James estimates half an hour or less as typical, with rare exceptions. When they fade, they can seldom be reproduced faithfully in memory, though they are recognized when they recur, and between occurrences their quality is felt to develop in inner richness and importance.

4. **Passivity**: although preparatory voluntary practices (fixing attention, performing bodily exercises, meditative techniques) can facilitate mystical states, once they set in the mystic feels their own will to be in abeyance — grasped and held by a superior power. James connects this mark to certain phenomena he treats elsewhere in the lectures as cognate — automatic writing, mediumship, prophetic speech — as varying degrees of the same subliminal irruption.

## Cross-Tradition Survey

James applies this four-mark framework across a deliberately wide range of material to argue for a common structural core beneath doctrinal variation: lower-grade mystical states (the sense of deepened meaning under nitrous oxide or alcohol — his own experiments with the former inform this section directly), nature mysticism, "cosmic consciousness" (citing R. M. Bucke), Yoga, Buddhist mysticism, Sufism, and the Christian mystical tradition (St. Teresa of Ávila, St. John of the Cross). His conclusion is not that all traditions say the same thing doctrinally, but that a common experiential core — the sense of union with something larger, felt as more real than ordinary sensory reality — recurs beneath the doctrinal overlays each tradition supplies to interpret it.

## The Pragmatic (Fruits) Criterion Applied to Mysticism

James extends his general "fruits, not roots" test (see [Healthy-Mindedness and the Sick Soul](healthy-mindedness-and-sick-soul.md)) specifically to mystical states: their authority for the person who has them is absolute and cannot be dislodged by argument, but that authority does not automatically extend to outsiders, nor does it settle metaphysical questions about the nature of ultimate reality. What can be assessed, empirically, is whether a given mystical experience produces the durable fruits James associates with genuine spiritual value: increased charity, humility, and courage; diminished egotism; and a lasting rather than merely momentary change in the person's orientation to life. Visionary or ecstatic states that leave no such fruits — that are "fruitless" or even destructive in conduct and character — are, on James's own criterion, to be judged unfavorably regardless of how vivid or ineffable they felt from the inside.

## Cross-Wiki Connections

### Near-Death Experience and Closet Mystics
Joan Borysenko's survey data on the prevalence of unreported mystical experience among ordinary Americans (see [Closet Mystics](closet-mystics.md)) — being "bathed in light," a sense of union, contact with the deceased — describes, sociologically, the same class of phenomena James catalogues from written testimony a century earlier; James's four marks (especially ineffability and the fear of ridicule it produces) also help explain why such experiences tend to stay in the closet: an experience that "defies expression" is, by its own nature, hard to share credibly.

### Collective Unconscious
James's account of the mystic's sense of union with a wider reality that is "more, not merely other" than the ordinary self runs in a parallel track to [Carl Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s [collective unconscious](collective-unconscious.md): both propose that ordinary waking consciousness is embedded in and occasionally opened onto something larger and shared, though James frames this in the vocabulary of subliminal consciousness and religious experience while Jung frames it as an inherited psychic structure populated by archetypes. See [William James](../thinkers/william-james.md) for the direct historical influence James had on Jung's thinking.

### Sacred Healing and Altered States
[Norman Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md)'s survey of cross-cultural mystical and healing traditions in [*Sacred Healing*](../sources/sacred-healing.md) covers much of the same devotional and mystical literature (Christian, Sufi, Buddhist) that James draws on, from a clinician's rather than a philosopher-psychologist's vantage point.

## Critique

**Category breadth**: James's four marks are broad enough to potentially include drug-induced states, certain psychiatric symptoms (depersonalization, some psychotic experiences), and ordinary aesthetic or emotional peak experiences, alongside what most religious traditions would consider genuine contemplative attainment. James is aware of this and treats it as a virtue (a maximally inclusive, non-doctrinal starting definition) rather than a flaw, but subsequent scholars of mysticism (e.g., the critique surveyed in the Routledge centenary edition's introductions) have argued the four marks under-differentiate phenomena that traditions themselves consider quite distinct.

**Note (mainstream contrast)**: Contemporary neuroscience of religion (e.g., Andrew Newberg's and Eugene D'Aquili's neuroimaging studies of meditators, or Michael Persinger's temporal-lobe stimulation research) has attempted to identify neural correlates for James's four marks. James's own methodological warning — that identifying a physiological or neurological correlate for a state settles nothing about its spiritual value (his "medical materialism" critique) — anticipates and directly rebuts the stronger reductive claims sometimes made from this research (e.g., that mystical experience simply *is* temporal lobe activity); this is a live tension the wiki's [Healthy-Mindedness and the Sick Soul](healthy-mindedness-and-sick-soul.md) page treats at greater length.
