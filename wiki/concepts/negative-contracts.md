# Negative Contracts

**Summary**: Unconscious behavioural agreements formed in childhood — particularly in parent-child relationships — that persist into adult life, creating relational traps based on a core belief about what one must do or be to receive love; a central concept in Brennan's *Light Emerging* (1993) for understanding how early wounds propagate through adult relationships into physical illness.

**Sources**:
- [Brennan, B. A. (1993). *Light emerging: The journey of personal healing*. Bantam Books.](../sources/light-emerging.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Overview

In Chapter 13 of *[Light Emerging](../sources/light-emerging.md)*, [Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md) introduces the concept of **negative contracts** as a tool for understanding why people repeatedly recreate painful relational patterns despite intending not to. Every relationship is structured, in part, by implicit contracts — unspoken agreements about how the parties will relate. Healthy contracts support mutual growth and creativity. Negative contracts, formed in childhood around wounds, lock both parties into behavioural patterns that perpetuate the wound rather than heal it.

Negative contracts are not agreements consciously chosen. They arise from the child's mind attempting to create safety and connection in conditions where genuine safety was not available. The result is a belief ("I must do X to receive Y") that becomes so fundamental to the person's relational world that it is experienced as truth rather than belief.

## The Structure of a Negative Contract

Brennan provides a chart format for mapping negative contracts with six components:

| Column | Label | What it contains |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Don't/Won't | The action the person believes they must perform to get their need met |
| 2 | Belief | What they believe they will receive if they perform the action |
| 3 | Unconscious Belief | The underlying assumption driving the contract |
| 4 | Immediate Price | The cost of performing the contract-action |
| 5 | Belief Results | The actual life pattern that results |
| 6 | True Price | The deep, life-limiting cost of maintaining the contract |

### Example: Gary's Caretaking Contract

Brennan traces the development of Gary's negative contract:

**Background**: Gary's mother is exhausted and preoccupied. She does not give Gary the attention he needs. Gary discovers through trial and error that helping and taking care of his mother produces attention. He conflates this attention with love. He concludes: *"To get love, I must take care of others."*

**The contract**:
- *Don't/Won't*: Take care of mother / of women in his life
- *Belief*: I will receive love
- *Unconscious Belief*: I must take care of the women I love in order to receive their love; if I don't take care of them, they won't love me
- *Immediate Price*: I give energy, time, and resources to caretaking without reciprocal care
- *Belief Results*: "Relationships suck my energy and don't fill my needs"
- *True Price*: I attract relationships that confirm the belief (needy, dependent women); the caretaking is exhausting and produces resentment; I isolate to avoid the drain; I never experience genuine love

**The reinforcement loop**: Each time Gary takes care of a woman and receives some form of approval, the contract is confirmed. Each time he fails to take care of her and loses the relationship, the contract is confirmed again. The contract is self-sealing — its very structure makes it nearly impossible to disconfirm from inside the contract.

## How Contracts Form

Negative contracts arise from the meeting of a childhood need with an inadequate environment. The child's cognitive development is too limited to accurately assess the situation:

- The child cannot understand the parent's depression, exhaustion, or limitations as belonging to the parent; instead, the child experiences these as contingent on the child's own actions
- The child cannot distinguish between "I am not getting attention *right now*" and "I will not receive love unless I perform this action"
- The child's logic creates the simplest available rule that explains the experience: "If A, then B; if not A, then not B"

The beliefs generated by negative contracts share characteristics with Brennan's [image conclusions](time-conglomerates.md):
- They are derived from limited childhood logic
- They are treated as absolute truths
- They are generalised from specific events to universal principles
- They recruit supporting evidence and dismiss contradicting evidence

## Dissolving a Negative Contract

Brennan provides a process for identifying and dissolving negative contracts:

**Step 1: Identify the contract**
Use the six-column chart. Start with a recurring relational problem and trace it back to the earliest instance of the same pattern. Identify the belief that underlies the pattern.

**Step 2: Trace the belief to its origin**
Find the childhood experience that created the belief. This is not about blaming the parent — the parent's limitations are understood as coming from their own wounds and circumstances. The goal is clarity about where the belief came from and why it made sense to the child at that time.

**Step 3: Observe the true price**
Map clearly how the contract is limiting life now — what areas of life are affected, what experiences have been unavailable because of the contract's operation.

**Step 4: Challenge the belief**
Test the belief against adult reality: "Is it true that I must take care of others to receive love? Is there evidence that people can receive love without being caretakers? Can I identify experiences that contradict the belief?"

**Step 5: Transform the behaviour**
The contract changes as the underlying behaviour changes. Gary stops caretaking women who don't need it. This will initially feel like abandonment (the contract predicts it); riding through this discomfort without reverting to the contract-behaviour is the core of the transformation process.

**Step 6: Track the results**
As the contract dissolves, the areas of life affected by it begin to shift. New relationship patterns become possible. The positive version (Figure 13-2 in the book) shows the life outcomes of dissolving Gary's contract: authentic relationships without energy drain, genuine love without caretaking.

## Contracts with Groups and with the Planet

Brennan extends the contract model beyond individual relationships:

**Group contracts**: Families, organisations, and cultures also operate under collective contracts. A family contract might be "we don't talk about feelings" or "achievement earns love." These contracts shape every individual within the group.

**Planetary contracts**: At the broadest scale, humanity's relationship with the earth is also shaped by a negative contract — one in which the earth's resources are exploited rather than respected, based on a belief that the earth is a resource rather than a living partner. Healing the individual-level negative contracts is, in Brennan's view, part of the broader project of healing humanity's relationship with the planet.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

**Myss's biography-is-biology**: Negative contracts are one of the primary mechanisms through which [biography becomes biology](biography-is-biology.md). The chronic energy expenditure required to maintain a contract, and the chronic areas of life deprivation the contract creates, deplete specific chakras and auric levels over decades.

**Myss's woundology**: [Woundology](woundology.md) describes the use of wound identity as social currency. Negative contracts are the structural mechanism behind woundology: the contract encodes the belief that the wound is the source of connection, so the wound must be maintained.

**Myss's Sacred Contracts**: Myss's [Sacred Contracts](sacred-contracts.md) are the positive soul-level agreements that give life its purpose. Negative contracts are their shadow counterpart — agreements that arise from wounded ego development and obstruct the fulfilment of Sacred Contracts.

**Keleman's patterns**: The [somatic distress patterns](somatic-distress-patterns.md) that Keleman describes — especially the compacted and rigid structures — reflect the somatic crystallisation of long-maintained negative contracts: the body organises itself around the chronic effort to perform the contract-action and avoid the feared loss.

**Jung's shadow projection**: The operation of negative contracts involves projection — the parts of the self denied by the contract are projected onto relational partners. Gary projects his need to be cared for onto women he then caretakes. Jung's [shadow](shadow.md) integration and Brennan's contract dissolution address overlapping territory.

## Critique

**Systemic dimensions**: Brennan's contract model focuses on individual psychology and dyadic relationships. Structural, social, and economic factors (poverty, racism, patriarchy) that constrain relational possibilities are not addressed. Some patterns that look like negative contracts are rational adaptations to genuinely limited environments.

**The intervention approach**: Brennan's chart-based approach is one of several tools for working with relational patterns. The Narrative Therapy approach to "externalising" problems, schema therapy's work with early maladaptive schemas, and attachment theory all address similar territory through different methods, each with their own evidence base.

**Note**: The concept of unconscious relational scripts based on childhood experiences is well-established in mainstream psychology (object relations theory, attachment theory, schema therapy). Brennan's specific contribution is the energy-field mapping of these patterns, which is not part of mainstream psychological frameworks.
