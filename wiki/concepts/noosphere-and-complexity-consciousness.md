# Noosphere and the Law of Complexity-Consciousness

**Summary**: Pierre Teilhard de Chardin's claim that matter and consciousness are two faces of the same underlying stuff — a "Without" and a "Within" — and that as matter becomes more complex, its interior consciousness deepens correspondingly, culminating in the **noosphere**: a planetary layer of collective human thought superposed over the biosphere, itself converging toward the [Omega Point](omega-hypothesis.md).

## The Within and Without of Things

Teilhard's foundational move, in *[The Phenomenon of Man](../sources/the-phenomenon-of-man.md)*, is to reject the sharp Cartesian split between mechanistic matter and immaterial mind. Every element of the universe, he argues, has two inseparable faces: a **Without** (its external, physical, measurable arrangement — the proper subject of ordinary physics and chemistry) and a **Within** (its interior, psychic dimension — "a kind of psychism, however rudimentary"). Even the atom, on this view, has some infinitesimal trace of interiority; what changes across the evolutionary ascent is not the presence or absence of a Within, but its degree of organization and intensity.

This is close to philosophical **panpsychism** (mind, or proto-mind, as a fundamental and ubiquitous feature of matter, not an emergent add-on unique to brains) — though Teilhard's version is explicitly evolutionary rather than static: the Within does not merely coexist alongside the Without at every level, it *deepens* as the Without becomes more intricately organized.

## The Law of Complexity-Consciousness

Teilhard proposes this correlation as a general law of the whole cosmic process: **as arrangements of matter become more complex — richer, more centred, more organized — their interior consciousness correspondingly increases.** He traces the progression through:

- **Pre-life**: the "granulation" of matter into atoms, then molecules, then "mega-molecules" (an intermediate zone he identifies, prophetically, with what would later be understood as viruses and giant proteins)
- **The cellular revolution**: the emergence of the cell as a qualitatively new, more tightly centred unit of organization, accompanied (Teilhard argues) by a qualitative leap in interiority
- **The expansion of life**: reproduction, multiplication, conjugation, association, and **controlled additivity** (Teilhard's term, borrowing the older biological concept of **orthogenesis**, for the directional accumulation of complexity across generations) driving an "invincible ascent" of life toward ever more complex, ever more conscious forms
- **Hominisation**: the "threshold of reflection" — man's distinguishing leap to not only *knowing* but *knowing that he knows* ("non seulement connaître, mais se connaître") — which Teilhard treats as the most decisive qualitative discontinuity in the entire series

Teilhard is explicit that this is offered as an empirical, if philosophically loaded, generalization from the fossil and biological record — "not the discovery of... causal relations... only... the experimental law of recurrence" — rather than a claim about the underlying mechanism of any single mutation or selection event.

## The Noosphere

As humanity's population, mobility, and above all its capacity for shared thought increase, Teilhard argues, a new planetary envelope is forming: the **noosphere** (a term he coined in 1925, from Greek *noos*, mind) — layered over the biosphere just as the biosphere is layered over the inorganic lithosphere. The noosphere is not a metaphor for culture in the ordinary sense; Teilhard means it literally, as the next real, physical-and-psychic stratum of the planet, produced by the same law of complexity-consciousness that produced the cell and the organism, now operating at planetary scale through the "confluence of thought" — science, communication, and the increasing interdependence of human minds.

The noosphere's own trajectory, on Teilhard's account, is convergent rather than merely expansive: increasing population pressure and increasing shared knowledge both drive humanity toward tighter psychic unification, culminating in the [Omega Point](omega-hypothesis.md) — see that page for the full treatment of Omega's four attributes (autonomy, actuality, irreversibility, transcendence) and its adoption by [Kenneth Ring](../thinkers/kenneth-ring.md) in *[Heading Toward Omega](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md)*.

## Relationship to Other Wiki Concepts

**[Collective Unconscious](collective-unconscious.md)**: Jung and Teilhard were near-exact contemporaries (Jung 1875–1961, Teilhard 1881–1955) independently proposing a supra-individual psychic layer binding humanity together — Jung's collective unconscious as an inherited, largely static substrate of archetypal structure, Teilhard's noosphere as an emergent, still-developing, historically accumulating layer with an explicit evolutionary direction. There is no evidence the two men influenced each other directly; the resemblance is a striking parallel rather than a documented intellectual debt, and the differences are real: Jung's collective unconscious explains the recurrence of the *same* symbolic patterns across cultures and eras, while Teilhard's noosphere is specifically about the *increase* of interconnection and complexity over historical time — a claim Jung's framework does not make and does not need.

**[Omega Hypothesis](omega-hypothesis.md)**: Ring's entire evolutionary framework for near-death experiences rests on Teilhard's law of complexity-consciousness and Omega Point, adopted largely without engaging Teilhard's own theological grounding for them (see that page's critique section).

## Critique

**Mainstream evolutionary biology** rejects the Law of Complexity-Consciousness as a *scientific* law: the modern evolutionary synthesis holds that natural selection has no built-in directionality, and that "increasing complexity" is neither guaranteed nor, where it does occur locally, evidence of any general cosmic trend toward consciousness. Julian Huxley — himself an evolutionary biologist and a personal friend of Teilhard's, who wrote the book's introduction — praised the noosphere concept as "valuable and thought-provoking" while explicitly declining to follow Teilhard into the stronger metaphysical claim that consciousness is a *goal* evolution is aimed at, rather than simply a contingent and locally advantageous outcome in one lineage. The philosopher-biologist Peter Medawar's 1961 review was considerably harsher, characterizing the entire argument as an exercise in obscurantism.

**Panpsychism itself** remains a minority but not fringe position in contemporary philosophy of mind (compare, within analytic philosophy, more recent integrated-information-theory-adjacent proposals); Teilhard's version is distinctive for embedding it in an explicitly evolutionary and theological narrative rather than treating it as a static metaphysical thesis.
