# Parental Fallacy

**Summary**: Hillman's term for the cultural and psychological error of attributing character, calling, and life destiny primarily to parental influence — a fantasy he argues is unsupported by biographical evidence, contradicted by behavioral genetics research, and culturally harmful.

**Sources**:
- [Hillman, J. (1996). *The soul's code: In search of character and calling*. Random House.](../sources/souls-code.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## The Claim

The parental fallacy, in [James Hillman](../thinkers/james-hillman.md)'s framework, is the assumption — pervasive in modern psychology, therapy, popular culture, and family discourse — that who a person becomes is primarily determined by what their parents did or failed to do. The fallacy takes many forms:

- The devoted, encouraging mother (Cole Porter, Van Cliburn, Pablo Casals) produced the greatness
- The cold, absent, hostile, or neglectful parent (Stravinsky, Diane Arbus, Barbara McClintock, Tina Turner) damaged or stunted the child
- The father's absence explains the son's violence, addiction, or emotional unavailability
- The mother's overprotection explains the child's inability to separate

Hillman argues all of these are versions of the same error: assigning causal power to parents that belongs, in his framework, to the daimon — the soul's own guiding image.

## The Biographical Evidence Against It

Hillman examines the mothers and fathers of dozens of eminent figures and finds no consistent pattern:

**Devoted, supporting mothers** produced exceptional children: Cole Porter (mother arranged piano lessons in a distant town), Van Cliburn (mother was his piano teacher), Pablo Casals (mother carried him on a mule through the Pyrenees to find a teacher).

**But so did neglectful, hostile, or absent mothers**: Barbara McClintock's mother was too overwhelmed to care for her and sent her to live with relatives — the solitude gave McClintock what she needed. Edna St. Vincent Millay's mother yanked her out of school over a dispute and left her alone most of the time — Millay flourished. Tina Turner: "no love from my mother or father" — yet she found her mission in music.

**Mothers who opposed their children's calling** still produced greatness: Stravinsky's mother never recognized *Le Sacre du Printemps*. Diane Arbus's mother wanted advantages, not vision. Ingmar Bergman's mother consulted a pediatrician about his "dangerous" fantasy life.

The variation goes in every direction. Hillman concludes: the calling was not produced by the parenting. The daimon selected the parents as the circumstances best suited to the soul's image — including the obstacles.

## The Behavioral Genetics Support

Hillman is not arguing solely from anecdote. He reviews the behavioral genetics research on twins (Robert Plomin, Judy Dunn) and finds:

- Identical twins raised apart show remarkably similar tastes, habits, careers, and preferences — suggesting strong genetic contribution to character
- Yet the *shared family environment* (same parents, same house, same values) accounts for surprisingly little of the variance between siblings and twins
- What accounts for differences is *unshared* environment — the individual's own idiosyncratic experiences, which the research cannot fully explain
- Creativity and traditionalism show different heredity profiles from IQ — suggesting multiple independent dimensions of character
- IQ heritability actually *increases* through childhood (peaking around ages 7-8, then declining) — contradicting the model that parental influence accumulates over time

Hillman reads these findings as consistent with his thesis: something individual and irreducible — the image — shapes the person in ways that neither genes nor parenting can fully account for.

## Why the Fallacy Persists

Hillman offers several explanations for the fallacy's cultural grip:

1. **Comfort**: The mother myth provides a stable explanatory framework. If parents caused the child, the world is understandable and controllable.
2. **Misplaced divinity**: The powers once attributed to ancestors, gods, and spirits have been collapsed into Mom and Dad. Parents have been "divinized" — assigned the causal force that properly belongs to the invisible realm.
3. **Ideology**: Post-Enlightenment, Protestant, individualist culture needs to explain outcomes through observable human causes. The daimon is invisible; the mother is not.
4. **Therapeutic convention**: Psychotherapy's default is to begin with the parents — Freud, Klein, Bowlby, Winnicott all centre the mother. The therapeutic hour reproduces the fallacy professionally.
5. **Political/legal utility**: Parental responsibility for children's outcomes can be legislated, insured, and adjudicated. Daimonic calling cannot.

## The Damage the Fallacy Causes

Hillman argues the parental fallacy harms in multiple directions:

- **Children**: Labelled, drugged, and pathologized for behaviours that are the daimon's early articulations (hyperactivity, school refusal, fantasizing) rather than the effects of bad parenting
- **Parents**: Burdened with guilt for outcomes they neither caused nor could have prevented; "smothering" and "absentee" as equal condemnations regardless of the actual character of the parenting
- **Society**: Investment in parenting ideology as the solution to social problems (violence, addiction, failure) that have structural causes the fallacy obscures
- **Psychology**: Theoretical poverty — a field that cannot account for uniqueness, calling, and the visible reality that children with similar parents become entirely different people

## The Alternative

Hillman proposes replacing the parental fallacy with the acorn theory: the child arrives with a specific image, and the parenting situation is one of the circumstances the daimon *used* to bring that image into expression. The child is not shaped by the parents; the parents are selected by the child's daimon as the environment best suited to the calling — whether that means encouragement, neglect, opposition, or absence.

This does not mean parents don't matter. It means their influence is real but *instrumental* — they provide the material circumstances the daimon works with, not the image the daimon is working toward.

## Critical Assessment

**Overstatement**: Hillman's polemic is deliberately extreme. Developmental psychology and attachment theory have accumulated substantial evidence that early parenting does affect psychological outcomes — attachment security, emotional regulation capacity, stress response, cognitive development. Hillman's framework does not adequately account for this evidence.

**The abuse question**: Hillman's argument that neglect and abuse may be "what the daimon needed" risks minimizing the real damage that harmful parenting causes. His biographical examples are all eminent people who survived and flourished; this is a deeply selected sample. Many people subjected to neglect or abuse do not emerge with callings clarified but with capacities significantly impaired.

**The ecological argument**: Hillman himself acknowledges that the world — place, culture, natural environment, ancestors — parents the child as much as Mom and Dad do. This is his most interesting correction to the nuclear-family focus of modern psychology: Edith Cobb's research on children's imagination and nature, D.W. Winnicott's "facilitating environment," Gibson's concept of ecological affordances. The parental fallacy's opposite is not the daimon alone but the daimon embedded in a world.

**Mainstream perspective**: Developmental psychology, pediatrics, and attachment theory maintain that parenting quality is a significant predictor of outcomes. The fallacy critique is taken seriously as a corrective to over-blaming parents, but the wholesale dismissal of parental influence has no empirical support.

## Relation to Woundology

Hillman's parental fallacy connects to [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s concept of [woundology](woundology.md) — the cultural pattern of using childhood wounds as permanent identity and social currency. Both Hillman and Myss challenge the therapeutic culture's investment in childhood damage as the primary explanation for adult suffering and limitation. Both propose that an image or calling is more constitutive of identity than the wounds that accompany it.
