# Past-Life Therapy

**Summary**: A psychotherapeutic approach in which clients are guided into relaxed or hypnotic states and invited to explore apparent memories of previous lives; used by therapists including Brian Weiss, Roger Woolger, and Morris Netherton to resolve fears, phobias, and repetitive patterns that do not yield to conventional approaches; contextualised by Borysenko through Henry Corbin's "imaginal realm" and the concept of samskaras (Sanskrit: soul impressions that carry across lifetimes).

## Overview

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) approaches past-life therapy with careful scepticism that nonetheless takes the phenomenon seriously. Her core position: no one can prove that the "memories" accessed in past-life regression are literal records of historical lives. But the therapeutic benefit for many people is genuine — and that benefit does not depend on resolving the metaphysical question.

In [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md), Borysenko shares two personal past-life regression experiences and describes the healing effect each produced, while consistently framing the experiences as encounters with the "imaginal realm" rather than as verified historical facts.

## The Clinical Evidence

**[Brian Weiss](../thinkers/brian-weiss.md)** (*Many Lives, Many Masters*, 1988): A psychiatrist and chairman of the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Sinai Medical Center in Miami, Weiss had neither intention nor training in past-life therapy when a patient began spontaneously regressing to apparent past lives during hypnotic sessions. Over eighteen months, the regression work resolved phobias that conventional therapy had not touched. Weiss published the case history despite knowing the professional risk — the courage Borysenko explicitly acknowledges — because the therapeutic outcomes were too significant to keep private.

**Roger Woolger** (*Other Lives, Other Selves*, 1988): A Jungian analyst who stumbled into past-life regression work with clients and developed a rigorous clinical framework. Woolger's crucial theoretical contribution: the *literal truth* of whether the past-life memories are historical is therapeutically irrelevant. "What emerges from the unconscious is a truth — a truth about the person's *psychic reality*, whether or not it corresponds to historical fact." A past-life memory of being a soldier killed in battle, whether or not it records an actual life, carries emotional and somatic truth that can be processed and healed.

Woolger references Ian Stevenson's (University of Virginia) research collecting cases from the 1960s of children who spontaneously recall detailed past-life information — names, places, family situations — that can subsequently be verified. Critics note that these cases come primarily from cultures (India, Sri Lanka, Turkey) where reincarnation is part of the religious framework, potentially introducing bias. Stevenson's methodology is more rigorous than most in the field, but the cases remain contested.

## The Imaginal Realm

Henry Corbin, a French scholar of Islamic mysticism, proposed the concept of the *'alam al-mithal* — the "imaginal world" or "imaginal realm" (Corbin carefully distinguished this from "imaginary," which connotes mere fantasy). The imaginal realm is:

- **More real than the physical world** (in the metaphysical hierarchies of Islamic philosophy)
- **Less real than the purely spiritual** (divine archetypes)
- A realm of genuine existence with its own geography, inhabitants, and laws
- The dimension where dreams occur, where NDEs unfold, where shamans travel, where UFO abductions and past-life regressions seem to take place

For Corbin and the Sufi philosophers he studied, the imaginal realm is not subjective fantasy but an objective level of reality that can be entered and explored. The experiences it yields are real at that level, with genuine transformative power, even if their relationship to ordinary space-time history is not one-to-one.

Borysenko uses Corbin's framework to contextualise past-life therapy: the regression accesses the imaginal realm, which holds the soul's patterns and karma in a form that can be processed and healed. Whether the "life" one accesses corresponds to a literal historical existence is the wrong question; the right question is whether the pattern retrieved carries genuine emotional and somatic truth that can be worked through.

Physicist Fred Alan Wolf (*Dreaming Universe*): the imaginal realm provides the "twilight zone" between physical and spiritual reality where dreams, NDEs, past-life memories, and shamanic journeys all occur. Time and space do not apply in this realm in the ordinary way.

## Samskaras: The Soul's Impressions

In Eastern philosophy (Sanskrit *saṃskāra* — impression, imprint), samskaras are the impressions left on the soul by significant actions and experiences. They are the mechanism of karma: an action creates an impression; the impression seeks to complete itself through future experience; the process continues across lifetimes until the impression is resolved (through understanding, compassion, and forgiveness) and dissolves.

Samskaras are not carried in the physical body or the ordinary mind but in a deeper level of the soul — which is why past-life therapy accesses them through the imaginal realm rather than through conscious memory. The "compulsion to repeat" that Freud observed in neurosis is, in Eastern philosophy, the action of samskaras in a single lifetime — the soul seeking to complete its unfinished business.

Elizabeth Lawrence's **Mari El healing** (a system developed from Reiki by Ethel Lombardi) operates directly with body-stored memories at the chakra level. In Borysenko's personal sessions with Lawrence, clusters of past-life memories surfaced in association with childhood experiences — the same patterns revealing their deeper stratigraphy. Beth's method: invoke helpers from the other side (Mari = Mary; El = Elohim, God), lay the client on the floor, hold the energy field at specific points, allow memories to surface in the present moment, and guide the client through understanding and forgiveness until the residue clears.

The concept of karma-cleaning: clearing samskaras through therapy, forgiveness, and understanding is, in Eastern philosophy, the process of resolving karma — not acquiring new negative impressions, and dissolving existing ones, so that the compulsion to recreate suffering diminishes.

## Borysenko's Personal Experiences

Borysenko shares two personal past-life regression experiences, both of which provided insight and healing for patterns in her current life:

**The Egypt regression** (through imagery therapist Magaly Rodriguez Mossman, at a hospital conference): an impression of having been a temple priestess in Egypt who betrayed her sister by bringing her into an initiation she was not ready for. The grief and guilt from this "memory" corresponded precisely to patterns of guilt Borysenko had felt in present-life situations where she believed she had betrayed others through her choices. The regression provided a context in which the pattern made sense and in which forgiveness became possible.

**The medieval English regression** (through past-life therapist Elena Burton, during a three-hour floor session at Borysenko's home): a life as a clairvoyant woman in medieval England during the Crusades, married to a man named Morgan who was later responsible for her father's death. Condemned as a witch, imprisoned, and starved to death while remaining loyal to her children. The regression revealed the roots of a fear-of-betrayal pattern that had haunted Borysenko's professional life, particularly around a colleague who had taken credit for her work. Understanding the "lifetime" context dissolved resentment that present-life analysis could not touch.

The Hall of Lives: in Borysenko's Yom Kippur meditation, she is guided through the "City of Lights" (an imaginal realm she had encountered years before) to the Hall of Lives — a space containing the book of each soul's history. A computer screen shows her current life's patterns in relation to previous configurations. The meditation resolves the fear/hurt pattern associated with the work-credit betrayal; the resentment that had followed her for years dissolves.

## Theoretical Frames: Does It Matter Whether It's Real?

Borysenko explores four possible explanatory frameworks:

**1. Literal reincarnation**: The soul inhabits successive bodies; past-life memories are genuine historical records. Believed by 25% of the world population (Hindus and Buddhists) and by 28% of Americans (per Gallup). Supported by Stevenson's children's research cases; challenged by the concentration of Stevenson's cases in cultures with prior belief in reincarnation.

**2. Genetic or cultural memory**: Information from ancestors or cultural history is encoded in the body/psyche and surfaces under hypnosis as apparent past-life memory. Does not require literal reincarnation; accords with known facts about epigenetic inheritance and unconscious cultural absorption.

**3. Imaginal archetypes** (Woolger's position, closely aligned with Corbin's framework): past-life memories access archetypal patterns from the collective unconscious (Jung). The "Mary Magdalene" or "ancient Egyptian priestess" that appears in many regressions is not a historical individual but an archetypal configuration that the soul inhabits as a pattern. Multiple people can "be" the same archetype because they are accessing the same archetype, not competing for the same historical slot.

**4. Therapeutic metaphor**: The past-life framework is a particularly powerful form of metaphor that allows the psyche to access and process experiences it cannot reach through ordinary biographical work. The regression is not "false" but operates at the level of meaning rather than fact.

Borysenko's synthesis: the therapeutic benefit does not depend on choosing between these frames. The soul patterns that surface — betrayal, guilt, unfinished grief, unexplained fear — carry genuine emotional and somatic reality. Processing them through understanding and forgiveness produces genuine transformation. Whether that transformation resolves a "lifetime" from 800 years ago or a deep psychological configuration that the "past life" metaphor helped access is less important than the fact that the pattern is resolved.

## Critique

**Falsifiability**: No method for verifying past-life memory against historical record is reliable. Stevenson's children's cases are the strongest evidence, and they remain contested. The therapeutic value can be real (and is documented) without establishing the metaphysical claim.

**Suggestibility**: Hypnotic and relaxation states dramatically increase susceptibility to suggestion. A therapist who believes in reincarnation may inadvertently elicit "memories" shaped by their expectations. Leading questions, tone of voice, and implicit expectations all influence what surfaces. Responsible practitioners (Woolger, Weiss) acknowledge this and are careful about the framing of sessions.

**False Memory Syndrome**: The recovered-memory controversies of the 1990s established that hypnotic states can generate vivid, emotionally compelling memories of events that did not occur. Past-life memories carry the same risk — compelling phenomenology does not establish historical fact.

**Power differential**: The therapist who navigates the imaginal realm on behalf of a client holds significant authority. Past-life therapy requires more than technical skill; it requires ethical clarity about the boundary between facilitation and suggestion.
