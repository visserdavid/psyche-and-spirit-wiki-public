# Pathologizing

**Summary**: James Hillman's term for the psyche's innate tendency to fall into disorder, symptom, and morbidity, treated not as a malfunction to be cured but as a necessary, meaning-bearing activity of soul-making — a direct challenge to both the medical model of mental illness and the humanist "peak experience" alternative to it.

**Sources**:
- [Hillman, J. (1975). *Re-visioning psychology*. Harper & Row.](../sources/re-visioning-psychology.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-14

---

## The Claim

In the second essay of [*Re-Visioning Psychology*](../sources/re-visioning-psychology.md), Hillman argues that psychopathology — symptom, breakdown, morbid fantasy, compulsion — is not an aberration that healthy psychic functioning would otherwise avoid, but an intrinsic and necessary capacity of soul: the ability to "create illness, morbidity, disorder, abnormality, suffering... as an aspect of any and all behavior and experienced reflectively." He coins **pathologizing** (the verb form) to name this activity, distinguishing it from **psychopathology**, the clinical-diagnostic category built on top of it.

His argument proceeds by identifying three historical "styles of denial" that have obscured pathologizing's necessity:

1. **Nominalism**: The Enlightenment and 19th-century psychiatric project (Kraepelin's diagnostic taxonomies, DSM-style classification) that isolates and names disorders as if the label captured a natural kind, when in fact — Hillman argues, citing Karl Jaspers's early critique — diagnostic categories vary by era, culture, and even the individual clinician's training, with no stable referent behind the name.
2. **Nihilism**: The anti-psychiatric response (Thomas Szasz, R. D. Laing, Michel Foucault) that denies psychopathology exists at all, reducing it to a social-control mechanism used by power to suppress deviance — a critique Hillman finds partly valid (psychiatric labeling *has* been abused as social control) but insufficient, because it denies pathologizing's genuine necessity along with its abuse.
3. **Transcendence**: The humanist-therapeutic response (epitomized by Abraham Maslow's "peak experience") that acknowledges suffering only as a stage to be moved through en route to growth, health, and self-actualization — sweeping pathology "under the carpet" of an idealized, one-sided vision of human flourishing that Hillman calls "tender-minded" (borrowing William James's phrase).

Against all three, Hillman proposes reading symptoms as **mythological language**: depression as Saturn (slowness, darkness, weight, the senex), hysteria as Dionysus (dismemberment, possession, multiplicity), panic as Pan (sudden terror with no rational object). The symptom is not a malfunction pointing to an underlying cause to be corrected; it is a god manifesting through affliction — "Gods become diseases," as Hillman quotes Jung. Therapy's task is not to eliminate the symptom but to recognize which archetypal pattern is speaking through it, restoring to the affliction its own dignity and intelligibility rather than treating it as noise to be silenced.

## The Alchemical Model

Hillman finds a historical precedent for this reading in alchemy: processes named putrefaction, mortification, dissolution, and calcination were not failures of the alchemical work but necessary stages of transformation (*opus contra naturam*) — the raw material had to be broken down before it could be refined. He reads modern psychopathology's loss of this framework (following the decline of alchemy as a living discipline) as the reason psychology today has no vocabulary for pathologizing except the language of disease.

## Critique of the Medical Model

Hillman traces the "medical model" of psychopathology to the same nominalist impulse: pathologizing is treated as something to be diagnosed, treated, and cured, on the analogy of physical disease. He argues this model has failed on its own terms — psychoanalysis and psychotherapy have not produced cures in the medical sense, because the phenomena in question (complexes, symptoms, fantasies) are not disease processes but forms of soul-activity that resist elimination. Trying to "cure" pathologizing, in Hillman's view, is a category error: one cannot dismantle soul-making without dismantling soul.

## Mainstream Consensus and Contrast

This is one of the sharpest points of tension between the wiki's archetypal/depth-psychological material and mainstream clinical psychiatry. **Note: mainstream psychiatry and clinical psychology continue to operate on diagnostic-nosological classification** (DSM-5-TR, ICD-11) precisely of the kind Hillman calls "nominalism" — not because clinicians believe categories perfectly capture natural kinds, but because reliable, replicable diagnostic categories are the practical precondition for treatment research, epidemiology, insurance reimbursement, and evidence-based care. Antidepressants, CBT protocols, and psychiatric hospitalization criteria all depend on the diagnostic stability Hillman treats as a historically contingent fallacy. Mainstream psychiatry would respond that Hillman's framework, whatever its interpretive richness, offers no mechanism for evaluating which interventions actually reduce suffering — a question it does not consider its job to answer, since (per Hillman) reducing suffering is not necessarily soul-making's aim.

This tension echoes, without duplicating, the wiki's existing critique of Hillman's collaborator-in-spirit [Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md), whose medical intuition claims are similarly noted (see [The Psychology of Illness](the-psychology-of-illness.md)) as running ahead of what controlled studies support — though Myss's claims are empirical (about causal mechanisms of disease) where Hillman's are explicitly anti-empirical by design (about the *meaning*, not the mechanism, of pathology).
