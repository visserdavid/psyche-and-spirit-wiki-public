# Polytheistic Psychology

**Summary**: James Hillman's argument that the psyche is inherently plural — animated by many archetypal/mythic patterns (Zeus, Hera, Dionysus, Hermes, Saturn) rather than unified under one dominant principle — and his explicit critique of Jung's individuation-toward-the-Self as a "monotheistic" imposition on a psyche that is actually polytheistic.

**Sources**:
- [Hillman, J. (1975). *Re-visioning psychology*. Harper & Row.](../sources/re-visioning-psychology.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-14

---

## The Claim

In the fourth essay ("Dehumanizing: Soul-Making") of [*Re-Visioning Psychology*](../sources/re-visioning-psychology.md), Hillman states his most consequential thesis: psychological monotheism — the assumption that psychic health means integration toward a single, unified centre — misdescribes the actual structure of the psyche, which is inherently **polytheistic**: a field of multiple, sometimes conflicting archetypal presences (Zeus, Hera, Dionysus, Aphrodite, Hermes, Saturn) each with their own logic, values, and style of consciousness, none of which is entitled to rule the others.

Hillman is explicit that this is not literal religious polytheism (he is not proposing worship of Greek gods) but a **psychological** claim: "we speak of Gods not to invoke them literally... but because the perspective psychology needs... draws on this range of models." The Greek pantheon simply offers the most fully differentiated set of archetypal patterns available in the Western tradition — richer and more articulated than any single alternative myth-system — which is why he returns to it repeatedly as a working vocabulary.

## The Critique of Jung's Self

This is Hillman's most direct break from [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md), whose Institute in Zurich he had directed before founding archetypal psychology. Jung's model of [individuation](individuation.md) treats [the Self](the-self.md) — the archetype of totality, symbolized by the mandala and the quaternity — as both the origin and the goal of psychological development: the ego's task is to progressively integrate the [shadow](shadow.md) and [anima/animus](anima-animus.md) into a conscious relationship with this unifying centre.

Hillman calls this a **"crypto-monotheism"**: even though Jung recognized and named a plurality of archetypal figures (Shadow, Anima, Trickster, Great Mother), his system still orders them beneath a single governing principle — the Self — toward which they are all understood to be developing. Hillman argues this repeats, in psychological form, the same monotheistic structure that displaced the Greek and Renaissance polytheistic imagination: one god (the Self) presiding over many demoted, subordinate figures (the archetypes). For Hillman, true psychological pluralism requires that no archetype be granted structural priority over the others — the psyche's multiplicity is not a problem awaiting resolution into unity but the actual, permanent shape of soul.

**Important nuance**: Hillman explicitly respects Jung and works largely within concepts Jung supplied (archetype, complex, anima, shadow) — he positions himself as a post-Jungian revising Jung's system from within, not rejecting it wholesale. He credits Jung with rediscovering personifying and treating complexes as genuinely autonomous (see [Soul-Making and the Imaginal](soul-making.md)). The disagreement is specifically about the *telos* of individuation: whether integration should aim at a unifying centre at all, or whether soul-making can proceed indefinitely without resolving into wholeness.

This tension is already partially visible in the wiki's existing treatment of [Individuation](individuation.md) and [The Self](the-self.md) (both developed from *The Soul's Code*'s Acorn Theory, which softens the disagreement into a difference between "development toward wholeness" and "the unfolding of a pre-formed calling"). This ingest supplies the sharper, more explicitly stated version of the disagreement as Hillman originally argued it — the accusation of *monotheism* specifically, not just a difference of emphasis.

## Dehumanizing: The Critique of Humanism

Hillman pairs the anti-monotheism argument with a critique of **humanist psychology** — the tradition (Melanchthon's Reformation-era coining of "psychologia," through Sartre's existentialism, to Maslow's self-actualization) that places the human ego, will, and self-development at the centre of psychological concern. Hillman argues this "measure of mankind is man" stance is itself a symptom of monotheistic consciousness: just as one God displaced many gods, one measure (the developing human ego) displaced the many perspectives (archetypal, mythic, daimonic) through which the Greeks and Renaissance Neoplatonists understood a life.

His term for the corrective is **"dehumanizing"** — deliberately provocative, meaning not cruelty but the decentring of psychology away from the human ego as the sole locus of meaning, toward soul, myth, and image as forces that move through but are not reducible to the individual human subject. Symptoms, emotions, and moral judgments alike are read as archetypally structured (Aphrodite's love, Ares's rage, Hermes's cunning) rather than as purely personal achievements or failures of will — a position that also grounds Hillman's rejection of moralism (locating good/evil in ego choice) in favour of reading conflicts as clashes between competing archetypal perspectives.

Hillman finds his historical model for this de-centred, polytheistic psychology in Renaissance Neoplatonism, especially **Marsilio Ficino** — "Doctor of Soul" to Cosimo de' Medici — whose philosophy treated the soul (not man) as "the centre of the universe" and who practiced imaginative dialogue with a personified pantheon of classical figures without confusing this with either literal religious devotion or scientific claim.

## Relationship to Myss's Archetypal System

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s [Sacred Contracts](sacred-contracts.md) framework — twelve archetypal companions per person, drawn from a gallery of over 70 patterns — shares Hillman's premise that multiple archetypal figures, not a single developmental trajectory, structure a life. But the systems diverge sharply in spirit:

- **Myss's archetypes serve a goal**: they are read in service of fulfilling a pre-birth Sacred Contract with the Divine — a teleological, integrative framework in which the twelve companions ultimately cohere around the soul's single purpose.
- **Hillman's archetypes resist a goal**: true polytheism, for Hillman, means the plurality is not supposed to resolve into a single narrative or purpose at all. Imposing a unifying Contract onto the archetypal multiplicity would, by Hillman's own argument, count as exactly the "monotheism" he criticizes in Jung — even though Myss cites Hillman (via the Acorn Theory) as a direct philosophical precedent. See [Archetypes](archetypes.md) for the fuller comparison across Jung, Hillman, and Myss.

## Critique

**The relativism objection**: If no archetype governs the others, what determines which pattern is authoritative in a given clinical or ethical situation? Critics argue Hillman's polytheism risks a moral and clinical relativism in which any behaviour can be redescribed as "Ares's rage" or "Hermes's cunning" without providing grounds for judgment or intervention — the same objection Hillman himself raises against nihilist anti-psychiatry in [Pathologizing](pathologizing.md), arguably without fully escaping it himself.

**The Jungian rejoinder**: Jungians (and the wiki's existing Self material) would respond that Jung's Self is not imposed unity but *discovered* unity — a symbol the unconscious itself spontaneously produces (mandala dreams, quaternity images) rather than a theoretical requirement Jung projects onto the data. Whether this genuinely escapes Hillman's monotheism charge, or simply relocates it, remains a live and unresolved disagreement between Jungian and archetypal psychology.

**Strongest version of Hillman's case**: the historical record does show real plurality and real conflict in lived psychic experience — competing desires, incompatible values, moods that arrive with their own logic — and a theoretical commitment to an underlying unity can function to suppress or pathologize that plurality prematurely, before it has been fully lived through and understood on its own terms.
