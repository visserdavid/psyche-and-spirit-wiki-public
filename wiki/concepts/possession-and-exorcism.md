# Possession and Exorcism (Peck)

**Summary**: M. Scott Peck's account of investigating, witnessing, and theorising about demonic possession and exorcism — beginning as a sceptic, arriving as a qualified believer after witnessing two exorcisms, and proposing that possession represents the extreme end of the same spectrum occupied by ordinary evil.

**Sources**:
- [Peck, M. S. (1983). *People of the lie: The hope for healing human evil*. Simon & Schuster.](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Peck's Investigation

Peck entered the subject of possession as a committed sceptic, aware that 99% of psychiatrists and most clergy did not believe demonic possession was a real phenomenon. He began investigating after the Charlene case raised questions he could not answer within the existing psychiatric vocabulary. He solicited referrals of suspected possession cases from clergy and other professionals, evaluated numerous cases, found most to be explicable within conventional psychiatric diagnosis, and identified two cases he concluded were genuine possession.

Both of those cases proceeded to exorcism. Peck witnessed both, participating in the healing team. His full descriptions were withheld from *People of the Lie* to avoid unbalancing the book; they appear in *Glimpses of the Devil* (2005).

## The Process of Possession

Based on his two cases and the broader possession literature (particularly [Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md)'s *[Hostage to the Devil](../sources/hostage-to-the-devil.md)*, 1976 — see [Martin's Stages of Possession](possession-process-martin.md) for Martin's own, more detailed four-stage entry model and six-stage ritual sequence, which Peck compresses), Peck describes possession not as a sudden event but as a **process of gradual surrender**:

- The person "sells" themselves to the demonic, often through **loneliness** — the demonic offers companionship that is accepted without full understanding of the cost
- Both cases had early involvement with occult practices (age 5 and 12 respectively), which Peck suggests increases vulnerability — not because the occult is inherently evil, but because it represents an attempt to obtain power for self-serving purposes ("magic" vs. "charism/gift" in Church terminology)
- The possessed person develops a **fixation** at the age of onset — psychologically functioning, in certain respects, as if development had stopped at that point
- A period of **Pretense** develops: the demonic hides behind the core personality, emerging only under stress or confrontation

## Diagnosis

Peck emphasises that possession is a diagnosis of last resort, reached only after a lengthy period of evaluation — six to nine months in his two cases. The distinguishing features, beyond clinical psychopathology:

1. **Dissociation with distinct personalities**: unlike multiple personality disorder, where personalities are co-present or alternate, the possession personality appears to be a distinct entity that does not acknowledge coexistence with the host
2. **The Pretense**: the demonic entity hides behind the core personality; the exorcism process involves penetrating the Pretense until the entity is brought into the open
3. **The 5% residue**: Peck describes 95% of the exorcism process as explicable through secular psychology (catharsis, group therapy, reprogramming, the integration of dissociated material) — but acknowledges a 5% that he cannot explain in those terms, including physical manifestations and the experience of a distinct non-human presence

## The Exorcism Process

Peck's two witnessed exorcisms shared several structural features:

**Team approach**: Both required teams of seven to nine trained professionals (psychiatrists, clergy, others) who worked in shifts over multiple days (up to three days, twelve to twenty hours per day). The team approach serves several functions:
- Physical restraint of the patient when necessary
- Rotation to prevent individual team members from being overwhelmed
- Multiple forms of expertise brought to bear

**Consent**: Both patients signed informed consent documents acknowledging they understood the procedure might fail, and that death was a theoretical risk. Peck argues this is non-negotiable — exorcism cannot be performed on an unwilling patient without crossing into a form of violation.

**Prayer**: The exorcism team prayed throughout, invoking God's presence and healing power. Peck describes the presence of God in the room as palpable, and the exorcism as essentially God's action rather than the exorcist's.

**The expulsion**: Both exorcisms concluded with a moment Peck describes as the departure of the Pretense — signalled by changes in the patient's demeanour, the room's atmosphere, and (in both cases) a deliberate act of faith by the patient (holding a crucifix, praying for deliverance). The exorcist's role at this moment is not to exercise personal power but to facilitate the patient's own choice.

**Post-exorcism therapy**: Both patients required intensive psychotherapy following the exorcism — in one case, an equivalent amount accomplished in fifty hours that had not been achieved in five hundred hours preceding. The exorcisms removed the blocking entity; the psychological integration still required dedicated work.

## The Nature of Satan

Based on the exorcism experiences, Peck offers a characterisation of what he encountered:

- **Father of Lies**: The entity spoke consistently in lies, half-truths, and distortions — "I don't hate Jesus, I'm testing" (said during an exorcism, later retracted). The defining characteristic is not intelligence but the compulsive generation of falsehood.
- **Extreme narcissism**: No capacity to understand love; recognises love as a reality but cannot comprehend it; threatened by it
- **Stupidity**: Peck is struck by the entity's lack of intelligence — its pride overwhelms its reasoning, causing it to overplay its hand and reveal itself
- **No independent power over physical reality**: Satan cannot kill or harm directly; it uses human beings as instruments; threats are lies
- **Loneliness**: Peck speculates that the demonic is driven by an existential loneliness that makes human company valuable — and that the soul's recovery of divine connection removes the hook by which possession maintains itself

Peck explicitly rejects nondualist frameworks (Christian Science, A Course in Miracles) that deny Satan's reality. He is equally explicit that this is based on *experience*, not *proof*, and that he cannot translate his encounter into persuasive evidence for anyone who has not had a similar experience.

## Relationship Between Possession and Ordinary Evil

Peck's most significant theoretical claim is that possession represents **the extreme end of the same spectrum** occupied by ordinary evil. The same dynamics apply:

- The refusal of God's reality in favour of self-will (in Peck's theological framing) or the refusal of growth and truth in favour of self-image (in secular terms)
- The progressive hardening that results from repeated choices to avoid self-knowledge
- The lies, the scapegoating, the inability to tolerate conscience

Ordinary evil people are not possessed — they are not owned by Satan in the way Peck's two cases were. But they have made sufficient surrender to the same dynamics that the difference may be one of degree rather than kind.

This claim is important for clinical practice: the therapist working with evil patients is, Peck suggests, working at a lower voltage of the same phenomenon that exorcists encounter. Appropriate caution, self-purification, and team support are warranted — not because the therapist will encounter literal demonic manifestation, but because sustained contact with evil (at whatever degree of intensity) has real effects on the healer.

## Connections to the Wiki

Possession and exorcism sit at the far edge of the wiki's territory, but several connections emerge:

The [channeling and spiritual dictation](channeling-spiritual-dictation.md) concept page explores Schucman's ACIM scribing and other forms of apparent non-ordinary transmission. Peck's possession cases represent the dark analogue: malevolent rather than benevolent transmission, dis-possessing rather than enlightening.

[Shamanic healing](shamanic-healing.md) tradition — as documented by Jeanne Achterberg — includes the concept of soul loss (intrusion of foreign energies into the psyche) and the shaman's role as the one who can retrieve the soul and expel the intruder. Peck's exorcism functions structurally similarly to shamanic soul retrieval: identifying an alien presence, summoning community and spiritual resources, and assisting the patient's own choice to reclaim their psyche.

The ACIM framework explicitly denies the reality of evil and Satan, characterising them as the ego's projections rather than external entities. Peck's direct experiential rebuttal of this position is one of the few places in the wiki where an author who has read ACIM explicitly rejects its ontology on empirical (experiential) grounds. See [Separation and Atonement](separation-and-atonement.md).

The [methodology of love](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md) described in Peck's concluding chapter applies as much to exorcism as to psychotherapy: the healer must become a "bearer of light" willing to absorb evil, which means becoming a battleground. The exorcism team's willingness to risk themselves — offering their homes, their health, their psychological stability — exemplifies this methodology at its most demanding.

## Critique

**The evidence question**: Peck's account of possession is, by his own acknowledgment, personal testimony rather than scientific evidence. The 5% residue he cannot explain psychiatrically could reflect the limits of current psychiatric understanding rather than the action of a supernatural entity. The "presence of God in the room" is a powerful subjective experience but not independently verifiable.

**Selection effects**: Two cases are not enough to build a taxonomy. Peck is aware of this and calls for research — which has not, to date, been conducted in the form he envisioned.

**The cultural dimension**: Possession phenomena are documented cross-culturally, but their form and meaning are culturally shaped. The specifically Judeo-Christian framing (Satan, demons, exorcism, the crucifix as healing symbol) may reflect the patients' cultural background as much as any ontological reality.

**Iatrogenic risk**: Suggesting to a person that they may be possessed introduces a powerful expectation that can organise psychopathology around a dramatic narrative. The benefits of the exorcism framework (community, intense engagement, spiritual meaning, a clear enemy) might be achievable without the possession diagnosis.

**Note**: Demonic possession and exorcism are not recognised within mainstream psychiatry as valid diagnostic or therapeutic categories. Multiple personality disorder (now dissociative identity disorder) and other dissociative conditions are the standard framework for the phenomena Peck describes.
