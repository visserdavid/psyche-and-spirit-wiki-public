# Martin's Stages of Possession

**Summary**: Malachi Martin's two-part model of demonic possession — a slow, consent-dependent four-stage process by which a person is progressively overtaken (Entry, Preparatory Judgments, Yielding Control, Extended Control) and a distinct six-stage sequence internal to the exorcism ritual itself (Presence, Pretense, Breakpoint, Voice, Clash, Expulsion) — presented as a description drawn from his mentor figure "Father Conor" and from the five cases in the book.

**Sources**:
- [Martin, M. (1992). *Hostage to the devil: The possession and exorcism of five living Americans* (Rev. ed.). HarperSanFrancisco. (Original work published 1976)](../sources/hostage-to-the-devil.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

## The Process of Possession (Four Stages)

Martin insists possession is a *process*, never a single event, unfolding typically over years and requiring the victim's continuing — if often unwitting — consent at every stage:

1. **Entry**: A "disposing factor" opens the person to influence. Martin's candidates include occult instruments (the Ouija board, spiritualist séance) and, more controversially, self-development or contemplative methods he judged doctrinally suspect — his central example is the Enneagram personality typology, which he argued induces an "aspiring vacuum": a mind stripped of defined concepts and a will stripped of defined goals, ready to accept whatever fills the space. Entry is also keyed to individual character and interest: in Martin's five cases, the entity's approach was tailored to a specific trait or longing — stubbornness, appreciation of femininity, loneliness, intellectual gifts, personal charisma.
2. **Preparatory Judgments**: The person makes a series of ordinary-seeming judgments — about values, relationships, or areas of expertise — that incrementally reorganize the personality around the entity's influence, without any explicit choice framed as "for or against God."
3. **Yielding Control**: Increasing pressure is exerted on thought, emotion, action, and will. The person still nominally could resist, but each act of yielding further habituates the loss of control; Martin's cases show this as a gradient, not a threshold — some subjects (in his account) never fully crossed it.
4. **Extended Control**: The entity's influence expands until it constitutes possession proper. Martin describes this stage as producing a compensatory "diabolic light" — an illusory expansion of confidence, charisma, or success that keeps the person from perceiving the loss of self-command, alongside a sharpened aversion to religious symbols, places, and language associated with God.

Martin is explicit that no controlled, reduced, laboratory-standard account of causation is available for any of this — the model is offered as pattern-recognition across his handful of cases and his mentor's accumulated experience, not as an empirically tested theory.

## The Stages of the Exorcism Ritual (Six Stages)

Distinct from the possession process itself, Martin (via "Father Conor's" teaching to the exorcist "Father Peter") names six phenomenological stages internal to the exorcism ritual:

1. **Presence**: On entering the room, the exorcist and assistants report a palpable, hostile atmosphere — described as a sense of being watched or gathered-upon rather than any specific sensory sign.
2. **Pretense**: The possessing entity hides behind the victim's ordinary personality and mannerisms, using the victim's own memories and speech patterns as camouflage, so the exorcist cannot yet address the entity directly.
3. **Breakpoint**: The Pretense collapses, usually amid escalating violence and disturbance; the point at which the entity, rather than the victim, begins to speak and act "in its own person."
4. **Voice**: A distinct, humanly unclassifiable manner of speech emerges — described in the text as reversed, overlapping, or echo-laden, resistant to being parsed into ordinary sentences.
5. **Clash**: The direct confrontation of wills between exorcist and entity — not a verbal exchange but, in Martin's description, a wordless contest that determines whether the entity is forced to identify itself and begin to lose its grip.
6. **Expulsion**: The entity's departure, marked by a change in the room's atmosphere and the patient's demeanor, and typically requiring an explicit act of faith or trust by the possessed person at the decisive moment.

Martin frames the exorcist's own suffering as substantial and lasting — the book's prologue and epilogue (the story of Father Michael Strong's earlier exorcism in 1948 Nanking/Hong Kong) is built around the claim that conducting an exorcism permanently marks the exorcist, independent of whether the rite succeeds.

## Connections to the Wiki

**[Possession and Exorcism (Peck)](possession-and-exorcism.md)**: [M. Scott Peck](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md) draws his own process model of possession directly from this framework, citing *[Hostage to the Devil](../sources/hostage-to-the-devil.md)* by name. Peck's version compresses Martin's structure into a broader "Pretense" concept and a comparable Breakpoint, but Peck does not adopt Martin's full six-stage ritual vocabulary or his four-stage entry model in the same detail; Peck's own two cases are also far more sparsely documented (he withheld full detail to *Glimpses of the Devil*, 2005) than Martin's five.

**[Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)](human-evil.md)**: Martin's account is the wiki's most explicitly supernatural theory of evil, standing apart from [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s psychological account of the unintegrated [shadow](shadow.md), [Fromm](../thinkers/erich-fromm.md)'s three-strand character syndrome (necrophilia, narcissism, incestuous-symbiotic fixation), and Peck's single-mechanism narcissistic-defense diagnosis. Where those three accounts locate evil's origin within the human personality — however severely disordered — Martin locates it in a genuinely external, non-human agency that requires but does not originate in human consent. This is a difference in kind, not merely of theoretical vocabulary, and none of the four accounts offers a controlled empirical test that would adjudicate between them.

**[Privatio Boni](privatio-boni.md)**: Jung's critique of the doctrine that evil is a mere privation of good has no direct engagement with Martin's material, but the contrast is instructive: Martin's entity is maximally substantial — an intelligence with will, memory, and strategy — the opposite pole from privation theory's claim that evil has no positive existence of its own.

**[Past-Life Complexes](past-life-complexes.md)** and **[Subtle Body](subtle-body.md)**: Roger Woolger's past-life fragments that "speak through" a client during regression therapy share a surface phenomenology with Martin's possession cases — an apparently alien voice or personality using the subject's body and vocal apparatus. The ontological claims diverge sharply: Woolger's clinical framework brackets the metaphysical question of whether the fragment is a literal discarnate memory, treating it therapeutically as dissociated psychic material regardless of its ultimate origin, whereas Martin insists on the entity's status as a wholly other, malevolent, non-human being, and treats the therapeutic bracket itself (psychoanalytic reduction of the "demon" to a psyche-fragment) as one of the entity's own deceptions.

**[Karma and Possession (Daskalos)](karma-and-possession-daskalos.md)**: The Cypriot mystic [Daskalos](../thinkers/daskalos.md), as recorded in [*The Magus of Strovolos*](../sources/magus-of-strovolos.md), offers the wiki's clearest same-phenomenon contrast to Martin: both posit possession by discarnate entities requiring a specialized ritual practitioner to resolve, but Daskalos's possessing spirits (departed humans, or morally simple "demons" capable of eventual evolution toward the Holy Spirit) are not the substantially and strategically evil agency Martin describes — a genuinely different theology of evil beneath a similar surface vocabulary. ## Critique

**No independent verification**: All six stages and all four process-stages are drawn from Martin's own reconstructed narrative of interviews with participants; no case files, medical records, or third-party clinical documentation accompany the book.

**Standard psychiatric alternative**: The phenomena described under Voice, Breakpoint, and Clash are consistent with dissociative identity states and severe conversion/dissociative episodes as documented in the clinical literature — categories that do not require positing an external entity. See [Possession and Exorcism (Peck)](possession-and-exorcism.md#critique) for the fuller discussion of this differential-diagnosis question, which applies equally here.

**The Enneagram claim**: Martin's specific argument that the Enneagram personality typology is a doctrinally dangerous "disposing factor" for possession is a minority position even within conservative Catholic commentary of the period, and reflects a broader pattern in the book of treating contemplative or psychological self-development practices as spiritually hazardous — a claim presented with the same narrative authority as the clinical material but resting on no comparable case evidence.

**Selection and narrative shaping**: Five cases, chosen and written by a single author with an explicit theological thesis to advance, cannot establish a general pattern; the neatness with which each case maps onto the six-stage ritual sequence is at least as consistent with retrospective narrative shaping as with an invariant underlying phenomenon.
