# Power and Healing

**Summary**: Myss's Second Principle: personal power — where we invest our energy, and whether that investment empowers or depletes us — is the fundamental determinant of biological health, and the recovery of power is the core of genuine healing.

## The Principle

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s Second Principle of energy medicine: *Personal power is the foundation of health.*

The energy field is not a passive container of biographical data. It is an active system governed by power — the investment and flow of life energy. Health is a function of how much power we retain in our own field, and illness is a consequence of sustained power depletion: energy chronically sent outward to others, objects, or circumstances that hold authority over one's sense of self.

## Power and Its Symbols

Myss introduces the concept of **power symbols** — the objects, roles, or relationships in which individuals vest their sense of power and identity. Common power symbols include money, authority, status, beauty, security, relationships. When a power symbol is threatened or lost, the individual experiences it as a loss of self, and this biological crisis follows.

She illustrates with a case: a successful businessman whose company collapsed in the 1987 stock market crash. Within months, he developed migraines, bowel disorders, and lower back pain. His health had been calibrated to his money-making. Money was his power symbol — the proof of his identity, freedom, and worth. When it disappeared, his body registered the loss of power as a biological event.

The key insight: *losing money did not cause his illness; losing his symbol of power did.* A different person might have experienced the same financial loss and not developed illness, because their identity was not vested in wealth. Power and its symbols are individual.

## Types of Power Orientation

Myss identifies two primary patterns in how people relate to power:

### The Acquisitioner
The acquisitioner is a person whose power circuits run predominantly *outward* — attached to an external power target (a person, substance, role, or status) from which they draw their sense of self. Their energy field is depleted because it is organised around attracting or maintaining the target rather than generating power from within.

Subtypes of acquisitioner:
- **The control-seeker**: invests energy in maintaining authority over others or circumstances; develops illness when control is lost (migraines, hypertension, ulcers — second and third chakra disorders).
- **The approval-seeker**: bases self-esteem entirely on others' opinions; expends energy in managing others' perceptions; depletes the third chakra.
- **The addict**: energy circuits attached to a substance or behaviour with the strength of a life-support system; healing requires detaching the circuit, not just abstaining.

The case of Anna — an HIV-positive woman whose addiction to cigarettes represented the boundary of the power she was willing to claim over her own life — illustrates the dynamic with stark clarity. Myss tells her: "Pick one — you want your cigarettes or you want to heal." The addiction was the energy circuit that kept Anna's power in the hands of the cigarettes and her pimp. Healing required reclaiming power beyond those boundaries.

### The Self-Directed Person
By contrast, individuals who have developed a stable internal sense of power — who act from values rather than fear, who meet challenges without losing their centre — generate health from within. The case of Sam (a former drug dealer who rebuilt his life after a car accident forced him to confront his values) and David Chetlahe Paladin (a Navajo Indian who survived Nazi torture camps and returned to heal others) exemplify the kind of power that transcends biological limitation.

## Power and Healing

Myss makes a critical distinction:

- **Curing**: The control or abatement of illness. Treatments may succeed at the symptomatic level. The physician holds the authority; the patient is relatively passive.
- **Healing**: The transformation of the biographical and energetic patterns that gave rise to illness. The patient must actively investigate and release the attitudes, memories, and beliefs that depleted their power. No healer can do this work on the patient's behalf.

This is the Third Principle: *All help is self-help.*

The distinction explains why treatments sometimes cure without healing (illness returns, or a different illness emerges) and why healing can occur even when curing does not (a person completes their spiritual work while their body continues to decline). Myss does not claim that healing always produces physical recovery — she explicitly states that healing can occur even as the body is dying.

## Healing as Power Recovery

The healing process, in Myss's model, involves:

1. **Identifying** which power symbols or external attachments are draining the energy field.
2. **Investigating** the biographical roots of the attachment — which fears, wounds, or tribal beliefs created the depletion pattern.
3. **Releasing** the attachment by reclaiming the energy circuits — an act of will that may require leaving a relationship, a job, a belief system.
4. **Acting**: Information alone does not heal. Change requires action — a choice made, a boundary drawn, a relationship entered or left, a pattern broken.

## Three Forms of Power (*Why People Don't Heal*)

In [*Why People Don't Heal*](../sources/why-people-dont-heal.md), Myss significantly extends the power framework by introducing three **forms of power** corresponding to the [Three Ages of Power](three-ages-of-power.md):

- **Tribal Power** (external): power derived from group membership, loyalty, and collective belief. Effective for survival; becomes pathological when it blocks individual development.
- **Individual Power** (internal): power generated through self-knowledge, personal choice, and authentic expression. The goal of the Piscean age; becomes pathological as narcissism when cut off from both Tribal and Symbolic.
- **Symbolic Power** (archetypal): power accessed through alignment with archetypal and spiritual forces — the form of power least vulnerable to external circumstance and most directly supportive of healing.

Healing in this expanded model requires integrating all three forms. A person operating purely on Tribal power is susceptible to any illness related to conformity, honour, and tribal stress. A person operating on Individual power may succumb to illnesses of self-imposed isolation or the resistance to change. Symbolic power, when cultivated, provides access to a grace that transcends biological probability — as illustrated by Myss's case histories of unexpected remission.

The [Cellular Energy Bank](cellular-energy-bank.md) metaphor operationalises this: the one hundred "dollars" of daily life force can be invested across all three levels, and healing requires redirecting investment from depleting patterns (especially [woundology](woundology.md)) toward the Symbolic level.

## Critique

**The philosophical strength**: The framework captures something clinically observed — that patient agency and attitude matter to outcomes. Research in psychoneuroimmunology confirms that perceived control, social support, and psychological engagement with illness affect survival rates in some conditions (notably cancer, cardiovascular disease). Myss's framework provides a rich model for understanding *why*.

**The overreach**: Moving from "agency matters" to "depleted personal power causes specific illnesses" is a leap the evidence does not fully support. People with strong self-esteem and centred power also develop illness; people who are chronically disempowered do not all become ill. The relationship is statistical and modulating, not deterministic and causal as Myss implies.

**The ethics of healing = self-help**: If healing ultimately depends on the patient, then patients who do not recover could be implicitly judged as having failed to do the work. Myss resists this reading, but her framework makes it structurally possible.
