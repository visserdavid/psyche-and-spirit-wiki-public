# Prayer and Healing

**Summary**: The documented and proposed role of prayer, intercessory healing, and faith in producing measurable health outcomes — central to Shealy's evidence base for sacred healing and to Dossey's Era III medicine framework, which situates prayer within a broader theory of non-local consciousness.

## Overview

[Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) argues that prayer and spiritual healing represent humanity's oldest and most widespread therapeutic modality. Across all major world religions, healing through prayer, ritual, and spiritual intervention has been central. See [Spiritual Healing Traditions](spiritual-healing-traditions.md) for the historical survey.

The modern question is whether prayer and spiritual healing produce effects that exceed what placebo and spontaneous remission can explain — and whether those effects can be documented in scientifically rigorous ways.

## Key Evidence Cited

### The Byrd Coronary Care Study

The most frequently cited study in the field is Randolph C. Byrd's 1988 randomised controlled trial of intercessory prayer in a coronary care unit (published in the *Southern Medical Journal*). Patients were divided into two groups: one received directed intercessory prayer from groups of Christians who knew the patient's name and condition; the other received no organised prayer. Neither patients nor medical staff knew which group was which.

Results: the prayer group had significantly better outcomes — fewer complications, fewer deaths, less need for ventilatory support. The study is considered methodologically stronger than most prayer research because of its blinding and randomisation.

Shealy cites this study as evidence that healing through prayer is not merely placebo — because patients did not know they were receiving prayer, the standard placebo mechanism (patient belief) cannot explain the differential outcome.

### Healer Case Documentation

Shealy collected over one hundred documented cases where patients recovered from conditions medicine had declared incurable — including brain tumours, cord tumours, metastatic cancers, autoimmune disorders, and congenital diseases. These cases were collected through healers including:

- **Olga Worrall**: Shealy obtained verified medical records for nine patients she healed. He followed hundreds of letters from patients attesting to her effectiveness, though most physicians did not respond to his requests for records.
- **Ostad Hadi Parvarandeh**: The most extensively documented healer in the book. One hundred records of healing were collected by Shealy — ranging from brain tumours and spinal cord tumours to metastatic sarcoma and osteogenesis imperfecta. Parvarandeh healed at a distance (by telephone, photograph, or simply knowing the patient's name), suggesting a non-local mechanism.

### EEG and Physiological Studies

Shealy's own laboratory measurements add a layer of objective data:
- Ostad Parvarandeh could alter patients' EEG patterns from a distance of 40–100 feet without patient knowledge — shifting alpha wave activity in documented patterns.
- Parvarandeh affected cardiac output (stroke volume) at a distance.
- Parvarandeh changed the hydrogen-bonding structure of sealed water bottles — measured by ultraviolet absorption.
- DHEA levels in patients rose or changed following healing sessions — though results were complex (two patients showed rises; most showed decreases, possibly reflecting mobilisation of DHEA reserves).

## Placebo and Faith

Shealy presents a nuanced view of the placebo effect. Physician belief (not just patient belief) influences outcome. Herbert Benson's 1979 study showed that physicians who believed in a treatment for angina pectoris achieved 90% success rates; those who did not achieved 30% — using the same intervention. The conclusion: the healer's intention and faith are part of the therapeutic agent, not merely background noise.

Emil Coué's early twentieth-century work — in which thousands of patients recovered through simple daily affirmations — was dismissed as placebo by the AMA. Shealy argues this dismissal was premature: placebo mechanisms (belief, expectation, intention) are themselves healing mechanisms, and understanding them does not diminish their value.

## Dossey's Era III Medicine Framework

In *Meaning & Medicine* [(Dossey, 1991)](../sources/meaning-and-medicine.md), Dossey situates prayer research within his Three Eras of Medicine framework (first published in *Recovering the Soul*, 1989):

- **Era I** (mechanical medicine): The body as machine; treatment is physical; mind is not a factor.
- **Era II** (mind-body medicine): Mind affects body within the same person; the domain of PNI, relaxation response, imagery, and placebo. Mind is acknowledged but confined to the individual.
- **Era III** (non-local medicine): Consciousness is not bounded by individual brain or body; healing can occur at a distance, independent of time and space. Prayer, therapeutic touch, and intuitive diagnosis at a distance are Era III phenomena.

The key characteristic of Era III: the effects are **non-local** — they occur even when neither patient nor practitioner is aware of the other's identity, and even when separated by 1,200 miles (as in Myss's intuitive diagnosis with Shealy). Dossey's table presents the three eras with their theoretical bases, definitions, and example therapies.

The Byrd study (intercessory prayer in the CCU) is an Era III event: the patients did not know they were receiving prayer; the intercessors were at a distance; the effects exceeded what placebo can explain on standard models.

## Additional Era III Evidence

Beyond the Byrd study, *Meaning & Medicine* presents:

**Therapeutic Touch and wound healing**: Daniel Wirth's double-blind study (1990): 44 patients given identical standardised skin wounds; subjects inserted their arms through holes in a wall, believing they were having biopotentials measured. Half had a therapeutic touch practitioner working on the wound side without contact; half did not. By Day 16, 13 of 23 treated subjects had wounds of zero size; none of the 21 controls had healed completely.

**TM and social peace**: Research from the Jerusalem 1983 TM study (D. W. Orme-Johnson et al.): on days when the meditating group was largest, war deaths in Lebanon decreased by 70% and crime in Israel fell. Analysis showed that group size changes preceded the social changes, supporting a causal interpretation. Dossey cites this as evidence that consciousness fields can extend beyond individuals.

**EEG synchrony across distance**: Jacobo Grinberg-Zylberbaum and Julieta Ramos (Universidad Nacional Autónoma de Mexico): pairs of subjects placed in separate Faraday cages instructed to meditate together. EEG analysis showed correlated brain wave patterns between paired subjects during periods they reported "communicating" — patterns not found in control sessions. One subject's EEG became that of their partner.

**Braud and Schlitz transpersonal imagery**: 13 experiments at the Mind Science Foundation showed that one person using calming or activating imagery could measurably alter another isolated person's electrodermal activity — with no physical contact and no subject awareness.

**Myss and Shealy's intuitive diagnosis**: Dossey explicitly cites *The Creation of Health* (Shealy & Myss, 1988) as clinical documentation of Era III medicine: Myss giving diagnoses from 1,200 miles away based only on the patient's name and birthdate, with 93% accuracy by Shealy's estimate. See [Medical Intuition](medical-intuition.md).

## *Healing Words*: The 131-Study Corpus

In *Healing Words* [(Dossey, 1993)](../sources/healing-words.md), Dossey presents Daniel J. Benor's survey of controlled healing trials as the most comprehensive assembly of this evidence:

- **131 controlled experiments** meeting scientific criteria, published across multiple languages and disciplines (parapsychology, nursing, alternative medicine, complementary medical research).
- **56 studies** showed results at p ≤ .01 (chance probability 1 in 100 or lower).
- **21 studies** showed results at p = .02–.05.
- Subjects ranged from bacteria, yeast, fungi, and cancer cells to plants, animals, and humans.
- Effects occurred at distances from inches to 15 miles and were not blocked by physical barriers (lead-lined rooms, Faraday cages).

Key individual studies:
- **Matthew Manning and cancer cells**: HeLa cancer cells in flasks; Manning's focused intention produced 200–1,200% changes in growth characteristics.
- **Bernard Grad's barley seeds (Estebany)**: Healer Oskar Estebany held a container of saline solution (which inhibits germination) for 15 minutes; seeds watered with "treated" saline grew significantly better than control seeds. Healing properties appeared transferable to materials — wool and cotton held by Estebany affected rat thyroid growth rates.
- **Mouse wound healing**: Estebany held cages of wounded mice 15 min/day × 14 days; treatment group healed significantly faster.
- **Mouse anesthesia awakening**: 19 of 21 controlled studies showed healers could accelerate awakening from anesthesia in mice.
- **Bacteria mutation control**: Subjects directed a strain of *E. coli* to mutate in specified directions; mutations followed subject direction.
- **Braud-Schlitz transpersonal imagery**: 13 experiments, 62 influencers, 271 isolated subjects; one person's calming/activating imagery measurably altered the other's electrodermal activity across rooms — without subject awareness.

The evidence covers both promotion and inhibition of biological processes — a critical point. Healing intention can both help and harm living organisms (see section on Negative Prayer below).

## Directed vs. Non-directed Prayer: The Spindrift Finding

*Healing Words* introduces the most practically surprising finding in the prayer research literature: non-directed prayer (no specified outcome; "Thy will be done") consistently outperformed directed prayer (specific outcome held in mind) in Spindrift's controlled biological experiments.

In the mold plate study, directed prayer produced no significant growth change; non-directed prayer produced statistically significant mold recovery. In seed germination experiments, non-directed prayer correctly steered over-hydrated and under-hydrated seeds toward their individual biological norms — without the practitioner knowing which batch needed what. The implication: the organism's own healing intelligence is more reliable than the healer's specification of what "healing" should mean.

This does not invalidate directed prayer. For introverted practitioners, non-directed approaches may feel natural; for extroverted, action-oriented people, directed visualisation may be more effective. Herbert Benson's research showed equivalent relaxation response effects using personal prayer phrases (Catholic, Jewish, Protestant) as with TM mantras — personality-matched prayer appears to work for everyone. See [Directed vs. Non-directed Prayer](directed-vs-nondirected-prayer.md).

## Personality and Prayer: Jung Applied

*Healing Words* applies Jung's introvert/extrovert typology to prayer styles. Introverts naturally gravitate toward contemplative, non-directed, wordless approaches; extroverts toward directed petition and specific intercession. Augustine Baker (17th-century Benedictine) already distinguished these paths. Dossey argues that forcing one type to pray in the style of the other produces "prayer neurosis" — alienation from prayer entirely.

The practical implication: no single prayer protocol should be prescribed. The Byrd CCU study's design — giving intercessors no instructions on how to pray — may have been an inadvertent strength, allowing each intercessor to pray in their natural style.

## Love as the Medium

David McClelland (Harvard Medical School): watching a documentary about Mother Teresa measurably raised students' salivary IgA (immune antibody) levels — even in students who disliked her. Adding a second step — recalling a time of feeling deeply loved, and a time of deeply loving another — raised IgA reliably on demand. McClelland used this to abort his own colds.

F. W. H. Myers (foundational parapsychologist) proposed love as the mechanism underlying all distant healing and telesomatics: it is love that allows one mind to transcend its bodily limitations and connect with another. Princeton PEAR Lab: "bonded pairs" (emotionally close couples) consistently produced larger effects on random event generators than individuals — the bond appears to amplify non-local correlation.

## Negative Prayer

*Healing Words* includes a chapter Dossey calls an "inquiry into black prayer" — the possibility that focused intention can harm. Evidence:
- Healer **Olga Worrall** refused to inhibit bacteria growth in Beverly Rubik's laboratory: "I refuse to hurt." Her protection of one bacterial sample from another's inhibiting influence produced statistically significant survival differences in the protected group.
- **Bernard Grad's petrified banana**: a man focused on desiccating fruit until a banana hardened to marble-like consistency within hours.
- **Uri Geller** inadvertently killed sperm cells while trying to alter their motility pattern.
- Laboratory studies show ordinary people can retard bacterial and fungal growth at distances of inches to 15 miles.
- Polynesian/Hawaiian **kahuna death prayer** (Max Freedom Long, 1917): works through the victim's unconscious guilt, not through suggestion; an ascending paralysis (feet → waist → collapse → death) distinguishes it from voodoo's suggestion-mediated mechanism.

Physicist Nick Herbert's analysis of Bell's theorem: the universe is "contagious" at the quantum level — action-at-a-distance was precisely what classical physics rejected, but Bell proved it exists. This does not prove distant hexing works at the human scale, but removes the a priori reason to dismiss it.

## Time-Displaced Prayer

Helmut Schmidt (Mind Science Foundation, San Antonio): subjects influenced random event generator output *retroactively* — events already recorded on tape but not yet observed. Psychologist Hans Eysenck and researcher Carl Sargent estimated Schmidt's results were one-in-a-million by chance; Ray Hyman (skeptic) acknowledged Schmidt's methodology was not easily dismissed.

Wheeler's "delayed choice" experiments in physics: an observer's present decision appears to retroactively determine which quantum path a particle took. Applied to medicine: the moment of observation (examination, test, biopsy) may "fix" what was previously a spectrum of possibilities. Dossey's proposal: prayer and intention during the interval between first suspicion of illness and diagnostic confirmation may influence which possibilities crystallise. This is speculative but physically coherent.

## Critique

Mainstream medicine's objections to prayer research are substantial:
- Most prayer studies are methodologically weak: poor blinding, outcome selection bias, multiple testing problems, and failure to account for intercessors' efforts varying across subjects.
- The Byrd study has not been consistently replicated. A larger, more rigorous follow-up study (STEP, 2006) found no benefit from intercessory prayer — and actually found slightly worse outcomes in patients who knew they were receiving prayer (possibly from the stress of expectation).
- The definition of "prayer" varies enormously between studies, making aggregation difficult.
- Even if prayer effects are real, the proposed mechanism (divine intervention, non-local consciousness) is not compatible with current scientific frameworks and has produced no coherent theoretical model.

**Note**: Mainstream medical consensus holds that prayer has not been demonstrated to produce specific healing effects beyond placebo in controlled trials. It may play an important role in patient coping, meaning-making, and wellbeing — but as a specific therapeutic agent for physical illness, the evidence base is insufficient and inconsistent.
