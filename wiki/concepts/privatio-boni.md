# Privatio Boni: Jung's Critique of Evil as Absence of Good

**Summary**: The Augustinian-Thomist doctrine that evil has no independent substance — that it is merely a privation (absence) of good, as darkness is the absence of light; Jung's extended argument that this doctrine, however logically coherent, has the psychological effect of denying evil's reality and thereby blocking its integration as [shadow](shadow.md).

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1968). *Aion: Researches into the phenomenology of the self* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 9, Part 2). Princeton University Press.](../sources/aion.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

## The Doctrine

*Privatio boni* ("privation of the good") is the classical Christian answer to the problem of evil within a monotheistic, omnibenevolent framework: if God is the sole source of being and is wholly good, evil cannot be a substance God created, because that would make God the author of evil. The solution, developed by Church Fathers including Basil the Great, Dionysius the Areopagite, and given its most systematic form by Augustine and later Thomas Aquinas, is to deny evil any positive existence at all. Evil is not a thing but a **lack** — a deficiency, corruption, or diminishment of a good that ought to be present, exactly as cold is not a substance but the absence of heat, and darkness not a substance but the absence of light.

Aquinas's formulation, which Jung quotes at length: "the name evil is signified [by] the absence of good... Evil is not a being, but only a being's evil." On this view, the devil and demons were created good and became evil only through a *deficiency of will* — a turning-away, not an added corrupting substance.

## Jung's Critique

Jung does not dispute that the doctrine is logically coherent within its own premises (Deus = Summum Bonum, "God = the Supreme Good," makes evil-as-substance impossible without abandoning either God's goodness or God's omnipotence). His objection is **psychological**, not theological: whatever its logical validity, the doctrine functions in lived religious and cultural life to make evil *unreal* — a mere shadow, an absence, something that will dissolve on inspection rather than something that must be actively engaged and integrated.

His central argument: **whatever is denied psychological reality does not disappear — it is projected**, exactly as the personal [shadow](shadow.md) is projected when unacknowledged. A civilization that has been formally taught, for a millennium and a half, that evil has no substance is a civilization poorly equipped to recognize evil when it appears at scale. Jung's own examples, writing in 1951: "call to mind what has happened and what is happening... concentration camps [and] dictator states" — Nazi Germany and Stalinist Russia as recent, catastrophic demonstrations of a shadow that "privation of good" theology had left the West unprepared to name and confront.

Jung's alternative formulation: psychologically, good and evil function as **real, coexistent halves of a totality** — a "complexio oppositorum" — neither of which can be reduced to a diminished form of the other. The Self, as the archetype of psychic wholeness, must in principle contain both; a symbol of totality (he argues at length in relation to [Christ as a Symbol of the Self](christ-as-symbol-of-the-self.md)) that structurally excludes darkness is, for that reason, an incomplete symbol of wholeness — however psychologically powerful and historically dominant it has been.

## Jung's Explicit Caution: Psychology, Not Metaphysics

Jung is careful to frame this as a psychological rather than theological claim: "Psychology... is not metaphysics... [it] has neither the inclination nor the competence to mix into these matters." He states repeatedly that he is describing how the *privatio boni* doctrine functions in the human psyche — what it does to a person's or a culture's relationship with its own darkness — not adjudicating whether it is theologically correct. Whether this distinction can be maintained consistently is itself contested (see Critique, below, and the parallel difficulty noted on [The Self](the-self.md) regarding Jung's Self/God-image equation).

## Connection to the Gnostic and Kabbalistic Material

Jung surveys pre-orthodox and heterodox traditions that resisted the *privatio boni* consensus — the Clementine Homilies' doctrine of "two sons of God" (one righteous, one Satanael), Bogomil and Cathar dualism, and rabbinic material on Yahweh's coexisting attributes of justice and mercy (Akathriel legend, the prayer that God's mercy "suppress His anger"). This body of material treats good and evil, or justice and mercy, as **genuinely paired forces requiring active balance** rather than one being the mere shadow of the other — closer to Jung's own psychological position than to the mainstream Augustinian consensus. This resonates with the wiki's existing Kabbalistic material: see [Sefirot](sefirot.md) and [Shekhinah](shekhinah.md), where the sefirotic column of Gevurah (Judgment/Severity) stands in permanent, necessary tension with Hesed (Mercy/Love) rather than being reducible to a deficiency of it — a structurally similar refusal to treat one pole as ontologically primary and the other as its mere absence.

## Tension with M. Scott Peck's Clinical Psychology of Evil

Jung's *privatio boni* critique bears directly on the wiki's other primary treatment of evil, [M. Scott Peck](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md)'s [Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)](human-evil.md). The two positions converge on the central claim — evil must be treated as psychologically and clinically real, not explained away — but diverge sharply on framing:

- **Jung** argues against the *privatio boni* tradition as a matter of general psychological theory: evil is a real pole of the archetypal totality, denied at the culture's peril.
- **Peck** goes considerably further, proposing evil as a **diagnosable clinical category** located in specific individuals (malignant narcissism, consistent scapegoating) rather than as a universal structural feature of the psyche that everyone must integrate as shadow.

Where Jung's framework implies that the shadow (including its evil aspect) is a *universal* developmental task — everyone has one, everyone must integrate it — Peck's framework implies a *categorical* distinction between ordinary people who fail to integrate their shadow (becoming merely difficult, defended, or neurotic) and a much smaller class of people whose failure to do so constitutes something closer to Jung's Antichrist: a systematic, compulsive weaponization of the unintegrated shadow against others. Peck's own diagnostic signal — the clinician's **revulsion** in the presence of an evil patient — is arguably a clinical operationalization of exactly the reality-of-evil claim Jung makes at the level of theory. See [Shadow § Peck's Clinical Contribution](shadow.md) for the fuller cross-reference.

## Contrast with Martin's Literal Possessing Entity

[Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md)'s *[Hostage to the Devil](../sources/hostage-to-the-devil.md)* offers the sharpest possible contrast to *privatio boni* theology available in this wiki: where the privation doctrine denies evil any positive substance at all, Martin's possessing entities are maximally substantial — intelligences with will, memory, and strategic purpose. Martin does not engage Jung's argument directly, but the two positions mark the opposite poles of the question this page addresses: is evil best understood as an absence (privation), a structural half of psychic totality (Jung's own alternative), or a discrete, external, intending agency (Martin)? See [Martin's Stages of Possession](possession-process-martin.md) and [Human Evil § A Fourth Account](human-evil.md#a-fourth-account-martins-literal-external-evil) for the fuller treatment.

## Critique

**Jung's own dualism risk**: Some theologians who engaged directly with Jung on this point — notably the Dominican priest Victor White, whose correspondence with Jung is referenced in *Aion* itself — argued that Jung's insistence on evil's independent reality tends toward the very Manichaean/Gnostic dualism (two co-equal cosmic principles) that mainstream Christian theology developed *privatio boni* specifically to avoid. Jung's response — that he speaks psychologically, not metaphysically, and is describing the archetype of the Self as a *complexio oppositorum* rather than asserting a two-God cosmology — is coherent in principle but, as with the Self/God-image parallel elsewhere in Jung's work, difficult to keep cleanly separated from metaphysical implication in practice.

**Circularity in Jung's counter-argument**: Jung criticizes *privatio boni* for being a *petitio principii* (begging the question) — assuming God = Summum Bonum and deriving the impossibility of substantial evil from that premise. But his own psychological alternative (the Self as a *complexio oppositorum* containing both good and evil) arguably begs a parallel question: it assumes the theoretical necessity of archetypal totality and derives evil's reality from that assumption, rather than establishing it independently.

**Note**: mainstream philosophy of religion continues to treat both privation theory and its alternatives (including process theology's real-evil positions) as live, unresolved positions in the problem of evil; Jung's psychological argument does not settle — and does not purport to settle — the metaphysical question, only the clinical and cultural one.
