# Pulsation

**Summary**: In Keleman's somatic framework, pulsation — the rhythmic expansion and contraction of cells, tissues, and organs — is the fundamental biological act, the ground of feeling, self-identity, and aliveness.

## Overview

Pulsation is [Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md)'s name for the most basic biological rhythm: the alternating expansion and contraction that begins at the level of the cell and organizes the entire organism. The body, in his framework, is not a container of life but a pulsating pump — a nested series of tubes, layers, and pouches all rhythmically expanding and contracting at different frequencies and amplitudes.

The heartbeat is the most obvious instance of pulsation, but Keleman identifies pulsatory activity at every level of biological organization: the nucleus pulsating within the cell, the brain pulsating 14–18 strokes per minute, the intestines undergoing peristaltic waves, the craniosacral fluid oscillating through the spine. Life is the maintenance of pulsatory organization against the forces of gravity and entropy.

## Pulsation and Feeling

Pulsation is not merely a mechanical fact — it is the generator of feeling and self-experience:

- **Expansion** generates the experience of reaching, asserting, taking in, being present.
- **Contraction** generates the experience of withdrawing, containing, consolidating.
- **The rhythm between them** — the continuum of ongoing expansion and contraction — is the ground of aliveness, vitality, and self-identity.

Feelings are not psychological events that secondarily affect the body; they are alterations in the quality, speed, amplitude, and pattern of pulsation. Fear speeds and tightens pulsation; sadness dampens it; love facilitates and deepens it.

## Pulsatory Patterns and Distress

When the organism encounters stress, shock, or chronic insult, the pulsatory continuum is disrupted:

- **Overbound disruption**: pulsation speeds, hardens, or tightens — the tissues brace against a threat. If chronic, pulsation becomes locked in a defensive high-tension pattern.
- **Underbound disruption**: pulsation slows, diffuses, or collapses — the tissues yield or withdraw. If chronic, pulsation becomes meandering or extinguished.

These disruptions, when habitual, create the characteristic body shapes Keleman calls [somatic distress patterns](somatic-distress-patterns.md).

## Waves, Diaphragms, and Pouches

Pulsatory waves move through the body in peristaltic tides from head to toe. Their flow is regulated by a series of diaphragms — transverse "valves" at the palate, glottis, thoracic inlet, respiratory diaphragm, and pelvic floor — that divide the body into pouches (head, chest, abdomen, pelvis). Each pouch has its own pulsatory character; the coordination of all pouches in a flowing wave sequence is the sign of somatic health.

When diaphragms stiffen, constrict, or collapse, they fragment the pulsatory wave — creating segmentation between body compartments and isolating feeling from expression.

## The Accordion and Inhibition (*Embodying Experience*)

*Embodying Experience* extends the pulsation concept with two additions:

**The accordion metaphor**: The body functions as an accordion pump — building and releasing pressure, forming language (and life) through the alternation of compression and elongation. The accordion is not a machine that holds tension; it is an instrument that requires both directions. Health is the full range of the continuum, not low tension. This metaphor underlies the HOw process: Step Two builds pressure (intensification); Step Three releases it bit by bit (disorganization); Step Five forms the new phrase.

**Inhibition as pulsatory intelligence**: The brain's capacity to *stop* — to delay, redirect, and differentiate pulsatory impulses — is the foundation of all learning and spontaneity. Keleman describes the brain as an organ of inhibition, not merely of activation. At the somatic level, the ability to inhibit a muscle contraction, sustain the pause, and choose a new response (Steps Three through Five of the HOw process) is what distinguishes conscious, reformable organization from automatic, repetitive organization.

This means that the pulsatory health Keleman aims at is not simply more expansion — it is a *wider range* of the expansion-contraction continuum, with greater voluntary access to both poles.

## The Three Layers of Existence

*Embodying Experience* also introduces a three-layer model of how pulsation organizes experience:

- **Prepersonal layer**: the instinctual, animal, inherited dimension — drives, appetites, gender, the body's organic rhythms. Pulsation at this layer is given; it cannot be constructed.
- **Personal/societal layer**: the dimension of socialization, relational training, and role. Pulsation here carries the imprint of cultural demand and interpersonal history.
- **Individuating layer**: the dimension that personalizes the prepersonal and societal inheritance — the layer of individual self-formation. Somatic education works primarily at this layer: reorganizing one's characteristic pulsatory patterns into a more consciously shaped personal form.

## Connection to Other Frameworks

Pulsation overlaps with several concepts in the wiki's existing literature:

- **Energy field** (Myss): where Myss describes the energy field as the medium between biography and biology, Keleman describes pulsation as that medium — but at the level of observable tissue dynamics rather than subtle energy. Both frameworks point to the body's capacity to be animated or depleted by biographical experience.
- **Bioelectromagnetism** (Shealy/Becker): Shealy's [bioelectromagnetic](bioelectromagnetism.md) view of the body as an electrically organized field finds a parallel in Keleman's view of the body as an electrochemically pulsating pump. Both resist the purely mechanical model of biomedicine.

## Critique

Pulsation as Keleman describes it is a real biological phenomenon at the level of the heart, gut, and brain. The extension of the concept to a single organizing principle unifying all somatic experience is philosophically compelling but difficult to test empirically. Mainstream physiology would agree that cardiovascular, respiratory, and neural rhythms are central to health, but would resist the claim that all emotional and psychological life can be understood through pulsatory dynamics alone. Somatic therapists in the Kelmanesque tradition report clinical utility of the concept; controlled outcome studies are lacking.
