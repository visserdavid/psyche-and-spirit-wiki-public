# Relaxation Response

**Summary**: Herbert Benson's term for the physiological counterpart to fight-or-flight — a state of deep rest achieved through focused meditation, breathing, or repetitive prayer, characterised by decreased heart rate, blood pressure, and stress hormone output; the clinical foundation of the Mind/Body Clinic's ten-week program.

## Discovery

Physiologist Walter Hess (Nobel laureate, 1949) demonstrated in cat brain stimulation experiments that the hypothalamus controls two opposed states: a sympathetic (ergotropic, activating) state and a parasympathetic (trophotropic, restorative) state. These states are mutually inhibitory — activating one suppresses the other.

[Herbert Benson](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md) and R. Keith Wallace documented the parasympathetic state in meditating humans at Harvard's Thorndike Memorial Laboratory (Beth Israel Hospital, Boston), measuring it as a distinct physiological condition — deeper than relaxed wakefulness, different from sleep. The context was the [fight-or-flight response](fight-or-flight-response.md): Benson had been researching hypertension and the stress response, and TM practitioners in 1968 persuaded him to investigate whether meditation could lower blood pressure. Benson named the counter-state the **relaxation response** and published it in *The Relaxation Response* (1975). Working with [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) at Beth Israel Hospital, it became the clinical cornerstone of the Mind/Body Clinic's treatment programme.

## Physiology

When the relaxation response is elicited:

| Parameter | Change |
|---|---|
| Heart rate | Decreases |
| Blood pressure | Decreases |
| Breathing rate | Decreases |
| Oxygen consumption | Decreases (more than in sleep) |
| Brain waves | Shift from beta → alpha rhythm |
| Muscle blood flow | Decreases; blood redistributes to brain and skin |
| Cortisol and adrenalin | Decrease |
| Immune function | Partially restored (reverses fight-or-flight immunosuppression) |

Twenty minutes of meditation produces a deeper metabolic drop (measured by oxygen consumption) than eight hours of sleep. This makes it a potent tool for nervous system recovery in chronically stressed patients.

## How to Elicit It

Benson identified a common mechanism across diverse contemplative practices: any technique that:
1. **Focuses attention** on a repetitive stimulus (a word, sound, breath, mantra, or movement)
2. **Passively disregards** intrusive thoughts rather than fighting them

...will reliably elicit the relaxation response, regardless of its spiritual content or cultural origin.

Techniques that reliably elicit it:
- Breath-focused meditation
- Mantra meditation (TM, centering prayer)
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Repetitive prayer (Rosary, repetition of a sacred word)
- Yoga (when held with relaxed attention)
- Rhythmic exercise with mental focus

The secular and sacred versions produce equivalent physiological effects. The specific content — the word or object of focus — is secondary to the act of focusing and letting go.

## The Anxiety Cycle and Its Breakers

In chronic stress, the fight-or-flight response is activated not by real physical danger but by conditioned responses, awfulizing, and worry. The result is a self-sustaining anxiety cycle:

1. Anxious thoughts → fight-or-flight activation
2. Fight-or-flight symptoms (racing heart, sweaty palms, dry mouth) → perceived as threatening
3. Perceived symptoms → increased anxiety → increased fight-or-flight activation

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) identifies two cycle-breakers in the Mind/Body Program:
- **Breathing**: focused diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic system and interrupts the cycle at the physiological level
- **Stretching**: releases the muscle tension that feeds back into the anxiety response

The relaxation response, once established through practice, becomes available on demand in stressful situations — a trained capacity for physiological regulation.

## Conditioning and the Relaxation Response

Robert Ader and Nicholas Cohen demonstrated that immune responses can be conditioned via Pavlovian mechanisms (saccharin acting as a conditioned stimulus for immune suppression). Borysenko extends this logic: if illness patterns can be conditioned, so can healing responses. Regular elicitation of the relaxation response conditions the body to shift toward parasympathetic dominance more readily, reducing baseline cortisol levels and improving immune regulation over time.

The regular meditation practice the Mind/Body Program prescribes is thus not merely a relaxation technique but a conditioning program — training the body to heal.

## Relation to Other Frameworks in the Wiki

**[Shealy's electrotherapy](electrotherapy.md)**: TENS, cranial electrical stimulation, and GigaTENS all aim to shift the nervous system toward parasympathetic dominance — the same physiological territory as the relaxation response, achieved through bioelectrical rather than psychological means.

**[Shealy's Five Sacred Rings](five-sacred-rings.md)**: Activating neurochemical pathways (DHEA, oxytocin, calcitonin) that are depleted by chronic sympathetic activation. The Sacred Rings work at a neurochemical level that complements the relaxation response's autonomic effects.

**[Brennan's healing sessions](spectrum-of-healing.md)**: Hands-on energy healing requires the patient to enter a deeply relaxed, receptive state — functionally equivalent to the relaxation response — before the healer's work can be effective.

**[Mindfulness](mindfulness.md)**: The Witness stance that mindfulness cultivates — observing the mind without being swept away by it — is the psychological expression of the state the relaxation response creates physiologically. The two practices support and deepen each other.

## Critique

**Scientific consensus**: The relaxation response is one of the best-established findings in mind-body medicine, with decades of replication. Its effects on blood pressure, anxiety, and stress physiology are well-documented in controlled research.

**Specificity questions**: Whether all techniques produce equivalent effects, or whether the specific type of meditation (mindfulness, compassion training, TM) produces meaningfully different outcomes beyond basic relaxation, remains an area of active research. Benson's generic model may underestimate technique-specific effects.

**Clinical translation**: Demonstrating physiological changes in laboratory conditions does not automatically establish clinical effectiveness for specific diseases. The stress-reduction claims are strong; claims about reversing established organic disease are more uncertain.

**Note**: Mainstream medicine accepts the relaxation response as a validated, clinically useful intervention for stress-related conditions. It is now standard in cardiac rehabilitation, pain management, and anxiety treatment programs worldwide.
