# Sabbatianism

**Summary**: The 17th-century Jewish messianic movement around Sabbatai Zevi and his prophet Nathan of Gaza — a mass movement that collapsed into theological crisis when its Messiah apostasized to Islam, producing history's most sustained Jewish mystical heresy: the paradoxical doctrine of the "holy sinner" and, in its radical (Frankist) form, the "holiness of sin."

**Sources**:
- [Scholem, G. (1995). *Major trends in Jewish mysticism* (3rd rev. ed.). Schocken Books. (Original work published 1941)](../sources/major-trends-in-jewish-mysticism.md)

## Overview

In 1665–1666, **Sabbatai Zevi** (1626–1676), a Kabbalistically educated Sephardic Jew from Smyrna, was proclaimed the Messiah by **Nathan of Gaza** (1644–1680), a young, charismatic prophet who experienced a decisive visionary revelation confirming Sabbatai Zevi's messianic identity. The movement spread with extraordinary speed across the entire Jewish Diaspora — Europe, the Middle East, North Africa — producing mass penitence, ecstatic joy, and the conviction, for many ordinary Jews, that thousands of years of exile were finally ending. It collapsed within a year: brought before the Ottoman Sultan and given the choice between execution and conversion, Sabbatai Zevi converted to Islam.

[Gershom Scholem](../thinkers/gershom-scholem.md)'s account, drawing on then-newly available primary documents from Sabbatai Zevi's and Nathan's own circle, gives this episode its first serious historical-psychological treatment.

## Sabbatai Zevi's Mental Illness

Scholem argues, from close reading of contemporary testimony, that Sabbatai Zevi exhibited a clear pattern of what would now be recognized as bipolar disorder ("manic-depressive" psychosis in Scholem's period terminology) from his teenage years onward — alternating states of profound depression and ecstatic, euphoric exaltation, without the disorganization of thought or personality that would indicate a different, more strictly psychotic illness. Crucially, Scholem does not treat this as debunking the movement: he argues Nathan of Gaza's theological genius was to give Sabbatai Zevi's manic states a positive doctrinal meaning (as periods of mystical "illumination," corresponding to Lurianic states of "the Countenance shining") and his depressive states another (as periods when the Messiah's soul had, in Lurianic terms, again fallen captive to the *kelipot*/husks — see [Lurianic Kabbalah](lurianic-kabbalah.md)) — turning what was, medically, a mood disorder into what believers experienced as the visible sign of a soul locked in cosmic struggle.

## The Crisis of Apostasy

The movement's real historical significance, for Scholem, begins not with Sabbatai Zevi's rise but with his fall. His conversion to Islam should have ended the movement entirely — and largely did, for the majority. But a substantial minority of believers, unable to abandon a conviction so total, developed instead an increasingly elaborate theology to explain **why the Messiah's apostasy was itself a necessary part of his redemptive mission**: drawing on the Lurianic doctrine of gathering scattered sparks from the kelipot, Nathan and later theologians (chiefly the ex-Marrano **Abraham Miguel Cardozo**) argued the Messiah must himself descend into the very heart of evil/impurity in order to redeem the sparks trapped there — his apostasy was not failure but the final, most difficult stage of *tikkun*, comparable to the Shekhinah's own descent into exile.

## The Doctrine of Holiness in Sin

The most radical theological consequence, developed by later, more extreme Sabbatians and taken to its furthest point by **Jacob Frank** (1726–1791) in 18th-century Poland, was the doctrine that if the Messiah's own transgression could be sacred, then deliberate transgression by his followers could likewise become a paradoxical vehicle of redemption — "a sin committed for its own sake" as a form of holiness. This produced antinomian and, in Frank's circle, explicitly libertine and self-consciously nihilistic practices, framed as accelerating redemption by actively subverting a Torah/law now held to belong only to the still-unredeemed old world. Frank himself eventually led a large group of followers into formal conversion to Catholicism in 1759.

## Historical Consequences: An Unexpected Bridge to Enlightenment and Reform

Scholem's most striking historiographical claim is that Sabbatianism — precisely because it was suppressed, denied, and its adherents' descendants motivated to hide their ancestry — became, one to two generations later, an important covert channel feeding the 18th–19th century Jewish Enlightenment (Haskalah) and religious Reform movements. Individuals and families with Sabbatian or Frankist backgrounds (which Scholem documents was, for a long period, a source of deep shame actively concealed even from later generations) were disproportionately represented among early reformers, a connection Scholem compares to the parallel, better-studied relationship between radical Protestant pietist/mystical sects (Anabaptists, Quakers) and the rise of Enlightenment rationalism in Christian Europe: a mystical, heretical religious rupture clearing intellectual and emotional ground that a later, more purely rationalist movement then occupied.

## Cross-Wiki Parallels

**The paradox of the holy sinner**: Sabbatianism's central theological problem — can a figure's transgression itself be redemptive? — has a structural (not historically connected) parallel in Christian theology's own paradoxes around the crucifixion of an innocent Messiah; Scholem explicitly compares and contrasts the two, noting that Jesus's death, however paradoxical theologically, was at least a death, while Sabbatai Zevi's apostasy required believers to sanctify an act of apparent betrayal and cowardice — a much harder psychological task, in Scholem's assessment, which he argues explains Sabbatianism's much greater doctrinal extremity and shorter, more contested afterlife compared to Christianity.

**Mysticism curdling into dogma or heresy**: This wiki has repeatedly noted the tension between a mystical tradition's original transformative charge and its later institutionalization (see, e.g., the Zohar's own gradual canonization in [Zohar: The Book of Enlightenment](../sources/zohar-book-of-enlightenment.md)). Sabbatianism is this wiki's most dramatic case of the opposite failure mode: a mystical-messianic charge that could not be safely contained by institutional religion at all, producing open heresy and schism rather than gradual absorption.

## Balance and Critique

**A cautionary case, not a template**: Scholem is careful not to romanticize Sabbatianism; he treats the antinomian and nihilistic excesses of its radical wing (particularly Frankism) as a genuine catastrophe for the individuals and communities involved, distinct from, though caused by, the same underlying religious energy that produced its more moderate and historically productive forms.

**Diagnosis at a historical distance**: Scholem's retrospective psychiatric diagnosis of Sabbatai Zevi (bipolar disorder) should be read with appropriate caution, as with any diagnosis made centuries after the fact from limited testimonial evidence rather than clinical observation — a methodological caveat that applies generally whenever this wiki's sources offer historical-psychiatric readings of figures who cannot be examined directly.
