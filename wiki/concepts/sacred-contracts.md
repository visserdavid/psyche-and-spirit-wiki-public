# Sacred Contracts

**Summary**: Caroline Myss's central thesis in her 2001 work: before birth, each soul enters into an agreement with the Divine specifying the lessons to learn, the people to meet, and the purpose to fulfil — and the life task is to discover and live out that agreement.

**Sources**:
- [Hillman, J. (1996). *The soul's code: In search of character and calling*. Random House.](../sources/souls-code.md)
- [Myss, C. (2001). *Sacred contracts: Awakening your divine potential*. Harmony Books.](../sources/sacred-contracts.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## The Concept

A Sacred Contract, in Myss's framework, is a pre-incarnation agreement between the soul and the Divine. Before birth, the soul selects — from a range of possibilities offered by guides or divine intermediaries — the key elements of its coming life: the lessons it agrees to learn, the relationships it will enter (including adversarial ones), the challenges it will face, and the contribution it will make. The individual then arrives on earth having forgotten the particulars of this agreement, and the life task becomes one of recognition and fulfilment.

The Contract is not deterministic. It coexists with free will: the soul chooses which challenges to face, but has latitude in how and when to respond. Myss describes the Contract as establishing the *form* of a life's curriculum while leaving the *content* of choices to the individual.

## Historical and Cross-Cultural Precedents

Myss grounds the concept in a wide range of traditions:

- **Plato's Myth of Er** (*Republic*, Book 10): Souls waiting to reincarnate choose their next life from a range of templates offered by the Fates, guided by a *daimon* (guardian spirit). They drink from the River of Forgetfulness before birth, losing explicit memory of their choice. [Plato](../thinkers/plato.md) is the most fully developed philosophical precedent Myss cites.
- **Vedic tradition (Mitra)**: The Aryan god Mitra oversaw contracts between humans and Heaven, judging compliance and issuing consequences for breach.
- **Norse tradition (Var)**: The goddess Var heard marriage vows and compacts and punished those who broke them.
- **Aztec tradition (Tezcatlipoca)**: God of night and fate, whose mirror reflected all human actions and held accounts.
- **Hebrew Bible**: Covenants between Yahweh and Noah, Abraham, and Israel — bilateral agreements with obligations on both sides — function as cultural templates for the idea of a binding human-Divine contract.
- **Christian, Islamic, and Buddhist traditions**: The lives of Abraham, Jesus, Muhammad, and the Buddha all follow a recognizable pattern of pre-destined mission, resistance, testing, and eventual surrender — which Myss reads as the five stages of Sacred Contract unfolding. See [Five Stages of Sacred Contract](five-stages-of-sacred-contract.md).

## The Contract and Free Will

Myss is careful to distinguish Sacred Contracts from fatalism. She argues:

- The *form* of the Contract (the lessons, the key relationships) is set before birth; the *responses* to it are chosen moment to moment.
- Grace can be drawn upon through prayer and meditation to strengthen the ability to fulfil the Contract.
- Refusing to engage the Contract's challenges does not eliminate them but delays or intensifies them — the "Appointment in Samarra" dynamic.
- The Contract evolves as the soul develops: initial terms may expand as insight grows.

## Subcontracts and Relationships

Every significant relationship in a life is, in Myss's framework, a subcontract: a specific agreement between two souls to meet, interact, and serve each other's growth. Adversaries, teachers, and lovers are all potentially contracted. The "animation factor" — the heightened electricity felt in encounters with certain people — signals that a subcontract is in play.

## The Role of Archetypes

The primary language through which the Sacred Contract operates is archetypal. Twelve archetypal patterns — four universal survival archetypes and eight personal companions — shape the energy patterns of the Contract. They govern which domains of life are most active, what challenges recur, and where healing is needed. See [Archetypes](archetypes.md), [Four Survival Archetypes](four-survival-archetypes.md), and [Archetypal Wheel](archetypal-wheel.md).

## Relationship to *Anatomy of the Spirit*

In [Anatomy of the Spirit](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md) (1996), Myss argued that life experiences and emotional stress become encoded in the body, eventually contributing to illness. *Sacred Contracts* extends this: those biographies are not random accumulations but expressions of pre-agreed soul assignments. The "biography is biology" principle ([Biography Is Biology](biography-is-biology.md)) is thus reframed — biography is the outward expression of a sacred curriculum.

## Critique

### From within holistic/spiritual traditions
Some critics in the New Age and spiritual communities argue that the framework risks determinism — if everything is pre-agreed, questions arise about how genuine the choices of a lifetime actually are. Myss addresses this by insisting on the priority of free will within the Contract's structure, but the tension is unresolved.

### Parallel framework: Hillman's Acorn Theory
[James Hillman](../thinkers/james-hillman.md)'s [Acorn Theory](acorn-theory.md), developed in [*The Soul's Code*](../sources/souls-code.md) (1996), is the closest parallel to Myss's Sacred Contracts in the broader holistic literature. Both frameworks share:

- Plato's Myth of Er as foundational myth (the soul choosing its lot before birth)
- The idea that a guardian companion (Hillman's [daimon](daimon.md); Myss's archetypal twelve) carries and guides the soul's image
- The claim that apparently negative life events serve the soul's development
- A critique of reductive causal explanations for who a person becomes

**Key differences**: Hillman's framework is psychological and imaginal — the daimon is an inner figure, not a divine covenant; the calling is characterized by image rather than by lesson and relationship. Myss's Contract is explicitly theological: a pre-birth agreement with the Divine, specifying assignments, relationships, and spiritual curriculum. Hillman's daimon is morally neutral (it can produce Hitler); Myss's Sacred Contract is oriented toward divine potential and healing. Hillman is primarily an interpreter of biography; Myss is primarily a practitioner of self-development.

### Philosophical critique
The model depends on the coherence of concepts — pre-birth soul, divine agreements, archetypal templates — that cannot be verified empirically. The Hillman parallel shows that these themes can be framed philosophically (imaginal psychology) without requiring theological commitments, but neither version is empirically testable.

### Mainstream scientific/medical critique
Note: mainstream psychology and neuroscience do not recognize pre-birth soul agreements or archetypal contracts as empirical phenomena. The framework operates entirely within a spiritual or metaphysical register. It is not falsifiable in the scientific sense. Therapeutic use of the archetypal wheel as a reflective tool for self-understanding does not require acceptance of the metaphysical claims.

### Risk of misapplication
Like [biography-is-biology](biography-is-biology.md), the Sacred Contracts framework risks implying that all of life's difficulties — illness, loss, injustice — are "meant" and serve a purpose. While Myss emphasizes that this is not the same as saying they are deserved, the distinction can blur in practice, potentially discouraging appropriate action or leading to self-blame.
