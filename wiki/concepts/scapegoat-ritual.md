# The Scapegoat Ritual

**Summary**: James George Frazer's comparative account of the periodic ritual transference of a community's accumulated evil, sin, sickness, or ill fortune onto a person, animal, or object, which is then expelled, exiled, or killed — documented from the ancient Near East and Mexico to the Roman Saturnalia, and, in Frazer's most controversial argument, connected to the mock-king humiliation preceding the Crucifixion of Christ.

**Sources**:
- [Frazer, J. G. (1994). *The golden bough: A study in magic and religion* (R. Fraser, Ed.). Oxford University Press. (Original work published 1890)](../sources/the-golden-bough.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-22

## The Pattern

Frazer documents a recurring ritual structure across widely separated cultures: a community's accumulated misfortune — illness, sin, guilt, bad luck — is treated as a quasi-physical substance that can be gathered up and transferred onto a carrier, who is then driven out, exiled, or killed, carrying the evil away with them and leaving the community purified. The carrier can be:

- **A person**: sometimes a condemned criminal, sometimes a specially selected temporary "mock king" given a period of license and privilege before being killed or expelled — Frazer connects this directly to his account of dying-and-rising sacred kingship (see [Dying-and-Rising God](dying-and-rising-god.md)).
- **An animal**: the literal biblical scapegoat (Leviticus 16), driven into the wilderness bearing the sins of Israel.
- **An object**: an effigy, a ritually prepared vessel, or — in the example Frazer traces to his own family history via an ancestor's account of Tibet — an ornate necklace presented to and carried away by the Teshu Lama.

Frazer surveys examples from ancient Near Eastern purification rites, Aztec and other Mexican ritual practice, and the Roman Saturnalia, in which the ordinary social order was inverted for a fixed period (masters served by slaves, a mock king appointed and given license) before being forcibly restored — a pattern Frazer reads as a controlled, periodic release-and-purge of accumulated social disorder.

## Frazer's Most Controversial Argument: The Crucifixion

In the expanded 1900 and 1906–1915 editions, Frazer devoted extended argument to reading the mockery of Christ before the Crucifixion — the crown of thorns, the purple robe, the derisive homage of "King of the Jews" — as a specific instance of the mock-king-as-scapegoat pattern found in the Saturnalia and related festivals: a condemned man given temporary royal honors before being killed to carry away the community's sin. Frazer never states the theological implication outright ("I make no reference to the parallelism, leaving readers to draw their own conclusions," he told his publisher), but the architecture of the expanded editions builds visibly toward this point. After public criticism from the folklorist Andrew Lang, Frazer progressively softened, then relegated to an appendix, then cut entirely, this argument from his own 1922 one-volume abridgment. The Oxford World's Classics edition used in this wiki (ed. Robert Fraser) restores the material — see [The Golden Bough](../sources/the-golden-bough.md) for the full publication history.

## Relevance to This Wiki

The scapegoat mechanism is a mythological-anthropological parallel to several psychological concepts already in the wiki: [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s concept of the [shadow](shadow.md) — the disowned, unintegrated parts of the self projected outward onto others — operates at the individual level in a way structurally similar to Frazer's collective ritual transference of evil onto an external carrier. [M. Scott Peck](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md)'s account of [Human Evil](human-evil.md) and the psychology of scapegoating within families and groups likewise describes a psychological mechanism of guilt-displacement onto a designated victim that mirrors Frazer's ritual pattern at the level of family and institutional dynamics.

## Critique

**From anthropology and ritual studies**: René Girard's later theory of the scapegoat mechanism (*Violence and the Sacred*, 1972 — not yet in this wiki; see `wiki/potential-sources.md`) both extends and substantially revises Frazer's account, arguing that scapegoating is not merely one ritual technique among others but the foundational mechanism generating religion and social order as such, by redirecting reciprocal violence onto a single unanimous victim. Girard's theory is taken more seriously by contemporary religious studies than Frazer's broader evolutionary framework, though it shares Frazer's comparative, cross-cultural method.

**From biblical scholarship and Christian theology**: Frazer's reading of the Crucifixion as one instance of a universal mock-king-scapegoat pattern is theologically unacceptable to orthodox Christianity, which insists on the historical and theological uniqueness of the Atonement rather than its status as a token of a general mythological type — the same tension already noted regarding Frazer's material in [Dying-and-Rising God](dying-and-rising-god.md).

**From mainstream anthropology**: as with the rest of Frazer's synthesis, the scapegoat material was assembled from secondhand ethnographic reports rather than fieldwork, and its comparative method (treating structurally similar-looking customs from unrelated cultures as instances of one underlying pattern) is now considered methodologically outdated — see [James George Frazer](../thinkers/james-george-frazer.md) for the fuller discussion of this critique.
