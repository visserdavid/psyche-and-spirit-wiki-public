# Sefirot

**Summary**: The ten qualities of the Divine described in the Jewish Kabbalistic tradition (Tree of Life), which Myss identifies as a third parallel mapping of the seven-level energy system alongside the chakras and Christian sacraments.

## Overview

The ten sefirot (singular: sefirah) are the ten attributes or emanations through which the Infinite (Ein Sof) reveals itself and continually creates the universe, according to the Kabbalistic tradition of Jewish mysticism. They are arranged in a pattern called the **Tree of Life** — a diagram with roots in the heavens and branches reaching down to earth — which depicts the structure of Divine unfolding and the path of human spiritual ascent.

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) draws on the sefirot, particularly as interpreted in the *Zohar* (the central Kabbalistic text), as cited through Daniel Chanan Matt's *Zohar: The Book of Enlightenment* (Paulist Press, 1983). She reads the sefirot as a third independent mapping of the same seven-level human energy architecture described by the [chakra system](chakra-system.md) and the [Christian sacraments](christian-sacraments.md).

## The Ten Sefirot

Myss lists the sefirot in descending order (from the Divine crown down toward earthly manifestation):

1. **Keter** (Crown): The highest sefirah; the point at which Divine inspiration first manifests, beyond all identity or specificity. Corresponds to chakra 7.

2. **Hokhmah** (Wisdom): The first contact point of Divine thought — energy before it takes form. Associated with the animus tone of manifestation. Paired with Binah; together they correspond to chakra 6.

3. **Binah** (Understanding/Intelligence): The Divine mother; the womb from which creation is born. The anima counterpart of Hokhmah. Paired with Hokhmah; corresponds to chakra 6.

4. **Hesed** (Love/Mercy/Greatness): The quality of love and greatness. Paired with Gevurah; together they correspond to chakra 5.

5. **Gevurah** (Judgment/Power): Also called Din — the quality of power and judgment. The right and left arms of God (with Hesed) must balance each other. Corresponds to chakra 5.

6. **Tif'eret** (Compassion/Beauty/Harmony): The heart of the Tree, described as the "sun" or centre. Balances all qualities of the sefirot. Corresponds to chakra 4.

7. **Nezah** (Endurance): The quality of stamina and the power to maintain strength. Paired with Hod; together they represent the legs of the Divine body. Corresponds to chakra 3.

8. **Hod** (Majesty): Described as the quality of integrity and majesty; one of the sources of prophecy. Paired with Nezah. Corresponds to chakra 3.

9. **Yesod** (Foundation/Phallus): The sefirah of creative force — the energy that merges and generates new form. The "Righteous One" (Proverbs 10:25), foundation of the world. Corresponds to chakra 2.

10. **Shekhinah** (also Malkhut — Kingdom; or Keneset Yisra'el — Community of Israel): The feminine, earthly presence of the Divine; the point at which Divine energy grounds in physical reality. Corresponds to chakra 1.

## The Alignment

Myss maps ten sefirot onto seven energy levels by pairing certain sefirot at the same level (Hokhmah/Binah, Hesed/Gevurah, Nezah/Hod) — consistent with the standard Kabbalistic pairing of complementary qualities. The resulting seven levels mirror the seven chakras and seven sacraments.

The key idea: just as human beings are described in Genesis 1:27 as created "in the image of God," the ten qualities of the Divine are also the ten qualities human beings are mandated to develop and refine over a lifetime. The sefirot are not merely descriptions of God's nature; they are a blueprint for human spiritual maturation.

## Tif'eret as Heart Centre

Myss pays particular attention to Tif'eret — the sixth sefirah, the heart of the Tree. It is described as compassion, beauty, and harmony; it balances all the other sefirot. Myss aligns it with the fourth chakra (heart) and the sacrament of Marriage. In her reading, the heart chakra is the energetic centre of the entire system — the point at which the inner and outer dimensions of power meet.

## Shekhinah as Tribal Anchor

The Shekhinah — the feminine, indwelling presence of God, associated with the community of Israel and with the earth — aligns with the first chakra's energy of tribal connection and community. The *Zohar* describes Shekhinah as the doorway through which the Divine enters: "One who enters must enter through this gate." Myss reads this as the energy truth underlying the first-chakra teaching: to ascend spiritually, one must first honour one's community and origins.

## Primary Source: The Zohar's Account

The *Zohar* — the central Kabbalistic text, composed by Moses de León in 13th-century Spain — provides substantially more detail and drama than Myss's synthesis captures.

### Ein Sof as Ground

Underlying the sefirot is *Ein Sof* (the Infinite), utterly beyond attributes or description. Ein Sof is never named in the Zohar directly; it is known only through its ten emanations. "Ein Sof is not hinted at in Torah, Prophets, or the words of the Rabbis." The sefirot relate to Ein Sof "as flame joined to coal" — inseparable but not identical. See: [Ein Sof](ein-sof.md)

### The Patriarchs as Living Sefirot

The Zohar reads the Genesis narratives as accounts of the Patriarchs embodying and attaining their respective sefirot:
- **Abraham** embodies *Hesed* (Love) — his test was whether love could survive the demand to sacrifice Isaac; having endured, he became the archetype of divine love
- **Isaac** embodies *Gevurah/Din* (Judgment) — the binding is his trial; he represents the rigor that must be balanced by love
- **Jacob** embodies *Tif'eret* (Beauty/Compassion) — the synthesis; Jacob wrestling with the angel and receiving the name "Israel" marks his attainment of the heart of the Tree

### The Sitra Ahra: The Other Side

The Zohar introduces the *Sitra Ahra* (the Other Side) as the demonic realm rooted in Gevurah/Din untempered by Hesed. Lilit and Samael are its personifications. The Sitra Ahra is not an independent evil but a consequence of divine imbalance; it empowers whenever sin separates Shekhinah from Tif'eret. The mystic must encounter and overcome the Sitra Ahra (as Jacob wrestled, as Abraham descended into Egypt) to complete spiritual development. This parallels Jung's [Shadow](shadow.md) work in structure.

### The Sacred Marriage: Tif'eret and Shekhinah

The central drama of the Zohar is the union and separation of the sixth sefirah (Tif'eret, the Blessed Holy One, masculine) and the tenth (Shekhinah, the Divine Presence, feminine). Their union generates souls and sustains creation; their separation (caused by human sin) is cosmic catastrophe. Human prayer, righteous action, and Torah observance restore the union. The Sabbath is the weekly enactment of their sacred marriage. See: [Shekhinah](shekhinah.md), [Tikkun](tikkun.md)

### Four Levels of Torah

The Zohar teaches that Torah — and by extension all sacred text — has four levels: garment (surface story), body (commandments), soul (hidden wisdom), and soul of the soul (the ineffable mystery). "Fools see only the garment." This four-level hermeneutic prefigures the four levels of reading in other traditions (Buddhist tantric levels of teaching, ACIM's distinction between letter and spirit) and adds exegetical depth to Myss's use of scriptural material.

## Critique

**From within Kabbalah**: Myss's use of the sefirot is selective and syncretic. Kabbalistic thought is a complex mystical cosmology embedded in Jewish law, liturgy, and community practice — not a seven-step personal development framework. The specific correspondences Myss draws (e.g., Yesod = chakra 2 sexuality/creativity) have some basis in Kabbalistic interpretation but are filtered through her own framework and do not represent mainstream Jewish scholarly consensus.

**On the synthesis**: The alignment of ten sefirot with seven chakras requires compression (pairing sefirot at certain levels), and the correspondences Myss proposes differ from those in other syncretic or Theosophical mappings. There is no single "correct" alignment; Myss's is one interpretation.

**On spiritual appropriation**: Non-Jewish use of Kabbalistic material — especially when detached from the Jewish communal and legal context in which it developed — raises concerns about the integrity of the framework being borrowed.
