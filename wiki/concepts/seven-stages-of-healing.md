# Seven Stages of Healing

**Summary**: Brennan's model of the patient's subjective journey through the healing process — seven stages from denial to creation, extending Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's five stages of death and dying with two post-acceptance stages of rebirth and active creation.

**Sources**:
- [Brennan, B. A. (1993). *Light emerging: The journey of personal healing*. Bantam Books.](../sources/light-emerging.md)
- [Kübler-Ross, E. (1969). *On death and dying*. Macmillan.](../sources/on-death-and-dying.md)

## Overview

In Chapter 7 of *[Light Emerging](../sources/light-emerging.md)*, [Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md) describes a recurring pattern she observed across hundreds of healing cases: the patient's experience of the healing journey follows a predictable sequence of stages, regardless of the nature of the illness. These stages are not strictly linear — patients spiral through them, sometimes revisiting earlier stages — but they constitute a map of the territory that illness demands be traversed.

Brennan builds on Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's famous five-stage model of death and dying (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) and extends it with two additional stages that describe what happens *after* acceptance: rebirth and creation. She argues that healing is not merely acceptance of limitation but the beginning of a genuinely new life.

> "Healing requires changes in mind, emotion, and spirit before change can occur in the physical. A person needs to reevaluate their relationship to issues involved in the healing process and set a new context."
> — Brennan (1993), Chapter 7

## The Seven Stages

### Stage 1: Denial

**What it is**: The refusal to acknowledge the illness, its severity, or the changes it requires.

**Function**: Denial is not pathological in itself — it serves as a necessary buffer, giving the psyche time to prepare for what cannot yet be fully confronted. Healthy denial is temporary; it protects while preparation occurs.

**Cautions**: Denial becomes destructive when it prevents seeking necessary medical treatment, when it persists beyond the initial shock, or when it blocks acknowledging the emotional and lifestyle dimensions of the illness. Health care professionals, Brennan notes, must be careful not to try to "pull" patients out of denial before they are ready — and must examine their own reactions to denial, which often create pressure on patients that is counterproductive.

**Clinical notes**: Patients in denial may: focus exclusively on treating the physical symptom and resist any exploration of psychological dimensions; change the subject when the illness is raised; insist they are fine when evidence clearly indicates they are not; take information about their condition and minimise or reframe it.

### Stage 2: Anger

**What it is**: The natural response when denial can no longer be maintained. "Why is this happening to me?" "This isn't fair." "I don't deserve this."

**Function**: Anger contains energy. Its emergence signals that the person is no longer entirely frozen in denial — the energy is moving. The anger is often displaced (toward the doctor, the diagnosis, God, a family member) rather than aimed at the actual source of pain.

**Cautions**: The healer's role at this stage is not to deflect or resolve the anger but to create space for it to be expressed. Trying to immediately move a patient from anger to acceptance is experienced as invalidation and typically produces entrenchment rather than movement.

### Stage 3: Bargaining

**What it is**: The attempt to negotiate with the illness, the divine, or the medical system. "If I change this, will I heal?" "If I do the right things, will it go away?" "Maybe if I just try harder..."

**Function**: Bargaining is the stage of conditional acceptance — a tentative engagement with the possibility of change, but with the hope of maintaining control. It is an important transition: the patient is beginning to consider that they might need to change, but is not yet ready to commit unconditionally.

**The "right treatment" search**: In this stage, patients often intensely search for "the right" treatment, healer, protocol, or diet — sometimes going through many practitioners in rapid succession. This search is part of bargaining: the hope that the right external intervention will produce healing without requiring deep personal transformation.

### Stage 4: Depression

**What it is**: The settling of the full weight of the situation. The loss — of the old life, of the belief in invulnerability, of a body that "worked" — becomes real. Grief, hopelessness, and exhaustion may all be present.

**Function**: Depression at this stage is not a pathology to be medicated away but a necessary encounter with reality. It is the phase of deepest surrender — not of acceptance (that comes next) but of the pretence that the old life can continue unchanged.

**Cautions**: Premature intervention to "cheer up" the patient in this stage can short-circuit the grief process and prevent the deeper surrender that makes genuine healing possible. The healer's job is to accompany, not rescue.

### Stage 5: Acceptance

**What it is**: The turning point. The patient stops fighting the reality of the illness and begins to engage with it — asking "What does this illness want me to change? What am I being asked to learn?"

**The reframe**: Acceptance, in Brennan's model, is not passive resignation ("I'm just ill and that's that") but an active reorientation: the illness becomes a teacher, a message, an opportunity for transformation that would not have arisen any other way.

**Energy-level change**: At the moment of genuine acceptance, Brennan observes a shift in the patient's auric field — a softening of the defensive patterns, an opening in the chakras, and often a brightening of the field overall. This shift is sometimes dramatic and rapid; it can precede noticeable physical improvement.

### Stage 6: Rebirth

**What it is**: The first expressions of new life — new energy, new possibilities, a sense of beginning again. The person starts to discover who they are without the old patterns, old identity, old ways of relating.

**Uncertainty and disorientation**: Rebirth is not immediately comfortable. The old identity has died; the new one is not yet formed. This can feel profoundly disorienting. Patients may not know what they want, what they value, or how to navigate familiar situations without the old defenses and contracts.

**Emergence of the Higher Self**: As the mask self loses its grip (following the genuine surrender of Stage 5), the [Higher Self](mask-self-lower-self-higher-self.md) becomes more accessible. Moments of authentic expression, creative impulse, and spontaneous joy begin to appear — often startling to the patient who had forgotten they were possible.

### Stage 7: Creation

**What it is**: The active building of a new life aligned with what was discovered in the healing journey. Health is no longer just the absence of illness but the active, creative expression of who one now knows oneself to be.

**Living the life task**: At this stage, connection to the [haric level](hara-level.md) becomes important: what is the soul's longing? What is the life task this illness has cleared the way for? Creation means aligning daily choices with the answers to these questions.

**Ongoing spiral**: The seven stages are not traversed once and done. Each new crisis or growth edge initiates a new pass through the spiral. A person who has healed from cancer may find themselves in denial again when faced with the end of a marriage, or the death of a parent, or a career transition. The stages become familiar territory — and each pass through them is typically faster and more conscious than the last.

## Seven Levels of the Healing Process

In Chapter 8, Brennan elaborates the *dimensions* of healing at each of the seven auric levels — complementing the stages model with a map of the areas of life that need attention at each level:

| Auric Level | Domain | Need for Healing |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (Etheric) | Physical sensation | Enjoyment of body; physical self-care; exercise |
| 2 (Emotional) | Feelings | Self-acceptance; self-love; allowing feelings to flow |
| 3 (Mental) | Thought | Clarity; honest mental process; resolving thought distortions |
| 4 (Relational) | Relationships | Loving interaction with family, friends, partner, colleagues |
| 5 (Etheric template) | Will/Truth | Speaking truth; aligning actions with values; commitment |
| 6 (Celestial) | Spirituality | Experiencing love, ecstasy, spiritual connection |
| 7 (Ketheric) | Universal mind | Serenity; understanding pattern; perceiving perfection |

Healing at all seven levels simultaneously constitutes the fullest healing — a transformation of the entire person rather than merely the resolution of a symptom.

## Two Ingredients for the Healing Journey

Brennan identifies two essential prerequisites for moving through all seven stages:

**1. Reframing illness as a life lesson**: The illness must be understood not as an assault on a life that was otherwise fine, but as a message about something that needed to change. This reframe does not blame the patient for their illness; it does ask them to take responsibility for the response.

**2. Honouring the needs at each level of the field**: Rather than pursuing only physical cures, the patient genuinely investigates and begins meeting the unmet needs at each auric level. This means asking at each level: "What do I genuinely need here? What have I been denying myself?"

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

**Kübler-Ross's stages**: Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's five stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) are explicitly the foundation of Brennan's model. Brennan retains all five and adds rebirth and creation as the post-acceptance stages that specifically pertain to healing (not just to dying).

**Myss's perspective**: [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) approaches healing stages implicitly through the [seven sacred truths](seven-sacred-truths.md) — each truth is a dimension of life that needs to be integrated for the corresponding chakra to function healthily. The healing journey in Myss's frame is the progressive aligning of one's life with the truths of each chakra, which parallels Brennan's seven-level needs model.

**The hero's journey**: Brennan's seven stages parallel the [hero myth](hero-myth.md) — the call (illness as challenge), the descent (stages 1-4), the transformation (stage 5), and the return with gifts (stages 6-7). Both models describe a non-optional journey through darkness as the precondition for a new life.

## Critique

**Stage models and individuals**: All stage models risk being applied as rigid sequences. Brennan herself emphasises that the stages are not strictly linear — patients spiral, revisit, and oscillate. The model describes tendencies and a general arc, not a mandatory sequence.

**Cultural variation**: The stage model assumes a psychologized understanding of illness as an opportunity for personal transformation. This assumption is more easily held in conditions of relative safety and resource sufficiency; for people facing poverty, chronic oppression, or structural violence, the "transformation" frame can be experienced as dismissive of the material conditions generating illness.

**Evidence base**: Kübler-Ross's original stage model was observed in interview-based research with dying patients; subsequent research has questioned whether stages are universal or sequential. Brennan's extension to healing (rather than dying) is clinical observation, not systematically studied.
