# Shamanic Healing

**Summary**: The oldest documented healing system in human history, characterised by deliberate entry into an altered state of consciousness (the Shamanic State of Consciousness) to obtain diagnostic and therapeutic information from non-ordinary reality — found across virtually all pre-industrial cultures and identified by Jeanne Achterberg as the prototype for all healing that uses imagination.

**Sources**:
- [Achterberg, J. (1985). *Imagery in healing: Shamanism and modern medicine*. Shambhala.](../sources/imagery-in-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## What Is Shamanism?

Shamanism is not a religion but a set of practices for accessing non-ordinary states of consciousness with healing intent. Key features — identified independently across cultures on every inhabited continent — are:

1. **Deliberate entry into and exit from an altered state of consciousness** (the Shamanic State of Consciousness, SSC), at will and with specific healing purpose
2. **Access to a non-ordinary reality** experienced as a spirit world, underworld, or upper world containing diagnostic and therapeutic information
3. **Return with specific knowledge** applicable to the patient in ordinary reality
4. **Use of spirit guides, power animals, or allies** as sources of knowledge and protection during the journey
5. **Communal healing context**: shamanic healing is a community event; the entire village may participate in ceremony

[Mircea Eliade](../thinkers/mircea-eliade.md), the historian of religions who systematised the comparative study of shamanism (*Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, 1951), described the shaman as a "technician of ecstasy" — a practitioner who uses mastery of altered states as a technical skill for communal benefit. This distinguishes the shaman from the mystic (whose altered states are privately spiritual) and from the psychotic (who is overwhelmed by non-ordinary experience and cannot re-enter ordinary reality at will).

Eliade's own primary-sourced treatment of shamanic vocation — previewed a few years before *Shamanism* in *[Rites and Symbols of Initiation](../sources/rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md)* (1958) — documents the "initiatory sickness" that typically inaugurates the shamanic vocation: visionary dismemberment by ancestral spirits or demons, reduction to a bare skeleton, and reconstitution with new organs and new blood before the future shaman wakes with the power to heal. Eliade is explicit that this crisis, however psychotic-seeming, is *resolved* rather than perpetuated by the initiatory pattern — the shaman is precisely the one who has integrated the crisis into a new, functioning personality, which is the same distinction Achterberg draws below between the SSC and psychosis. See [Symbolic Death and Rebirth](symbolic-death-and-rebirth.md) for Eliade's fuller argument.

## Historical Depth

The earliest archaeological evidence of shamanic practice consists of cave paintings from Les Trois Frères, France (approximately 17,000 BCE), depicting a figure interpreted by archaeologists as a costumed ritual practitioner dancing with animal hybrid features — the classic posture of shamanic transformation. Rock art and burial evidence across Siberia, the Americas, Africa, Australia, and Central Asia suggests that shamanic practice is at least 20,000 years old and possibly older.

[Jeanne Achterberg](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md) argues in [*Imagery in Healing*](../sources/imagery-in-healing.md) that this universality — shamanic features recurring without documented cultural contact — suggests shamanic practice engages a real human capacity rather than representing cultural invention. The SSC, and the healing knowledge accessed within it, may be as "hard-wired" into human neurology as language or social bonding.

## The Shamanic State of Consciousness (SSC)

Achterberg, drawing on Michael Harner (*The Way of the Shaman*, 1980) and Charles Tart's theory of altered states, characterises the SSC as:

- Voluntarily entered and exited, with full recall upon return
- Phenomenologically different from sleep, dreaming, hypnosis, psychosis, and ordinary waking consciousness
- Associated with heightened access to imagery — vivid, autonomous, and informationally rich inner representations
- Accessible via specific induction methods available without chemical assistance

**EEG correlates**: Research on related altered states (sensory deprivation, REST — Restricted Environmental Stimulation Therapy — and out-of-body experiences) suggests the SSC is associated with **theta wave activity** (4–7 Hz), the brain rhythm associated with reverie, creativity, hypnagogic imagery, and the boundary between waking and sleep. Theta activity is normally suppressed in ordinary waking consciousness (dominated by beta, 14–40 Hz) but emerges during relaxed attention, meditation, and states of creative absorption.

**The critical distinction from psychosis**: The schizophrenic is overwhelmed by non-ordinary experience and cannot exit at will; the shamanic healer controls the boundary and uses it as a tool. This distinction is clinically significant: shamanic traditions develop practitioners by training the capacity to enter and exit the SSC reliably, not by inducing overwhelming experiences. Some researchers (including Achterberg) note that poorly-supported individuals who enter the SSC without a cultural framework may be vulnerable — the "shamanic crisis" can look like psychosis in cultures without a holding container for such experiences.

**Lawrence LeShan's "reality 2"**: [Lawrence LeShan](../thinkers/lawrence-leshan.md) independently characterised two consciousness modes in healers and mystics: "reality 1" (ordinary sensory-perceptual reality, in which objects are discrete, time is sequential, and causation is local) and "reality 2" (a unitary field in which all things are interconnected and time is non-sequential). Achterberg identifies LeShan's reality 2 as the secular-academic description of the same state the SSC gives access to.

## Induction Methods

Shamanic traditions worldwide have developed multiple methods for reliable SSC induction without chemical assistance:

**Rhythmic percussion**: Monotonous drumming at 4–7 beats per second drives theta wave activity — as documented in EEG studies of drumming and in research on "photic driving" (the brain's tendency to entrain to rhythmic stimulation). This is the most widely documented and cross-culturally consistent induction method; Harner's cross-cultural comparison finds it at the centre of shamanic practice on every inhabited continent.

**Sweat lodges and heat**: Sustained high-temperature exposure (sauna, sweat lodge, steam bath) induces vasodilation, altered body awareness, and mild hypercapnia (elevated CO2 from rebreathing in enclosed spaces) — all of which shift consciousness. Many Native North American traditions use the sweat lodge as the preparatory context for healing ceremony.

**Fasting and sleep deprivation**: Reduced caloric intake and sleep produce neurological changes that facilitate altered states. Vision quests in Plains Indian traditions combine fasting, isolation, and sleep deprivation over four days.

**Isolation and darkness**: Sensory reduction (darkness, silence, confinement) removes ordinary perceptual anchoring and facilitates theta-range activity. REST research confirms behavioural and perceptual changes after 1–2 hours of sensory reduction.

**Plant medicines**: Ayahuasca, peyote, datura, Psilocybe mushrooms, Amanita muscaria — used across Amazonian, Mesoamerican, Native North American, and Eurasian traditions respectively. These alter brain chemistry more dramatically than the non-chemical methods; they are associated with longer and more intense SSC experiences but are culturally embedded in highly structured ritual frameworks designed to manage the experience.

**Intense physical exertion**: Spirit dancing (as in the Salish Coast Salish traditions of the Pacific Northwest) uses sustained physical movement, exhaustion, and hyperventilation to produce altered states as part of healing initiation ceremonies.

## The Shaman as Diagnostician and Healer

The shaman's role combines what modern medicine separates:

- **Diagnosis**: Reading the patient's condition through SSC-accessed imagery — frequently described as perceiving the "spirit cause" of the illness (lost soul parts, intrusive spiritual objects, ancestral imbalances)
- **Treatment**: Journeying on behalf of the patient to negotiate with spirit forces, retrieve lost soul fragments, extract intrusive elements, or receive specific healing information
- **Prescription**: Providing the patient with tasks, rituals, plant medicines, or community ceremonies to enact the healing
- **Prognosis**: In some traditions, the shaman reports whether the case is treatable within their skill range or should be referred to another specialist

This parallels, structurally, the role of the [medical intuitive](medical-intuition.md): a practitioner who accesses diagnostic information through non-sensory means and reports it to the patient or physician. [Caroline Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) and [Norman Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) represent the contemporary Western clinical equivalent.

## Shamanism and Modern Medicine: Achterberg's Thesis

Achterberg argues that shamanism and modern medicine are not opposed but are different expressions of a single healing lineage — specifically the lineage that uses imagination as its primary therapeutic vehicle.

Her argument structure:
1. Shamanism is the oldest and most universal healing system: it predates every other recorded medical tradition
2. The core shamanic mechanism is imagery (SSC-accessed inner representation used diagnostically and therapeutically)
3. Every subsequent healing tradition in the historical record — Asclepian dream incubation, Galenic humoral medicine, medieval herbal and ritual healing, Renaissance natural magic, modern hypnosis, biofeedback, and visualisation — preserves and adapts this core mechanism
4. Modern [psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md) provides the biochemical account of *how* imagery affects physiology (through hypothalamic-pituitary-immune-endocrine pathways)
5. Shamanism was right — not metaphorically but literally — in asserting that inner representations change bodily states

The historical interruption between shamanism and modern medicine, in Achterberg's account, was the Cartesian split (17th century) and the earlier suppression of the women-healer tradition through the witch trials (15th–17th centuries). Both events severed medicine from its imagination-based roots, producing the exclusively mechanistic paradigm that dominated until psychoneuroimmunology began reassembling the picture in the 1970s.

## Critique

**Romanticisation risk**: Achterberg is careful to note that shamanic healing had high failure rates — shamans referred cases they could not treat, and practices varied enormously in effectiveness. The uniformity of *form* (SSC, spirit journey, imagery) does not imply uniformity of *outcome*. Pre-contact indigenous populations still died of infections, parasites, injuries, and famine. Achterberg does not claim shamanism outperformed biomedicine for trauma or infection; she claims it accessed something biomedicine has abandoned.

**Cultural context dependency**: SSC induction methods, spirit world maps, and healing rituals are deeply embedded in specific cultural frameworks. Extracting the "active ingredient" (rhythmic drumming, imagery, community participation) from its cultural context risks diluting or distorting the tradition. Harner's "core shamanism" (the cross-cultural extraction approach) has been criticised by indigenous practitioners for exactly this reason.

**Evidence limitations**: The phenomenological and anthropological evidence for shamanism's efficacy is extensive; the randomised-controlled-trial evidence is essentially absent. Achterberg presents shamanism as establishing the historical proof of concept for imagery-based healing, not as a controlled-study-validated intervention.

**Note**: From a mainstream medical perspective, the documented effects of shamanic healing (including spontaneous remission in contexts of ceremonial healing) are most plausibly explained by Type 1 mechanisms (relaxation, placebo, meaning-making, social support, endorphin release) rather than Type 2 (transpersonal transmission). Achterberg holds both options open.
