# Shekhinah

**Summary**: The feminine divine presence in Jewish Kabbalah — the tenth sefirah, the earthly face of God, Bride of Tif'eret — whose exile with Israel and yearning for reunion is the Zohar's central emotional and cosmological drama; her restoration through human action (tikkun) gives the spiritual life ultimate stakes.

## Overview

The Shekhinah (שְׁכִינָה, from *shakhan* — "to dwell") is the tenth and lowest sefirah, also called *Malkhut* (Kingdom), *Keneset Yisra'el* (Community of Israel), the Moon, the Earth, the Garden of Eden, the Holy Apple Orchard, the Mirror, and the Queen. She is the point at which the divine energy, cascading downward through all nine preceding sefirot, grounds itself in physical reality and human experience.

The term appears in rabbinic literature to mean God's immanent presence — the divine dwelling in the world. The Zohar radicalises this: the Shekhinah is not just God's presence but a distinct persona within the divine — the feminine face of the Infinite.

---

## The Sacred Marriage

The Shekhinah is the Bride of Tif'eret (the sixth sefirah, the Blessed Holy One, the King). Their union is the central aim of Kabbalistic practice and the source of all cosmic vitality: souls are born from their union; creation is sustained by their love. The Sabbath evening is the weekly celebration of their sacred marriage (*hieros gamos*) — mystics ritually enact the reunion through prayer and intention.

Human sin — particularly idolatry and immorality — ruptures this union by empowering the Sitra Ahra (the Other Side), the demonic force. The Sitra Ahra seduces the Shekhinah away from Tif'eret, defiling her. Righteous action and prayer restore the union.

The image of Tif'eret and Shekhinah as the cosmic couple provides the Zohar's erotic charge: "Come see — the Blessed Holy One does not place His abode in a place where no female is found. Blessings are found only in a place where the female is found."

---

## The Exile of the Shekhinah

The Zohar's most theologically significant innovation is the exile of the Shekhinah: just as Israel is in historical exile, so the divine feminine presence is in cosmic exile — separated from her Husband, accompanying her children into dispersion. "Wherever Israel went into exile, the Shekhinah went with them" (Talmud, developed extensively by the Zohar).

The Zohar uses this framework to reframe Jewish persecution: Israel is not abandoned by God — God Herself is in exile with Israel, suffering with the nation, yearning for reunion. The King (Tif'eret) watches from afar: "He climbs roofs, runs stairs, scales walls, peers through holes to catch a glimpse, and starts to cry at the wall."

This theology of divine suffering has profound implications for healing:
- Pain is not evidence of divine abandonment but of divine solidarity
- Human action — prayer, Torah, ethical behaviour — directly affects the divine realm (see [Tikkun](tikkun.md))
- The goal of spiritual life is not only personal redemption but cosmic repair

## Shekhinah as the Gate

The Zohar teaches: "One who enters must enter through this gate" (Zohar 1:7b) — the Shekhinah is the opening, the gate, the first point of contact with the divine realm. Myss maps this to the first chakra: tribal identity and community connection as the necessary foundation for spiritual ascent. One cannot bypass the Shekhinah on the way to Ein Sof; one enters the divine through embodied communal life, not by bypassing it.

This is a fundamental correction to Gnostic spirituality's tendency to flee the body and matter: in the Zohar, the physical world (symbolised by Shekhinah) is not an obstacle to the divine but its earthly face.

## The Shekhinah and the Feminine

The Zohar's Shekhinah is the most developed expression of the divine feminine in Jewish tradition. Her attributes:
- **Receptive**: She receives the flow from all nine preceding sefirot and grounds it
- **Reflective**: She is the Mirror — she shines with reflected light, not her own; prophets below Moses see through her (the "mirror that does not shine")
- **Communal**: She is *Keneset Yisra'el* — the collective soul of Israel
- **Vulnerable**: She can be defiled by sin, separated from her husband, and driven into exile
- **Earth/Moon**: She reflects the light of the Sun (Tif'eret) and rules cycles of time
- **Gate**: She is the opening through which all human prayer and divine blessing flows

## Relation to Other Wiki Concepts

**Northrup's Female Energy System**: Northrup's identification of the uterus and lower female centres as the site of creative power and communal identity parallels the Shekhinah's functions as earth, community, creativity, and the grounding point of divine energy. Both frameworks agree that female embodied experience is sacred rather than inferior. See: [Female Energy System](female-energy-system.md)

**Myss's Seven Sacred Truths**: Myss maps Shekhinah/Malkhut to the first chakra — tribal power, community, survival. The Shekhinah as communal soul grounds the entire seven-level system. See: [Seven Sacred Truths](seven-sacred-truths.md)

**ACIM's Holy Spirit**: The Shekhinah functions as the divine mediator between the transcendent Father (Ein Sof) and the human soul — the immanent aspect of the divine that accompanies, comforts, and guides. This is structurally similar to ACIM's Holy Spirit. The key difference: ACIM's Holy Spirit is not in exile; the Shekhinah is. See: [Ego vs. Holy Spirit](ego-vs-holy-spirit.md)

**The divine feminine across traditions**: The Shekhinah is the Jewish name for what Campbell calls the Goddess as Revealing Power ([Maya: Three Powers](maya-three-powers.md)), what Tibetan Buddhism calls the Dakini, and what Northrup and Myss locate in the lower female chakras. All are the feminine face of the sacred — embodied, communal, earthy, and vulnerable.

## Critique

**Gendered metaphysics**: The Zohar assigns feminine qualities (receptivity, reflection, vulnerability) and masculine qualities (initiative, light-generation, judgment) to the sefirot in ways that map onto traditional gender stereotypes. Feminist Kabbalah scholars (notably Judith Plaskow and Lynn Gottlieb) have argued that the Shekhinah, despite being divine, reinforces rather than liberates women from patriarchal associations.

**The exile narrative**: The framing of the Shekhinah as perpetually in exile, dependent on male action for restoration, has been critiqued as projecting Jewish historical helplessness onto the divine feminine rather than empowering it.

**From mainstream science**: No empirical evidence exists for a feminine dimension of divinity whose exile and restoration depends on human ethical action. The tikkun concept presupposes a metaphysics in which human moral action affects the cosmic structure — which mainstream science would classify as unfalsifiable.
