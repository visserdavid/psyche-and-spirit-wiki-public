# Somagrams

**Summary**: Drawings of one's somatic image — the experienced, felt body-form — introduced by Keleman in *Embodying Experience* (1987) as a diagnostic and therapeutic tool for making visible the organization of one's somatic patterns, conflicts, and biographical history.

**Sources**:
- [Keleman, S. (1987). *Embodying experience: Forming a personal life*. Center Press.](../sources/embodying-experience.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

A somagram is a drawing — not a realistic anatomical illustration but a somatic projection: an attempt to represent the *felt interior* of one's bodily organization. A somagram shows what the body's experience looks like from inside: areas of constriction and tension, zones of deadening or numbness, impulses held in check, energetic configuration in relation to others, the whole somatic story of one's characteristic stance in the world.

Keleman introduces somagrams as a practice tool within the [HOw Process](how-process.md). They function at Step One (what is my current somatic image?) and Step Five (what new somatic form is emerging?), though they can be made at any stage.

## Purpose and Use

Somagrams serve several functions:

**Revealing the public somatic layer**: The outer image one projects — the somatic statement one's body makes to the world — is often more visible to others than to oneself. Drawing this image brings it into explicit awareness.

**Making the interior visible**: The interior somatic image — the felt quality of constriction, excitation, longing, armour — is not usually accessible to verbal description alone. Drawing it bypasses the interpretive layer and makes the somatic organization directly visible.

**Tracking change over time**: A series of somagrams made across a period of therapeutic work maps the arc of reorganization. Patterns that were invisible or taken for granted become evident when drawn repeatedly.

**Creating a diagnostic language**: Somagrams communicate information about somatic organization that verbal language cannot easily carry. They show whether an area is overbound or underbound, whether energy is rising or falling, whether contact with others is sought through inflation or withdrawal.

## Case Examples from *Embodying Experience*

Keleman presents extended somagram sequences for several clients:

**Joel** drew two initial somagrams: one showing fire and wildness in the torso (arrows trying to reach the skin surface), contained by boundary lines at the shoulders, throat, and sides; a second showing the pattern of insult and wounding — the ingrained posture of rejection. His later somagrams tracked the reorganization of this pattern through work with the HOw process, developing a range of graduated contact (shifting eye contact rather than total engagement or total withdrawal).

**Louise** drew her pattern as a board stiffened at the back with a few hinges — the back holding the body in action while the front reached forward. Her somagrams revealed the split between reaching impulse and defended back, and the work of reorganizing them into an integrated front-back pulsation.

**Betty** produced nine somagrams tracing the evolution of her identity through different relational configurations with her father and mother: the girl-child receiving, the son competing with the father, the daughter fighting the corporation, the woman seeking gender with the father, the frightened girl with the mother, the defended daughter, and the desired new woman with wolf-belly and deer-reflex disorganized.

In each case, the somagrams did not precede the therapeutic work — they arose from it, and in turn oriented the next step.

## Somagrams and Story

Keleman argues that the body organizes itself around stories, and stories organize the body. The somagram captures both: it is simultaneously a somatic image and a narrative gesture. When a client draws a somagram and then writes a brief story to accompany it, the drawing reveals the somatic truth the story is built on — the actual muscle configuration underlying the verbal self-description.

Using the five steps in conjunction with somagrams:
- Step One: draw the somagram that represents the current image or situation
- Step Two: intensify the posture/tension pattern in the somagram
- Step Three: disorganize it (draw what the image looks like as it loosens)
- Step Four: draw what emerges from the pause
- Step Five: draw the new somatic form and practice enacting it

## Relationship to Other Diagnostic Frameworks

**Myss's [medical intuition](medical-intuition.md)**: Both somagram-making and Myss's energy reading attempt to make the invisible organization of the body visible. Myss reads the energy field; Keleman reads the somatic image. Both methods are non-standard by the criteria of mainstream medicine. The somagram has the advantage of being producible by the client themselves, making the reading a first-person act rather than a passive reception.

**Jung's [mandala](mandala.md)**: Jung used mandala-drawing as a method for accessing unconscious material and representing the state of the psyche in a given period. Somagrams perform an analogous function at the somatic level — they make the body's organization visible in the same way that mandalas make the psyche's organization visible. Both are projective image-based diagnostics.

## Critique

Somagrams have not been the subject of systematic validation research. Their clinical utility is reported by practitioners and clients within the Keleman tradition, but the interpretation of somagrams requires trained clinical judgment — there is no standardized coding system, no inter-rater reliability data, and no controlled outcome studies comparing somagram-based work to other therapeutic modalities.

The practice does, however, align with broader trends in expressive arts therapy and art therapy, where image-making is used as a therapeutic and diagnostic tool. These fields have a larger research base, and while the specifics differ from Keleman's somagrams, the general principle — that drawing can access and reorganize experience that verbal language cannot reach — is supported by a growing body of clinical and neurological research.
