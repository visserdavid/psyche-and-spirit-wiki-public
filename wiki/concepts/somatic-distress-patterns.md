# Somatic Distress Patterns

**Summary**: Keleman's typology of four characteristic body shapes — rigid, dense/compact, swollen, and collapsed — that result from chronic stress responses and encode a person's biographical history in their physical form.

**Sources**:
- [Keleman, S. (1985). *Emotional anatomy: The structure of experience*. Center Press.](../sources/emotional-anatomy.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

[Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md)'s central clinical contribution is his identification of four recurring patterns in which chronic stress, developmental injury, and biographical insult reshape the body's structure. These patterns are not disease categories or character flaws — they are biographical solutions: the shapes a body learns to maintain in order to survive the particular pressures of its developmental history.

Each pattern has:
- A characteristic **posture and anatomy**
- A characteristic **pulsatory quality** (see [Pulsation](pulsation.md))
- A characteristic **psychological style** and emotional world
- A characteristic **relational pattern**
- A corresponding approach to **somatic education** (reorganization)

## The Four Patterns

### 1. Rigid — Pulled Back (Withholder)

**Anatomy**: The body pulls backward and elongates. The thoracic vault expands; the diaphragm is pulled taut. Muscles of the spine, buttocks, and thighs contract, creating a stance of restrained tension. Pulsation is speeded and localized — excitation is contained rather than expressed.

**Biography**: Develops in families where independence was first encouraged and then met with humiliation or withdrawal of support. The person learns to withhold — to hold back assertion, tenderness, and need — as protection against disappointment.

**Psychology**: Strong, proud, independent, solitary, reliable. Underlying: fear of abandonment and dependency. The person makes others pursue; controls by withholding. States of mind include: self-righteous, rageful, daring.

**Relational style**: Dominates through silence and unavailability; challenges others; competes; needs to lead.

**Somatic education direction**: Soften and come to ground; allow sadness and longing; balance assertion with tenderness; restore pulsation to surface.

---

### 2. Rigid — Pulled Up (Dominator)

**Anatomy**: The body pulls upward and compresses. The neck elongates, the chin rises, the chest is braced in an inspiratory position. Muscles of the spine and neck stiffen the upper body; the pelvis is tucked. Pulsation is speeded but compressed — excitation gushes upward and cannot discharge through the lower body.

**Biography**: Develops where performance is demanded, tenderness shamed, and dominance is the family currency. The child stiffens against vulnerability and develops an elongated, braced stance as armor.

**Psychology**: Authoritative, rigid, blaming, inspiring, matriarchal/patriarchal. Demands and controls. Underlying: fear of weakness, collapse, and vulnerability.

**Somatic education direction**: Help yield; soften contractions of neck and lumbar spine; let chest descend; encourage pulsation to move downward.

---

### 3. Dense/Compact (Compacted)

**Anatomy**: The body is squeezed and compressed inward. The pouches are compacted — head pulled down toward chest, waist narrowed, pelvis pressed. The person takes up minimal space. Pulsation is speeded under pressure but cannot expand outward.

**Biography**: Develops in families where shame and humiliation accompanied displays of assertion or independence. The person "makes themselves small" — compresses the form to avoid the punishment that visibility or expansion brought.

**Psychology**: Stubbornly resistant, enduring, pushing against constraint. Underlying: fear of humiliation, loss of control, abandonment. The person holds: holds in, holds on, holds back.

**Somatic education direction**: Encourage elongation; separate pouches; allow expansion upward and outward; restore pressure to surface.

---

### 4. Swollen (Merging/Invasive)

**Anatomy**: The body inflates and loses boundary definition — pear-shaped, puffed, porous. The chest retreats while the abdomen protrudes. Pulsation is chaotic and meandering — excitation leaks outward rather than being contained.

**Biography**: Develops in families with an emphasis on closeness, merger, and emotional enmeshment. The child inflates to belong — boundary between self and other dissolves.

**Psychology**: Grandiose, seductive, chameleon-like, socially expansive, promises more than is delivered. Underlying: fear of not belonging, of being left without others to give form to the self.

**Somatic education direction**: Compress and deflate; build walls; teach assertion; organize boundaries using the body wall; increase internal pressure.

---

### 5. Collapsed (Withdrawn/Resigned)

**Anatomy**: The body sinks and caves. The chest collapses into exhalation, shoulders fall, the abdomen protrudes forward passively, the legs buckle. Pulsation is extinguished or minimal — there is insufficient muscular tone to sustain uprightness against gravity.

**Biography**: Develops in families with abandonment, indifference, deprivation, or chronic neglect of the child's aliveness. The person gave up trying to fill; excitation found no response and subsided.

**Psychology**: Resigned, despairing, receptive, sympathetic, resentful, obedient. Lives in fantasy. Underlying: fear of hostility, helplessness, and lack of support. Victimized, waiting to be rescued.

**Somatic education direction**: Inflate; build excitation; encourage desire and self-esteem; build support through tissue; restore pulsatory vitality.

## Combinations and Layers

Keleman is explicit that no real person is purely one type. Bodies are combinations of patterns across different layers and pouches — a person may have a rigid outer layer (muscular defense) covering an inner collapse (visceral depletion); or a swollen surface concealing dense compaction beneath. Understanding a person's shape requires identifying which combination of overbound and underbound patterns is present across the different layers.

## Connection to Other Wiki Concepts

These patterns are the somatic expression of what Myss describes in [the psychology of illness](the-psychology-of-illness.md) — the characteristic ways in which biographical experience becomes body. The rigid pattern's withheld assertion echoes the power issues Myss locates in the first and third chakras; the collapsed pattern's resignation echoes the victim consciousness she identifies as a precursor to illness; the swollen pattern's boundary dissolution echoes [woundology](woundology.md)'s wound-as-currency dynamic.

The key difference: Keleman's patterns are descriptive of body structure observable by any trained clinician; Myss's patterns are read energetically through [medical intuition](medical-intuition.md).

## Critique

Keleman's typology is clinically derived and has no independent empirical validation. Character typologies are common in body-centered and depth psychologies (Reich's character types, Lowen's bioenergetic characters, DSM personality categories) — they offer heuristic utility but do not constitute established science. The specific family-of-origin histories Keleman assigns to each type are also clinical observations, not controlled findings. Mainstream psychology would regard these as hypotheses requiring investigation, not established conclusions.
