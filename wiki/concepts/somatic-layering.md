# Somatic Layering

**Summary**: Keleman's anatomical framework describing the body as organized into three embryonic layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm), structured as tubes and pouches, each layer carrying a distinct quality of experience and emotion.

**Sources**:
- [Keleman, S. (1985). *Emotional anatomy: The structure of experience*. Center Press.](../sources/emotional-anatomy.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

[Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md) grounds his somatic psychology in developmental anatomy. The body is not a uniform mass but a layered, tubular structure that arose from a single cell through a process of differentiation into three primary layers, organized into a series of tubes and pouches. Each layer has its own quality of [pulsation](pulsation.md), its own type of motility, and its own contribution to the totality of felt experience.

## The Three Layers

### Ectoderm — Skin, Nerves, Brain
The outermost layer: the communication and boundary layer. It encompasses the skin, the nervous system, and the cortex. Its mode of experience is surface, contact, information-processing, and the establishment of individual identity. It mediates the boundary between self and world.

The ectoderm "thinks" in images, light, touch, faces, and movement — the quick world of sensation and recognition.

### Mesoderm — Muscle, Bone, Cartilage
The middle layer: the support and action layer. It provides the structural scaffolding of the body and the means for voluntary movement. The mesoderm's mode is weight, effort, resistance, and assertion. It mediates between inner impulse and outer action.

The mesoderm "thinks" in terms of stretching, compression, pressure, and rhythm.

### Endoderm — Viscera, Organs
The innermost layer: the metabolic and motility layer. It encompasses the digestive organs, lungs, heart, liver, and other viscera. Its mode is wave, tide, fluidity, peristalsis, and the deep rhythm of internal life.

The endoderm "thinks" in terms of liquidity, hunger, fullness, excitation, and the slow tides of hormonal life.

## Tubes and Pouches

Beyond the three layers, the body is organized as a tube within a tube — an axis running from mouth to anus, with the brain and cord as a secondary tube. This tube is organized into three major pouches: **head** (thought, sensation, communication), **chest** (emotion, respiration, assertion), and **abdomen/pelvis** (metabolic depth, sexuality, grounding). The pouches are separated by diaphragms — transverse valves that regulate the flow of pulsatory waves from one compartment to another.

Health involves the coordinated, wave-like pulsation of all pouches together. Illness and distress involve the fragmentation of this coordination — diaphragms stiffen, pouches lose their relationship, and the body segments into isolated zones of excessive tension or collapse.

## Layering and Biography

Each layer records biographical experience in its own way:
- Traumas that freeze the boundary layer produce surface withdrawal, skin sensitivity, and disconnection from contact.
- Traumas that stiffen the muscular layer produce bracing, rigidity, and defensive assertion.
- Traumas that collapse the organ layer produce chronic metabolic depression, hormonal dysregulation, and deep exhaustion.

This is the anatomical basis of Keleman's claim that "life makes shapes" — the body's layers are the medium in which biographical experience becomes physical structure.

## Relationship to Chakras

The parallel with [Myss's chakra system](chakra-system.md) is striking but not identical. Both frameworks organize the body vertically (head to pelvis) and assign different experiential qualities to different regions. Myss's seven chakras map developmental and spiritual themes onto body regions; Keleman's three pouches map pulsatory qualities and developmental stages onto the same regions. The frameworks are structurally analogous but methodologically incompatible: Keleman works entirely within observable anatomy; Myss works with subtle energy that is read intuitively.

## Critique

The embryological layer model (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) is established developmental biology. Keleman's extension of these layers into psychological qualities — claiming that the mesoderm "thinks" in effort and the endoderm in fluidity — is interpretive and not derived from controlled research. While clinicians in the somatic tradition find the framework useful, it has the status of a heuristic rather than a proven theory. Mainstream psychology does not recognise tissue layers as carriers of distinct psychological experience.
