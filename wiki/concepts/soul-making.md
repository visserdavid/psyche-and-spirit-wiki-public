# Soul-Making and the Imaginal

**Summary**: James Hillman's central organizing concept — psychology's task is not curing, developing, or unifying the psyche but deepening its capacity for image, meaning, and reflection ("soul"), recovered above all by *personifying*: treating moods, symptoms, and complexes as autonomous imaginal figures rather than mechanisms to be explained away.

## Soul vs. Spirit

Hillman opens [*Re-Visioning Psychology*](../sources/re-visioning-psychology.md) by borrowing and reworking a phrase from a John Keats letter: the world as "the vale of Soul-making." He distinguishes two structurally opposed orientations of psychic life:

- **Soul**: deepening, particularizing, valley-like, attached to death, imagination, and ambiguity. Soul "makes meaning possible... by means of reflection" and is expressed through fantasy, dream, symptom, and image.
- **Spirit**: ascending, abstracting, peak-like, oriented toward transcendence, unity, and clarity. Spirit is expressed through discipline, meditation, and the pursuit of the One.

Hillman insists these are not enemies but frequently confused, with modern psychotherapy (and much of Eastern-derived meditation practice imported to the West) collapsing the distinction — treating soul's ambiguity, pathology, and multiplicity as obstacles that spiritual technique should resolve. This soul/spirit polarity is the conceptual root of Hillman's insistence that psychology (a discipline of soul) should not be confused with religion or spiritual discipline (concerned with spirit), even though both concern "ultimates."

## Personifying: The Imaginal Method

The core technique of soul-making is **personifying**: approaching psychic contents — moods, symptoms, complexes, dreams — as autonomous persons or figures, rather than reducing them to mechanisms, causes, or diagnostic labels. Hillman traces this practice historically:

- The Greeks and Romans built altars to personified powers (Fame, Fortune, Modesty, Peace) without asking "are these delusions?" — the personifying itself was the mode of engaging soul
- The 17th-century "nominalist" turn (epitomized by Marin Mersenne, a contemporary and antagonist of Descartes) emptied words and images of substance, treating them as arbitrary labels rather than living presences — a precondition for the modern reduction of psyche to mechanism
- Freud rediscovered personifying unintentionally through concepts like the Censor, the Superego, and the Oedipus complex — mythological figures in clinical disguise
- [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md) rediscovered it more deliberately: naming the Shadow, the Anima/Animus, the Self, and treating complexes as possessing genuine autonomy and intentionality rather than being mere associative clusters

For Hillman, this is not a psychotherapeutic technique layered onto an otherwise mechanistic psyche — it is the discovery that the psyche *already* speaks in persons, images, and myths, and that depersonifying (treating a dream figure as "just" a repressed wish, a symptom as "just" a chemical imbalance) is itself a historically contingent, imaginatively impoverished choice, not a neutral scientific description.

## The Imaginal Realm

Hillman treats fantasy-images as the basic unit of psychic reality — not raw material to be interpreted into concepts, but the finished products through which the psyche knows anything at all. "Man is the imagemaker; his substance consists of images; his being is imagination." This grounds his rejection of purely linguistic or purely biological accounts of mind: psychology's proper method is to follow the image (amplification), not decode it into a concept (interpretation).

## Relationship to Jungian Active Imagination

Hillman explicitly credits Jung (from 1916 onward) with developing methods of engaging the imaginal through dialogue with psychic figures, but criticizes later disciples and Gestalt-derived techniques for domesticating the method — using imaginal engagement in the service of ego-strengthening rather than letting figures retain their own autonomy. Where Jung's [individuation](individuation.md) treats confrontation with the [shadow](shadow.md), [anima/animus](anima-animus.md), and eventually [the Self](the-self.md) as stages toward integration, Hillman treats personifying as an end in itself: the psyche's multiplicity does not need to be resolved into unity to be soul-making, only witnessed and engaged. See [Polytheistic Psychology](polytheistic-psychology.md) for the full argument against integration-toward-wholeness as psychology's goal.

## Relationship to Myss

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s practice of [symbolic sight](symbolic-sight.md) — reading a life situation for its archetypal pattern rather than only its literal particulars — parallels Hillman's personifying: both refuse to take an event at face value and instead read it as the expression of a mythic or archetypal figure. The frameworks diverge in purpose: Myss's symbolic sight is diagnostic and oriented toward healing action (a Sacred Contract to be fulfilled, a lesson to be learned); Hillman's personifying resists being harnessed to any goal, including healing — the figure is to be witnessed and engaged on its own terms, not resolved.

## Critique

**Unfalsifiability**: Hillman's insistence that psychic contents be engaged as autonomous persons rather than explained causally removes the possibility of empirical testing. There is no way to confirm or disconfirm that a symptom "is" a mythic figure speaking rather than, say, a treatable anxiety disorder with a known neurochemical profile. Mainstream clinical psychology and psychiatry regard this as a fatal weakness for anything claiming clinical relevance, however rich it may be as a hermeneutic or literary practice.

**The therapy question**: Hillman's own position is that soul-making is not therapy in the symptom-relief sense, and he resists the idea that personifying "cures" anything — which sidesteps the empirical objection but also limits the framework's applicability to patients seeking symptom relief rather than meaning.

**Strongest defense**: Hillman would respond that psychology's insistence on causal, mechanistic explanation is itself an unexamined metaphysical commitment (to a "spirit"-style unity and clarity) rather than a neutral scientific starting point — and that soul-making offers a phenomenologically richer account of lived psychic experience (dreams, symptoms, complexes genuinely do present themselves as persons with intentions, independent of whether this is "objectively" true) than reductive causal models allow.
