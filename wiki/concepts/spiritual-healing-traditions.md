# Spiritual Healing Traditions

**Summary**: A cross-cultural survey of the role of prayer, ritual, healing sites, and spiritually gifted healers in human history — from ancient Greece and Rome through the world religions to the twentieth century revival of spiritual healing.

**Sources**:
- [Shealy, C. N. (1999). *Sacred healing: The curing power of energy and spirituality*. Element Books.](../sources/sacred-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

[Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) opens *Sacred Healing* with a comprehensive historical survey demonstrating that healing and religion have been inseparable across every human culture and era. The separation of medicine from spirituality is, in this view, a brief two-hundred-year anomaly produced by the Cartesian revolution and the subsequent dominance of pharmaceutical medicine.

The core historical thesis: every religion that survived history has included healing as a central function, because the population will not long follow a tradition that cannot heal.

## Ancient World

**Pre-literate traditions** (100,000+ years ago): Trepanning — boring holes in the skull to release spirits — is among the oldest documented medical procedures, suggesting that the spirit/disease nexus has been fundamental since the beginning of recorded human activity.

**Greece**: The Hippocratic tradition (460–370 BC) combined physical diagnosis with dietary and lifestyle medicine. But alongside this rational tradition, Greek religion maintained healing temples (Asclepeia) with sacred springs at sites including Thermopylae and Aedepsos. Healing was attributed to the gods — particularly Asclepius, whose staff (entwined with a serpent) remains the symbol of medicine.

**Rome**: Romans maintained sulfur springs and sacred healing sites (Aquae Abulae, Tibus). The early Christian overlay added miraculous healing as proof of divine grace; by 395 AD, Church hospitals used icons, relics, and the intercession of saints alongside prayer.

## Healing Via Sacred Water and Places

A recurring theme across traditions is the healing power of specific water and specific places:
- Springs at Lourdes, France (established 1858 following Marian apparitions) remain the world's most visited healing site.
- Sacred rivers across cultures — Ganges and Jumna in India, the Nile in Egypt, the Jordan in Israel, the Tiber in Rome — are associated with purification and healing.
- Mountain peaks, cemeteries, and sites associated with saints have served as healing shrines.
- The bodies of incorruptible saints (e.g., Saint Rita of Cascia, 1381–1456) were venerated as sources of healing for centuries after death.

## World Religions and Healing

Shealy presents quotations from ten world religions on prayer, immortality, peace, love, health, and the Golden Rule — demonstrating that these teachings are not merely analogous but nearly identical in their essentials. This convergence underpins his thesis that **spirituality transcends religion**: the core principles — love, forgiveness, compassion, prayer, attunement to the Divine — produce health regardless of the specific theological system in which they are practised.

The traditions represented: Christianity, Confucianism, Buddhism, Hinduism, Islam, Sikhism, Judaism, Jainism, Zoroastrianism, Bahá'í Faith, and Shinto.

## Mind Cure and New Thought Movements

William James's *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) identified "mind cure" as a significant healing movement emerging from Christianity, transcendentalism, spiritualism, and Hinduism. James argued that its success — in treating what he called "invalidism, nervous disorders, and general spiritual malaise" — rested on:
- The power of faith and expectation.
- The adoption of positive over negative emotional states.
- Alignment with what he called "the divine."

The mind cure tradition includes: Mary Baker Eddy's Christian Science (body of sin as illusion); the Unity movement (Myrtle and Charles Fillmore); Religious Science (Ernest Holmes); and New Thought broadly. These movements share the conviction that consciousness — properly aligned — can reverse physical illness.

## Notable Historical Healers

Shealy surveys healers across the twentieth century who attracted documentation:

- **Harry Edwards** (England): received 9,000 letters per week from patients; claimed 80% improvement rate, 30% cured, 10% "completely cured" in his assessment. Emphasised that healing energy comes from a divine source — not from the healer.
- **Olga Worrall** (USA): conducted healing services for thirty-five years at Mount Washington United Methodist Church, Baltimore. Shealy collected verified records of nine patients whose healing he considers documented.
- **Katherine Kuhlman**: large-scale healing services; hundreds experienced dramatic healings in her presence.
- **Edgar Cayce**: 15,000 trance readings, two-thirds health-related. See [Edgar Cayce](../thinkers/edgar-cayce.md).
- **Philippine psychic surgeons**: Shealy personally witnessed Tony Santiago and Tony Agpaou in 1976. He could not explain what he observed — apparent extraction of blood and tissue from patients without surgical instruments — and was unable to detect fraud despite close observation, including bathroom and drawer checks.

## The Revival of Spiritual Healing

Shealy cites surveys from the 1990s showing:
- 82% of Americans believe prayer can heal (1996 Time survey).
- 77% believe God intervenes to cure illness.
- 83% of women and 73% of men believe faith helps healing (1997 USA Weekend).
- 56% report that faith has personally helped them heal.

He argues this revival reflects a failure of managed care medicine to meet human needs for meaning, healing, and relationship — not merely the atavistic persistence of pre-scientific belief.

## Critique

The historical survey Shealy provides is broadly accurate as cultural history but selective in its framing:
- The fact that every culture has practised spiritual healing does not demonstrate that spiritual healing works — the argument from universality is not an argument for efficacy.
- The history of healing sites like Lourdes, while including reported miracles, also includes millions of pilgrims who were not cured. The Lourdes Medical Bureau has authenticated approximately 70 cures as "extraordinary" out of hundreds of millions of visits — a vanishingly small success rate by any clinical standard.
- The incorruptibility of saints' bodies, while attested by credible historical witnesses, has natural explanations (environmental conditions, embalming, partial preservation) that do not require supernatural agency.
- The Philippine psychic surgeons have been investigated by stage magicians and skeptics who have documented sleight-of-hand techniques producing similar apparent manifestations.

Nevertheless, the core argument stands: the systematic exclusion of spirituality, meaning, and the healer-patient relationship from modern medicine is a historical aberration with genuine costs to patient wellbeing.
