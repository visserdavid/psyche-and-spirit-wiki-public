# Spiritual Optimism

**Summary**: Borysenko's term for the worldview that treats life as blessing, growth, and the gradual return to love — contrasted with spiritual pessimism (the guilt-centred belief that life is punishment for sin); grounded in the Perennial Philosophy, the Prodigal Son parable, Gnostic wisdom, and Matthew Fox's Via Positiva; with a pointed critique of the New Age "you create your reality" doctrine as a warped form of the same pessimism it claims to transcend.

## The Core Distinction

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) identifies two fundamental orientations toward life's difficulties — illness, loss, failure, suffering — that she calls **spiritual pessimism** and **spiritual optimism**:

**Spiritual pessimism**: Life's difficulties are punishments. Illness is evidence of sin, unworthiness, or insufficient faith. The self is fundamentally damaged (Augustine's *original sin*). God is primarily a judge. This orientation generates the syndrome of [guilt and soul sickness](guilt-and-soul-sickness.md): a self that lives under permanent sentence.

**Spiritual optimism**: Life's difficulties are occasions of growth and return. Illness is a teacher, not a punishment. The self is fundamentally worthy — a Prodigal Son or Daughter whose wandering is temporary. God is primarily a loving parent. This orientation generates [grace](forgiveness.md) and the possibility of genuine healing.

The distinction matters medically because it determines whether a person responds to crisis with meaning-seeking (which Martin Seligman links to health) or with helplessness (which Seligman demonstrates leads to depression, immune suppression, and death). John and Mark — two AIDS patients of Borysenko's — illustrate the clinical stakes: John died two months after diagnosis in the grip of pessimism; Mark lived two more years embracing the disease as a passage and a teacher.

## Augustine and the Roots of Spiritual Pessimism

Elaine Pagels (*Adam, Eve, and the Serpent*, 1988) traces the dominant pessimistic strand of Western Christianity to St. Augustine (354–430 CE). Against the earlier Christian view that Adam and Eve were blessed with freedom and dignity — and that their story described humanity's moral agency — Augustine reinterpreted Genesis to mean that the Fall was total: all of humanity was enslaved to sin through Adam's act. The body's unruliness (Augustine's own battle with sexual impulse) became, in his reading, the proof of humanity's damaged nature.

Augustine chose guilt over helplessness: the doctrine of original sin provided an *explanation* for suffering (humanity sinned and deserved it) that at least located the cause within human agency, rather than confronting the randomness and injustice of a world in which innocent people suffer.

Pagels notes that this logic persists wherever guilt is preferred to helplessness: it is easier to believe one *deserved* misfortune than to accept that misfortune has no cosmic accountant. But the cost is the identity belief — "I am fundamentally damaged" — that feeds soul sickness.

## Three Ways of Explaining What Happens

Borysenko (drawing on Martin Seligman's attributional research on pessimism) identifies three explanatory frameworks people use when difficulties arise:

1. **Self-blame**: "This is my fault" — which in its healthy form motivates learning but in its pessimistic form becomes the guilt-spiral "proof of my permanent inadequacy"
2. **Other-blame**: projecting responsibility onto people, forces, or fate — which avoids insight but also avoids the guilt-spiral
3. **Chance/God-as-judge**: attributing events to random forces or divine punishment — neither offers the agency of self-awareness nor the comfort of genuine cosmic benevolence

Spiritual optimism offers a fourth framework: **meaningful participation in a learning process**. Events are understood as having purpose without implying punishment. Viktor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning* — written from Nazi concentration camps — documents that this fourth orientation is available even in extreme suffering and is the one that most reliably supports survival and dignity.

## Matthew Fox and the Via Positiva

Matthew Fox (Catholic priest and theologian, author of *Original Blessing*, 1983) provides Borysenko's primary alternative theological framework. Fox argues that Christianity has been dominated for six centuries by a "fall-redemption" theology (Augustine's legacy) that sees life as defined by sin and the need for redemption. He contrasts this with what he calls the **Via Positiva** — the affirmative path that celebrates creation as gift, life as blessing, and the body as sacred:

- Creation-centred spirituality: the universe is an expression of God's love, not a fallen realm of punishment
- **Original Blessing** (in contrast to original sin): the primary human condition is gift and dignity, not damage and guilt
- B. F. Skinner's behavioural research: punishment suppresses behaviour but does not produce intrinsic motivation; love and reward create genuine change. If punishment is an ineffective way to change human behaviour, Fox asks, why would an intelligent God design the universe around it?
- Mystical tradition within Christianity: Hildegard of Bingen, Meister Eckhart, Julian of Norwich, Francis of Assisi all articulate creation-centred spirituality that was marginalised by the Augustinian mainstream

Fox eventually faced a year of church-imposed silence for his views — which Borysenko reads as a family's attempt to silence the uncomfortable child (the parallels to the shadow bag are explicit).

## The Prodigal Son as Model of Spiritual Optimism

The parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15) is Borysenko's central narrative illustration of spiritual optimism. The story:
- Son demands inheritance early, squanders it, falls into destitution
- "Coming to himself" — the moment of self-awareness — he decides to return home and confess
- The father, seeing him "while yet a great way off," *runs* to meet him, not to judge but to celebrate
- The feast, the ring, the robe — extravagant reception without preconditions
- The elder son's resentment is a teaching about the dangers of the pessimistic orientation: having never strayed, he has also never experienced the fullness of the return

Borysenko's reading: the father's response is the model of God's love — not the judging God of pessimism but the celebrating God of optimism. The son does not return to perfect himself but to come home. The return itself is the healing. The Father's love does not wait for moral qualification.

This is spiritual optimism in action: mistakes are occasions for growth and reunion, not evidence of damnation.

## The Perennial Philosophy

Aldous Huxley (*The Perennial Philosophy*, 1944) identified a common thread across twenty-five centuries and all major world traditions: the conviction that the purpose of human life is to realise one's enduring connection to Divine consciousness — and that this realisation is the source of peace, wisdom, and compassion. The Perennial Philosophy appears in:

- Hindu Vedanta
- Buddhist Mahayana teaching
- Christian mysticism (Meister Eckhart, John of the Cross)
- Islamic Sufism
- Jewish Kabbalah
- Chinese Taoism

Borysenko reads the Perennial Philosophy as the consistent backbone of spiritual optimism: every tradition, when freed from its fear-based institutional encrustations, arrives at the same recognition that life is a journey toward love, not a punishment away from it.

## Gnostic Wisdom and the Kingdom Within

Elaine Pagels (*The Gnostic Gospels*, 1979) describes the Nag Hammadi manuscripts — 52 texts discovered in an Egyptian desert jar in 1945, buried around 400 CE, written in the first centuries of the Common Era alongside the canonical New Testament.

The Gospel of Thomas (one of these manuscripts) quotes Jesus teaching:
- "The Kingdom of God is not a place — it is a state of consciousness existing within you"
- When asked how to enter the Kingdom, Jesus does not prescribe fasting or prayer but says: "Do not tell lies, and do not do what you hate" — honouring one's own instincts and authentic experience
- "Disrobe and tread upon your garments" — remove the masks and trample the persona; let the Natural Child emerge

This is gnostic (*gnosis* = direct knowing) spirituality: the Kingdom is accessed not through doctrine or institutional membership but through self-knowledge and authentic experience. Borysenko reads this as the ancient form of the spiritual optimism she practises clinically: healing begins when the person connects to their own inner authority — the Self — rather than outsourcing it to a judge-god.

## New Age Guilt: A Critique

The "you create your reality" doctrine of New Age thought — popularised by Louise Hay (*You Can Heal Your Life*), appropriated from Shirley MacLaine's version of Eastern karma philosophy, and later critiqued by Ken Wilber — appears to offer freedom from pessimism but actually reinstates it in new clothing:

- Original sin (Augustine): "You are ill because humanity sinned"
- New Age guilt (Louise Hay et al.): "You are ill because you created it with your thoughts"

The difference in framing conceals the identical move: illness as evidence of personal failure. Wilber:
> "New Agers managed to regard diseases as stemming from some sort of origin — and then went one step further and interpreted this to mean they are giving themselves their disease as 'lessons'... 'What are you trying to teach yourself with this cancer?'"

Borysenko agrees: illness is neither divine punishment nor mental creation in the simple sense. Genetics, environment, chance, and the complex web of contributing factors — including (but not exclusively) emotional patterns — all participate. The spiritual optimism she advocates acknowledges that one's responses to illness can shift its course; it does not claim that one's negative thinking *caused* it.

The karmic misreading: karma is the law of cause and effect in Eastern thought — actions have consequences. It is not a value judgment and does not imply cosmic punishment. To say "your cancer is your karma" as a blame statement is a misreading of the teaching.

## Fire in the Soul: Extending the Framework (Borysenko, 1993)

In [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md), spiritual optimism is no longer merely a therapeutic orientation but the central premise of the book's cosmology — tested against the hardest cases: the death of a friend's seventeen-year-old son in a car accident, Holocaust survival, AIDS in both its fearful (Jay) and faithful (Leslie) forms.

The key extensions:

**Einstein's question as cornerstone**: Borysenko identifies Albert Einstein's statement — that the most important question a person can ask is "Is the universe a friendly place?" — as the foundational variable from which all other beliefs cascade. Leslie's faith-based equanimity and Jay's fear-based punishment theology are two different answers to Einstein's question, and those answers determine the clinical trajectory.

**Frankl's demonstration**: [Viktor Frankl](../thinkers/viktor-frankl.md)'s survival of four Nazi concentration camps through the cultivation of meaning provides the most extreme possible test of spiritual optimism. Frankl did not survive because he was in a friendly environment; he survived because he maintained a framework of meaning that converted unavoidable suffering into a task. This is spiritual optimism not as a comfort but as a survival variable. Frankl's own term for this stance — **tragic optimism**, developed in his 1984 postscript to [*Man's Search for Meaning*](../sources/mans-search-for-meaning.md) — is a more philosophically minimal cousin of Borysenko's spiritual optimism: it claims only that meaning remains available regardless of whether the universe is friendly, without Borysenko's further theological claim (Prodigal Son, Perennial Philosophy, Gnostic wisdom) that the universe is fundamentally loving. See [Logotherapy](logotherapy.md) for the full framework and this parallel spelled out.

**Job's story (Mitchell translation)**: Stephen Mitchell's translation of the Book of Job recovers a reading suppressed by the Groveling-Job tradition: Job does not capitulate to divine power; he reaches a direct, shattering encounter with divine reality that dissolves his prior framework for suffering. Borysenko reads this as the book's archetype for spiritual optimism: not "God punishes the sinner" (the friends' position) but "suffering burns away the explanations and reveals the face of love."

**[Stages of faith](stages-of-faith.md) (Fowler)**: The pessimism/optimism distinction maps onto Fowler's developmental stages. Spiritual pessimism (guilt-punishment theology) is primarily a Stage 2 cosmology; spiritual optimism (Prodigal Son; Via Positiva; Perennial Philosophy) is Stage 5. Development through the stages, catalysed by crisis, is the process by which a person moves from one orientation to the other — which is why crisis, properly held, is an opportunity for spiritual deepening rather than destruction.

**[Crisis as initiation](crisis-as-initiation.md)**: The initiation framework — Victor Turner's liminality, Campbell's night sea journey, Frankl's logotherapy — is spiritual optimism's anthropological and psychological substrate. If crisis is initiation, then the universe that permits crisis is not hostile but pedagogical. Spiritual optimism and the crisis-as-initiation framework are mutually supporting: each provides the context that makes the other coherent.

## Connections to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Woundology](woundology.md)** (Myss): spiritual pessimism is the theological backdrop against which woundology thrives — when the self is fundamentally unworthy and life is punishment, wound identity becomes a refuge. Spiritual optimism offers the alternative: the self's worth is not in question; wounds are teachers, not verdicts.

**[Healing vs. Curing](healing-vs-curing.md)**: spiritual optimism is the orientation that makes healing (wholeness, peace, return to love) possible even when curing (disease elimination) is not available. Mark (AIDS, two-year survivor) achieved healing without curing; John (AIDS, two-month survivor) lacked the spiritual optimism that might have opened that possibility.

**[Five Myths of Healing](five-myths-of-healing.md)** (Myss): Myss's analysis of the cultural myths that block healing (including the myth that disease is punishment for spiritual inadequacy) converges with Borysenko's spiritual pessimism critique. Both identify a culturally transmitted guilt-framework as a primary obstacle to healing.

**[Grace and Charism](grace-and-charism.md)**: Grace (in Borysenko's framework) is what flows through the cracks opened by spiritual optimism — the divine energy that tips the balance when personal effort reaches its limit. Spiritual pessimism blocks grace by framing God as judge rather than generous parent.

## Critique

**Theological**: Borysenko's reading of Augustine and the Christian tradition is theologian-level but selective. Many Christian theologians would argue that the pessimism/optimism binary oversimplifies a complex tradition that contains both fall-redemption and creation-centred strands throughout its history. Augustine himself wrote extensively about grace and divine love.

**Scientific**: The claim that spiritual optimism causally produces better health outcomes is plausible and partially supported (Seligman's research on optimism and health; Frankl's observations on meaning and survival) but not demonstrated in controlled studies on spiritual orientation per se.

**Privilege question**: Hillman's parallel concern (in [*The Soul's Code*](../sources/souls-code.md)) applies here: the capacity to read suffering as purposeful teacher rather than punishment may be more available to people with social, economic, and psychological resources. The severely traumatised, the structurally oppressed, and the neurobiologically depressed may find the spiritual optimism framework inaccessible without support.
