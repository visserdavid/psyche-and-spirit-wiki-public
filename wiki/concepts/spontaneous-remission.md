# Spontaneous Remission

**Summary**: The documented phenomenon of unexpected or unexplained recovery from serious illness — including certified miracle cures, the Krebiozen paradigm case, and the spectrum from rational to paradoxical healing described by Larry Dossey — raising questions about the relationship between consciousness, meaning, and biological self-repair.

## Overview

Spontaneous remission refers to the unexpected improvement or complete resolution of a serious illness without conventional medical explanation. The phenomenon ranges from well-documented cases in the mainstream literature (remissions in certain cancers) to certified miraculous cures (Lourdes) to the paradigmatic placebo-reversal cases that Dossey uses to explore the role of meaning in healing.

Dossey argues in *Meaning & Medicine* (1991) that spontaneous remissions are not anomalies to be explained away but windows into a domain of healing that medicine's mechanistic framework cannot account for — and that approaching this domain requires a different stance than the "doing" orientation of conventional treatment.

---

## The Krebiozen Paradigm Case

The most frequently cited case in the spontaneous remission literature is the Krebiozen case, which Dossey presents as a paradigm of meaning-as-medicine:

A cancer patient whose tumours were "as large as oranges" received injections of Krebiozen, a drug being studied as a cancer treatment. His tumours "melted like snowballs on a stove." He was discharged from hospital.

Several weeks later, the patient read newspaper reports suggesting the studies were showing Krebiozen to be ineffective. His tumours returned. His physician, suspecting the mechanism, administered injections of plain water while telling the patient he was receiving a superior new form of Krebiozen. The tumours disappeared again. Then the patient read a final American Medical Association pronouncement declaring Krebiozen worthless. He died within days.

The case illustrates that:
1. The active biological agent was not the chemical composition of what was injected but the **meaning** the patient assigned to the injection.
2. The reversal of meaning reversed the physiological process — in both directions.
3. This is not a "mere" placebo effect but a demonstration that meaning is a primary biological variable.

---

## The Lourdes Miracle: Delizia Cirolli

Dossey presents the case of Delizia Cirolli as an example of a certified miraculous cure. Delizia, a girl from the slopes of Mount Etna, was diagnosed in 1976 with a bone tumour of the knee (first diagnosed as neuroblastoma, later re-examined as Ewing sarcoma — itself a tumour type for which spontaneous remission at age five had never been reported). She received no conventional treatment. Her mother took her to Lourdes in August 1976; her condition subsequently deteriorated. By Christmas she recovered, got out of bed, and walked. X-rays documented complete bone repair; biopsies showed no tumour.

Her case was reviewed and certified as a miraculous cure by the International Medical Committee of Lourdes, which employs medically qualified examiners. Dossey does not interpret this as evidence for divine intervention; he presents it as documented evidence that healing events occur outside the framework of what the rational/causal model of medicine can explain.

## Other Cases

- **Robert**: Told he had three months to live from widespread cancer; decided to live differently — married, became an Episcopal priest — and within a month X-rays showed no trace of cancer. His cardiologist attributed it to his treatment, his psychologist to therapy, his radiologist to cobalt treatment. Robert himself: "I decided I wasn't going to curl up my toes and die."

- **Prayer case**: A patient with documented lung cancer (biopsy confirmed) declined surgery. A year later, chest X-rays showed "no evidence of existing carcinoma." No intervening treatment; the patient's minister and congregation had prayed for him.

- **The 50-year-old teacher**: Developed throat cancer. In psychotherapy the night before surgery, he broke down and recounted witnessing a planned murder. Four days later the tumour was gone. Dossey cites Charles Weinstock (New York Psychosomatic Cancer Study Group): he consistently found a change in the patient's life situation preceding cancer remission.

## The Spectrum from Rational to Paradoxical Healing

Dossey arranges healing approaches on a spectrum:

**Rational healing** (left end of the spectrum): surgery, medication, radiation, biofeedback, psychological counselling. These have mechanisms that science can specify and results that follow predictably from the intervention. "Doing" therapies.

**Paradoxical healing** (right end): prayer, miracle cures, spontaneous remission, placebo effects that reverse lethal conditions. These produce results without explicable mechanism. Results that science cannot currently predict or control. "Not-doing" or "being" therapies.

Key observations about the paradoxical end:
1. The more one tries to make a miracle happen, the less likely it becomes — the demand for control subverts the conditions that allow it.
2. Most documented spontaneous remissions involve a shift in the patient's stance: from fighting to accepting, from controlling to surrendering, from grasping to releasing. De Vaca (16th-century explorer whose healing powers emerged after the spiritual crisis of shipwreck) and Keizo Funatsu (buried in an Antarctic blizzard, who experienced a womb-like peace and surrendered his survival to the universe) are Dossey's mythological-level examples.
3. Lourdes functions institutionally as a site of collective emptiness — not because the water is chemically special but because the journey, the ceremony, the despair, and the release create conditions in which the self's habitual control relaxes enough for something else to enter.

## The "Superintelligence" Within

Lewis Thomas (Sloan-Kettering Cancer Centre) introduced the concept of a "superintelligence" within the organism that the Fishskin Boy case (see [Meaning & Medicine](../sources/meaning-and-medicine.md)) makes visible. Mason's hypnotic suggestion activated a healing capacity the organism possessed but had never mobilised: the ability to replace the thickened, infected skin of congenital ichthyosis with normal skin.

Thomas wrote: the body has "a kind of superintelligence that conceals itself until there is a crisis, that emerges only in emergencies. We have the ability to self-heal, but to activate that ability, we have to make contact with the forces within."

This concept parallels:
- The Inner Self Helper of MPD research (Allison, Damgaard): a figure within patients who has no fear or guilt and seems to possess full knowledge of the patient's history and what they need to heal
- Aldous Huxley's "Not-I": the healing intelligence that operates most effectively when the ego's efforts are suspended
- The [core star](core-star.md) in Brennan's model: the divine essence at the centre of the human energy field that is always healthy and can be connected to through healing

## The Role of Saints and Mystics

Dossey observes a counterintuitive pattern: the great saints and mystics — figures who by any account were deeply spiritually developed — do not appear to have been protected from serious illness. Jiddu Krishnamurti died of pancreatic cancer. Ramana Maharshi died of stomach cancer. Suzuki Roshi (who helped bring Zen to America) died of stomach cancer. Saint Bernadette (who reported the visions at Lourdes) died of bone cancer at 35.

Dossey's interpretation: these figures inhabited a relationship to illness and death that was different from ordinary patients, not because they were better at healing but because they had accessed the paradoxical domain in which illness and death cease to be the primary categories of evaluation. Their relationship to the meaning of their bodies was different — not that they rejected illness but that they found their illness did not define or diminish them.

This is not a therapeutic strategy but a description of what the paradoxical end of the healing spectrum looks like when fully inhabited. It does not produce immunity from illness; it produces a freedom that does not depend on being well.

## Critique

**The evidence problem**: Most spontaneous remission cases are individual case reports or case series, not controlled studies. Selection bias is severe: physicians report remissions they find remarkable; they do not systematically record cases where expected remissions did not occur. The Institute of Noetic Sciences' database of spontaneous remissions (Brendan O'Regan, 1987) was an attempt to systematise this; it showed the phenomenon occurs in virtually every known cancer type but cannot quantify its frequency or identify reliable predictors.

**The Krebiozen case**: Widely cited but not published in a peer-reviewed journal and not independently verified. Its rhetorical power in the meaning-and-health literature exceeds its evidential weight.

**The Lourdes cures**: The International Medical Committee of Lourdes has certified 70 cures as miraculous since Bernadette's time (out of thousands of submitted cases). The committee's criteria are stringent: the cure must be instantaneous, complete, durable, and without medical explanation at the time of certification. These cases are genuinely unexplained; whether they constitute evidence for divine intervention or for an unusual activation of the organism's healing capacity (Thomas's superintelligence) is a matter of interpretation.

**The meaning-as-cure claim**: Even if meaning is a real biological variable (which the evidence strongly supports), it does not follow that meaning change is sufficient to cure established illness. Spontaneous remissions occur; they appear to be associated with profound life changes; but they cannot be reliably produced by any psychological or spiritual intervention. The paradoxical point — that trying to produce them may prevent them — means this domain is structurally resistant to study.

**Note**: Mainstream medicine acknowledges that spontaneous remissions occur in certain cancers (particularly certain melanomas and renal cell carcinomas) through known immunological mechanisms (immune surveillance). For most cancers and serious illnesses, spontaneous remission is rare and not currently predictable. The role of psychological, spiritual, or meaning-based factors in these events remains scientifically open.

## Siegel's Contribution: Self-Induced Healing

[Bernie Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md) prefers the term "self-induced healing" to "spontaneous remission" — emphasising the patient's active role in unexpected recoveries. [(Siegel, 1986)](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

**The Simonton data**: O. Carl Simonton's programme at the Cancer Counseling and Research Center (Fort Worth) treated 159 patients none of whom were expected to live one year. Of these: 19% saw their cancers regress; 22% stabilised; the average survival time of those who died was double the statistical prediction for their disease type and stage. These results, achieved without any new pharmacological intervention, represent what Siegel calls self-induced healing — outcomes that the patient's own altered physiology and psychology produced.

**Pelletier's five characteristics**: Kenneth Pelletier studied people who recovered against the odds and identified five shared characteristics:
1. A profound change in attitude, typically involving prayer, meditation, or contemplative practice
2. A profound restructuring of relationships — people now placed on an equal footing rather than given power over the patient
3. A change in diet — food taken seriously as nourishment rather than habit
4. A sense of personal responsibility for all aspects of life
5. A feeling that the recovery was not a gift or remission but something they had participated in creating

These characteristics constitute a profile of psychological self-transformation that coincides with, and Siegel believes mediates, the physiological change.

**Achterberg and Lawlis imagery data**: In a study of 200 cancer patients, [Jeanne Achterberg](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md) and G. Frank Lawlis predicted two-month outcomes (remission vs. deterioration vs. death) at 93% accuracy — based entirely on analysis of patients' drawings and mental imagery. The Image CA tool achieved 100% accuracy in identifying patients who would die within two months. (Siegel cites 95% accuracy when referencing this study; Achterberg's primary source reports 93% for remission prediction and 100% for death prediction.) The predictive variable was not a blood marker or clinical finding but the vividness, activity, and symbolic content of the patient's internal representation of the illness, treatment, and immune system. This finding suggests that the patient's mental model of the illness is not merely a belief but a physiological fact. See [Mental Imagery and Healing](mental-imagery-healing.md).

**Self-cure cases**: Siegel's clinical record includes multiple cases of patients who substantially exceeded their prognoses or achieved apparent remission after making profound life changes — leaving destructive relationships, changing careers, beginning to express suppressed emotions, finding or restoring a sense of meaning. He does not claim these as proofs but as data requiring explanation: a medical system that explains spontaneous remissions as "errors" or "luck" is, he argues, foreclosing the most clinically important questions.

The connecting principle between Siegel's self-induced healing and Dossey's spontaneous remission framework is the same: meaning — the patient's relationship to their life, their illness, and their future — is a primary biological variable, not a secondary psychological epiphenomenon.

## Joy's Contribution: Pancreatitis Recovery and Command Therapy Cases

[W. Brugh Joy](../thinkers/w-brugh-joy.md) in [*Joy's Way*](../sources/joys-way.md) (1979) contributes two distinct types of evidence to the spontaneous remission literature:

**Joy's own pancreatitis**: Joy had suffered repeated bouts of pancreatitis during his medical career. After leaving medicine in January 1974 following an inner directive received during meditation, the condition resolved completely and permanently within six weeks. He had not undergone any new medical treatment. Joy interprets this as an instance of the body signalling a needed biographical change and healing once that change was made — the paradigm case of his [Transformational Process](transformational-process.md) framework and the most vivid illustration in this wiki of [biography is biology](biography-is-biology.md) applied to remission.

**Command Therapy cancer cases**: In his clinical practice (1973–74) Joy developed and taught a six-step visualisation protocol he called Command Therapy, used with cancer patients. He reports observing cases in which patients who practised the protocol three times daily experienced regressions and unexpected improvements in their conditions. He acknowledges the absence of controlled comparisons but cites Simonton's data as providing a comparable evidential base. What distinguishes Joy's cases from Simonton's is the mechanism proposed: where Simonton's model is psychoneuroimmunological (mental imagery modulates immune function), Joy's invokes the energy field and the soul's signal-capacity — the body heals when the soul's message is received. See [Command Therapy](command-therapy.md).

**Joy's convergence with Dossey**: Joy is not acquainted with Dossey's framework at the time of writing, but his clinical observations parallel Dossey's spectrum from rational to paradoxical healing. Joy's Transformational Process belongs firmly at the paradoxical end: healing arises not from treatment but from transformation, and cannot be produced by deliberate "doing."

## Achterberg's Contribution: Attitude Change as the Common Factor

[Jeanne Achterberg](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md) in [*Imagery in Healing*](../sources/imagery-in-healing.md) (1985) provides an independent survey of the spontaneous remission literature — drawing on the bibliography of 400+ documented cases compiled by Eric Peper and Kenneth Pelletier — that converges with the Siegel and Dossey accounts from a different angle.

**The single most consistent factor across 400+ cases**: a **change in attitude** — described variably as:
- A renewed sense of hope or will to live
- Entry into prayer, meditation, or contemplative practice
- A renewed sense of purpose or meaning
- An acceptance of death that paradoxically liberated the patient from the fear of it

Achterberg notes that "attitude change" is reported by case documenters who do not share a common theoretical framework — physicians, anthropologists, clergy, and patients themselves — which strengthens the observation. It is not a theoretical imposition but a field finding.

**The immune mechanism**: Achterberg proposes that attitude change alters the neuroendocrine-immune communication that either suppresses or enables the immune system's surveillance capacity. The endorphin pathway is specifically invoked: positive emotional states (hope, purpose, the "will to live") release endorphins and enkephalins, which enhance T cell proliferation and NK cell activity (see [Psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md)). Conditioned immune suppression (Ader and Cohen) operates by the same mechanism in reverse: hopelessness and resignation — learned associations — suppress immunity as reliably as a pharmacological immunosuppressant.

**The Image CA finding**: Achterberg's Image CA research (predicting two-month cancer outcomes at 93% accuracy from patients' mental imagery) is the laboratory-research expression of the same observation. The patient who draws their immune cells as large, numerous, and powerful — who has an internal representation of their body winning the fight — is expressing the attitude that the spontaneous remission literature identifies as the precursor to unexpected recovery. The imagery score is a window into the patient's implicit attitude, not just their aesthetic preferences.

**Convergence across sources**: The attitude-change factor that Achterberg identifies from the remission literature, the "five characteristics" Pelletier identified from survivor interviews ([Siegel](../thinkers/bernie-siegel.md)), the spectrum from "fighting spirit" to "helplessness/hopelessness" that Pettingale et al. found predictive of 10-year breast cancer survival, and [LeShan](../thinkers/lawrence-leshan.md)'s claim that cancer patients who reconnect with their "life meaning melody" show the most dramatic responses — all describe the same phenomenon from different research traditions.

## Weil's Contribution: S.R.'s Hodgkin's Disease and Wart Cures

[Andrew Weil](../thinkers/andrew-weil.md) in *Health and Healing* (1983) contributes two distinct lines of evidence to the spontaneous remission literature.

### S.R.'s Hodgkin's Disease: A Documented Miracle Cure

The most fully documented case in Weil's book involves a 26-year-old woman (identified as "S.R.") diagnosed in 1967 with Stage III Hodgkin's disease. She declined standard radiation, became pregnant, and underwent limited radiation that her physicians acknowledged was insufficient to constitute treatment. She deteriorated to 92 pounds and, by her own account, lost the will to survive.

Her physician arranged a guided LSD session — not as an experimental treatment for the cancer but to help her confront death. During this experience, S.R. had a sudden flash of insight: she recognised that she was choosing to die. She decided to live. In the weeks following, she:

- Left her husband and moved back with her family
- Changed her academic programme
- Began working with Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's dying patients
- Undertook Gestalt therapy, encounter group work, and found a Zen teacher
- Changed her diet (eliminated sugar, introduced whole foods)
- Began daily meditation and visualisation

She rallied, gave birth to a healthy child, and had no evidence of disease by 1973 — a six-year cancer-free period at time of publication. Weil calls it "a documented miracle cure."

**The mechanism Weil proposes**: LSD had no capacity to cure Hodgkin's disease. The drug created conditions for a conversion experience that transformed S.R.'s relationship to her life. The causally active agent was the change in consciousness — which then expressed itself through the biological interventions (altered diet, stress reduction, social restructuring) that followed. The physician's quoted comment: "I have no doubt that people with illness can choose to live or die."

This case parallels Dossey's Krebiozen paradigm in its structure (meaning as the active variable) but is more fully documented and the conversion moment more explicitly articulated.

### Wart Cures as Evidence for the Mechanism

Weil argues that wart cures — HPV-infected tissue clearing in response to folk remedies with no antiviral chemistry — provide the clearest evidence for the mechanism by which spontaneous remissions occur: a mind-directed activation of the immune system. See [Placebo Effect](placebo-effect.md) for the full analysis.

The argument: if the mind can switch on immune-mediated clearance of a viral infection (wart), the same immune machinery (NK cells, T cells) is in principle activatable against other viral infections, tumours, and cancers. Spontaneous remissions may represent the same mechanism operating at a larger biological scale, triggered by the kind of profound life change and consciousness shift documented in S.R.'s case.

Weil's indictment of medicine: scientists do not study wart cures. The phenomenon is treated as amusing folk medicine rather than as evidence for the most clinically important question — how the mind directs the immune system.
