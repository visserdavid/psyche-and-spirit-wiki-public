# Stages of Faith

**Summary**: James Fowler's six-stage developmental model of how faith grows over a lifetime — from the literal, mythic thinking of childhood through the paradox-tolerating, truth-integrating stages of mature spiritual adulthood — used by Borysenko to explain why some people are unable to find growth in crisis and why certain religious frameworks block rather than enable healing.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

---

## Overview

[James Fowler](../thinkers/james-fowler.md)'s *Stages of Faith: The Psychology of Human Development and the Quest for Meaning* (1981) offers a developmental psychology of religious belief that parallels the cognitive stage theories of Piaget and the moral development work of Kohlberg. Fowler argues that faith is not primarily about the content of belief (what one believes) but about the *structure* of meaning-making (how one believes) — and that this structure develops through identifiable stages over a lifetime.

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md) uses Fowler's framework in [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md) to explain a pattern she encounters repeatedly: why some patients are able to find growth and meaning in serious illness, while others are trapped in frameworks that make growth impossible. The framework also explains why therapists, doctors, and clergy operating from early faith stages can actively impede the healing process of patients who have moved beyond those stages.

## The Six Stages

### Stage 1: Intuitive-Projective Faith
Characteristic of early childhood, though some adults never leave it. The child's imagination is active but unregulated — God and reality are understood through vivid images and stories that remain largely unconscious. The gift of this stage: the capacity for profound imaginative engagement with the sacred. The danger: "possession of the child's imagination by images of terror and destructiveness" (Fowler). Adults operating primarily from Stage 1 live in a world of symbols, images, and associations that can be sources of profound spiritual experience or profound fear.

### Stage 2: Mythic-Literal Faith
School-age children begin operating at Stage 2. Imagination declines; story and symbol are taken with "uncompromising literalness." The world revolves around fairness and retribution: reward for good behaviour, punishment for bad. Stories provide identity and meaning, but the capacity to step outside them and reflect on their meaning is absent. "An eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth" level thinking.

**Clinical significance**: Jay (Borysenko's AIDS patient) and the Texan Christian counsellor who confronted her at a lecture both operate from Stage 2. Jay's belief that AIDS was divine punishment for homosexuality is Stage 2 cosmology: sin leads to punishment, there is no other explanation available within the framework. The counsellor's insistence that the world was created in six calendar days and that evolution contradicts God represents the same structure: a literal story that cannot be questioned without threatening the entire identity.

**Why Stage 2 blocks healing**: A Stage 2 framework translates illness directly into punishment for sin (personal failure, insufficient faith). This activates guilt and shame rather than meaning-seeking. Borysenko's Leslie operates from a much later stage; her response to cancer is "I don't know why this happened — my intent is to seek health and keep loving." This is cognitively and spiritually unavailable to a Stage 2 believer.

### Stage 3: Synthetic-Conventional Faith
Emerges in adolescence. The person begins to take responsibility for their beliefs but typically does not yet examine them critically — ideology is accepted without stepping back to analyse it. Identity is defined by group membership and social expectations. The authority is external (the group, the tradition, the institution).

Many adults remain at Stage 3 throughout their lives. The gifts: community, belonging, shared narrative. The limitation: beliefs are invisible (one doesn't know what one believes, only what "we" believe); challenge to group norms is experienced as personal attack.

### Stage 4: Individuative-Reflective Faith
Typically emerges in early adulthood or midlife, often catalysed by loss, disillusionment, or encounter with people who believe differently. The person steps outside the group's worldview and begins to ask: What do *I* believe? Why? The authority moves inside. This stage can be experienced as a loss of community and certainty.

Fowler: the transition is often triggered by "clash and contradictions between stories which lead to reflection on the meaning of those conflicts... literalism breaks... and disillusionment with one's teachers and teachings follows." The crisis of faith that follows — which can feel like the loss of God — is the necessary passage toward a larger faith.

### Stage 5: Conjunctive Faith
Occurs in midlife or later. The person lives with paradox — can hold apparently contradictory truths simultaneously — and thrives on it. Myths and symbols are grasped at a new depth: not literally (Stage 2) but as pointers to realities that exceed the capacity of any single framework. Stage 5 people can integrate parts of their experience they had previously denied or suppressed as threats to their worldview.

Borysenko's Leslie operates from Stage 5: she does not need to explain her cancer as punishment or as meaningful gift — she can hold the unknowability of why it happened while finding meaning in how she responds. The counsellor who cannot accept evolution alongside creation is Stage 2 encountering Stage 5 thinking, which — from Stage 2 — appears heretical.

### Stage 6: Universalising Faith
Rare. Fowler cites Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., Mother Teresa, Dag Hammarskjöld, Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Abraham Heschel, and Thomas Merton as examples. Stage 6 does not mean perfection or self-actualisation. It means a commitment to what Fowler calls "making the Kingdom come" — a selfless enactment of understanding in which the divisions between self and other, in-group and out-group, are genuinely overcome by compassion. Stage 6 people often experience rejection and danger from the communities they come from precisely because they threaten Stage 3 identity structures.

## Movement Between Stages

Movement through Fowler's stages is not automatic. It tends to require one of two catalysts:
1. **Tragedy or loss that breaks the existing framework** — illness, death, failure, encounter with profound injustice
2. **Encounter with people operating at a later stage** — whose capacity to hold more complexity creates both attraction and threat

The movement is also not irreversible: people can regress under stress. Jay regressed from Stage 4 (individual responsibility) to Stage 2 (sin-punishment framework) when diagnosed with AIDS.

## Relationship to Spiritual Optimism

[Spiritual pessimism](spiritual-optimism.md) — the framework in which suffering is punishment and the self is fundamentally unworthy — is primarily a Stage 2 cosmology. The [Prodigal Son](spiritual-optimism.md) parable, in Borysenko's reading, is the Stage 5 framework: the father who runs to meet the returning son without waiting for the account of sins is not operating from Stage 2 fairness/retribution logic. He is operating from a reality in which love precedes and transcends justice.

The [Via Positiva](spiritual-optimism.md) (Matthew Fox's creation-centred spirituality) and the [Perennial Philosophy](closet-mystics.md) (Huxley's cross-traditional convergence) are Stage 5 and Stage 6 orientations. The [dark night of the soul](closet-mystics.md) is typically a Stage 3-to-Stage-4 transition — the period when the conventional faith framework collapses and the Stage 5 capacity to hold paradox has not yet emerged.

## Connections to Other Wiki Frameworks

**[Crisis as Initiation](crisis-as-initiation.md)**: Victor Turner's liminal state maps onto the Stage 3-to-Stage-4 transition. The mwadi is stripped of Stage 3 identity (group membership, social role, conventional belief) and held in the liminal space until the Stage 4 individual self can emerge. Stage 4 is the initiate's new identity.

**[Guilt and Soul Sickness](guilt-and-soul-sickness.md)**: William James's "soul sickness" — the chronic shame-based orientation — is, in Fowler's terms, a Stage 2 orientation that has been internalised as identity. The cure requires moving toward at least Stage 4, where the self's worth is no longer determined by compliance with an external judge-god.

**[Woundology](woundology.md)** (Myss): Woundology — using wound identity as social currency — can function as a Stage 3 substitute community ("the group of people who were harmed as I was harmed"). Stage 4 movement requires releasing the group identity that woundology provides.

## Critique

**Developmental hierarchy**: Stage models imply that later = better. Critics note that Stage 5/6 thinking, detached from communal roots, can produce spiritual individualism without social accountability. Stage 3's communal faith has real psychological and social benefits that Stage 5 doesn't automatically replace.

**Western bias**: Fowler's model was developed primarily through interviews with educated Western adults. How well it captures non-Western or non-individualist forms of faith development is an open question.

**Research base**: Fowler's stages are clinically observed and theoretically compelling but not established through large-scale empirical validation comparable to Piaget's cognitive stages. The stage descriptions remain conceptual frameworks rather than empirically confirmed developmental categories.
