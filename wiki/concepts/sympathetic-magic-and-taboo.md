# Sympathetic Magic and Taboo

**Summary**: James George Frazer's foundational distinction between homeopathic magic ("like produces like") and contagious magic (things once in contact remain connected), embedded in his three-stage scheme of cultural evolution — Age of Magic, Age of Religion, Age of Science — in which magic and science share a hidden kinship as both assume a fixed, impersonal order of cause and effect, while religion alone appeals to the goodwill of personal supernatural agents.

**Sources**:
- [Frazer, J. G. (1994). *The golden bough: A study in magic and religion* (R. Fraser, Ed.). Oxford University Press. (Original work published 1890)](../sources/the-golden-bough.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-22

## The Two Laws of Sympathetic Magic

Frazer organizes the entire ethnographic record of magical practice under two principles, both instances of what he calls the Law of Sympathy — the belief that things can act on one another at a distance through a hidden sympathetic connection:

- **The Law of Similarity (homeopathic or imitative magic)**: like produces like, or an effect resembles its cause. A sorcerer can harm a person by damaging an image of them; a rain-dance imitates falling rain to cause rain to fall.
- **The Law of Contact or Contagion (contagious magic)**: things once in physical contact continue to act on each other after the contact is severed. Hair, nail clippings, or clothing can be used to work magic on the person they came from, because a sympathetic bond persists.

Frazer treats both as products of an error in association — mistaking an association of ideas in the magician's own mind for a real causal connection in the world — but stresses that the underlying logic (a fixed, predictable relationship between cause and effect that can be discovered and manipulated) is identical to the logic of science, even though the specific causal claims are false.

## The Three Ages: Magic, Religion, Science

Frazer's most famous and most disputed structural claim is a unilinear scheme of cultural evolution:

1. **Age of Magic**: humans believe nature operates by fixed, impersonal law and attempt to manipulate that law directly (rain-dances, image-magic, etc.).
2. **Age of Religion**: when magic is repeatedly seen to fail, humans conclude that nature is instead governed by personal, willful supernatural agents, and shift from *manipulating* nature to *supplicating* it — prayer and propitiation replace technique.
3. **Age of Science**: when propitiation, too, proves unreliable, humans return to the assumption of impersonal natural law, but now test that assumption rigorously through controlled observation rather than assuming it dogmatically, as in the Age of Magic.

On this scheme, magic and science are closer to each other than either is to religion: both assume the universe runs on a fixed order that can be understood and used; religion alone breaks with this assumption by conceding events to the discretion of personal gods. Frazer regarded contemporary occultism and spiritualism — investigated seriously by some of his own contemporaries — as a dangerous "relapse" into the magical stage disguised in scientific trappings, an anachronism rather than a genuine survival of valid technique.

## Taboo

Frazer treats taboo as a still-undifferentiated ancestor of the sacred/profane distinction: in the "taboo" mentality, the holy and the abhorrent are not yet separate categories, only different specific instances of things "set apart" from ordinary contact. Sacred kings, corpses, menstruating women, and criminals could all be subject to structurally similar taboo restrictions — hedged about, avoided, or ritually quarantined — regardless of whether the culture regarded them as holy or polluting. This treatment anticipates, and is a direct source for, later comparative-religion treatments of the sacred/profane boundary — see [Mircea Eliade](../thinkers/mircea-eliade.md)'s more historically disciplined version of the same distinction in *[Rites and Symbols of Initiation](../sources/rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md)*.

## Relevance to This Wiki

Frazer's "action at a distance" language for sympathetic magic is picked up directly, if provisionally, in [Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md)'s discussion of Bell's theorem and quantum non-locality in *[Healing Words](../sources/healing-words.md)*: Dossey notes that Bell's theorem demonstrates the universe is fundamentally "contagious" (in Frazer's sense) at the particle level, though he is careful that this does not itself validate distant-healing or hexing claims at the human scale — it only removes the a priori physical objection to them. This is a rare case of a mainstream physics finding being invoked, cautiously, to partially rehabilitate a category (contagious/sympathetic connection) that anthropology otherwise treats as a paradigm case of magical (i.e., non-causal) thinking.

## Critique

**From mainstream anthropology**: Frazer's three-age scheme (Magic → Religion → Science) is now regarded as a discredited unilinear evolutionary model — it assumes all cultures progress through the same fixed sequence, ranks "primitive" magical thought as an earlier developmental stage of "civilized" religious and scientific thought, and was assembled from secondhand reports rather than fieldwork. Modern anthropology, especially after Bronisław Malinowski's fieldwork-based studies of Trobriand magic, treats magic, religion, and science as coexisting, functionally distinct systems within a single culture rather than successive evolutionary stages — Trobriand islanders used magic and empirical technique side by side for the same practical tasks (fishing, gardening) without treating them as rival explanatory systems.

**From the history and philosophy of science**: Frazer's claim that magic and science share a common logical structure (a lawful, manipulable universe) has proven durable and is echoed, differently, in the sympathetic-magic-adjacent discussions of non-locality above — even critics of Frazer's evolutionary scheme often retain this specific structural observation.
