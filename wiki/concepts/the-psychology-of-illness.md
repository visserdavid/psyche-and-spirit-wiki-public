# The Psychology of Illness

**Summary**: The framework, central to Myss and Shealy's work, holding that emotional patterns, unresolved conflicts, and biographical investments in fear or powerlessness precede and contribute to the development of physical illness.

## Overview

The psychology of illness is the study of how psychological states — emotions, beliefs, relationship dynamics, and biographical history — contribute to the development and course of physical disease. In [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s framework, this is not a secondary or contributing factor but the primary level at which illness originates.

This perspective reframes illness from something that happens *to* a person (a random attack by a virus or genetic misfortune) to something that emerges *from* a person's life — from the accumulated energetic weight of unexpressed emotion, unresolved conflict, chronically depleted power, and violated truth.

## The Pre-Illness Stage

A key observation across Myss's case studies: illness does not arise suddenly. It follows a sequence:

1. **Energy depletion**: The person invests energy chronically in fear, resentment, attachment to external approval, or other draining patterns. The corresponding chakra loses power.

2. **Warning signals**: The body begins to communicate the depletion through fatigue, depression, loss of vitality, vague discomfort, or specific symptoms in the relevant body region. These signals are often dismissed or treated symptomatically.

3. **Illness manifestation**: If the underlying energetic pattern is not addressed, the depletion progresses to the point of physical pathology — an organ or system fails in its structure or function.

This sequence suggests that energy medicine is primarily a **preventive** framework: illness is detectable (intuitively) and addressable before it becomes physical.

## Recurring Patterns

Myss's fourteen years of case studies led her to identify consistent correspondences between stress patterns and illness locations:

- **Feelings of entrapment, resentment, and responsibility without fulfilment** → pancreatic and stomach disorders (third chakra)
- **Issues of rejection, loss of nurturing, and grief in relationships** → breast and heart conditions (fourth chakra)
- **Inability to speak one's truth, suppression of will, fear of confrontation** → thyroid and throat conditions (fifth chakra)
- **Betrayal, violation of trust, loss of creative power** → reproductive and lower digestive problems (second chakra)
- **Toxic tribal dynamics, unresolved family wounds, victimhood** → immune disorders, bone problems (first chakra)

## Case Studies from the Source

**The dentist**: Developed pancreatic cancer after years of feeling trapped in a career he despised. His energy readings showed burdened responsibility and emotional exclusion. Norm Shealy identified the pattern and urged change; the man refused to alter his life and died.

**Julie and Joanna**: Two women with breast cancer in similar marriages (contemptuous husbands, depleted self-respect). Julie invested all her power in her husband's approval and died within a year. Joanna recognised the violation, reclaimed her boundaries, demanded change, divorced when it wasn't forthcoming, and recovered.

**The woman with depression and neck pain**: [Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md)'s patient who lost power by defining herself entirely through her ability to control her adult children. No treatment could succeed until the power dynamic was addressed.

**The businessman post-1987 crash**: Health calibrated to money-making; when his financial power collapsed, his biology followed within months.

## Consciousness and Illness: The Third Dimension

Beyond the emotional-biographical dimension, Myss proposes a third dimension: consciousness itself. The degree to which a person is willing to live according to their ideals, tell their truth, and align with a purpose larger than ego survival affects not only their psychology but their cellular vitality.

She draws on concepts like:
- The **dark night of the soul** (John of the Cross): a spiritual crisis in which old meanings collapse and new ones have not yet emerged — often precipitating illness that serves as a catalyst for transformation.
- **Spiritual awakening through illness**: some illnesses are initiations — forced encounters with the self that would not have happened otherwise. The person who recovers having done the inner work often describes the illness as the beginning of their real life.

## The Role of Action

A consistent theme across the case studies: *knowing is not healing*. Peter (the HIV patient) recovered because he adopted a full healing programme with genuine commitment. The dentist received the same level of insight and chose not to act. The difference in outcome was not the quality of the intuitive reading but the response to it.

Myss insists that healing is not an intellectual event. It requires specific action: leaving situations, changing behaviours, making different choices, releasing attachments. The body heals when the spirit's energy is no longer tied up in maintaining patterns that contradict health.

## Borysenko's PNI Mechanism

[Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s Mind/Body Clinic work [(Borysenko & Rothstein, 1987)](../sources/minding-the-body.md) provides the most detailed biochemical account of the mechanism by which psychological states produce illness — the same mechanism Myss invokes but does not detail.

[Psychoneuroimmunology](psychoneuroimmunology.md) (PNI) shows that:
- Stress hormones (cortisol, adrenalin) released by the fight-or-flight response directly suppress T-lymphocyte and natural killer cell activity
- Conditioned psychological responses (learned associations between context and illness behaviour) can suppress immunity independently of the original stressor
- Exam-period stress reduces IgA antibody levels — the first line of defence against respiratory infection
- Sustained NK-cell suppression under chronic stress may increase vulnerability to cancer and viral infection

The key insight: 75% of physician visits are for stress-related conditions. The psychological patterns that Myss describes — entrapment, resentment, loss of self-worth, unresolved grief — are not metaphors for illness; they generate the cortisol and adrenalin that measurably compromise immune competence.

Borysenko's clinical complement to Myss's diagnostic framework: where Myss identifies *which* biographical pattern is active, the PNI model explains *how* it becomes physiological.

## Hay's Contribution: The Disease Index

[Louise Hay](../thinkers/louise-hay.md) in [*You Can Heal Your Life*](../sources/you-can-heal-your-life.md) (1984) presents the most explicit and systematised version of the psychology-of-illness framework as a practical reference: an A-to-Z **disease index** mapping ailments to their probable mental causes, accompanied by a "new thought pattern" (affirmation) for healing each one.

Where Myss works through chakra correspondences and Keleman through somatic morphology, Hay works through the **symbolic meaning of body areas**: each body part represents a psychological domain, and disease in that area reflects dis-ease in the corresponding mental territory. The head represents how we present ourselves to the world; the heart represents love and joy; the colon represents the ability to let go; the joints represent flexibility and the willingness to change direction.

**Selected mappings from the disease index**:
- *Arthritis*: "Feeling unloved. Criticism. Resentment." → "I am love. I now choose to love and approve of myself."
- *Cancer*: "Deep hurt. Longstanding resentment. Deep secret or grief eating away at the self." → "I lovingly forgive and release all of the past."
- *Heart attack*: "Squeezing all the joy out of the heart in favour of money or position." → "I bring joy back to the centre of my heart."
- *Overweight*: "Fear; need for protection; running away from feelings." → "I am at peace with my own feelings."
- *Ulcers*: "Fear. A strong belief that you are not good enough." → "I love and approve of myself."

**The four root patterns**: Hay identifies four psychological states as the sources of most illness: resentment (cancer), criticism (arthritis), guilt (pain), and fear (tension → multiple conditions). These are the most health-depleting expressions of a single underlying cause: the absence of self-love.

**Relation to Myss**: Hay's body-area symbolism parallels Myss's chakra-illness correspondences almost exactly: the throat (speaking one's truth; chakra 5), the heart (love; chakra 4), the lower back (financial fear; chakra 1-2), the genitals (sexuality and creativity; chakra 2). The two frameworks reach the same mappings through different conceptual routes — Hay through New Thought metaphysics and Myss through energy anatomy. See [Seven Sacred Truths](seven-sacred-truths.md) for the chakra framework and [Thought Causes Illness](thought-causes-illness.md) for Hay's full disease index and its critique. ## Keleman's Somatic Complement

[Stanley Keleman](../thinkers/stanley-keleman.md) provides a secular, anatomical complement to Myss's framework. Where Myss traces the path from psychological pattern to illness through the energy field and chakras, Keleman traces it through the body's pulsatory structure and somatic layering.

Keleman's [somatic distress patterns](somatic-distress-patterns.md) describe how the body's physical form reflects biographical history — rigid structures encasing withheld emotion, collapsed structures recording the absence of vitality, swollen structures reflecting dissolved boundaries. These structural patterns parallel the chakra-based illness correspondences Myss identifies: the rigid, overbound pattern that cannot yield echoes the first- and third-chakra power blocks Myss associates with immune and digestive disorders; the collapsed, underbound pattern echoes the surrendered self-worth Myss associates with breast and heart conditions.

The key divergence: Keleman makes no claims about energy fields, spiritual causes, or soul-level purpose. His framework is secular and observational — accessible to clinical workers who do not share Myss's spiritual premises. This makes the Keleman-Myss convergence particularly significant: two frameworks with entirely different methodological assumptions point to the same biographical-somatic link.

## Peck's Complementary Question: When Illness Radiates Outward

[M. Scott Peck](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md) in [*People of the Lie*](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md) addresses the mirror image of the psychology of illness: what happens when the accumulated biography does not produce physical disease but instead produces systematic harm to others?

Peck's evil patients (Bobby's parents, Roger's parents, Charlene, Sarah) share with the biography-is-biology patients the same root dynamic — the refusal to acknowledge the shadow, the failure to bring suppressed truth to light — but the direction of damage is reversed. Where Myss's ill patients somatise their unexpressed biography into the body, Peck's evil people externalise it into destruction of others.

Both are expressions of the same fundamental failure: the refusal of self-knowledge. The difference may be partly constitutional, partly circumstantial, and partly the presence or absence of genuine conscience (the capacity to feel pain at one's own harm to others). The person who can feel conscience has a pathway inward — through the guilt — toward the shadow material and potential healing. The person who cannot tolerate conscience has only the outward pathway: project, scapegoat, destroy the evidence.

This suggests the psychology of illness and the psychology of evil are complementary expressions of the same blocked dynamic. See [Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)](human-evil.md).

## Critique

**Convergence with mainstream research**: Psychoneuroimmunology (PNI) provides substantial evidence that psychological states affect immune function, cardiovascular health, cancer progression, and many other physiological outcomes. Chronic stress, depression, social isolation, and lack of perceived control are all associated with increased illness risk. In this broader sense, a psychology of illness is well-supported.

**The specificity problem**: The specific mappings Myss proposes — particular emotional patterns causing particular diseases — are not confirmed by epidemiological research. Cancer, for instance, does not show a reliable "cancer personality" in large studies. The direction of causality is also unclear: does chronic sadness contribute to cancer, or does pre-symptomatic cancer contribute to sadness?

**Structural factors**: Illness is shaped by poverty, environment, access to care, racism, and other structural factors that cannot be reduced to individual psychology. A framework that locates illness primarily in personal biography risks obscuring these systemic determinants.

**Note**: The claim that specific biographical or emotional patterns *cause* specific diseases is not supported by current epidemiological or clinical research. The relationship between psychological states and physical health is real but complex, probabilistic, and cannot be reduced to the illness-emotion correspondences Myss describes.
