# The Self

**Summary**: In Jungian psychology, the Self is the archetype of psychic totality and wholeness — the centre and circumference of the entire psyche, encompassing both consciousness and the unconscious; the goal of individuation and the theoretic precursor to Myss's soul and Hillman's acorn.

## Definition

The Self (German: *das Selbst*) is Jung's term for the archetype of totality — the wholeness that encompasses both the ego (conscious identity) and the unconscious (all that the ego has not yet integrated). It is:

- The **centre** of the psyche as a whole, not only of consciousness (the ego is the centre of consciousness; the Self is the centre of the whole psyche)
- The **circumference** — the totality, the container of everything both known and unknown
- The **goal** of [individuation](individuation.md) — the increasingly complete realisation of one's full potential
- An **archetype** — not a philosophical abstraction but a living psychic reality that manifests in specific images

> "I have called this centre the self. Intellectually the self is no more than a psychological concept, a construct that serves to express an unknowable essence which we cannot grasp as such, since by definition it transcends our powers of comprehension."
>
> — Jung, *Conscious, Unconscious, and Individuation* [(Jung, 1969, par. 247)](../sources/collected-works-9-1.md)

## The Self vs. the Ego

The most fundamental distinction in Jungian psychology:

| Ego | Self |
|-----|------|
| Centre of consciousness | Centre of the total psyche |
| What I know about myself | What I am, known and unknown |
| Acquired through development | Innate; precedes and transcends development |
| Subject of experience | The experiencer and the experienced |
| Can be wrong, deceived, limited | The Self "knows" more than the ego |

The ego is a complex within the larger Self, not its master. Psychological maturity involves learning to relate to the Self rather than identifying the Self with the ego. The pathology of ego-inflation is precisely this identification: the ego believes it is the whole psyche and attempts to organise life entirely from its own limited perspective.

## Symbols of the Self

Because the Self is, by definition, beyond direct representation, it manifests through symbols. Its characteristic symbolic forms include:

- **The [mandala](mandala.md)**: Circular images with a centre, often with fourfold (quaternary) structure — the most common and universal symbol of the Self
- **The quaternity**: Four-fold structures (cross, square, tetrahedron) representing the completion of the opposites
- **The lapis (philosopher's stone)**: In alchemy, the miraculous substance that transforms base matter into gold — the Self as the goal of the Great Work. [*Mysterium Coniunctionis*](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md) develops this at length: the lapis is alchemy's "thousand-named" substance, paradoxically both base and precious, hidden and everywhere — the same paradoxical language Jung elsewhere uses to describe the Self's resistance to single-valued definition (see "The Self vs. the Ego," above)
- **The child**: The Divine Child archetype embodies the Self as it exists at the beginning (origin) and end (goal) of individuation — whole before differentiation, whole again after integration
- **The circle**: Completeness, infinity, no beginning or end — the Self as the container of all opposites

## Approaching the Self via the Mana-Personality

*Two Essays on Analytical Psychology* gives the clearest clinical account of how the ego arrives at a genuine (non-inflated) relationship with the Self at all: once the [anima or animus](anima-animus.md) has been substantially differentiated from the ego, the numinous potency ("mana") that figure carried does not disappear — it becomes available, and the ego's characteristic temptation is to claim it, producing the **[mana-personality](mana-personality.md)** (identification as hero, magician, or saint). Recognizing that this freed value belongs to neither the ego nor to any single figure the ego can name is, for Jung, the precise threshold at which "the self" first becomes visible as something the ego relates to rather than something the ego can be or possess. This gives concrete clinical content to the more abstract formula (below) that the Self "transcends our powers of comprehension."

## The Self and God-Image

Jung makes an explicit and controversial claim: psychologically, the Self and the traditional God-image (Imago Dei) are structurally identical. The archetype of the Self generates the same symbolic expressions as religious traditions generate for God — mandala, quaternity, light, wholeness. This is not a theological claim (that the Self *is* God) but a psychological one (that the psyche's experience of the Divine and the psyche's experience of its own wholeness are structurally indistinguishable).

This parallel is demonstrated through the history of mandala symbolism (which appears in Christian, Buddhist, Hindu, and Navajo traditions with the same structural features) and through the mandala drawings of patients, which spontaneously reproduce the same imagery without cultural exposure to the tradition.

## The Self in the Process of Individuation

In [individuation](individuation.md), the Self is both the initiating impulse and the final destination. The Self "calls" the ego through:

- Disturbing dreams that challenge the ego's comfortable self-image
- Synchronicities that point toward a different life direction
- Illness that forces confrontation with dimensions of the self the ego has ignored
- Creative impulses that transcend the ego's intentions

The process of individuation is the ego's gradually deepening relationship with the Self — not dissolution of the ego into the unconscious (inflation) but a conscious partnership in which the ego serves the Self's direction while maintaining its own orientation and responsibility.

## Relevance to Myss and Hillman

### Sacred Contracts as Self-Expression

[Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s concept of [Sacred Contracts](sacred-contracts.md) — pre-birth soul agreements specifying the lessons, relationships, and purpose of a lifetime — is, in Jungian terms, a description of the Self's inherent direction. The contract is not created by the ego; it exists prior to the ego and asserts itself through the ego's choices and experiences. What Myss frames theologically (a pre-birth agreement with the Divine), Jung frames psychologically (the Self's dormant plan that shapes individual fate).

### Hillman's Acorn as Self

[James Hillman](../thinkers/james-hillman.md)'s *acorn theory* — the idea that the soul carries an image of its own completed form from birth — is a reformulation of the Jungian Self. Hillman's daimon (the guardian figure that guides the soul toward its unique calling) corresponds to the Self's anticipatory, forward-looking dimension that Jung describes in the individuation essay.

### The Eighth Chakra

In [*Sacred Contracts*](../sources/sacred-contracts.md), Myss introduces an eighth chakra above the crown as the seat of the Sacred Contract — the point where the soul's pre-birth agreement is stored. This corresponds structurally to the Jungian Self: a centre that transcends ego-consciousness and holds the totality of the soul's purpose.

## The Self as Adam/Anthropos and the Unus Mundus

In [*Mysterium Coniunctionis*](../sources/mysterium-coniunctionis.md), Jung extends the Self's "beginning and end" status (already established for the [Child Archetype](child-archetype.md)) to the alchemical **Adam** figure — the primordial Anthropos, an originally androgynous totality-being who only later differentiates into Adam and Eve, male and female. Adam's undivided wholeness is read as a cosmological restatement of the same claim: totality precedes differentiation, and individuation is not the avoidance of opposites but their conscious re-union after genuine differentiation. See [Sol, Luna, and the Alchemical Coniunctio](coniunctio.md) for the full mythological sequence (the King's death and rebirth, the wounding of Sol by Luna) through which this re-union is dramatized.

Jung's closing chapters push one level beyond the individual Self, to the **[Unus Mundus](unus-mundus.md)** — "the one world" underlying the split between psyche and matter, which the Self (as the totality of *one person's* psyche) is understood to locally express. This is the deepest ground the wiki's Jungian material reaches, and it connects directly to the physics-of-consciousness material found in [Implicate Order](implicate-order.md) and [The Holographic Universe](../sources/the-holographic-universe.md).

## Christ as an Empirical Symbol of the Self

In [*Aion*](../sources/aion.md), Jung extends the God-image parallel (above) into a full historical case study: [Christ as a Symbol of the Self](christ-as-symbol-of-the-self.md). Because the Christ-image is doctrinally defined as *sine macula peccati* ("without spot of sin") and evil is denied substantial reality by the Christian doctrine of [privatio boni](privatio-boni.md), Jung argues Christ is a symbol of totality that structurally excludes the shadow — with the historical consequence that the excluded darkness returns as the doctrinally anticipated figure of the **Antichrist**. This is *Aion*'s central extended argument and gives the wiki's abstract discussion of the Self's "dual creative/destructive nature" (below, and via the Bath Badgerd tale) its fullest historical elaboration.

## Gnostic Symbols of the Self and the Quaternio Structures

*Aion*'s closing chapters catalogue an extensive body of Gnostic and comparative material — the Naassene Anthropos, Monoïmos's self-knowing point/monad, Meister Eckhart's soul-spark, Plotinus's account of the soul's circular self-movement — organizing it around fourfold **quaternio** diagrams (the Anthropos, Shadow, Paradise, and Lapis Quaternios) that directly anticipate the alchemical schema of *Mysterium Coniunctionis*. See [Gnostic Quaternios and the Structure of the Self](gnostic-quaternios.md) for the full treatment.

## The Self in *Man and His Symbols* (von Franz)

[Marie-Louise von Franz](../thinkers/marie-louise-von-franz.md)'s treatment in [(Jung et al., 1964)](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) extends the CW 9.1 account with several concrete dimensions:

**The Naskapi Great Man**: An Eastern Canadian Indigenous people who live in extreme isolation rely entirely on dreams for guidance. Their inner guide — the Mista'peo or "Great Man" — is an exact cultural parallel to the Jungian Self: an inner source of wisdom accessible through attentive dreaming, functioning as compass and teacher.

**Dual nature of the Self**: The Self has both a creative and a destructive face. The Persian fairy tale of Bath Badgerd illustrates this: the First Man (Gyomart) has hidden a diamond (the Self) at the centre of a round bath-house. A demonic parrot guards it, petrifying all who approach without the right inner preparation. The hero (Hatim) can only succeed by shutting his eyes and trusting — not by imitating the approach of others or by clever strategy.

**Self in art**: [Aniela Jaffé](../thinkers/aniela-jaffe.md)'s section in the same volume shows how the circle symbol — the Self's primary visual form — appears spontaneously not only in Tibetan mandalas and Navajo sand paintings but in the ground plans of ancient cities (Rome, Washington D.C., Angkor Wat), cathedral rose windows, and across the history of modern painting. The unconscious search for the circle in contemporary abstract art (Kandinsky, Klee, Pollock) is read as a cultural symptom: a civilisation seeking a new centre to replace the God-image that has faded.

## Critique

**The God-image parallel**: Jung's equation of the Self with the God-image is the most contested aspect of his work. Critics argue that:
- It conflates psychological experience with ontological claim
- It reduces theology to psychology (or psychology to theology, depending on perspective)
- It imports religious meaning into a framework that claims scientific status

Jung himself was carefully agnostic: he claimed only to describe the psychological function of the God-image, not its metaphysical validity. This distinction is difficult to maintain in practice.

**The paradox of the unknowable centre**: If the Self is by definition beyond the ego's comprehension, it cannot be directly known or described. All descriptions of the Self are symbolic approximations. This creates a theoretical circularity: the concept is confirmed by its own inaccessibility.
