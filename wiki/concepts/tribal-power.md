# Tribal Power

**Summary**: The first chakra's domain — the energy of group identity, collective belief, family loyalty, and tribal survival — and its foundational role in individual health and spiritual development.

**Sources**:
- [Myss, C. (1996). *Anatomy of the spirit: The seven stages of power and healing*. Harmony Books.](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

"Tribal power" is [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md)'s term for the energy content of the first chakra. The tribe is not merely the biological family — it is any group whose collective identity, beliefs, and codes of behaviour shape the individual from birth. This includes families, nations, religions, ethnic groups, peer groups, and cultures.

The first chakra grounds the individual in the world of matter and community. Its energy is the energy of belonging, survival, loyalty, honour, and justice. Every person begins life entirely embedded in their tribe — absorbing its beliefs, fears, superstitions, and codes of conduct without the capacity to evaluate or choose them.

## Tribal Culture

The tribe introduces the individual to the world. It provides:
- Food, shelter, belonging, and protection (survival needs)
- A code of behaviour and honour (the framework for navigating relationships)
- A narrative about reality (who God is, what life means, how the world works)
- Inherited beliefs, attitudes, and superstitions

These inputs shape the first chakra's energy. They are not simply information — they are energetically charged and encoded in the body's tissue, especially in the bones, legs, immune system, and spine.

## The Sacred Truth: All is One

The first-chakra truth is "All is One" — we are interconnected; what we do to others returns to us; care for the community is care for the self. Honouring this truth generates stability and immune strength. Violating it — through betrayal of family bonds, tribal shame, or disloyalty — depletes the first chakra and can produce autoimmune disorders, skeletal problems, and depression.

## Group Biology: Epidemics as Tribal Events

One of the more striking propositions in *Anatomy of the Spirit* is Myss's reading of epidemics as expressions of tribal consciousness:

- The polio epidemic of the 1930s–40s: Myss connects it to the Great Depression — the economic collapse activated deep fear and powerlessness in the cultural body (chakra 1). American children, absorbing their tribe's dis-ease, expressed it as the polio virus. Recovery came with the restoration of tribal pride and purpose through World War II.

- The HIV/AIDS epidemic: Myss reads the epidemic's differing patterns across cultures as reflections of each culture's tribal power issues — victimisation and disempowerment in the US (via drug users and marginalised communities); machismo and lack of women's voice in Latin cultures; the expression of collective victimhood.

This is a highly contested interpretation (see Critique below).

## Toxic Tribal Power

Tribal loyalty is a virtue — but it can become a toxin when it requires the individual to suppress their own needs, truth, or growth in service of the group. Myss describes several forms of toxic tribal power:

- **Loyalty over self**: A family that relies on an abused child's income to survive teaches the child that their survival depends on suppressing truth. Tony's story — abused by an employer as a child, silenced by his immigrant family who needed his wages — illustrates the health cost of loyalty enforced at the price of self-betrayal.

- **Inherited belief patterns**: George, who believed his life would effectively end at sixty because that's what his family expected, was living according to a tribal programme rather than his own spirit. Unplugging this belief freed him.

- **Justice as vengeance**: Tribal justice — "eye for an eye" — can trap individuals in cycles of vengeance that damage the first chakra and make physical healing impossible. Patrick's case (a man from Northern Ireland whose leg injuries would not heal while he maintained association with a paramilitary organisation) illustrates this directly.

## The Four Tribal Energies

Myss identifies four key energies in the first chakra:

1. **Loyalty**: The commitment to stand by one's tribe. Can become pathological when loyalty requires self-harm.
2. **Justice**: The tribal sense of right and wrong, fairness and punishment. When invested in vengeance, it becomes a biological drain.
3. **Honour**: The code that gives tribal members dignity and a frame of reference. Erosion of honour is erosion of identity and health.
4. **Bonding rituals**: The ceremonies (Baptism, initiation rites) that affirm membership and responsibility.

## Separating from the Tribe

The journey of individuation — the movement from chakra 1 to chakra 3 — requires that the individual gradually differentiate their own beliefs and values from those inherited from the tribe. This is not rejection of the tribe but transcendence: carrying forward what serves development, releasing what does not.

This process is challenging because tribal energy has the force of survival instinct. Challenging family beliefs, leaving a religion, or diverging from tribal expectations feels — energetically — like risking annihilation. The first chakra does not distinguish between physical survival and tribal belonging.

## Critique

**The epidemic claim**: The proposition that polio arose because of Great Depression fear, or that HIV patterns reflect cultural victim consciousness, is not supported by epidemiology, virology, or public health research. These are symbolic/archetypal interpretations, not causal explanations. The actual drivers of epidemic disease (viral mutation, transmission routes, population immunity) are well understood by mainstream science and do not involve collective consciousness.

**Note**: The claim that cultural beliefs or collective fear can cause or shape viral epidemics is not supported by mainstream infectious disease research. While psychosocial factors affect individual vulnerability to some diseases, the idea that a culture's psychological state determines which virus spreads and who gets infected is not consistent with the scientific evidence.

**Victim-blaming at scale**: If the HIV epidemic reflects the victim consciousness of the cultures where it thrives, this risks implying that affected communities are responsible for their epidemic through their collective psychology — an ethically fraught conclusion.

**The strength of the framework**: As a map of *how group beliefs shape individual development*, the tribal power concept is valuable. The recognition that inherited beliefs operate below conscious awareness and are encoded in the body aligns with much in developmental psychology and attachment theory, even if the specific energetic claims go beyond what science supports.
