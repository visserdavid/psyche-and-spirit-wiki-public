# Trickster Archetype

**Summary**: The most primitive hero figure — amoral, appetite-driven, lacking coherent identity — who represents the collective shadow before integration; present across world mythologies as the sacred fool, the coyote, Loki, Mercurius; the agent of creative disorder that disrupts rigid order and opens the possibility of transformation.

## Definition

The Trickster is one of the most ancient and widely distributed archetypal figures. He appears across world mythologies with consistent features: he transgresses social and cosmic rules without concern for consequences; he is simultaneously creator and destroyer, wise fool and bumbling buffoon; he is driven by appetite (hunger, sexuality) without moral restraint; and he is ultimately the agent of transformation, precisely because he disrupts the complacency of established order.

In [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s framework (developed in *On the Psychology of the Trickster Figure*, included in [CW 9.1](../sources/collected-works-9-1.md)), the Trickster is an archetype of the [collective unconscious](collective-unconscious.md) — he personifies the collective shadow, the shared dark side of culture, before it has been differentiated and integrated.

## The Winnebago Trickster

[Joseph L. Henderson](../thinkers/joseph-henderson.md) opens his analysis in [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) with the Winnebago Trickster cycle as the first of four sequential hero narratives representing the evolution of consciousness:

The Winnebago Trickster lacks:
- Bodily integrity (his right and left hands fight each other; he carries his detachable penis in a box)
- Social awareness (he stumbles into and out of situations without understanding their meaning)
- Moral concern (he tricks, steals, and harms without awareness of doing wrong)
- Memory and learning (he repeats the same mistakes without recognising patterns)

Yet through his endless misadventures — each one a humiliation that teaches him, slowly and painfully, the consequences of ignoring reality — the Trickster gradually develops the proto-social awareness that the more evolved heroes (Hare, Red Horn, Twin Heroes) will carry further. He is the beginning, not the end, of the hero's journey.

### The Primary Ethnographic Source: Radin's Wakdjunkaga Cycle

[Paul Radin](../thinkers/paul-radin.md)'s [*The Trickster*](../sources/the-trickster.md) (1956) is the primary ethnographic source both Jung and Henderson draw on. Radin's translation of the Winnebago cycle names the figure Wakdjunkaga ("the tricky one") and follows him through roughly fifty episodes: he deserts a war party to cohabit with a woman; his own right and left arms fight each other over a piece of buffalo meat; he unknowingly kills his adoptive "brother's" (the fox's) children through carelessness; his detachable penis, kept in a box, is dispatched independently across a lake to seduce a chief's daughter and is gradually whittled down to nothing by an outraged chipmunk; he is tricked by his own anus, which he believes is a separate, insubordinate companion; he gets his head permanently stuck inside an elk skull he had used as a disguise; and, after being progressively humbled and slowly acquiring rudimentary awareness of consequence, he removes the obstacles from the Mississippi river, eats a final ritual meal, and ascends to heaven, leaving Earthmaker's world in the charge of Turtle and Hare.

Radin's own extensive analytical essay (Part Three of the volume) is more cautious than either commentator's reading built on top of it. He argues the Winnebago material approaches a "pure" trickster type — appetite, wandering, and sexuality with the surrounding culture-hero and creation material stripped away — but stresses that trickster cycles vary considerably across North American tribes in moral valence and divine status; some tribes (Northwest Coast Raven cycles) integrate the trickster directly into creation myth, while others (the Oglala Dakota's Iktomi/"Spider") show a trickster figure explicitly demoted from an earlier divine status by later systematizing mythographers. Radin also identifies deliberate satire in the Winnebago cycle — send-ups of specific tribal institutions including the warpath feast, puberty-fasting rites, and the Thunderbird/Bear clan chieftainship contest — arguing the cycle functioned in part as a sanctioned outlet for protest against the obligations of Winnebago social and religious order.

## Cross-Cultural Forms

The Trickster appears across the world's mythologies with structural consistency:
- **Native American**: Coyote (southwestern tribes), Raven (Pacific Northwest), the Winnebago Trickster
- **Norse**: Loki — creator of catastrophe and agent of transformation, ultimately responsible for Ragnarök
- **Greek**: Hermes — the divine trickster, messenger of the gods, lord of boundaries and crossings, patron of thieves and traders
- **West African / African American**: Anansi (spider), Br'er Rabbit
- **Medieval European**: The court jester, the Fool of the Tarot
- **Alchemical**: Mercurius — the spirit of matter, who is simultaneously the starting point and the goal of the alchemical work; duplex (two-faced), operating in both light and dark

## The Trickster and Hermes: Kerényi's Comparative Reading

[Karl Kerényi](../thinkers/karl-kerenyi.md)'s commentary essay in *[The Trickster](../sources/the-trickster.md)*, written independently of Jung's for the same volume, reads Wakdjunkaga against the Greek trickster-god Hermes and the wider European picaresque tradition (Petronius, Rabelais, Goethe's *Reynard the Fox*, Thomas Mann's *Felix Krull*). Kerényi finds real family resemblance — phallic emphasis, boundary-crossing, comic reversal, association with theft and cunning — but argues the two figures occupy structurally different positions. Hermes remains a god within the Olympian pantheon: a psychopomp, a "spirit of life" whose office ranges across the entire span of experience, from the phallic to the poetic to the guidance of souls after death. Wakdjunkaga has no such settled office — his wanderings simply end, unredeemed, when he departs the earth. Kerényi names this contrast Hermes's "limitlessness" against Wakdjunkaga's more narrowly bounded "spirit of disorder": Hermes is disorder that still serves a cosmic order; Wakdjunkaga is disorder without portfolio. Consistent with his method elsewhere in *[Essays on a Science of Mythology](../sources/essays-on-a-science-of-mythology.md)*, Kerényi explicitly declines to resolve the Trickster into a single explanatory theory (rejecting, for instance, Kristensen's "divine deceiver" hypothesis), preferring to let the mythologem remain irreducible to theory.

## The Trickster as Collective Shadow

Jung's analysis — first published as the closing essay of *[The Trickster](../sources/the-trickster.md)* and later reprinted in CW 9.1 — interprets the Trickster as the **collective shadow** before integration — the personification of everything that collective life must exclude to maintain its order. The Trickster embodies:
- Appetite without restraint (what culture suppresses as "animal")
- Amorality without guilt (what civilisation excludes as "criminal")
- Creative disruption without responsibility (what institutions exclude as "chaos")

Because these are the excluded contents of collective life, the Trickster must be periodically released. This is the psychological function of Carnival, fool's days (Saturnalia, April Fool's Day), trickster rituals in tribal cultures, and comedy in general: by ritually enacting the trickster's transgressions in a bounded form, the community releases the accumulated tension of its exclusions without actually dissolving the social order.

Jung's fuller essay traces this function through an unusually wide range of parallels: Mercurius in alchemy; the medieval Catholic Church's own "Feast of Fools" (*festum fatuorum*) and "Feast of the Ass" (*festum asinorum*), in which clergy elected a mock bishop, dances and masquerade took place inside the church building itself, and the liturgy was parodically brayed — practices only suppressed by ecclesiastical decree between the 1440s and 1580s; the folklore simpleton (Hanswurst, Tom Thumb); and reported poltergeist phenomena, which Jung reads as exhibiting the same low, mischievous, undifferentiated level of "intelligence" as the mythic Trickster. He also proposes a loose correspondence between the successive stages of the Trickster's bodily disintegration and reintegration and the chakra sequence (citing Arthur Avalon's *The Serpent Power*) as a further marker of an archaic level of consciousness the myth encodes.

## The Trickster and Transformation

The Trickster is not merely disruptive; he is creative. His disruption of the established order creates the space in which new possibilities can emerge. Jung notes that Mercurius — the alchemical Trickster — is both the prima materia (the raw chaos at the beginning of the work) and the lapis (the philosopher's stone at its completion). The Trickster is where the transformation begins, not where it ends; but without him, the transformation cannot begin.

In individual psychology, Trickster energy appears when the persona has become too rigid — when the collective role has so completely dominated the personality that the shadow can no longer be contained. The eruption of Trickster-like behaviour (rule-breaking, appetite-following, creative chaos) in a previously ordered life is a signal that the shadow is demanding attention.

## Relationship to the Shadow

The Trickster is closely related to — but not identical with — the [Shadow](shadow.md). The shadow is the personal dimension: the individual's own repressed, unlived qualities. The Trickster is the collective dimension: the archetype of the pre-moral unconscious that exists beneath any culture's self-image.

The Trickster becomes the Shadow when the cultural order is so organised that it projects all transgression onto a scapegoat group (criminals, heretics, minorities) rather than integrating the creative chaos it represents.

Jung is explicit in his fuller essay that civilization does not resolve this level of the psyche but *forgets* it — and that forgetting is not the same as its disappearance. What is forgotten rather than integrated persists underground, breaking out in individual pathology, in collective political "monkey tricks" enacted by whole nations without any individual recognizing the pattern, and in the mobilized collective shadow that produces historical catastrophe. He treats it as psychologically significant, not incidental, that the Trickster cycle's ending gestures toward the saviour figure: the possibility of transforming "disaster" into liberation depends on recognition and integration of shadow content, not on its further suppression. See [Trickster and Civilization as Forgetting](#trickster-and-civilization-as-forgetting) below.

### Trickster and Civilization as Forgetting

Radin's own comparative essay anticipates Jung's psychological reading from the anthropological side: he writes that "civilization" can itself be understood, in part, as a sustained act of forgetting the trickster level of experience — an argument Jung takes up directly, framing the Trickster cycle's persistence in folklore, Carnival, and even ecclesiastical ritual as evidence that the forgetting is never complete. This gives the wiki's shadow material an explicit historical mechanism: what a culture will not integrate, it does not eliminate but drives into recurring, quasi-sanctioned ritual outlets (Carnival, the medieval Feast of Fools) or, when those outlets weaken, into unrecognized collective projection.

## Note: Empirical Status

The Trickster as a universal archetype is an interpretive hypothesis. The cross-cultural similarities are documented but their explanation — whether by collective unconscious, evolutionary psychology, cognitive universals, or cultural diffusion — remains debated. Radin's own comparative chapter is more cautious on this point than either commentator's essay built on top of it: he stresses that trickster cycles vary considerably in moral valence and divine status across tribes, and that isolating a "pure" trickster type required him to set aside considerable culture-hero and creation material that many tribes integrate directly into their own trickster narratives. Mainstream anthropology treats Radin's Winnebago fieldwork as historically significant but cautions against the further step — taken more emphatically by Jung and Kerényi than by Radin himself — of generalizing a single ethnographic case, transcribed from one narrator (Sam Blowsnake), into a universal psychological or mythological structure. See [The Trickster § Critique](../sources/the-trickster.md) for the fuller discussion.
