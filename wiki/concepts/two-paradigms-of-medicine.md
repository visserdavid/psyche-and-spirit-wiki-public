# Two Paradigms of Medicine

**Summary**: The contrast — and proposed complementarity — between allopathic medicine (disease originates outside the person: viruses, bacteria, genetics) and holistic medicine (disease develops from within: stress, emotion, biography), as analysed by Myss and Shealy.

## Overview

In [*The Creation of Health*](../sources/creation-of-health.md), [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) and [Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) systematically compare two paradigms for understanding illness. They argue that these paradigms are not competing alternatives but complementary approaches: allopathic medicine addresses the physical level at which disease has manifested; holistic medicine addresses the energetic and biographical level at which disease originates.

Myss's formulation: "Medicine represents the mind of health care. Holistic medicine represents the heart of health care."

## Paradigm 1: Allopathic Medicine

The allopathic paradigm locates the cause of disease in external agents or internal physical malfunction:

- Viruses, bacteria, and other pathogens *invade* and *attack* the body
- Genetic predisposition creates inherent biological vulnerabilities
- Environmental toxins (asbestos, radiation, pollution) damage tissue
- The body's own regulatory systems fail (cancer as mutation; diabetes as receptor dysfunction)

Within this framework:
- **Emotions have no direct causal role**: they are not considered measurable contributors to disease in the way that viral loads or cholesterol levels are
- **Treatment** is directed at the physical level: drugs, surgery, radiation
- **The patient is largely passive**: the physician acts on the disease body

**The explanatory gap**: Shealy notes a persistent anomaly that the allopathic paradigm cannot explain — why, within a group of people exposed to the same risk factor (cigarette smoking, asbestos, viral exposure), only a fraction develop disease. The majority of smokers never develop lung cancer. This statistical reality implies that additional factors — individual to each person — determine vulnerability. Allopathic medicine does not systematically study what those factors are.

## Paradigm 2: Holistic Medicine

The holistic paradigm locates the origin of disease in the person's inner state:

- Illness develops from the *inside out*: emotional patterns, unresolved conflicts, biographical stress, and withdrawn personal power deplete the body's energy field before any physical pathology appears
- External agents (viruses, bacteria) can only take hold in a host whose inner resources have been depleted by biographical stress — they are triggers, not causes
- The **energy level precedes** the physical level: disruptions are detectable at the energetic stage before any clinical test would show abnormality

The holistic paradigm does not deny the reality of viruses, bacteria, and genetics. It claims that these operate on a foundation of inner state — that the same pathogen produces illness in one person and not in another because of differences in that inner state.

## The Naming Problem

Myss explicitly challenges the term **"alternative medicine"**:

> The word 'alternative' suggests a secondary or supplementary approach — one that implies the system lacks something that conventional medicine has. This is precisely backwards. Holistic medicine is not an alternative to conventional medicine; it is the missing half of it.

She prefers **"holistic medicine"** as a term — and uses this to distinguish it from any specific therapy or technique. Holistic medicine is not acupuncture or homeopathy; it is a way of approaching the healing person that incorporates different therapies, including conventional ones, in the service of whole-person understanding.

## The Complementarity Thesis

The book's central argument about the two paradigms: they need each other.

- **Medicine without holism**: treats the organ but not the person; removes the tumour but leaves the pattern that generated it; produces repeated illness because the underlying cause is never addressed. The dentist who develops pancreatic cancer (see [Disease Energy Profiles](disease-energy-profiles.md)) dies because the surgery without inner change leaves the pattern intact.
- **Holism without medicine**: can become a sophisticated form of avoidance. The Lori case (see [The Creation of Health](../sources/creation-of-health.md)) illustrates this precisely: a patient who refused surgery and pursued alternative therapies was using the alternative framework to avoid confronting her cancer's reality. She died.

The ideal is an integrated practice in which medicine provides diagnosis, crisis intervention, and the physical-level treatment — while holistic assessment identifies the biographical context, the underlying stress pattern, and the inner change the patient needs to make for healing to be sustained.

## Behavioural Medicine as Bridge

[Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md) identifies **behavioural medicine** as the institutional bridge that has emerged between the two paradigms. Beginning with Ivan Pavlov's conditioning research, B.F. Skinner's behaviourism, and Elmer Green's biofeedback work at the Menninger Foundation in the 1960s, behavioural medicine established the scientific basis for the claim that psychological states have physiological correlates — and that those correlates can be measured and modified.

Key developments cited:
- **Biofeedback** (Elmer Green): demonstrated that autonomic processes previously thought involuntary could be brought under conscious regulation, including blood pressure, heart rate, and skin temperature
- **Psychoneuroimmunology** (PNI): the scientific discipline studying how psychological states affect immune function — now a legitimate research field within mainstream medicine
- **The hostility research** (Duke University, University of North Carolina): prospective studies showing that hostility, cynicism, and anger significantly predict cardiac mortality — the first large-scale epidemiological validation of a biographical-emotional variable as a disease risk

Shealy argues that PNI's emergence represents the beginning of the paradigm merger: mainstream medicine is learning, case by case, that emotions have biological reality.

## Relationship to Larry Dossey's Three Eras

[Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md)'s framework of three eras of medicine provides a complementary map:

- **Era I** (allopathic): body as machine; local, mechanical causation
- **Era II** (mind-body): psychological states affect physical health; the arena of behavioural medicine and holistic approaches
- **Era III** (non-local): healing that transcends the individual body — prayer, distant healing, the research documented in [*Sacred Healing*](../sources/sacred-healing.md)

The two-paradigm framework in *Creation of Health* operates primarily at the boundary between Era I and Era II.

## Weil's Perspective: A Third Position

[Andrew Weil](../thinkers/andrew-weil.md) in *Health and Healing* (1983) arrives at the two-paradigm problem from a different starting point than Myss and Shealy — not from energy medicine and medical intuition, but from a clinical survey of every major healing system worldwide. His position adds a third element to the analysis: a critique of holistic medicine itself.

### Weil's Agreement with the Holistic Case

Weil shares the central holistic premise: allopathic medicine cannot define health, focuses on disease to the exclusion of healing, and has systematically ignored evidence — placebo response, spontaneous remission, mind-body connection — that does not fit its mechanistic framework. His nine principles ([Nine Principles of Health and Illness](nine-principles-of-health.md)) constitute an independent philosophical case for the holistic paradigm's core claims.

He also agrees with Myss and Shealy that the agents of illness (germs, toxins, carcinogens) are not its causes — susceptibility is — and that susceptibility has psychological, biographical, and spiritual dimensions.

### Weil's Critique of Holistic Medicine in Practice

Where Weil diverges sharply from the holistic camp is in his critique of holistic medicine's execution. Having helped found the American Holistic Medical Association and attended its early conferences, he concluded that holistic medicine had no coherent philosophy, accepted unproven modalities uncritically, and cultivated what he called a "fantasy of sweetness and light":

- **Applied kinesiology** (muscle-testing to diagnose energetic imbalances) was adopted at holistic conferences without controlled validation
- **Bach flower remedies** and **colour therapy** were presented as established healing modalities without evidentiary basis
- Conferences generated enthusiasm but no clinical protocols or measurable outcomes
- The holistic critique of allopathic medicine's aggression was not replaced by a coherent alternative — it was replaced by the assumption that pleasant therapies are inherently better

Weil's verdict: holistic medicine had become an ideology without a philosophy — united by what it opposed (the allopathic model) rather than by what it stood for.

### Weil's Model: Pragmatic Integrative Medicine

Weil's alternative is not a new paradigm but a clinical practice that uses all paradigms pragmatically. His model integrative physician:

- Is primarily trained in allopathic medicine (and uses it for emergencies, trauma, acute infection, surgical repair — what it is good at)
- Refers to acupuncturists, osteopaths, biofeedback therapists, nutritionists, and healers based on what the specific case requires
- Chooses therapeutic tools based on evidence and clinical judgment, not ideological alignment
- Addresses susceptibility — psychological, nutritional, spiritual — not only agents

This is a more demanding model than the complementarity thesis because it requires the physician to evaluate each healing system on its merits rather than accepting holistic approaches on the basis of philosophical sympathy. It aligns with what has since been formalised as integrative medicine — but Weil's 1983 version is more sceptical of alternative medicine's evidence base than most subsequent integrative medicine frameworks.

### Convergence and Divergence

| Dimension | Myss & Shealy | Weil |
|---|---|---|
| Core claim | Disease originates inside-out; biography shapes biology | Agents ≠ causes; susceptibility is primary |
| View of allopathic medicine | Incomplete but necessary; addresses the physical level | Good for emergencies and acute disease; philosophically deficient |
| View of holistic medicine | The missing heart of healthcare | Philosophically correct but practically incoherent in execution |
| Practical model | Integration of energy medicine with conventional diagnosis | Pragmatic case-by-case use of all available systems |
| Role of consciousness | Energy field and biographical patterns determine organ health | Mind-body unity is an empirical fact; belief can activate healing |

## Critique

**The complementarity thesis assumes equal validity**: By arguing that the two paradigms complement each other, Myss and Shealy risk obscuring the fact that one paradigm has a vastly larger evidence base than the other. The claim that holistic medicine is the "heart" of healthcare is not validated by the same kind of large-scale, blinded, reproducible evidence that validates many allopathic treatments.

**The explanatory gap is not proof**: Shealy is right that allopathic medicine cannot fully explain individual variation in disease susceptibility. But the fact that external-factor explanations are insufficient does not prove that biographical-emotional explanations are correct. The missing variable could be genetic, microbiological, or environmental in ways not yet understood.

**Alternative medicine's record**: The Lori case illustrates a genuine problem: holistic medicine's emphasis on the person's inner capacity for self-healing can, in individual cases, lead to avoidance of effective conventional treatment. The complementarity thesis needs to be operationalised — which cases call for medical intervention first, which for inner work first — rather than left as a general principle.
