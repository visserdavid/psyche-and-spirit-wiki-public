# World Navel

**Summary**: Joseph Campbell's term for the recurring mythological image of a fixed cosmic center — Buddha's Immovable Spot, the World Tree, the temple threshold, the Kaaba — through which "energies of eternity break into time"; the spatial anchor of the [hero myth](hero-myth.md)'s Departure and Return.

**Sources**:
- [Campbell, J. (1949). *The hero with a thousand faces*. Pantheon Books.](../sources/the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-20

---

## The Image

Across mythologies, Campbell observes, the site where the hero's adventure begins or culminates is marked as a **World Navel**: a point where the created world connects back to its inexhaustible source. Forms include the Buddha's Immovable Spot beneath the Bo Tree, the Norse World Tree Yggdrasil, the Hindu cosmic mountain Sumeru, the omphalos stones of Greek sanctuaries, the Kaaba at Mecca, and — at domestic scale — the hearth, altar, or temple threshold of ordinary religious architecture. Temples are typically built with portals at the four directions converging on a central shrine, so that entering a temple ritually re-enacts approaching the World Navel itself.

Campbell describes the World Navel as the point from which "grace, food, and energy" pour continuously into existence, alternately imaged as flowing water, falling light, or sacrificial substance (blood, milk, seminal fluid) — the same image appearing, in condensed form, in the [Ultimate Boon](hero-myth.md) stage of the monomyth, where the hero obtains an inexhaustible dish, elixir, or grail at the source.

## Ambivalence of the Center

The World Navel is morally ambivalent in Campbell's account: it "yields the world's plenitude of good and evil" alike, and the deities associated with it (Kali, the Hindu god Shiva, the trickster-divinity Edshu of Yoruba myth) are not straightforwardly benevolent. Campbell tells the Yoruba tale of Edshu — who walks between two farmers wearing a hat colored differently on each side, so that each sees only his own half and the friends nearly kill each other over the discrepancy — as an illustration that the center of the world, properly understood, contains and reconciles contradiction rather than resolving it into simple moral order. See [Trickster Archetype](trickster-archetype.md) for the wiki's fuller treatment of Edshu and related trickster figures.

## Relation to Axis Mundi and Rites of Passage

The World Navel functions in Campbell's schema much as the threshold does in [Arnold van Gennep](../thinkers/arnold-van-gennep.md)'s [rites of passage](rites-of-passage-schema.md): van Gennep documents in ethnographic detail how societies treat literal doorways, boundary stones, and territorial crossings as structurally identical to social transitions, arguing this spatial grounding is not merely metaphorical. Campbell's World Navel generalizes the same insight to cosmic rather than social geography — the physical center of a mythological world is treated as continuous with, and often identical to, the psychological center of transformation the hero must reach. Where van Gennep's threshold marks a passage *between* states, Campbell's World Navel is the fixed point *from which* all states are held to originate.

## Eliade's Axis Mundi in Shamanic Initiation

[Mircea Eliade](../thinkers/mircea-eliade.md) documents the same cosmic-center image at ritual scale in *[Rites and Symbols of Initiation](../sources/rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md)*: Siberian and Central Asian shamanic initiation rites center on climbing a birch pole or tree (the *axis mundi*) connecting earth, sky, and underworld, notched with steps representing the levels of heaven the shaman ascends to receive consecration from the gods. Where Campbell's World Navel anchors mythological narrative, Eliade's climbing pole is the same center enacted as physical ritual — the initiate does not read about the world's fixed point but bodily ascends it. See [Symbolic Death and Rebirth](symbolic-death-and-rebirth.md).
