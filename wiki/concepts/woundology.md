# Woundology

**Summary**: Myss's term for the cultural pattern of using personal wounds as social currency — defining oneself by one's traumas, wielding them to control relationships, and thereby choosing a wound-based identity over the work of genuine healing.

**Sources**:
- [Myss, C. (1996). *Anatomy of the spirit: The seven stages of power and healing*. Harmony Books.](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md)
- [Myss, C. (1997). *Why people don't heal and how they can*. Harmony Books.](../sources/why-people-dont-heal.md)

## Overview

Woundology is one of the most culturally pointed concepts in *Anatomy of the Spirit*. [Myss](../thinkers/caroline-myss.md) observed, across her clinical and teaching work, that the therapeutic culture of the late twentieth century had — with the best intentions — created a system in which personal trauma had become a primary currency of social identity, intimacy, and power.

The term captures the paradox: the language of healing had been appropriated to serve the opposite of healing — entrenching people in their wound-identities rather than empowering them to move beyond them.

## How Woundology Works

The pattern Myss describes:

1. **Wounds as introduction**: People increasingly define themselves by their wounds at the point of first social contact — naming their abuse history, addiction, diagnosis, or trauma as their primary identity marker, often within moments of meeting someone new.

2. **Wounds as intimacy bond**: "Wound-mating" occurs when two people bond around shared wounds — each agreeing to support the other's wound-story, carry the other's wounded responsibilities, and accept the other's limitations as excuses rooted in injury.

3. **Wounds as control**: Wounds are used to claim special status, set limits on others' behaviour, avoid accountability, and impose obligations — "You have to understand me because of what I've been through."

4. **Wounds as power**: The wounded person may hold considerable power in a relationship or group by virtue of their wound. Others modify their behaviour, absorb responsibilities, and suppress their own needs to accommodate the wounded person's history.

## The Illustrative Scene

Myss describes watching a woman named Mary decline a scheduling request by citing her commitment to an "incest survivors workshop" — and then, within twelve seconds of meeting two strangers at a restaurant, announcing that she was an incest survivor. When Myss later asked why Mary felt the need to communicate this history to strangers so quickly, Mary replied: "So they'd know who I was."

For Myss, this captures the essence of woundology: the wound has become the identity. And investing one's identity in one's wound means investing one's energy in *staying* wounded — because the wound is what makes one "someone."

## The Distinction Myss Draws

Woundology is not the same as:
- **Acknowledging wounds**: Naming and feeling the truth of what happened is necessary for healing.
- **Healing work**: Therapy, support groups, and grief work serve genuine healing when they move people *through* their wounds rather than entrenching them in wound identity.
- **Seeking support**: There is nothing wrong with asking for help or receiving compassion.

The problem is when the wound becomes a permanent address — a home one returns to rather than a place one passes through.

## The Energy Interpretation

In Myss's framework, woundology is an energy analysis: people who invest their identity in their wounds are sending their energy into the past — into the wound-story — rather than into the present, where healing and life are possible. This chronic investment of power in the past depletes the energy system and prevents the very recovery that is ostensibly being sought.

The woundologist's relationship to power is acquisitionary: they draw power from the wound (as a source of attention, compassion, and control) rather than generating power from within. See [Power and Healing](power-and-healing.md).

## Woundology as a Social Phenomenon (*Why People Don't Heal*)

In [*Why People Don't Heal*](../sources/why-people-dont-heal.md), Myss substantially develops the concept of woundology by giving it a cultural history and a social mechanics. Where *Anatomy of the Spirit* introduced the term almost in passing, *Why People Don't Heal* devotes an entire opening chapter to it.

Myss traces woundology to the 1960s–80s convergence of the therapeutic culture, the trauma-awareness movement, and the politics of group identity. This combination gave people both the language and the social incentive to identify publicly with their wounds. "Wounds became red badges of courage," she writes — markers of authenticity, suffering, and moral legitimacy in a culture that had finally decided to take emotional pain seriously.

The result: support groups, therapy circles, and healing workshops had generated a new form of social currency. The "deeper" one's wound, the more attention, compassion, and authority one commanded. Healing, paradoxically, threatened this currency.

Myss identifies several social mechanisms:
- **Wound bonding**: Intimacy established through the mutual disclosure of wounds — "sharing histories" as a shortcut to trust that bypasses the slower development of genuine relationship.
- **Wound contracts**: Implicit agreements in which friends or partners accept the other's wound-based limitations, moods, and demands as terms of the relationship.
- **The woundology economy**: Legal systems, social media, and therapeutic institutions that reward the declaration of harm — making it financially, socially, or emotionally more profitable to remain wounded than to heal.

She introduces the [Cellular Energy Bank](cellular-energy-bank.md) as the energetic explanation: sustaining woundology requires continuous investment of life force in the wound-story, which is therefore unavailable for healing.

The remedy she proposes is [Spirit Retrieval](spirit-retrieval.md) — calling energy back from the wounds without needing to erase or deny them.

## Critique

**The genuine insight**: Myss identified a real cultural phenomenon. The overlap between therapeutic culture and identity politics in the 1980s–90s did create conditions in which "survivor" identity could become primary and self-perpetuating. Subsequent cultural theorists have noted similar patterns — the use of trauma narratives as social capital.

**The risk of the concept**: Applied carelessly, "woundology" can become a tool to dismiss legitimate suffering. Telling a person in genuine pain that they are "using their wounds as currency" risks re-traumatising them and reinforcing the cultural message that they should not ask for too much help.

**The timing question**: Myss's framework does not clearly specify when wound-processing becomes woundology. Extended grief, complex PTSD, and deeply entrenched trauma may require years of work. The line between necessary processing and wound-based identity is not always clear.

**The social/structural dimension**: Some critics would argue that what Myss calls woundology is partly a rational response to social conditions in which wounds *are* the only available leverage — in contexts of systemic injustice, the wound may be legitimately the only card in one's hand.
