# A New Science of Life

**Summary**: Rupert Sheldrake's 1981 hypothesis that biological form and behaviour are organised not solely by genes and known physical laws but by non-local "morphic fields," built up and reinforced across time by a process he calls morphic resonance — a deliberately testable alternative to mechanistic biology that provoked one of the most public controversies in recent science publishing.

## Overview

Rupert Sheldrake trained as a biochemist and cell biologist at Cambridge (Clare College; Frank Knox Fellow, Harvard; Fellow of Clare College; Rosenheim Research Fellow of the Royal Society) before spending the early 1970s at the International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics in Hyderabad, India, where he began to doubt that mechanistic, gene-centred biology could ever fully explain how living form comes into being. *A New Science of Life* is his attempt to sketch a testable alternative.

The book opens by surveying biology's unsolved problems: **morphogenesis** (how a fertilised egg reliably becomes a particular species-typical form), **regulation and regeneration** (how developing or damaged systems reach the same end-state by different routes — Driesch's classic sea-urchin embryo experiments), **instinct and learning**, and **evolution** itself. Sheldrake argues that the conventional appeal to "genetic programmes" smuggles in exactly the teleological, goal-directed language it claims to have eliminated — DNA specifies proteins, not shape, and identical DNA gives rise to radically different tissues (a human arm and leg share the same cell types and genome, yet take different forms).

Sheldrake's proposed alternative is **[morphic resonance](../concepts/morphic-resonance.md)**: the hypothesis that a system's form is not fixed by timeless physical law alone but is influenced, non-energetically and without attenuation over distance, by the cumulative resonance of all previous similar systems. A newly synthesised chemical crystallises with difficulty the first time and progressively more easily thereafter, he argues, not only because of physical seed contamination but because crystallisation itself, once it has happened, makes the same form more probable elsewhere. Applied to biology, morphic resonance is offered as a unified account of inheritance, instinct, habit formation, and — most provocatively — a mechanism by which a behaviour learned by one population of animals could make the same behaviour progressively easier for genetically similar, but never-contacted, populations to learn (Sheldrake's central piece of evidence is [William McDougall's 32-generation Harvard rat-learning experiment](../concepts/morphic-resonance.md#the-mcdougall-rat-experiments)).

The book closes by explicitly separating science from metaphysics: Sheldrake argues that once the hypothesis of formative causation is granted, at least four different metaphysical positions remain equally compatible with it (modified materialism, an efficacious conscious self, an immanent creative universe, and a transcendent creative source) — and that choosing among them is a matter for philosophy and religion, not biology.

## The Book-Burning Controversy

*A New Science of Life* triggered an unusually public scientific controversy. Following a largely sympathetic *New Scientist* feature (18 June 1981) and generally favourable newspaper reviews (*Sunday Times*, *Guardian*), the journal *Nature* published an anonymous editorial by editor John Maddox on 24 September 1981 titled "A book for burning?" — arguing that Sheldrake's hypothesis was untestable pseudoscience dressed in the language of orthodoxy, and naming the book "the best candidate for burning many years." The editorial provoked a lengthy correspondence in *Nature*'s letters pages (including a strong defence from physicist Brian Josephson) and a BBC Radio 4 debate between Maddox and Sheldrake. See [Morphic Resonance § Reception and Critique](../concepts/morphic-resonance.md#reception-and-critique) for the substance of the exchange.

## Key Takeaways

- Biological morphogenesis, regeneration, instinct, and evolution remain, in Sheldrake's account, genuinely unsolved by mechanistic biology, whatever its successes in molecular genetics.
- **Morphogenetic fields** are proposed as real, if non-energetic, causal structures — a "formative causation" analogous to but distinct from the four recognised physical forces.
- **Morphic resonance** is the proposed mechanism: form and behaviour are shaped by resonance with all relevantly similar past systems, unattenuated by distance, with effects that should in principle be experimentally detectable.
- The hypothesis is explicitly offered as scientific and falsifiable — Sheldrake proposes specific experiments (crystallisation timing, cross-population rat learning) — a point central to the dispute over whether it deserves to be taken seriously as science at all.
- The book carefully brackets metaphysics: it argues formative causation is compatible with several different final worldviews and does not itself adjudicate between them.
