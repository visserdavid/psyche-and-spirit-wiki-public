# Archetypes: Who Are You?

**Summary**: Myss's 2013 popular guide introducing ten contemporary archetypes — Advocate, Artist/Creative, Athlete, Caregiver, Fashionista, Intellectual, Queen/Executive, Rebel, Spiritual Seeker/Mystic, and Visionary — written with Cristina Carlino to bring the archetypal framework to a mass audience via a companion quiz website.

## Overview and Context

*Archetypes: Who Are You?* is Myss's most accessible and explicitly popular book, written at the invitation of Cristina Carlino, founder of the digital platform ArchetypeMe.com. Carlino had a formative personal experience: during a corporate crisis that stripped her of her identity as CEO, she reorganised her understanding of herself through the archetype lens — recognising she had spent years living as a Caregiver and Queen/Executive rather than her true Visionary archetype. This insight led her to found ArchetypeMe.com and recruit Myss to write the companion book.

Myss frames the book as a natural continuation of twenty-five years of teaching: she argues that the language of archetypes has moved from the realm of psychology (Jung, *Sacred Contracts*) into contemporary social discourse, where people instinctively recognise archetypal patterns without knowing the theoretical framework. The book aims to give that instinct a vocabulary.

The book's central metaphor is the [Inner Net](../concepts/inner-net.md): the unconscious web of archetypal power patterns through which every choice, thought, and relational impulse is transmitted. This is Myss's update of Jung's collective unconscious for the internet age — archetypes as the original interconnected network.

## The Ten Archetypes

The book profiles ten archetypes identified as the most relevant to contemporary women (though Myss stresses applicability to men). Each archetype is the representative of a larger **archetype family** — a cluster sharing the same power theme. The structure of each chapter is uniform:

- **Life Journey**: the core drive animating this archetype
- **Unique Challenge**: the central tension or difficulty the archetype faces
- **Universal Lesson**: the soul-level teaching carried by this archetype's experience
- **Defining Grace**: the specific spiritual virtue empowering the archetype's highest expression
- **Inner Shadow**: the fear-driven expression that blocks fulfilment
- **Male Counterpart**: how the archetype expresses in men
- **Myths**: the cultural stories and beliefs associated with this archetype
- **Lifestyle Challenge**: how the archetype shapes daily habits and life choices
- **Step into Your Archetype**: practical exercises

For the full profile of each archetype, see [Ten Contemporary Archetypes](../concepts/ten-contemporary-archetypes.md). For the defining graces specifically, see [Archetypal Grace](../concepts/archetypal-grace.md).

## Key Themes and Arguments

### The Inner Net
Myss introduces the Inner Net as the central metaphor of the book: archetypes form an interconnected power grid in the unconscious, through which every thought, feeling, and choice transmits energy. The archetype is like a computer program — a set of myths and a reservoir of symbols — that organises and energises life. All of life is a power negotiation across this grid: "Speak or stay silent? Act or wait? Trust intuition?" Every decision is a power calculation. See [Inner Net](../concepts/inner-net.md).

### Archetype Families
Each of the ten archetypes represents a family of related patterns. The Caregiver, for example, encompasses the Mother, Nurturer, Rescuer, Teacher, and Companion — all share the power theme of nurturing and responding to need. The Rebel family includes the Maverick, Warrior, and Hero. Recognising the family gives more precision than the representative name alone.

### Sacred Contracts as Foundation
The archetypal patterns profiled here are governed by [Sacred Contracts](../concepts/sacred-contracts.md) — the pre-birth soul agreements that assign specific archetypal companions to each lifetime. The 2013 book treats these ten archetypes as the contemporary expression of that deeper system, now made accessible for self-identification and daily use.

### Biography, Myth, and Illness
Myss returns to her core [biography-is-biology](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md) thesis through the device of myth-crisis: illness is often the shattering of an outgrown myth. A woman with colon cancer had organised her life around the myth of "living by the sea with my lawyer husband and two children." When that myth proved hollow, she swallowed the lie for years — until the cancer forced the truth. Healing required not medical treatment alone but the creation of a new myth. This case study, recounted in the Introduction, crystallises the book's therapeutic logic: an archetype's myths, when they become cages rather than maps, generate illness.

### The Wounded Healer Interlude
The book introduces the Wounded Healer as a bridge archetype — not one of the ten core profiles but a significant figure. The Wounded Healer's journey begins with a wound (accident, illness, loss) that destroys the illusion of invincibility and forces a paradigm shift: from power as status/security to healing as partnership with the spirit. This produces recoveries from "unspeakable" situations and the capacity to assist others. Myss uses this figure to anchor the idea that not all archetypes serve healing initiation — only specific ones are wound-initiated.

### The Rebel's Four Expressions
The Rebel archetype receives the most differentiated treatment in the book. Myss identifies four distinct expressions:
1. **Noble Rebel**: Rebelling against tyranny and injustice at great personal cost — Gandhi, King, Mandela, the American Founding Fathers.
2. **Anarchist Rebel**: Symbolically breaking through when all other channels are closed — Lech Wałęsa, Mohamed Bouazizi; the Arab Spring.
3. **Social/Civil Rebel**: Organised protest for systemic change — suffragettes, Vietnam War protestors, Occupy Wall Street.
4. **Feminist Rebel**: Rebelling through life choices rather than street action; three waves of feminist movement; today's expression through individual self-determination.

### The Spiritual Seeker's Poverty Myth
A central section of the Spiritual Seeker chapter challenges the cultural myth that spirituality requires poverty, celibacy, or social isolation. Myss traces this to centuries of religious tradition dividing heaven and earth, and argues the Spiritual Seeker archetype is moving into the world — not away from it. The archetype's lifestyle challenge is awakening intuition as intelligence, not mystical power: "intuition monitors behaviour and health responses, alerting you to bring balance."

### The Visionary's Dark Shadow
The Visionary chapter introduces the Dark Visionary — the shadow expression of the archetype's capacity for seeing possibilities. Where the light Visionary envisions constructive futures, the Dark Visionary becomes fixated on catastrophe (Nostradamus, zombie/apocalypse culture, psychics feeding on clients' fears). The dark shadow is triggered when imagination falls into fear-based cycles, projecting failure onto every initiative.

## Relationship to Earlier Myss Works

This book is the least theoretically dense of Myss's major works and the most practically oriented. Its relationship to the earlier corpus:

- **Sacred Contracts (2001)**: The Gallery of Archetypes in *Sacred Contracts* contains 70+ archetypes; *Archetypes: Who Are You?* selects ten contemporary representatives and develops them in depth. The two-book system is complementary: *Sacred Contracts* provides the philosophical foundation; *Archetypes* provides the popular application.
- **Anatomy of the Spirit (1996)**: The myth-crisis thesis and biography-is-biology are restated in accessible form.
- **Why People Don't Heal (1997)**: The woundology concept is implicit throughout — especially in the Caregiver's shadow (losing self) and the Fashionista's Ugly Duckling syndrome.

## Critique

**Empirical basis**: The ten archetypes are selected through cultural observation rather than systematic research. No controlled study validates the archetype assignments or the defining graces. The ArchetypeMe.com quiz is a self-report instrument without psychometric validation.

**Gender framing**: The book is explicitly addressed to women and frames the ten archetypes primarily through female experience. While Male Counterpart sections are included, the book's framing reflects a gendered understanding of archetypal expression that may not translate universally.

**Popular simplification**: The rich complexity of Jungian archetypal theory — as presented in *The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious* (CW 9.1) — is compressed into self-help chapters. The chapter structure (Life Journey, Unique Challenge, etc.) imposes a uniform template that can oversimplify the variability within each archetype.

**Positive contribution**: Despite these limits, the book succeeds in making the archetypal framework accessible to readers who would never engage with Jung or *Sacred Contracts*. The Inner Net metaphor is genuinely creative — it translates the collective unconscious into a contemporary idiom. The Rebel taxonomy (four expressions) is one of the most analytically useful passages in the Myss corpus.
