# Embodying Experience: Forming a Personal Life

**Summary**: Stanley Keleman's 1987 companion volume to *Emotional Anatomy*, presenting the practical five-step HOw methodology for working with somatic organization — moving from the theory of how life makes shapes to the practice of how shapes can be consciously reorganized.

## Overview

*Embodying Experience* is the explicit companion to *Emotional Anatomy* (1985). Where *Emotional Anatomy* presents the theory — pulsation as the ground of life, the three embryonic layers, the four somatic distress patterns, insults to form — *Embodying Experience* presents the methodology: a five-step process Keleman calls the **HOw process** for engaging, intensifying, disrupting, and reorganizing one's somatic patterns.

The book's central argument is that insight alone does not produce change. Understanding that one has a rigid structure, or that one's chronic neck tension originates in childhood demand for performance, does not dissolve the pattern. What produces change is learning to work directly with the muscle contractions, pulsatory patterns, and somatic images that organize one's existence — through incremental intensification, deliberate disorganization, and the formation of new responses.

The book is structured as a practical manual, with self-reflection exercises throughout, illustrated case studies (Morris, Jim, Sarah, Joel, Louise, Betty, Angela, Ellen, Larry, Mary), and a sequence of chapters moving from the organizing process through daily life, inner life, somagrams, and the formative journey.

## The HOw Process: Five Steps

The five-step HOw process is the book's central contribution. "HOw" refers to the fundamental question underlying somatic self-inquiry: *how does one organize oneself?* The five steps address this question practically.

**Step One — What is my image/situation/structure?**
Identify the current somatic image: the posture, gesture, stance, or story that organizes the present moment or the situation in question. This is surface-level recognition — what the body is doing right now. The question is not "why do I do this?" but "what am I doing, somatically?"

**Step Two — How do I create/maintain this image?**
Identify the specific muscle contractions, postural configurations, and pulsatory patterns that generate and sustain the image. Intensify the pattern deliberately. The purpose is not catharsis but perception: by voluntarily heightening the contraction (squeezing, stiffening, holding), one gains clear knowledge of the body's organizational strategy — the muscles involved, the layers engaged, the quality of feeling produced.

**Step Three — How do I stop doing this? (Disorganization)**
This is the accordion step. Having intensified the pattern in Step Two, now incrementally release it — not fully let go, but loosen a little at a time. This disorganization of the habitual pattern is the critical move: it interrupts the automatic perpetuation of the structure and creates a gap in which new organization can emerge. Keleman specifies that one should not relax immediately from the beginning; one must first intensify (Step Two), then let go incrementally (Step Three).

**Step Four — What happens when I stop? (Incubation)**
Remain in the disorganized state — the pause, the silence, the somatic hiatus. This is the incubation stage: a state neither of action nor of waiting-for-action, but of receptive inner attention. At this stage, suppressed impulses, memories, feelings, and somatic images emerge — not forced but allowed. Keleman distinguishes this from insight (cognitive) or catharsis (emotional release): it is somatic gestation, the organism reorganizing itself from the inside.

**Step Five — What do I do with what I have learned?**
Form a new response and practice it. The emergence from Step Four must be translated into action — not by reverting to the old pattern, but by consciously shaping a new somatic response from the material Step Four has made available. This step requires rehearsal and ongoing practice. The pattern does not change in one session; it reforms through repetition.

## The Accordion Metaphor

Keleman develops the accordion as the primary metaphor for the organizing process. The accordion builds pressure, releases it, and builds again — it is not a machine that holds tension but an instrument that forms language through the alternation of compression and elongation. The human body works the same way: health is not low tension but the full range of the continuum, from full expansion to full contraction and back. The HOw process, especially Steps Two and Three, mimics and engages this accordion action.

## Inhibition as Core Competency

A counterintuitive insight central to the book: spontaneity, creativity, and growth depend on the capacity for **inhibition**. The brain is not a switching device but an organ of delay — its most sophisticated function is *stopping*, slowing, and redirecting impulses. Inhibition is not suppression; it is the somatic intelligence that prevents one impulse from running its automatic course so that a wider range of responses becomes available.

The four steps of the HOw process before Step Five are essentially an education in inhibition: by stopping the automatic pattern (Step Three), sustaining the pause (Step Four), and choosing a response (Step Five), the practitioner develops the somatic capacity to interrupt habitual organization and make it conscious.

## Three Layers of Existence

Keleman describes existence as organized in three layers:

- **Prepersonal layer**: the instinctual, animal, inherited dimension — appetites, drives, gender, the body's organic processes. This is given; it cannot be constructed or chosen.
- **Personal/societal layer**: the dimension of training, socialization, role, and relational pattern. This is the cultural and relational overlay that society stamps onto the prepersonal.
- **Individuating layer**: the dimension that makes the prepersonal and personal specifically one's own — the layer of individual organization that emerges from the dialogue between instinct and society. This is the layer that the HOw process most directly engages: the task of therapy and somatic education is to personalize one's prepersonal and social inheritance rather than being possessed by either.

## Motor Memory

One of the book's significant insights is that memory is not primarily cognitive (a recollection of images and stories) but somatic (a re-enactment of muscle patterns). Every experience is stored as a configuration of muscular contraction and pulsatory pattern. To recall an event fully is to re-enact the muscle patterns associated with it. This is why talking about memories may not produce change — the muscle patterns that organized the experience persist independently of the story.

The HOw process works with motor memory directly: by intensifying and then disorganizing the muscular patterns, it permits the historical organization encoded in those patterns to emerge and reform.

## Somagrams

*Embodying Experience* introduces **somagrams**: drawings made by clients of their somatic image — their experienced body-form, including areas of tension, constriction, deadening, impulse, and pattern. Somagrams are not realistic anatomical drawings but somatic projections — attempts to represent the felt interior of one's organization.

## Case Studies

The book includes ten extended case studies illustrating the HOw process in practice:

- **Morris** (conflict avoidance at work): uses Steps to disorganize the defensive bracing of a man who learned to freeze and wait as his primary organizational stance
- **Jim Doer** (performance compulsion): a "doer" identity organized around a stiff spine and compressed belly; learns to restructure the holding pattern
- **Sarah** (computer operator, over-extension): discovers how the posture of reaching extends through the torso in compressive arcs; learns to keep the pulsatory range open during work
- **Joel** (relational containment): somagrams reveal wildness contained by lines at shoulders and throat — the daimon of intimacy held back by the enforcer
- **Louise** (back as board, reaching from behind): the split between the reaching-front and the defended-back
- **Betty** (daughter/son confusion): three somagrams track the daughter-to-father, son-to-father, and woman-to-father layers of a gendered identity conflict
- **Angela** (bracing for contact-approval): overbound, rigid structure; learns to distinguish between the protective contraction and the longing beneath it
- **Ellen** (formlessness and exploitation): underbound, swollen structure; the task is building form rather than releasing it
- **Larry** (collapsed chest, fear of rejection): the collapse and the spasm mask each other; Steps Two and Three gradually differentiate the inhibiting layers
- **Mary** (adultified child/performer): the lifted chest and the pelvis-held-up organize a sexual-object image; Steps Three and Four permit the child underneath to surface

## Relation to *Emotional Anatomy*

The two books form an integrated system:
- *Emotional Anatomy* establishes the **theoretical framework**: pulsation, somatic layering, insults to form, the four distress patterns, and the atlas of somatic-emotional experience.
- *Embodying Experience* presents the **practical methodology**: the HOw process, the accordion technique, somagrams, and case-level application of the theory.

Together they constitute Keleman's complete account of somatic education. A practitioner reading only *Emotional Anatomy* would know what to look for; reading only *Embodying Experience* would know how to work with it.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths**: The HOw process is one of the more explicitly teachable somatic methodologies. The five-step structure gives practitioners a repeatable framework. The emphasis on incremental intensification (rather than immediate release) is clinically sophisticated and distinguishes Keleman from cathartic approaches. The case studies are detailed and illuminating.

**Limitations**: The book is written in a compressed, somewhat telegraphic style (a consequence of the PDF compression is especially evident here) that makes it challenging to follow without familiarity with Keleman's earlier work. The methodology is described at a high level of abstraction; a practitioner would need additional training to apply it competently. There is no systematic outcome data.

**Mainstream critique**: Like *Emotional Anatomy*, the framework operates in a clinical-experiential register that mainstream psychology cannot easily evaluate. The claims about motor memory and somatic reorganization are consistent with contemporary research in embodied cognition and trauma therapy (notably Levine's Somatic Experiencing), but the specific Keleman methodology has not been the subject of controlled trials.
