# Emotional Anatomy: The Structure of Experience

**Summary**: Stanley Keleman's 1985 illustrated study of how life experience, stress, and emotion shape the physical form of the body — arguing that "life makes shapes" and that the body's structure is the visible record of biographical history.

**Sources**:
- [Keleman, S. (1985). *Emotional anatomy: The structure of experience*. Center Press.](emotional-anatomy.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

---

## Overview

*Emotional Anatomy* is a foundational text of somatic psychology. Through 126 drawings and accompanying text, Stanley Keleman argues that the body is not a fixed biological container but a living, pulsating, continuously shaped process — and that the shapes a person's body assumes over a lifetime are the direct record of their emotional history, relationships, and biographical stresses.

The book's central claim: **"Life makes shapes."** The shapes are part of an organizing process that embodies emotions, thoughts, and experiences in structure. Structure in turn orders the events of existence.

## Key Themes

### The Body as a Pulsating Pump

Keleman begins with embryology. Life organizes itself as a series of tubes, layers, and pouches — from the first cell through the layered adult organism. The fundamental biological act is [pulsation](../concepts/pulsation.md): the rhythmic expansion and contraction of cells, tissues, and organs that generates the sense of aliveness, self-identity, and the capacity for feeling.

The organism is described as "a pulsating pump" — a series of interconnected pouches (head, chest, abdomen, pelvis) and tubes organized around gravity, maintained by wave-like peristaltic movement.

### The Three Layers

The body develops from three embryonic layers, each associated with a different quality of experience:

- **Ectoderm** (skin, nerves, brain): the communication and boundary layer — touch, sensation, self-identity at the surface.
- **Mesoderm** (muscle, bone, cartilage): the support and action layer — structure, posture, movement, assertion.
- **Endoderm** (viscera, organs): the metabolic and motility layer — digestion, rhythm, deep pulsation, the "ocean" of internal life.

Feeling and emotion are not located in any single layer but arise from the interplay of all three.

### Insults to Form

Life inevitably brings insults — stresses, shocks, emotional injuries, and developmental challenges that alter the body's pulsatory patterns. Keleman identifies two primary stress reflexes:

- **Overbound responses**: the organism braces, stiffens, and hardens as defense — expansion is interrupted, the form becomes rigid and compressed.
- **Underbound responses**: the organism yields, swells, or collapses — containment fails, boundaries dissolve, pulsation becomes meandering or extinguished.

These reflexes, when chronic, become structural. The body "learns" its defensive shape and maintains it beyond the original insult. See [Insults to Form](../concepts/insults-to-form.md) and [Somatic Distress Patterns](../concepts/somatic-distress-patterns.md).

### The Four Somatic Distress Patterns

Chronic stress responses crystallize into four recognizable body shapes, each with characteristic posture, psychological style, emotional qualities, and relational patterns:

| Pattern | Stance | Orientation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| **Rigid** | Pulled back (withholder) or pulled up (dominator) | Overbound; braced against collapse or invasion |
| **Dense/Compact** | Squeezed, compacted, pulled inward | Overbound; pressed against abandonment |
| **Swollen** | Inflated, porous, pear-shaped | Underbound; boundaries dissolved outward |
| **Collapsed** | Sunken, resigned, caved | Underbound; pulsation extinguished inward |

Each pattern represents not a pathology but a biography — the body's solution to the particular insults and relational dynamics of a person's history.

### Somatic Reality and Education

No person is purely one type. Real bodies are combinations of patterns across layers and pouches. Education (Keleman's term for therapeutic change) works not by relaxation or ideal posturing but by reorganizing pulsatory patterns — restoring the range of expansion and contraction that chronic stress has narrowed.

### Somatic Interactions

The final chapter describes how body shapes interact with one another in relationship. Anatomy is the foundation of intimacy: contact between persons is contact between layers — surface touching surface, pulsation encountering pulsation. Love and intimacy require the ability to allow one's layers to be affected by another.

## Connection to Myss and Shealy

Keleman's "life makes shapes" parallels Myss's ["biography is biology"](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md) with striking precision — but from a secular, somatic-developmental rather than spiritual-energetic direction. Where Myss locates biographical encoding in the energy field and chakras, Keleman locates it in the body's physical layers, tubes, and pulsatory patterns. Both frameworks hold that:

1. The body carries the record of lived experience.
2. Stress and emotional injury alter biological structure.
3. Healing requires addressing the level at which the encoding occurred.

The key divergence: Keleman makes no claims about energy fields, spiritual dimensions, or soul-level purpose. His framework is phenomenological and developmental — grounded in embryology, anatomy, and clinical observation rather than spiritual intuition.
