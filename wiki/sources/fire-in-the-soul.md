# Fire in the Soul: A New Psychology of Spiritual Optimism

**Summary**: Borysenko's third major book (1993) extends her psychospiritual project from the individual (guilt, inner child) to the cosmic: why do bad things happen, how is crisis initiation rather than punishment, and what practices — centering prayer, forgiveness, courage, compassionate action — allow suffering to become the fire that refines rather than destroys the soul.

## Overview

*Fire in the Soul* (1993) is [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s third major book and marks a significant shift in her project. Where *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* (1987) provided clinical techniques for stress-related illness, and *Guilt Is the Teacher, Love Is the Lesson* (1990) traced chronic suffering to the shame-based personality and the broken inner child, *Fire in the Soul* addresses the most fundamental question of all: **Why do bad things happen to good people?**

The book argues that what appears as catastrophe — illness, loss, trauma, addiction, depression — is more precisely understood as *initiation*: the soul's passage through darkness toward a deepened wisdom, compassion, and connection to love. The metaphor is not accidental. In Borysenko's reading, the phoenix's fire is not destruction; it is the process of transformation. The book's task is to supply the framework, the research, and the practices that allow people in the midst of their own fires to understand what is happening and to find the courage to emerge.

## Part One: Basic Beliefs

### The Central Question and Two Archetypes

The book opens through two patients from Borysenko's Mind/Body Clinic:

**Leslie** — a woman in her forties who lost her husband to stroke, raised two daughters alone, found a lump in her breast after working her way back up. Her response: "I don't know why these things happen. I don't believe in theories." Her faith is love-based: life's challenges are part of growth; her intent is to seek health, love relationships, and stay present. She does not explain why. She heals forward.

**Jay** — a New York artist in his thirties, diagnosed with AIDS. Raised Southern Baptist; in crisis he regressed to childhood belief: AIDS as punishment for homosexuality. He adopted the opposite tack: the New Age "I create my reality" doctrine — believing he could affirmatively think his way to cure. When this failed to cure him, the framework became a second level of self-blame. Borysenko's critique: the "you create your reality" doctrine is spiritual pessimism in optimistic clothing — it converts illness into evidence of mental failure.

### Job's Story: The Mitchell Translation

Borysenko reads the Book of Job through Stephen Mitchell's translation, which dramatically reframes the conventional interpretation. In the King James version, Job grovels before God's power; in Mitchell's translation, the Hebrew verb traditionally rendered "despise and repent" is more accurately translated as "I reject [old ideas] and take comfort [in mortality]." Job does not submit to power; he reaches a direct encounter with divine reality that dissolves his prior framework for understanding suffering.

Borysenko's Job teaches: suffering is not punishment. It is the process by which the ego's safe explanations are burned away until the person can see God's face — which, paradoxically, is the face of love. Mat's father understood this in giving his son's memorial book the title *Love Is the Answer*.

### Fowler's Stages of Faith

[James Fowler](../thinkers/james-fowler.md)'s model of faith development (six stages) provides Borysenko's developmental framework for understanding why some people — like the Texan Christian counsellor she describes — cannot receive information that challenges their current framework:

- **Stage 1 (Intuitive-Projective)**: images of God as powerful protector vs. terrifying destroyer
- **Stage 2 (Mythic-Literal)**: fairness and retribution; story taken literally; Jay and the counsellor operate here
- **Stage 3 (Synthetic-Conventional)**: ideology accepted without examination; identity invested in group norms
- **Stage 4 (Individuative-Reflective)**: responsibility for own beliefs; disillusionment as catalyst
- **Stage 5 (Conjunctive)**: lives with paradox; integrates contradictions; Leslie operates here
- **Stage 6 (Universalising)**: Gandhi, King, Mother Teresa, Merton — selfless commitment to human dignity

Tragedy and loss can catalyse movement between stages; therapists, pastors, and counsellors operating from Stage 2 can actively block patients' healing if they cannot hold the Stage 5 paradox.

## Part Two: Transformation of Fear

### Crisis as Initiation

Borysenko draws on Victor Turner's anthropological research on liminal rites of passage to reframe personal crises as initiations rather than pathologies. In Turner's model, rites of passage in all cultures involve three phases: separation from one's previous role; liminality (dwelling between two worlds, the "mwadi" who is neither what they were nor what they will become); reincorporation with a new identity.

Borysenko's childhood neurosis — severe OCD at age ten, with ritualised hand-washing and obsessive ordering — becomes, in retrospect, a classic night sea journey: the mwadi experience that preceded her emergence as a healer. The poem she wrote during the worst of it ("Light / Somewhere in the night a light shines...") was a communication from the Self — the stable loving core that Jung, Maslow, and Assagioli all describe.

Viktor Frankl's logotherapy: in Nazi death camps, the people who survived were those who found or maintained a sense of meaning in their suffering. Not meaning that justified the suffering — but meaning that the self could orient around, creating a "why" sufficient to sustain the "how." The initiation frame does exactly this: it converts the dark night from obstacle into threshold.

Stanislav Grof's LSD research identified four stages of the birth/rebirth process that appear in controlled therapeutic settings and that parallel the initiation structure:
1. **Cosmic Engulfment** — the onset, characterised by overwhelming anxiety and imagery of being swallowed
2. **No Exit** — claustrophobic, corresponding to labour contractions; hell-states; hopelessness
3. **Death-Rebirth Struggle** — the birth canal; cataclysmic imagery; agony and ecstasy coexist; hope appears
4. **Death and Rebirth** — ego annihilation, blinding light, liberation, cleansing

[Ka-Tzetnik 135633](../thinkers/ka-tzetnik.md) (Yehiel De Nur, Auschwitz survivor) underwent five sessions of LSD therapy with Professor Bastiaans in Leiden, re-experiencing Auschwitz trauma at cellular depth and emerging from a vision of fire and phoenix — the salamander that lives in fire for seven years and resurrects — as a transformed witness rather than a traumatised prisoner. Borysenko reads this as an extreme case of the initiation pattern.

### Healing Fear across Space and Time

Borysenko describes several personal encounters with "cosmic agents" — people who appeared with uncanny timing and information — as evidence that help from beyond the ordinary is available when asked for. The ticket agent who divined her struggles; Magaly Rodriguez Mossman's imagery session that uncovered a past-life pattern in Egypt; Elizabeth Lawrence's Mari El healing sessions that cleared samskaras from the body's energy field.

Henry Corbin's concept of the "imaginal realm" (not imaginary but a genuine level of reality between the physical and the purely spiritual) provides the theoretical framework for understanding where past-life memories, NDE experiences, and shamanic journeys occur. In this realm, there is neither time nor space; the "memories" retrieved in past-life regression are not necessarily historical facts but are real at the level of the soul's experience and can carry genuine healing.

Brian Weiss's *Many Lives, Many Masters* and Roger Woolger's *Other Lives, Other Selves* provide clinical documentation of past-life regression work. Ian Stevenson's research (University of Virginia, from 1960s) provides the most systematic empirical evidence: children across cultures who recall details of previous lives, verified against historical records.

### Forgiveness: The Aramaic Depth

*Fire in the Soul* deepens the forgiveness teaching from *Guilt Is the Teacher* by recovering the Aramaic dimension of Jesus's teaching. Neil Douglas-Klotz's *Prayers of the Cosmos* translates the Lord's Prayer from Jesus's original Aramaic, revealing depths obscured by Greek-to-English translation. The petition "Forgive us our debts as we forgive our debtors" becomes, in Aramaic, *washboqlan*: "Loose the cords of mistakes binding us, releasing the strands we hold of others' guilt" — an image of untying rather than absolution.

The karma-ending meditation at Yom Kippur: Borysenko describes being guided in meditation to the City of Lights — a realm she encountered years earlier — and shown a computer screen displaying her fear/hurt pattern across lifetimes. The meditation became a moment of resolution: forgiving the actors in her theatre of souls, releasing the pattern.

The Circle of Women healing circle provides the communal container in which forgiveness becomes possible. The Robin Casarjian weekend ("Deep Healing: Mending the Wound of Unworthiness") and the dream that night — "Karma Ending" — where creatures representing unresolved energy patterns ceased attacking once recognised — illustrate forgiveness operating at the level of collective karma, not just personal grievance.

Anger as self-imprisonment: Borysenko demonstrates this with teenagers in a Boston school — including gang members. A kinesiology demonstration shows that when a person accesses the feeling of anger, their arm strength collapses under two fingers of pressure; when they access a loving memory, strength returns. The steel of anger is revealed as weakness; the fire of love as strength.

### Three Stages of Courage

**Stage 1 — Willful courage**: White-knuckling through fear; the ten-year-old Borysenko willing herself back to school through OCD; the college junior confronting the snake nightmare through willpower alone. Necessary but insufficient; it maintains functioning while preventing healing.

**Stage 2 — Psychological courage**: Facing and healing the past. Sam's case: a man in his forties with asthma and bronchitis linked to his father's accidental death when Sam was eight. Sam blamed himself, became the "man of the house," suppressed joy and spontaneity, and developed asthma whose attacks were triggered by situations where he felt he had disappointed people. Healing required mourning the childhood loss, experiencing the grief and anger, understanding the situation from an adult perspective, and forgiving those involved. Six months later his demeanour had changed — the chronic tension gone, he looked ten years younger.

**Stage 3 — Spiritual courage**: Seeing with the eyes of the heart. Borysenko's exchange with her dying mother (March 1988): receiving the gift of courage, giving the gift of compassion. The vision of her mother as a woman giving birth — her mother's soul being born through death. The dream of the School of Spies that followed, where she accepted being a student of courage. The recognition that true courage flows not from will (Stage 1) or psychological clearing (Stage 2) but from a shift in perception: seeing life from the perspective of the Higher Self, where fear loses its ultimate claim.

Lucid dreaming as a metaphor: Kenneth Kelzer's *The Sun and the Shadow* teaches that in a lucid dream, the moment you recognise you are dreaming, the threat dissolves — not because the content changed but because your relationship to it changed. Tibetan dream yoga cultivates lucidity as preparation for the Bardo states after death. The courage of Stage 3 is this: knowing that one is the river, not the boats (thoughts/fears) that float on it.

## Part Three: Practices of the Awakening Sacred

### Centering Prayer

Thomas Keating's centering prayer is the contemplative practice at the heart of Part Three. Unlike mantra-based meditation (where a word or phrase is repeatedly returned to), centering prayer uses a "sacred word" not as repetition but as *pointer* — a reminder of the meditator's intent to dwell in God's presence. When thoughts arise, the word is gently reintroduced — not to block thoughts but to express the intent to release them and return to silence.

Keating's "river of presence" metaphor: consciousness is the river; thoughts are boats. In ordinary thinking, one's attention is on the boats, and the river is invisible. In centering prayer, the practitioner learns to identify with the river — the presence that underlies all thought — rather than with any particular boat.

This is a significant departure from Benson's relaxation response model (which uses a mantra repeated on the out-breath to elicit physiological calm). Centering prayer aims not at physiological relaxation but at *communion with the divine presence* — though the physiological effects of calm attention are a natural accompaniment. The difference is the *intent*: one is therapeutic, the other is contemplative.

Borysenko integrates both: the relaxation response clears the physiological noise that makes contemplation difficult; centering prayer opens the deeper silence in which the divine can be received.

### Prayer: Evidence and Practice

Randolph Byrd's double-blind CCU study (published 1988, *Southern Medical Journal*): 400 patients admitted with heart attacks were randomly assigned to receive distant intercessory prayer (from prayer groups) or to serve as controls. Neither patients nor staff knew who was in which group. The prayed-for group required fewer ventilators and respirators, had less pulmonary oedema, and needed fewer antibiotics.

Spindrift Foundation's research: non-directed prayer (holding a person in light, praying "Thy will be done" rather than specifying an outcome) proved more effective than directed prayer (specifying the desired outcome) in promoting germination in salt-damaged seeds. Borysenko reads this as evidence that prayer which aligns with the divine intelligence — releasing outcome to God — is more effective than prayer that attempts to instruct God.

Thich Nhat Hanh's walking meditation and the metta loving-kindness practice complete the prayer section, framing compassion as action: beginning with oneself ("May I be at peace..."), extending to loved ones, then to those in conflict, then to all beings and the earth.

### Compassionate Action

Peace Pilgrim (Mildred Norman, 1908–1981): having walked more than 25,000 miles through the United States for peace, she embodied the principle that spiritual practice must culminate in service. Her philosophy: love your enemies, which means seeing past the behaviour to the human being; act from love rather than reaction; accept the consequences of one's values.

The Tibetan Buddhists under Chinese occupation: Borysenko's husband Myrin attended the First International Conference on Holistic Health in Bangalore (1989), visited Tibetan refugee camps in India, and found Tibetans not consumed by hatred toward the Chinese but actively rebuilding their culture, preserving their spiritual tradition, and blessing their oppressors. The 1989 Nobel Peace Prize to the Dalai Lama became evidence that forgiveness-as-resistance can succeed where hatred cannot.

Etty Hillesum (1914–1943), from her diaries: writing from the Westerbork transit camp weeks before her death in Auschwitz — "I no longer believe that we can correct anything in the world outside us that we have not first corrected in ourselves. And that seems to me to be the only lesson to be learned from this war."

Healing circles: Borysenko's quarterly solstice and equinox community healing circles — twenty-five to fifty people coming together to sing, meditate, pray, and offer support to whoever comes to the centre of the circle asking for help. The model is neither therapy nor church service but a third thing: the ancient human practice of communal holding.
