**Summary**: Borysenko (1990) — a psychospiritual diagnosis of guilt as "soul sickness" (William James), tracing its roots in childhood shame, broken interpersonal bridges, and the shadow bag; offering a three-part path of healing through inner child work, spiritual revision, and the practice of forgiveness.

## Context and Origin

This is Borysenko's second major book, following the New York Times bestseller *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* (1987). Where the first book provided a clinical programme — teaching relaxation, meditation, and mindfulness to Mind/Body Clinic patients — this one investigates the deeper *why*: why do people remain trapped in stress-producing patterns even after learning effective self-regulation tools?

The answer Borysenko arrives at is guilt — not the conscience-forming remorse she calls "healthy guilt," but the chronic, shame-rooted condition that [William James](../thinkers/william-james.md) named **"soul sickness"**: a pervasive sense of unworthiness that organises the personality around self-protection rather than self-expression, generating the stress that worsens or creates illness.

The book opens with a first-person account of Borysenko's own car accident — a near-head-on collision she reads retrospectively as an act of grace that forced her to examine the guilt-driven workaholic patterns she had observed in thousands of patients but had not yet confronted in herself.

---

## Structure

The book has three parts, each addressing a different sphere of knowledge:

**Part One: Psychological Beginnings** — The historical, biographical, and bodily territory of guilt. Childhood shame, broken bridges, masks, and the shadow bag.

**Part Two: Spiritual Beginnings** — The soul and spirit dimension. The Self, mystical experience, religion vs. spirituality, the dark night of the soul, grace, and the question of spiritual optimism versus pessimism.

**Part Three: Compassion — The Flower of Psychospiritual Growth** — The relational territory. Forgiveness (of self and others), relationships as mirrors and teachers, and compassion as the culminating fruit of the journey.

## Part One: Psychological Beginnings

### The Bodymind-Soul Connection

Borysenko opens with the physiology familiar from her previous book: guilt produces chronic fear-based thinking, which activates the fight-or-flight response, suppressing immune function and producing the 75% of illness she considers stress-related. But she adds a layer: the problem is not stress management but the *source* of the stress — the soul-level belief in unworthiness.

She invokes [Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md)'s three eras of medicine:
- **Era I**: Material medicine — drugs, surgery, the body as machine
- **Era II**: Mind-body medicine — consciousness affects health; Borysenko's clinical territory
- **Era III**: Non-local healing — shared consciousness, prayer, intention at a distance

Her argument is that guilt requires an Era III response: not medication (Era I) or even meditation techniques (Era II) alone, but a reconnection to Spirit — a conversion of the underlying worldview from fear to love.

### Healthy Guilt and Unhealthy Shame

Borysenko distinguishes two phenomena often conflated under the word "guilt":

**Healthy guilt** functions as conscience — the signal that one has violated one's values. It produces remorse, motivates responsibility, and leads to repair. It is a *passing* emotional state that deepens self-knowledge and compassion. The student caught cheating who feels genuine remorse and reflects on what drove the lapse — this is healthy guilt functioning properly.

**Unhealthy shame** (John Bradshaw's "shame identity") is not about what one *has done* but about what one *is*. Shame says "I am defective" rather than "I made a mistake." It is chronic, identity-level, and generates a personality organised around self-protection. Borysenko identifies twenty-one expressions of this condition: perfectionism, compulsive helping, chronic apologising, late-night worry spirals, projection, people-pleasing, difficulty saying no, fear of criticism, inability to accept compliments, and so on.

The distinction: guilt teaches (feedback about conduct); shame wounds (conviction about worth).

### The Inner Child Drama

The core model of Part One traces how healthy children — what Borysenko calls the **Natural Child** (Rabbi Abraham Heschel's *amazement*, the "possessed by God" vitality of genuine childhood) — lose access to their essential selves through the dynamics of conditional love.

**The Interpersonal Bridge** (Gershen Kaufman's concept): love flows along bridges of trust and mutuality between two selves. When a bridge is broken — through shaming, abandonment, inconsistency, or contempt — the child experiences existential danger. The child concludes: "This must be my fault." Shame follows.

To protect the broken bridge, children develop:
- **Masks** (Alice Miller's "false self"; Virginia Satir's people-pleasing persona): the conditioned personality adopted to win approval
- **The Shadow Bag** (Robert Bly's metaphor): the sack in which everything shamed gets stored — anger, sexuality, spontaneity, courage, need, impulse. The shadow bag grows heavier throughout life; what is not consciously owned erupts in accidents, illness, and acts of character
- **Subpersonalities** (Roberto Assagioli's psychosynthesis): the cast of characters (victim, rescuer, achiever, femme fatale) that take on independent life and drive behaviour without the person's conscious awareness

Borysenko traces this through the extended case of Stephanie — a perfectionist advertising executive whose first-chakra connection to her mother's death, her father's emotional absence, and a sexual betrayal at eighteen had produced a personality organised entirely around self-protection, with recurring asthma as its bodily signature.

### Healing the Inner Child

Borysenko offers several practices for contacting and comforting the wounded child:

- **Mirror countdown visualization**: A guided technique using a mirror image in imagination, accessed via a countdown, to reach the scene of a childhood shaming and offer the child what it needed
- **Daily child visits**: A practice of greeting the inner child morning and evening, listening to its feelings, and offering the love and reassurance it did not receive
- **Four-step mindful listening**: Respectful listening → accepting feelings without judgment → comforting oneself → checking whether the process is complete
- **Journal writing**: James Pennebaker's research shows that writing traumas improves immune function and reduces illness; disclosing suppressed material reduces the metabolic cost of holding it

James Pennebaker's experiment: students who wrote about traumas *with their feelings* showed improved immune markers and fewer physician visits over the following six months, compared to students who wrote about neutral topics or traumas without feelings.

## Part Two: Spiritual Beginnings

### The Self That Doesn't Change

Underlying the parade of subpersonalities is something that does not change: what Borysenko variously calls the **Natural Child**, the **Inner Self Helper** (Dr. Ralph Allison's term from dissociative identity disorder research), the **Witness**, or the **Self**. This is not a spiritual abstraction but an observable stability — the person who is recognisably the same self despite the lifetime of changed masks.

Borysenko connects this to [Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md)'s hero's journey: the search for the Self is the mythological structure underlying every spiritual path. Snow White's sojourn in the forest, the Prodigal Son's return, the Holy Grail quest — all are maps of the same journey inward.

Roberto Assagioli's psychosynthesis provides the clinical framework: integrating the cast of subpersonalities under the direction of the Higher Self, which serves as conduit for the divine without itself dissolving the personality.

### The Nation of Closet Mystics

Andrew Greeley (University of Chicago, National Opinions Research Council) surveyed Americans in 1973 and again in the 1980s and found:
- 35% report having felt "a force that seemed to lift them out of themselves"
- 5% report being "bathed in light" (an experience comparable to Paul on the road to Damascus)
- Two-thirds report experiences of ESP
- Half of adults report contact with deceased loved ones
- 5% report having had near-death experiences (1984 Gallup data)

Crucially, Greeley found these experiencers tend toward above-average mental health, education, and intelligence — not pathology or suggestibility. Yet most keep the experiences secret, fearing ridicule or the label of psychosis.

Borysenko reads this as evidence of a "nation of closet mystics" — a population whose spiritual experiences outpace the religious and scientific frameworks available to interpret them. The silence is a social wound; the experience is genuinely widespread.

See [Closet Mystics](../concepts/closet-mystics.md).

### Religion vs. Spirituality

Borysenko (citing [Rachel Naomi Remen](../thinkers/rachel-naomi-remen.md)): "Religion is dogma — a set of beliefs; spirituality is a set of practices that give rise to experience."

She distinguishes:
- **Spiritual pessimism** (much of institutional religion): life as punishment, sin as the primary human condition, God as judge, illness as evidence of unworthiness. St. Augustine's doctrine of original sin as the theological foundation.
- **Spiritual optimism**: life as blessing, growth, and the gradual recovery of connection to the Source. Matthew Fox's *Via Positiva* / *Original Blessing*; the Prodigal Son parable; Gnostic wisdom (the Gospel of Thomas from the Nag Hammadi library — "the Kingdom is within you").

**New Age spiritual pessimism** (Ken Wilber's critique): the "you create your reality" doctrine appears to offer freedom but in practice simply moves the guilt inward — illness becomes a failure of positive thinking rather than a punishment from God. Wilber calls this "narcissistic megalomania." Borysenko agrees: neither "God is punishing you" nor "you caused your cancer by negative thinking" is an accurate or compassionate account of how illness arises.

### Dark Night of the Soul and Grace

The journey to enlightenment passes through a **dark night of the soul** (St. John of the Cross; Rabbi Isaac Eizik Komarno's diary account from 1806–1874): a period of dryness, depression, and confrontation with the shadow that precedes the ego's dissolution and the emergence of Self-centered consciousness. This is not pathology but the necessary death before rebirth.

**Grace** is the divine force that tips the balance. Borysenko uses the metaphor of a seesaw: personal effort can move one to 49% of the way; the remaining fraction requires grace. Grace is attracted by asking for help — prayer, surrender, willingness to change. The physician Sam's death scene in *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* recurs here as the embodiment of grace.

## Part Three: Compassion — The Flower of Psychospiritual Growth

### Forgiveness

Joseph Campbell's definition: compassion — the ability to "suffer with" another, leaving one's own sphere of concern to enter the life of another — is the "flower of psychospiritual growth," the culminating capacity of the hero's journey. The Grail legend (Wolfram von Eschenbach's *Parzival*) reaches its resolution when the hero, having learned through suffering, asks the Grail King: "What ails thee?" — an act of compassion that was the required question from the beginning.

Borysenko offers a **six-step process for forgiving oneself**:
1. Take responsibility for the action (without self-blame or denial)
2. Confess the nature of the wrong (to God, a person, a support group)
3. Look for the good points — fight the depression that follows exposure of the shadow
4. Make amends where possible (without creating additional harm)
5. Ask God for help (prayer as living connection to the Source)
6. Reflect: what has been learned?

And a **six-step process for forgiving others** (parallel to the above):
1. Recognise what you are holding
2. Confess the story to a neutral listener
3. Look for the good points in the person who hurt you
4. Consider what action, if any, needs to be taken
5. Ask God for help releasing the anger
6. Reflect: what has this person taught you?

Forgiveness does not mean condoning, forgetting, or restoring a damaging relationship. It means releasing the ego's grip on the role of victim — the position that, while emotionally understandable, imprisons the holder in the past and blocks healing.

### Relationships as Mirrors and Teachers

Falling in love is a "preview of coming attractions" — a grace-given glimpse of the union of Self with Self. The sustained relationship, however, is the real work: learning to see the partner not as the projected ideal but as a distinct person whose differences, shadows, and wounds are precisely what is needed for growth.

The David and Sandy case: a surgeon's Logos/defensive masculinity and a nurse's Eros/self-effacing femininity producing escalating projection and blame, resolved through the forgiveness steps and the recognition that each partner's charge represented childhood pain, not present reality.

Carlos Castaneda's "petty tyrants" concept, as interpreted through don Juan: difficult people are appointed teachers. What they provoke reveals exactly what needs to be developed.

The culminating image: Pierre Teilhard de Chardin's formulation — love is "the blood of evolution," the force that draws all things toward unity. The Sanskrit greeting *Namaste* — "I honour the Universe that dwells in you" — as the goal of all relationship.

## Key Thinkers Introduced or Developed

- **[William James](../thinkers/william-james.md)**: "soul sickness" as the name for the guilt-shame syndrome; *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) as the foundational text on mystical experience and its healing potential
- **Robert Bly**: The shadow bag — the sack everyone drags through life containing everything that was shamed and disowned; material that composts and grows wilder if not reclaimed
- **John Bradshaw**: Healing the Shame That Binds You; "shame identity" as the core wound; distinction between shame (passing emotion) and shame identity (character structure)
- **Gershen Kaufman**: The Interpersonal Bridge as the structural metaphor for love and trust; *Shame: The Power of Caring*
- **Alice Miller**: Drama of the Gifted Child; the false self formed by accommodation to parental needs; the mask that precedes genuine contact
- **Matthew Fox**: *Original Blessing*; Via Positiva vs. fall-redemption theology; Augustine's original sin as the root of spiritual pessimism
- **Ken Wilber**: Critique of New Age "you create your reality" as a form of guilt disguised as empowerment
- **Elaine Pagels**: *Adam, Eve, and the Serpent* and *The Gnostic Gospels*; the Nag Hammadi manuscripts and the Gospel of Thomas; Adam and Eve as a myth of consciousness awakening, not a doctrine of sin
- **Jeremy Taylor**: Dreamwork; dreams as multi-level communications from the Higher Self serving wholeness
- **Viktor Frankl**: *Man's Search for Meaning*; meaning as the determinant of survival; Etty Hillesum's journals from Auschwitz
