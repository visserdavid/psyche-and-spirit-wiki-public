# Heading Toward Omega

**Summary**: Ring's second NDE book (1984) moves beyond cataloguing the experience to document its systematic aftereffects — the personal transformations, value changes, spiritual reorientation, and psychic development that follow NDEs — and proposes that NDEs are catalysts in humanity's evolutionary ascent toward what Teilhard de Chardin called the Omega Point.

**Sources**:
- [Ring, K. (1984). *Heading toward omega: In search of the meaning of the near-death experience*. William Morrow.](heading-toward-omega.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Overview

*Heading Toward Omega* is [Kenneth Ring](../thinkers/kenneth-ring.md)'s second major work on near-death experiences, building on his first book *Life at Death* (1980). Where *Life at Death* documented the phenomenology of the NDE — its consistent structure and stages — *Heading Toward Omega* asks what the experience *means*: why do people change so profoundly after an NDE, and what does the aggregate of these changes signify for humanity?

The book is grounded in the **Omega Study**, Ring's multi-method research programme collecting data from over 100 NDErs using a battery of validated questionnaires, in-depth interviews conducted at what Ring calls the "Near Death Hotel" (his Connecticut home, which became a gathering point for NDErs and researchers), and third-party ratings from people who knew the NDErs before and after their experience. Foreword by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross.

**APA citation**: Ring, K. (1984). *Heading toward omega: In search of the meaning of the near-death experience*. William Morrow.

## The Omega Study: Design and Sample

Ring designed a multi-instrument research programme to move beyond the anecdotal:

- **Sample size**: Varied by instrument — 111 NDErs as primary sample; 26 completed the full questionnaire battery; 172 completed the Religious Beliefs Inventory (through IANDS)
- **Primary instruments**:
  - *Life Changes Questionnaire (LCQ)*: 42-item scale measuring changes across life domains (appreciation for life, concern for others, materialism, quest for meaning, etc.)
  - *Behavior Rating Inventory (BRI)*: Third-party observer instrument — respondents asked three persons who knew them before the NDE to rate behavioral changes; independent confirmation of self-reports
  - *Psychic Experience Inventory (PEI)*: Measures changes in incidence of clairvoyance, precognition, OBEs, déja vu, intuition, and contact with spirit guides
  - *Religious Beliefs Inventory (RBI)*: 12-item scale measuring shift toward "spiritual universalism"
  - *Future Scenario Questionnaire (FSQ)*: Measures endorsement of a shared planetary vision
- **Qualitative data**: Two-hour taped interviews, written correspondence from IANDS archives, near-death Hotel conversations; three extended case histories examined in depth (Barbara Harris, Stella, Tom Sawyer)

Ring acknowledges significant methodological limitations: no random sampling, no control group, no tests of statistical significance (except on the RBI). He presents findings as hypotheses for future controlled research rather than definitive conclusions.

## The Core NDE: Anatomy of the Experience (Chapter 3)

Ring presents 14 extended case histories from the Omega sample to map what he calls the "core NDE" — the deepest form of the experience, distinguished from ordinary NDEs by intensity and completeness. The 16 identifying elements of the core NDE:

1. Speed of approach; acceleration toward the light
2. The light — brilliant, white, non-blinding, perceived as personal/aware
3. Sense of the light's presence as a person or being
4. Feeling of love — overwhelming, unconditional
5. Acceptance, total
6. Forgiveness — experienced as absolute, effortless
7. Sense of homecoming, recognition
8. Communication (not verbal; direct transfer of understanding)
9. Knowledge of universal principles imparted by the light
10. Life review — panoramic, simultaneous, from perspective of those affected
11. Knowledge that only love and learning matter
12. Music
13. Environments of surpassing beauty
14. Cities of light (reported by a subset)
15. Yearning to remain — the coming-back as anguish
16. Return reluctant or compelled

Representative case: **Tom Sawyer**, a truck driver with no prior education beyond high school, who after his NDE began spontaneously accessing knowledge of quantum physics. He quoted Max Planck from memory, enrolled in physics courses, and amazed professors by having already read the four texts they assigned — books he had selected without knowledge of the syllabus. Ring interprets this as an example of the light imparting transcendent knowledge.

**Joe Geraci's** formulation captures the essence: "It becomes you, you become it. It's just... peace and love and brightness part of.... I mean it's just — knowing, you're knowing part of it."

## Personal Transformation: The Three Case Histories (Chapter 4)

Ring observes that returning from the core NDE is often experienced as anguish — subjects do not want to come back, and many go through a period of depression, grief, and disorientation. Medical staff who dismiss or pathologise the experience compound the difficulty. Key finding: NDE frequently ends marriages (spouses unable to relate to the transformed person).

**Barbara Harris**: A Midwesterner who had grown up entirely oriented to pleasing others. Her NDE triggered a life review that dissolved twenty years of accumulated shame in a single experience. "Years and years of psychoanalysis hadn't brought me what I was experiencing." She went on to become deeply involved in consciousness research and healing.

**Stella** (pseudonym): Raised in the conservative Baptist Deep South, married with five children, had never expressed her own opinions. After her NDE, she received communication directing her to find her biological family (she was adopted). The search revealed she was Jewish. She converted, divorced, became a businesswoman, and was invited to serve on the White House Council on Children and Youth.

**Tom Sawyer**: The quantum physics case. After his NDE, Tom also reports sustained heightened intuitive and empathic capacities. His wife confirms the transformation. His case illustrates both the intellectual and the relational dimensions of post-NDE change.

Ring frames these transformations as NDEs enabling individuals to "come to a sense of self" and "actualize potentials" that were previously blocked by conditioning. The NDE strips away the constructed personality and exposes the "core self" — what Barbara Harris calls "the person I felt I was restricting becoming."

## Value Changes: The LCQ Findings (Chapter 5)

The Life Changes Questionnaire data (26 NDErs; supplementary data from 42 interviewees and 62 correspondents) shows consistent shifts:

| Domain | Direction | Magnitude |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Appreciation for life and nature | Strong increase | Consistent |
| Concern for and love of others | Strong increase | Consistent |
| Concern for impressing others | Decrease | Consistent |
| Interest in material possessions | Decrease | Consistent |
| Quest for meaning/consciousness | Strong increase | Consistent |

These findings are corroborated by the BRI — third-party observers confirm the changes they perceive in the NDErs they know. The modal number of raters (out of 11 items) who observed positive changes was 8, supporting the reliability of self-reports.

Ring argues the value shifts are direct outgrowths of the core NDE: the life review creates first-hand knowledge that love is the only thing that matters; encountering the light dissolves attachment to status and material success; the sense of cosmic love generalises to all beings.

## Spiritual Orientation: The RBI Findings (Chapter 6)

Using the Religious Beliefs Inventory with 172 respondents (76 NDErs; 30 death survivors; 66 others), Ring documents a consistent shift among NDErs toward what he calls "spiritual universalism" — a world view incorporating seven elements:

1. **Inward spirituality** — felt closeness to God without reliance on institutional religion
2. **Deemphasis of formal religion** — church attendance may decrease; doctrine becomes less important
3. **Conviction of life after death** — near-universal (90%+ of NDErs)
4. **Openness to reincarnation** — significant increase after NDE; 58% of NDErs report increase (PEI data)
5. **Sympathy for Eastern religions** — increased openness to Buddhism, Hinduism, Eastern thought
6. **Belief in unity of religions** — a majority of NDErs endorse the view that all religions share a common core
7. **Desire for a universal religion** — higher endorsement among NDErs than all other groups

Key finding: The shift toward universalism is strongest among NDErs who are *not* mainline Christians — 7.56 average universalism score vs. 4.45 for mainline Christian NDErs. Ring interprets this as NDErs who are already less doctrinally constrained being freer to integrate the universalist implications of the experience.

As NDEr Janis puts it: "I'm not religious, I'm spiritual. I'm not a churchgoer... I know there's a God. God exists. He's the essence of God... I know it by experience."

## Psychic Development: The PEI Findings (Chapter 7)

The Psychic Experience Inventory data shows 58% of the 26 Omega NDErs reported increases in psychic phenomena following their NDE. Most increased: contact with a source of wisdom (96%), increased intuition (80%), déja vu (one-third), and OBE frequency.

Ring draws support from two independent studies:
- **Bruce Greyson's study** (80 IANDS NDErs): found statistically significant increases (p < .0001) in three categories — waking ESP, OBEs, and encounters with apparitions — compared to non-NDEr death survivors
- **Richard Kohr's ARE study** (84 NDErs, 105 death survivors, 358 others): NDErs consistently outscored both groups on psi and psi-related phenomena, waking and dream ESP, and OBE frequency

Ring contextualises these findings in the Eastern spiritual literature: both the Buddhist *Visuddhimagga* and Patanjali's *Yoga Sutras* document that psychic *siddhis* (powers) arise as by-products of sustained meditative practice — not as goals but as signs of deepening consciousness. The same authorities warn that attachment to these powers obstructs further development.

**Flashforwards (PFs)**: Ring documents a subset of NDErs who, during the life review phase, perceived future events in their own lives — "memories of the future." Several correspondents report that these scenes subsequently occurred. The most extensively documented case involves Janis, who described six to seven specific scenes, five of which she claims were subsequently fulfilled. Ring treats PFs as a precognitive phenomenon linked to the altered time-perception of the NDE state.

## Planetary Visions: The FSQ Findings (Chapter 8)

Ring located 16 NDErs who reported receiving visions of Earth's planetary future during their NDE — typically in the context of information imparted by a guide or being of light. The consistent scenario:

1. **Geophysical upheaval**: earthquakes, volcanic activity, landmass changes, pole shift
2. **Economic and social collapse**
3. **Risk of nuclear conflict** (with uncertainty about inevitability)
4. **A subsequent era of peace and brotherhood**

Ring administered the Future Scenario Questionnaire (174 respondents) and found that 28.2% of the general sample endorsed the PV scenario — with no significant difference between NDErs and non-NDErs. He concludes the PV content cannot be explained as NDErs uniquely internalising a culturally available zeitgeist, but also that the visions should not be taken as prophetic certainties.

Ring's preferred interpretation draws on quantum physics: if the "many-worlds interpretation" (Everett-Wheeler-Graham, 1957) is correct, multiple futures exist simultaneously; NDErs may be accessing a probabilistic *range* of futures, not a fixed destiny. One respondent described seeing three distinct futures (A, B, and C) during her NDE, with futures B and C corresponding to the PV scenario. This interpretation transforms the visions from doomsday prophecy into statements about probabilities contingent on human choices.

## The Kundalini Hypothesis: Biological Basis (Chapter 9)

Ring's most original theoretical contribution. He proposes that the NDE is not unique to the death crisis but belongs to a broader *family of transformative experiences* (confirmed by comparing NDEr accounts with non-death-crisis mystical experiences, including a detailed case from Nancy Clark, who had a full NDE-like experience while delivering a eulogy).

The unifying mechanism Ring proposes is **kundalini energy** — the Sanskrit term for a dormant bioenergy held to lie at the base of the spine, which when activated by various triggers (including, Ring hypothesises, the suspension of breath during cardiac arrest or drowning) rises through the chakra system producing transformative experiences.

**Evidence for the kundalini-NDE connection**:
- Post-NDE symptoms reported by Omega subjects include heat sensations, energy movement up the spine, colors and light phenomena, luminous experiences in ordinary waking consciousness, reduced need for sleep, spontaneous healing abilities — all documented in the kundalini literature
- Hiroshi Motoyama's kundalini awakening (through prolonged Yoga practice) parallels the NDE phenomenologically: out-of-body states, encounter with brilliant light, experience of cosmic consciousness, expanded healing abilities
- Gopi Krishna's writings on kundalini describe the same value transformation, psychic development, and genius-flowering that Ring documents in NDErs
- Barbara Harris's 54-page documented account of post-NDE symptoms reads like a clinical kundalini case study (heat, energy movement, colors, luminous experiences)

**Proposed mechanism**: During cardiac arrest, drowning, or other near-death events, disruption of breathing may trigger a kundalini response — the body's bioenergetic emergency activation. The kundalini surge then mediates the NDE itself (the tunnel, the light, the consciousness expansion) and, having been activated, continues to produce transformative effects in the person's life.

Ring cites Gopi Krishna's statement that kundalini arousal energises the brain to "a level of functioning" beyond ordinary capacity — the "ultraconsciousness" that Stanley Dean (1975) had already documented as a distinct experiential category characterised by brilliant light, cosmic love, knowledge, the dissolution of fear of death, and subsequent transformation.

**Caution**: Ring acknowledges the hypothesis cannot yet be confirmed because instruments to reliably detect kundalini arousal (e.g., Motoyama's chakra machine) have not been cross-validated. He presents it as the most promising framework available, not a demonstrated fact.

## The Omega Hypothesis: NDEs and Human Evolution (Chapter 10)

Ring's culminating thesis: NDEs are not merely individual transformative events — they are catalysts in the evolution of human consciousness toward what Teilhard de Chardin called the Omega Point.

**Teilhard de Chardin's framework**: The Jesuit paleontologist [Pierre Teilhard de Chardin](../thinkers/pierre-teilhard-de-chardin.md) argued in *[The Phenomenon of Man](the-phenomenon-of-man.md)* (1959) that evolution is not merely biological but also psychic — the universe is developing toward increasing complexity and consciousness, tending toward a convergent state he called the Omega Point, where consciousness reaches its maximum. Ring draws only on Teilhard's optimistic vision of that convergence; see [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md) for how this compares with Teilhard's own, more ambivalent treatment of evil and a possible "paroxysm" scenario.

**John White's "Homo noeticus"**: Ring draws on John White's concept of an emerging form of humanity characterised by:
- Deeper awareness of the self and universe
- Love-based rather than fear-based motivation
- Integrated rather than sequential logic
- Identity embracing rather than isolated from others
- Psychic/intuitive faculties used for compassionate purposes
- Commitment to a culture founded on love and wisdom

Ring argues that Omega NDErs — individuals who have undergone deep transformations following core NDEs — match the Homo noeticus profile. They are prototypes of what humanity is evolving toward.

**The scale**: Based on Gallup (1982) data, approximately 8 million adult Americans had had NDEs by the time Ring wrote. Ring argues NDEs are increasing as resuscitation technology improves. The transformative experience is not limited to NDErs — it belongs to a family of experiences (kundalini awakenings, spontaneous mystical experiences, meditation breakthroughs) all of which produce similar changes.

**Rupert Sheldrake's morphic resonance**: Ring draws on Sheldrake's *A New Science of Life* (1981) — specifically the hypothesis that morphic fields allow behaviour learned by members of a species to become more accessible to other members through a process of "morphic resonance." William McDougall's experiment (rats learning a maze faster over twenty generations, with the improvement manifesting even in unrelated rat populations) suggests that learning leaves a field imprint accessible to others. Peter Russell applies this to consciousness: "If individuals begin to raise their levels of consciousness, a field of those states will become easier for others to move in that direction."

Ring's conclusion: 8 million NDErs are the "monkey islands" — if a critical mass reaches a new level of consciousness, it becomes easier for the species as a whole to follow. The Omega Point is not a distant eschatology but an emergent process already underway.

**Caroline Myss connection**: Ring explicitly acknowledges Myss's contribution in the acknowledgments ("I would also like to thank Caroline Myss for reading the manuscript and for her criticisms") and cites her in the endnotes on the limits of NDEr transformation ("transformation of consciousness does not guarantee that an individual will become a saint, but it does afford one an increased opportunity for growth").

## Critique

**Methodological**: Ring's own acknowledged limitations — no random sampling, no control groups, no tests of significance for most findings — mean the Omega Study is hypothesis-generating, not hypothesis-confirming. The sample is heavily self-selected (people who sought out IANDS, who came to Ring's door, who wrote letters describing their experiences).

**Kundalini hypothesis**: The parallel between NDEr symptoms and kundalini awakening is suggestive but not established. The same symptoms (heat, energy sensations, altered consciousness) appear in numerous other contexts (anxiety disorders, psychotic episodes, fever) and are not uniquely explained by kundalini. No physiological measurement of kundalini has been independently validated.

**Planetary visions**: The 16 PV cases constitute a tiny, self-selected sample. The predictions (upheaval by 1988) largely did not occur as described. Ring's "alternative futures" interpretation rescues the visions from falsifiability but simultaneously removes their evidential weight.

**Evolutionary thesis**: The Sheldrake-based "morphic resonance of consciousness" is speculative. Sheldrake's own hypothesis remains contested in biology; its extension to human consciousness evolution is extrapolation. The "Homo noeticus" concept is unfalsifiable as Ring uses it.

**Note**: Mainstream neuroscience offers physiological explanations for NDE phenomenology (temporal lobe activation, REM intrusion, hypoxia, endorphin release) that are biologically plausible without requiring the kundalini or evolutionary frameworks. These explanations are not mutually exclusive with Ring's interpretation — the mechanism and the meaning may both be real — but Ring's framework makes additional claims beyond what the data establishes.
