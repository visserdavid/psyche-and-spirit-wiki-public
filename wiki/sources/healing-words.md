# Healing Words

**Summary**: Dossey's 1993 survey of the scientific evidence for prayer and non-local healing — 131 controlled experiments reviewed, a landmark CCU prayer study examined, the directed vs. non-directed prayer distinction introduced, and the theoretical case made that consciousness is non-local and that prayer is an expression of that non-locality.

**Sources**:
- [Dossey, L. (1993). *Healing words: The power of prayer and the practice of medicine*. HarperSanFrancisco.](healing-words.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Overview

*Healing Words* (HarperSanFrancisco, 1993; 436 pages plus four appendices) is Larry Dossey's most systematic book on prayer. It grew from an encounter with the prayer research literature during his clinical practice — a body of over 130 controlled experiments showing measurable effects of prayer and healing intention on living organisms, which had been largely ignored by mainstream medicine.

The book is structured in three parts: Part One situates prayer within Dossey's Three Eras of Medicine framework and explores what non-local consciousness implies for healing. Part Two examines factors influencing prayer's efficacy — the unconscious mind, personality types, directed vs. non-directed strategies, love, time displacement, and physician beliefs. Part Three reviews the controlled experimental evidence.

**APA citation**: Dossey, L. (1993). *Healing words: The power of prayer and the practice of medicine*. HarperSanFrancisco.

## Part One: Understanding Prayer and Healing

### Saints, Sinners, and Health (Chapter 1)

Dossey opens with a counter-intuitive observation: many of the most spiritually realised figures in history — Buddha, St. Bernadette, Jiddu Krishnamurti, Sri Ramana Maharshi, Suzuki Roshi — died of illness. Spiritual attainment does not guarantee health. This directly confronts the New Age equation of spiritual development with physical wellbeing.

Jesus's response to his disciples' question about the man born blind is Dossey's touchstone: "Neither this man nor his parents sinned; he was born blind so that God's works might be revealed in him" (John 9:1–3). Illness does not imply fault. The purpose of illness may be hidden from mortals.

Dossey distinguishes **prayerfulness** from **prayer**: prayerfulness is a state of consciousness — a sense of alignment, acceptance, and oneness with the Absolute — that pervades action rather than being confined to formal prayer acts. The Japanese cancer regression cases documented by Yujiro Ikemi (1975, Kyushu University) — five patients whose cancers spontaneously regressed following diagnosis — illustrate prayerfulness: not petition for cure, but renewed devotion and acceptance of God's will. Key features: absence of anxiety or depression following diagnosis; faith that overshadowed the cancer diagnosis; no compulsion to fight or banish the disease; commitment to meaning and relationships.

### The Reach of Mind (Chapter 2): Three Eras

Dossey's framework situates prayer within the evolution of medicine:

- **Era I** (from 1860s): Mechanical medicine; body as machine; mind has no causal role; treatments are physical (drugs, surgery, radiation).
- **Era II** (from 1950s): Mind-body medicine; mind affects the body within the person; the domain of psychoneuroimmunology, biofeedback, imagery, and placebo effects.
- **Era III** (emerging): Non-local medicine; consciousness is not bounded by individual brain, body, or moment in time; healing can occur at a distance and retroactively. Prayer, therapeutic touch, intuitive diagnosis, and distant healing are Era III phenomena.

The theoretical basis of Era III: physicist John Stewart Bell's 1964 theorem demonstrated that subatomic particles, once in contact, remain correlated regardless of subsequent separation — change one and the other changes instantaneously, no matter the distance. Bell's theorem proved non-locality is a real feature of the universe, not a theoretical oddity. Nick Herbert (Quantum Reality, 1985) argues this applies beyond the quantum domain.

Dossey cites Myss and Shealy's intuitive diagnosis protocol as a clinical example of Era III: Myss diagnosed patients from 1,200 miles away, given only their name and birthdate, with 93% accuracy — her mind demonstrating non-local access to clinical information. See [Medical Intuition](../concepts/medical-intuition.md).

## Part Two: Factors Influencing Efficacy

### Prayer and the Unconscious Mind (Chapter 3)

Prayer cannot be confined to waking, conscious, verbal petition — because the unconscious mind constitutes the majority of psychic life. Isaac the Syrian (cited through Richard J. Foster) believed the Holy Spirit prays through a person without ceasing, including during sleep.

Key insight: the unconscious may work *against* the conscious intentions of the person praying. In Carol's case, a patient who "took 100% responsibility for creating her reality" developed a ruptured appendix (which gives days of warning signs she suppressed). In the CCU, the survival benefit of *denial* — believing one isn't having a heart attack — is documented: deniers have lower adrenaline, lower blood pressure, fewer arrhythmias, and better 24-hour survival than "honest" patients.

Implications for prayer: directing prayer at desired outcomes may conflict with unconscious processes that know something the conscious mind does not. The non-directed approach — "Thy will be done" — allows the unconscious and the organism's innate healing intelligence to participate without being overridden.

Dream prayer: Dossey documents historical and contemporary cases of healing occurring through dreams, citing Soviet transmission experiments (V. M. Bekhterev's commission, 1920s) suggesting that imagery from one mind enters another's unconscious during sleep.

### Do Prayers Go? (Chapter 4): Non-local Consciousness

Prayer does not "travel" — it does not involve sending information or energy across distance. Based on Bell's theorem, correlated particles do not communicate; their correlation is not sent, it exists. Similarly, Dossey argues that consciousness may be fundamentally non-local — not individual minds connected across space, but something more like a unified field in which apparent separation is secondary.

This is why "Thy will be done" approaches work: they are not requests for the Absolute to intervene from outside, but invitations for reality to manifest its own wisdom. The Law of Reversed Effort (Aldous Huxley): the harder one tries to control, the more the desired outcome eludes. Prayer as invitation, not engineering.

### How to Pray (Chapter 5): Directed vs. Non-directed Prayer

Dossey distinguishes two fundamental prayer strategies:

**Directed prayer**: A specific outcome is held in mind (pray for the cancer to disappear, the pain to subside). Common in Western petition prayer.

**Non-directed prayer**: No specific outcome is held. "Thy will be done." The universe is allowed to find the best resolution without the pray-er specifying what "best" means.

The **Spindrift experiments** (John and Bruce Klingbeil, Spindrift organization, Salem, Oregon): controlled laboratory experiments testing both approaches on biological systems (germinating seeds, mold cultures on rice agar). Consistent findings: non-directed prayer outperformed directed prayer — typically two to four times more effective. In the mold experiment, directed prayer produced no growth change; non-directed prayer produced consistent growth rings. In seed experiments, non-directed prayer correctly steered beans toward their individual biological norms (dehydrated beans gained water; over-hydrated beans lost it) without the practitioner knowing which batch needed what.

The personality factor: Herbert Benson's research showed that the same relaxation response was elicitable using personal prayer phrases (Catholic: "Hail Mary"; Jewish: "Shalom"; Protestant: "Our Father") as with the TM mantra. Carl Jung's introvert/extrovert distinction applies: introverts naturally gravitate toward contemplative, non-directed approaches; extroverts toward directed, outcome-specific petition. Neither is superior — both produce genuine effects. Forcing an introvert to pray in an extrovert style (or vice versa) creates "prayer neurosis" and dropout. See [Directed vs. Non-directed Prayer](../concepts/directed-vs-nondirected-prayer.md).

### Love and Healing (Chapter 6)

David McClelland (Harvard Medical School): students watching a documentary of Mother Teresa showed elevated salivary IgA (antibody) levels — even students who said they disliked or mistrusted her. When McClelland added a second step — asking students to recall a time they felt genuinely loved, and a time they had genuinely loved another — the IgA rise was reproducible on demand. McClelland used this technique to abort his own colds.

F. W. H. Myers (foundational parapsychologist) proposed that love is the mechanism underlying telesomatics — the way distress, suffering, or distress of one person triggers symptoms or awareness in another at a distance. The Princeton PEAR Lab's random event generator experiments showed that "bonded couples" (emotionally close pairs) produced effects two to four times larger than individuals — and that the effect persisted across distance and time.

Dossey's caveat: love has been weaponised in New Age health literature. The dictum "love is the reason for healing" creates guilt when healing fails. Dossey invokes Jung's warning that Eros (love) is not domesticated — it contains shadow, paradox, and ambiguity. Job experienced the shadow side of a loving God. Love occupies a place in healing — but it is not a guarantee, not a formula, and not a grounds for self-blame.

### Time-Displaced Prayer (Chapter 7)

Physicist Helmut Schmidt (Mind Science Foundation, San Antonio): subjects could influence the output of random event generators (REGs) *retroactively* — events already recorded on tape but not yet observed. The act of observation in quantum physics "fixes" possibilities into actualities; until observed, events remain superpositions of possibilities. Schmidt's experiments suggest that unobserved, recorded events are still "plastic" and influenceable.

Physicist John Archibald Wheeler's "delayed choice" experiments: an observer's present decision appears to retroactively determine which quantum path a particle took in the past.

Implications for medicine: routine diagnostic testing may "fix" the medical past — converting plastic possibilities into actualised disease states. Dossey does not advocate avoiding examinations, but suggests a window of possibility exists between the first suspicion of illness and its confirmation through observation. Prayer imagery and intention during this window may affect which possibilities crystallise.

"Time-displaced illness": some diseases appear to precede their causes — tuberculosis that develops the bacterium later, spontaneous cancers that vanish before being confirmed. Dossey sees these as evidence that causation is not strictly sequential.

### Doctor Beliefs Matter (Chapter 8)

Jerry Solfvin's research: physician belief in a treatment affects treatment outcomes even in double-blind studies designed to eliminate the physician as a variable. Three studies of Miltown (meprobamate): when prescribed by believing physicians, it outperformed placebo; when prescribed by skeptical physicians, it did not. The suggestion: physician consciousness penetrates the experimental conditions.

Doctor "hanging crepe" — painting the worst picture to protect one's reputation — may function as a nocebo: patients can die fulfilling a physician's prophecy. Dossey cites a woman who lived 15 years with untreated breast cancer, insisting to all physicians that surgery would "let the air in and make it spread." The prophecy proved correct — she died shortly after surgery.

Rolling Thunder (Native American shaman) anecdote: Rolling Thunder came to Dossey's clinic with neck pain. Dossey took his history, examined him, discussed healing philosophies, and gave muscle relaxant samples. Rolling Thunder demonstrated that a skilled healer can operate within multiple healing systems simultaneously without compromising either. Flexibility and tolerance — not doctrinal adherence — are the key physician virtues.

### Negative Prayer (Chapter 9)

Evidence that focused intention can harm living organisms:
- Olga Worrall (renowned healer) refused to inhibit bacteria growth in Beverly Rubik's laboratory: "I refuse to hurt." Her consent allowed her to protect one bacterial sample from another's inhibiting influence — the protected bacteria survived at significantly higher rates.
- Bernard Grad's "petrified banana": a man who claimed to desiccate fruit by focusing held a banana for hours; it hardened to marble-like consistency. Grad retained it, mounted it on a chain, and demonstrated its rock-hardness at lectures.
- Uri Geller experiment (Zvi Bentwich, immunologist): Geller inadvertently killed sperm cells in a container he was trying to alter the motility of.
- Polynesian/Hawaiian death prayer (kahuna tradition): described by Max Freedom Long (1917) as working through the victim's unconscious guilt — not through suggestion or voodoo mechanisms. The victim must have a "guilt armor" for the death prayer to penetrate.
- Laboratory: controlled studies show that ordinary people can inhibit growth of fungi, bacteria, and yeast through focused intention — from distances up to 15 miles.
- Michael Harner (anthropologist, Jivaro fieldwork): distant hexing was standard practice among Jivaro shamans, always performed by teams of 2–3 for safety.

Physicist Nick Herbert: Bell's theorem demonstrates the universe is fundamentally "contagious" at the particle level — "magic" (Frazer's word for action at a distance) is woven into the fabric of reality. This does not prove distant hexing works at the human scale, but it removes the a priori reason to dismiss it.

## Part Three: Evidence

### God in the Laboratory (Chapter 10)

Dossey opens at the Western Wall in Jerusalem, describing the ritual of writing prayers on scraps of paper and inserting them in the wall cracks — and his moment of unable-to-pray stillness, writing only "I want to know the truth." He uses this as a metaphor for science itself: Galileo and Bacon as the original "wall-truth seekers," asking the universe to reveal its workings through experiment.

People object to studying prayer on grounds of sacrilege (prayer doesn't need science) or hopelessness (science can't prove prayer). Dossey argues that investigating prayer is not bringing God into the laboratory but bringing the laboratory to God — inviting the universe to reveal its workings. Spindrift researchers (Christian Science affiliated) were censured by their own church for testing prayer; the charge was heresy.

### Prayer and Healing: Reviewing the Research (Chapter 11)

**Benor's survey of 131 controlled healing studies** (Daniel J. Benor, M.D., *Complementary Medical Research*, 1990; expanded *Healing Research*, 1993): 131 experiments meeting controlled trial criteria, across fungi/yeast, bacteria, cancer cells, plants, animals, and humans. 56 studies showed results at p ≤ .01 (chance explanation 1 in 100). 21 studies showed results at p = .02–.05.

Key studies examined:
- **Byrd CCU study** (1988, *Southern Medical Journal*): 393 CCU patients randomly assigned; prayer group had 5× fewer antibiotic requirements, 3× less pulmonary edema, no intubations (vs. 12 controls). Detailed critique: born-again selection bias; no monitoring of whether prayer occurred; no control of prayer strategies (directed vs. non-directed); no skill assessment of intercessors; "name factor" (intercessors knew names and conditions — prayer may have leaked to controls). Dossey regards the study as "suggestive" but not conclusive.
- **Braud-Schlitz transpersonal imagery**: 13 experiments, 62 influencers, 271 subjects; isolated rooms; one person's calming/activating imagery measurably altered another's electrodermal activity. Effect did not appear to diminish with distance; worked without subject awareness.
- **Matthew Manning and cancer cells**: holding hands over flasks of cancer cells, Manning produced 200–1,200% changes in growth characteristics.
- **Bernard Grad's barley seeds** (Estebany, McGill University): saline-damaged barley seeds grew significantly better when the saline container had been held by healer Oskar Estebany for 15 minutes. Healing properties appeared transferable to materials (saline, wool, cotton).
- **Mice wound healing**: Estebany held cages of wounded mice 15 min/day × 14 days; treated mice healed significantly faster than controls.
- **Mouse anesthesia awakening**: 19/21 studies showed healers could accelerate awakening from anesthesia in mice.
- **Bacteria mutation control**: subjects instructed a strain of *E. coli* to mutate in directed or reversed-directed ways; results showed mutations followed subject direction.

**The Redlands experiment** (1951, University of Redlands): 45 volunteers divided into three groups — psychotherapy alone (65% improvement), self-directed prayer alone (no improvement), prayer therapy combined with personality test feedback (72% improvement). Methodological problems prevent strong conclusions but suggest prayer combined with self-knowledge exceeds either alone.

**Childhood leukemia prayer study** (1965): widely cited by skeptics as proof prayer doesn't work; Benor documents fatal design flaws — no comparability assessment between groups, only 28 patients total, no specified outcome criteria, no monitoring of whether prayer occurred.

### What Is Healing? (Chapter 12)

Lawrence LeShan's description of the healer's state: entering a mode in which "my ordinary desires and wishes and the 'I' of every day" are absent — becoming one with the subject in focused compassion. Healers consistently describe this as losing the sense of separation. Effects of healing do not diminish with distance, are not shielded by barriers, and are not blocked by time (retroactive healing).

Dossey's twelve reasons why scientists reject evidence for healing (following Benor): paradigm blindness, cognitive dissonance, fear of mysticism, desire for control, fear of being influenced, career protection, lack of replicability serving as post-hoc justification, demanding a "dose" structure that healing does not have. But over 300 studies (131 controlled healing studies + 149 mind-body influence studies reviewed by Braud) show measurable effects in roughly half. This body of evidence — ignored, not refuted — represents one of the largest unreported anomalies in modern science.

## Appendices

- **Appendix 1**: Benor's full table of 131 controlled healing trials — reference list of 99 studies
- **Appendix 2**: Five Japanese spontaneous cancer regression cases (Ikemi, 1975) — detailed narratives of five patients (Y. H., K. N., K. A., K. K., Y. Y.) all showing a common pattern: no anxiety or depression after diagnosis; faith overriding fear; commitment to meaning and devotion; regression following not fighting but accepting
- **Appendix 3**: NRC (National Research Council) reports on meditation and parapsychology (1988, 1991) — Dossey critiques the NRC's exclusion of Dillbeck/Orme-Johnson TM meta-analysis; and the NRC's suppression of Rosenthal and Harris's commissioned evaluation of parapsychology research quality (which found parapsychology's methodology equal to or better than comparison sciences)
- **Appendix 4**: Summary of local vs. non-local mind-body effects — a table classifying healing effects by sensory/non-sensory and intrapersonal/interpersonal/transpersonal axes
