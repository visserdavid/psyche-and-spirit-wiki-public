# Health and Healing: Understanding Conventional and Alternative Medicine

**Summary**: Weil (1983) provides a systematic philosophical and clinical survey of every major healing system, anchored in an original argument that health means wholeness and balance, nine principles governing health and illness, and a detailed investigation of mind-mediated healing — including the most rigorous treatment of placebo response and wart cures in this wiki.

**Sources**:
- [Weil, A. (1983). *Health and healing: Understanding conventional and alternative medicine*. Houghton Mifflin.](health-and-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Author and Context

Andrew Weil (b. 1942) trained at Harvard Medical School and became one of the most prominent advocates for integrative medicine in North America. *Health and Healing* (1983) was his foundational theoretical statement — written from lectures at the University of Arizona College of Medicine before the term "integrative medicine" existed — arguing that conventional medicine's failure to define health leaves it structurally unable to practise healing.

Weil later expanded these ideas in *Spontaneous Healing* (1995), *Eight Weeks to Optimal Health* (1997), and dozens of other works. The 1983 edition is the most philosophically dense and critically rigorous.

---

## Part I: A Case for Homeopathy

The book opens with a first-person account. In 1980 Weil developed recurrent oesophageal spasm for which his gastroenterologist offered only a name and the suggestion to experiment with antacids. On the way to see an old friend (now a homeopath), Weil decided to use his own condition as an experiment. His friend prescribed a single dose of sulfur 30X. The pain never returned.

Weil does not claim this proves homeopathy works. He uses the case to model the epistemological honesty he demands throughout the book: he cannot distinguish between a pharmacological cure, a course-of-illness resolution, and a placebo response — and this uncertainty defines the challenge of evaluating all healing systems.

Chapters 2–3 give a detailed history of homeopathy: Hahnemann's origin story (1790 cinchona experiment), the Law of Similars, the Law of Infinitesimals, the Avogadro limit problem, Boyd's chloride dilution replications (1954), and the mainstream immunity to investigating an anomaly that could, if real, require revising chemical theory.

## Part II: What Is Health?

### Health as Wholeness and Perfection

Weil argues that Western medicine cannot define health because it has abandoned the philosophical and religious roots of the concept. Tracing the word:

- **Health** comes from the Proto-Indo-European *kailo* — whole, uninjured, of good augury — the same root that gives **holy**, **hale**, **whole**, and **heal**
- **Cure** comes from Latin *cura* (care, management)
- **Medicine** from the Indo-European root meaning "to measure" — to establish order

Health, holiness, and wholeness share a root because historically they were the same concern: medicine, religion, and magic were unified until allopathic medicine decided to become a science.

Two properties define wholeness: completeness (all components integrated) and balance. Weil uses Black Elk's vision of the hoop of the nation as a cultural expression of the same intuition — the circle as symbol of wholeness, the breaking of the circle (life in square houses on reservations) as the symbol of power lost.

The caduceus (Hermes's staff) is read as the integration of opposites: two snakes (expansion and limitation, light and dark, yang and yin) winding around a central axis, crowned with wings. The insight: balance arises from the arrangement of opposing forces, not from the elimination of one pole. Saturn (limitation, the Grim Reaper) is exalted in Libra (balance) — limitation is necessary for equilibrium.

### Health as Balance (Dynamic Equilibrium)

Health is not a permanent state but a dynamic equilibrium — the body constantly adjusting to changing stresses. Weil keeps a personal symptom diary as evidence: over any one-week period, a healthy person experiences dozens of minor disturbances that resolve without intervention. The goal is not to eliminate all fluctuation but to maintain the capacity for self-correction.

## Part III: The Nature of Healing

Healing is an innate biological property of the organism, not something given by medicine. Three components:

1. **Reaction** (inflammation, fever): the body's active response to injury or infection — often misread as the illness itself and suppressed with drugs, thereby impairing healing. Fever above 102°F enhances immunity (Kluger research); suppressing it may give pathogens an advantage.

2. **Regeneration**: the replacement of damaged cells with new ones. Corneal cells regenerate within 24 hours (Weil's snow blindness). Liver cells multiply within hours of injury. This capacity is vast and often underutilised because medicine focuses on suppression rather than support.

3. **Adaptation**: the adjustment of the organism to altered conditions — loss of a limb, a chronic disease, a changed life circumstance. Adaptation is not defeat; it is the organism's achievement of a new equilibrium.

Wound healing is Weil's model for all healing: clean the wound, let the body do its work, do not obstruct. He finds no evidence that any substance accelerates wound healing — only evidence that substances (iodine, hydrogen peroxide, herbal salves) delay it by irritating tissue. The principal conclusion: medicine's job is to remove obstructions, not to give the body something it lacks.

## Part IV: The Array of Therapeutic Choices

Weil surveys every major system:

### Allopathic Medicine

Strengths: emergencies, trauma, acute infections, surgical repair. Weak points:
- **Naming disease** gives patients an object to hand their fear to but obscures the individuality of illness
- **Battle/war imagery** (killing germs, fighting cancer, defeating disease) encourages aggressive treatment and ignores the patient's inner resources
- **Overuse of drugs**: most interactions between people and pathogens produce no disease; drugs are given before susceptibility is addressed
- **Patient dependence**: the system structure discourages self-responsibility

### Homeopathy, Osteopathy, Naturopathy, Chinese Medicine

Treated with serious historical attention. Chinese medicine's five-element theory and twelve-organ model are presented as a sophisticated coherent system that allopathy has refused to study. Naturopathy's emphasis on supporting the body's healing capacity (hygiene, diet, rest) is aligned with Weil's own framework.

### Shamanism, Faith Healing, Psychic Healing

The shaman as the 20,000-year original practitioner: using altered states (theta-wave drumming, plant medicines, fasting) to enter the patient's psyche and address causes invisible to ordinary perception. Faith healing at Lourdes: Jerome Frank's *Persuasion and Healing* analysis — the effective ingredient is the patient's investment of belief (the "Lourdes phenomenon": distance of journey correlates with healing outcome). No native of Lourdes has ever been cured.

Edgar Cayce and José Arigo: Weil examines their cases seriously. Cayce's trance readings accessed medical knowledge beyond his conscious mind's capacity; Arigo performed apparent surgical procedures with a pocket knife to which patients reported no pain and demonstrated no infection. Weil's conclusion: the mechanism is unknown, the phenomena appear real, their medical applications (recommendations of compound remedies, treatments that helped patients) produced results that neither placebo alone nor standard medicine can easily explain. He invokes Jung's collective unconscious as a possible framework for the unconscious knowledge trance states can access.

### Holistic Medicine

Weil is sympathetic but critical. He helped found the American Holistic Medical Association and attended early conferences. His complaints:
- No coherent philosophy; too many incompatible practices under one banner
- "Fantasy of sweetness and light": the tendency to emphasise pleasant therapies and deny the dark side of reality (illness as a manifestation of reality, not an intrusion)
- Applied kinesiology accepted without evidence
- Conferences produce no measurable clinical progress

Weil's model integrative physician: an allopath who uses referrals to acupuncturists, osteopaths, biofeedback therapists, healers, and massage practitioners — making treatment choices based on what the case requires, not what system one belongs to.

## Part V: Mind and Body

This is the philosophical core of the book.

### Wart Cures as Paradigm

Warts — tissue infected with papillomavirus — routinely resolve in response to folk remedies that have no antiviral chemistry: rubbing with a raw potato, a piece of meat, a coin, burying the object at midnight. Hypnosis produces wart cures. Weil himself cured a plantar wart in a student using ice rubbing + daily light visualisation — the wart disappeared in three weeks.

Weil argues that wart cures are the single clearest evidence of a mind-directed immune mechanism:
- The cure involves viral clearance — a real biological event
- The mechanism involves the immune system (antibody-mediated cell destruction)
- The switch is located in the mind: the mechanism can be turned on by belief and expectation
- **Scientists do not study this phenomenon**: dermatologists treat wart cures as amusing anecdotes rather than windows into the most important question in medicine — how the mind directs the immune system

He extends the argument to tumours, calcifications, arterial plaques: if the same mechanism can be directed at cancer cells as at wart cells, the implications are profound.

### Placebo Response

The most detailed treatment in this wiki. See [Placebo Effect](../concepts/placebo-effect.md) for the updated page. Key Weil contributions:

**Five misconceptions doctors hold about placebo response**:
1. Placebo responses cannot be distinguished from treatment effects on a clinical basis → False: they are not distinguished because doctors refuse to try
2. Placebo reactors are a distinct personality type (suggestible, needy, less educated) → False: anyone can respond given the right circumstances
3. Only sugar pills count as placebos → False: all treatments have a placebo component
4. Efficacy requires the patient to believe the treatment is real → Partially false: the belief that works is somatic/gut-level (based on experience and association), not intellectual
5. Placebo effects can be screened out by blinded trials → Partially false: the trial design itself creates placebo conditions

**Nocebo** (negative placebo): voodoo death as the paradigm; overactivity of the parasympathetic branch causes heart stoppage.

**Active placebos**: drugs work partly through pharmacological effects and partly through the beliefs and associations they activate. Weil's marijuana research (1968, Science) showed that naive users who received placebo reported effects learned from experienced users — demonstrating that drug effects are partly socially learned states, not pure pharmacology. This means that the drug "enables focusing of belief," not just the pharmacological action.

### S.R.'s Hodgkin's Disease: A Mind-Mediated Cure

The most fully documented case in the book. S.R., a 26-year-old woman, was diagnosed in 1967 with Stage III Hodgkin's disease. She declined standard radiation, became pregnant, and underwent limited radiation that was insufficient to constitute treatment. She deteriorated to 92 pounds and lost the will to survive.

Her physician arranged a guided LSD session — not as treatment for the cancer but to help her confront death. Under the drug's influence, S.R. had a flash of insight: she realised she was choosing to die. She decided to live. In the weeks following:
- She left her husband and moved back with her parents
- Dropped psychology, enrolled in a different programme
- Began working with Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's dying patients
- Started Gestalt therapy and encounter groups, found a Zen teacher
- Changed her diet (eliminated sugar, added whole foods)
- Began daily meditation and visualisation of energy flowing through her body

She rallied, gave birth to a healthy child, and has had no evidence of disease since 1973 — a 6-year cancer-free period at the time of writing (longer known subsequently). Weil calls it a "documented miracle cure." Her physician: "I have no doubt that people with illness can choose to live or die."

Weil notes: LSD had no capacity to cure Hodgkin's disease. The drug created the conditions for a flash of insight — a conversion experience — that transformed S.R.'s relationship to her life. The medical interventions that followed (radiation, diet, meditation, social restructuring) then had a physiological substrate to work with. The causally active agent was the change in consciousness.

### Powers of Mind

Additional cases of mind-body capacities not explicable by standard mechanisms:
- **Hypnosis**: produces blisters or prevents blisters from contact with objects, depending on the suggestion — demonstrating nerve-mediated control of inflammatory response
- **Biofeedback**: allows conscious regulation of blood flow, heart rate, Raynaud's disease
- **Psychedelic states**: disappearance of allergies and asthma; skin insensitivity to normally painful stimulation; fire walking — all interpreted as evidence of latent capacities that altered states reveal

The common thread: the barrier between conscious intention (cortex) and the brain centres controlling involuntary functions (brainstem, ANS) is not absolute. Belief, set, and setting can bridge it. Medicine does not exploit this because its model excludes consciousness as a determinant of health.

## Part VI: Health and Healing in the Year 2000

### Doctors Must Learn from Physicists

Weil's central epistemological argument: quantum physics overthrew the Newtonian mechanistic model of reality in 1900–1930. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle established that the observer's choice of measurement affects what is measured. Relativity showed that mass and time depend on the observer's state of motion. These results established consciousness as integral to the description of physical reality.

Medicine did not follow. As physics moved to models where consciousness shapes reality, medicine entrenched itself in the mechanistic model that physics had abandoned. Doctors "act as if they were physicists of the 19th century."

Using Einstein's metaphor: a theory is a picture of reality, not reality itself. A new, better theory does not demolish the old barn and replace it with a skyscraper — it reveals more of the landscape, showing the old picture as a special case of a more general one. Newtonian mechanics is a special case of relativity for low velocities.

Weil's argument: medicine needs a theory of health and healing that accommodates consciousness as a determining variable — not as a curiosity but as a central component of the model.

### New Medicine for the New Century

Practical recommendations:
1. Start investigating anomalies: wart cures, placebo responses, spontaneous remissions — these are not curiosities to dismiss but data that reveal the most clinically important mechanisms
2. Use the placebo response deliberately rather than screening it out
3. Teach patients self-responsibility: the physician's role is primarily educational
4. Build integrative practices that draw on all healing systems based on what each case requires
5. Abandon the fantasy that medicine is a science on the model of physics — accept it as an art drawing on science, with mysteries at its core

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths**: The etymological argument for health-as-wholeness is philosophically original and historically grounded. The nine principles are empirically derived from clinical observation and consistent with subsequent research. The placebo analysis anticipates Benson's "remembered wellness" and Dossey's "meaning as primary variable" by a decade.

**Limitations**: 
- Weil's case against applied kinesiology and Bach flower remedies is fair, but his enthusiasm for homeopathy's anomaly status exceeds the evidence (Boyd's studies were not replicated)
- The S.R. case, though compelling, is a single case report without control comparison
- His critique of holistic medicine's incoherence applies partly to his own synthesis: nine principles do not constitute a testable theory

**Note**: Mainstream medicine has since incorporated mind-body research (PNI, biofeedback, mindfulness-based stress reduction) more extensively than Weil's 1983 prognosis suggested was likely. However, the placebo response remains largely unexploited as a deliberate therapeutic tool, and consciousness is still not a central variable in medical education.
