# The Heart of Man: Its Genius for Good and Evil

**Summary**: Erich Fromm's 1964 psychoanalytic study of human destructiveness, arguing that evil is rooted in three convergent orientations — necrophilia (love of death and the inanimate), malignant narcissism, and incestuous-symbiotic fixation — which together form the "syndrome of decay," opposed by a "syndrome of growth" built from biophilia, mature love, and independence.

**Sources**:
- [Fromm, E. (1964). *The heart of man: Its genius for good and evil*. Harper & Row.](heart-of-man.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

## Overview

Published as Volume XII of Ruth Nanda Anshen's *Religious Perspectives* series, *The Heart of Man* is Fromm's most concentrated statement on the psychological sources of destructiveness — revising and, in his own account, freeing from a purely biological frame the Freudian concept of the death instinct. Fromm insists he is not a "Freudian" in the school sense but is trying to complete Freud's discoveries within a humanist rather than nineteenth-century materialist frame of reference. The book was written under the shadow of the two World Wars, the Holocaust, and the nuclear standoff of the early Cold War, and its closing chapter applies the analysis directly to contemporary "present-day moral problems."

## Man: Wolf or Sheep?

Fromm opens by rejecting both halves of a standard dichotomy — that humans are either innately savage ("wolves") or a docile majority manipulated by a violent minority ("sheep"). Both views, he argues, mistake obedience and social conformity for an explanation of atrocity. Drawing on the Old Testament account of the Fall, he reads Adam and Eve's disobedience not as corruption but as the necessary first act of human self-awareness and freedom — the beginning of history rather than its ruin.

## Forms of Violence

Before turning to pathology, Fromm distinguishes several forms of violence by their underlying motive rather than their outward form: **playful** violence (skill display, without intent to destroy), **reactive** violence (defense of life, freedom, or dignity — including violence provoked by frustration, envy, or revenge), **compensatory** violence (substituting destruction for a creative potency the person cannot otherwise exercise — illustrated by Camus's Caligula), and **archaic blood-thirst** (violence as ritual fusion with life through killing, illustrated at length through Flaubert's *Legend of St. Julian the Hospitaler*). Only the last categories shade into the deeper pathology the book is centrally concerned with: necrophilia.

## Necrophilia and Biophilia

See [Necrophilia and Biophilia](../concepts/necrophilia-and-biophilia.md) for the full treatment. In brief: Fromm proposes a fundamental character polarity between **necrophilia** (the love of death, decay, and the inanimate — a passion to transform the organic into the mechanical, the living into the controlled) and **biophilia** (the love of life, growth, and structure). He illustrates necrophilia through Hitler, Himmler, and Eichmann, and — more controversially — through a reading of [Carl Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s own death-preoccupied dreams and behavior recorded in *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, which Fromm treats as an example of the orientation held in productive balance rather than allowed to dominate.

## Individual and Social Narcissism

See [Individual and Group Narcissism](../concepts/individual-and-group-narcissism.md). Fromm extends Freud's concept of narcissism from its original clinical, individual-pathology setting to groups — nations, races, religions, classes — arguing that group narcissism functions psychologically like individual narcissism (an inflated self-image requiring defense against any evidence that would puncture it) while being far more dangerous, since it recruits the otherwise healthy loyalty and self-esteem needs of ordinary members.

## Incestuous Ties

See [Incestuous-Symbiotic Fixation](../concepts/incestuous-symbiotic-fixation.md). Fromm reworks Freud's Oedipal/mother-fixation theory, separating the craving for maternal protection, certainty, and unconditional belonging from specifically sexual desire, and extending "mother" to clan, nation, race, and soil as symbolic objects of the same fixation. He distinguishes a benign, non-pathological form (ordinary attachment) from a malignant form he calls **symbiosis** — the wish to dissolve the boundary between self and the fixation-object entirely, most extremely expressed as a longing to return to the womb.

## The Syndrome of Decay and the Syndrome of Growth

Fromm's central diagnostic claim is that necrophilia, malignant narcissism, and incestuous-symbiotic fixation are not independent traits but **converge** in the most destructive personalities, reinforcing one another into what he calls the **syndrome of decay** — his clinical portrait of Hitler is built explicitly around this convergence. Its opposite, the **syndrome of growth**, is the convergence of biophilia, mature (object-related, non-narcissistic) love, and independence. Most people, in Fromm's account, sit somewhere between the two poles rather than at either extreme.

## Freedom, Determinism, and Alternativism

The closing chapter argues against both strict determinism and libertarian free will, proposing instead what Fromm calls **alternativism**: humans are constrained by character structure and circumstance to a limited, genuinely narrowing set of live possibilities at any given moment, and freedom consists in the effort of *awareness* — of one's own motives, of the situation, of the consequences of choice — that keeps more than one of those possibilities actually open. He illustrates the narrowing with the image of a smoker rationalizing each cigarette, and with an extended discussion of the "hardened heart" of Pharaoh, arguing that repeated refusals of self-examination progressively close off the capacity to choose the good, until at the extreme (his examples: Hitler, Stalin) no freedom of choice remains at all. He reads Spinoza, Marx, and Freud as sharing this same position beneath their surface labels as "determinists."

## Connections to the Wiki

Fromm's analysis is the explicit theoretical source for [M. Scott Peck](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md)'s clinical psychology of evil — Peck names Fromm as one of his two principal teachers on the subject (the other being [Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md)) and draws his concept of malignant narcissism directly from this book. See [Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)](../concepts/human-evil.md) for the fuller comparison of Fromm's and Peck's frameworks, and for how both relate to [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s treatment of evil as [*privatio boni*](../concepts/privatio-boni.md) in *[Aion](aion.md)*.

Fromm's incestuous-symbiotic fixation is a direct, partly convergent, partly diverging counterpart to Jungian [individuation](../concepts/individuation.md): both frame psychological maturity as a hard-won separation from an original fusion state, but Fromm derives the fixation from the literal mother-infant bond and its symbolic extensions (clan, nation, race), while Jung frames the parallel movement as differentiation from the [collective unconscious](../concepts/collective-unconscious.md) and the [persona](../concepts/persona.md) rather than from a specific familial attachment.
