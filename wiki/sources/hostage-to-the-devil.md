# Hostage to the Devil

**Summary**: Malachi Martin's account of five pseudonymous cases of demonic possession and Roman Catholic exorcism in mid-twentieth-century America, framed by his own theological argument that possession is a distinct process of gradual, consenting surrender to an external malevolent entity — not reducible to mental illness — and structured by a named sequence of stages (Presence, Pretense, Breakpoint, Voice, Clash, Expulsion).

**Sources**:
- [Martin, M. (1992). *Hostage to the devil: The possession and exorcism of five living Americans* (Rev. ed.). HarperSanFrancisco. (Original work published 1976)](hostage-to-the-devil.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

## Overview

*Hostage to the Devil* is Malachi Martin's report on five real, pseudonymously disguised cases of alleged demonic possession and exorcism that he investigated in the United States. First published in 1976, it became one of the most influential popular accounts of possession and exorcism in English, shaping both public imagination and — more consequentially for this wiki — the vocabulary later writers on the subject use, including [M. Scott Peck](../thinkers/m-scott-peck.md) (see [Possession and Exorcism (Peck)](../concepts/possession-and-exorcism.md)). This edition carries a new 1992 preface in which Martin, writing from New York, describes what he sees as a worsening climate of possession and institutional Church reluctance to respond to it.

The book opens with a long framing essay laying out Martin's theological and clinical argument, closes each case with reflection on what it illustrates, and bookends the five main cases with a short prologue and epilogue account of an earlier exorcism (Father Michael Strong's 1948 exorcism of "Thomas Wu" in wartime Nanking and Hong Kong), used to establish the lifelong cost exorcism exacts from the exorcist himself.

## The Five Cases

Each case pairs a possessed person (given a pseudonym and a symbolic entity-name for the possessing spirit) with an exorcist:

1. **Marianne K.** ("Zio, Friend, Smiler" as the possessing presence) — a young woman drawn into what Martin frames as an intellectual/mystical seduction ("the Man," an apparition met in adolescence) that escalated into total personality dissolution; exorcised by **Father Peter**, mentored by the Rome-based **Father Conor**.
2. **Father Jonathan** ("Father Bones, Mister Natch") — a priest whose possession, in Martin's telling, grew out of doctrinal and pastoral drift following the Second Vatican Council; exorcised by the anthropologist-priest **Father David M. ("Bones")**.
3. **Richard/Rita** ("Virgin Girl, Fixer") — a case Martin frames around confusion of gender and sexuality; exorcised by **Father Gerald**.
4. **Jamsie Z.** ("Uncle Ponto, Mushroom, Souper") — a radio personality whose possession Martin connects to loneliness and consenting companionship with the entity; exorcist **Father Mark A.**
5. **Carl V.** ("Rooster, Tortoise") — a case involving intellectual pride and parapsychological interests; exorcist unnamed in the material read for this ingest in the same detail as the other four.

Martin reports that none of the five human subjects were, by the book's account, permanently destroyed by the experience; short epilogue notes on their later lives are offered (e.g., one became a hermit noted for holiness; another had a long career in radio).

## Martin's Framework

See [Martin's Stages of Possession](../concepts/possession-process-martin.md) for the detailed content: a four-stage process of consenting entry (Entry, Preparatory Judgments, Yielding Control, Extended Control) that Martin holds precedes possession over months or years, and the six named stages of the exorcism ritual itself (Presence, Pretense, Breakpoint, Voice, Clash, Expulsion) attributed in the text to Father Conor's teaching.

Martin insists throughout that possession is a *process*, not an event, that it requires ongoing consent from the possessed person (a "disposing factor" — involvement with occult practices, spiritualist tools like the Ouija board, or, in Martin's account, contemplative methods he considers doctrinally suspect such as the Enneagram — creates a psychological "vacuum" that the entity can occupy, but the will must continue to assent at each stage), and that it is diagnostically and phenomenologically distinct from dissociative or psychotic illness, though he acknowledges many referred cases turn out to be exactly that.

## Critique

**Evidentiary status**: Martin's claims — a literal, discrete, non-human malevolent entity capable of taking over volition and producing physical signs (stench, cold, poltergeist-like phenomena) — are entirely outside what mainstream psychiatry, clinical psychology, or academic religious studies treat as evidence-based. The cases are presented as personal reporting and reconstructed interviews, not clinical documentation; no independent verification of the physical phenomena described is offered or possible from the text itself.

**The standard differential diagnosis**: The clinical phenomena Martin describes — dramatic shifts in apparent personality and voice, dissociative "voices," self-injurious behavior, extreme distress — overlap substantially with the presentation of dissociative identity disorder, psychotic illness, and culturally-shaped dissociative/conversion syndromes (a category recognized cross-culturally in psychiatric literature as "possession trance" and included descriptively, without endorsing the possession explanation, in diagnostic manuals). Martin is aware of this alternative and argues his cases were referred only after such explanations were exhausted — but the book offers no case records, testing data, or independent clinical corroboration by which a reader could adjudicate that claim.

**Martin's own credibility**: Malachi Martin's reliability as a narrator is independently contested — see [Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md) for the biographical detail bearing on this. Several claims across his career (about Vatican sources, secret documents, and insider access) were disputed by colleagues and journalists during his lifetime, which should inform how much evidentiary weight a reader gives his first-person case reconstructions here, independent of the question of possession's reality.

**Cultural shaping**: As with the [Possession and Exorcism (Peck)](../concepts/possession-and-exorcism.md) material this book directly influenced, the specifically Roman Catholic idiom of the phenomena described (Latin rite, crucifix, holy water, the name of Jesus) is consistent with the culturally-relative character possession phenomena take worldwide — a pattern that does not by itself disprove Martin's ontological claims, but is the kind of evidence that mainstream religious studies and anthropology treat as central rather than incidental.
