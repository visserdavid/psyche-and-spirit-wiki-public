# Imagery in Healing

**Summary**: Jeanne Achterberg's 1985 synthesis of shamanism, the history of imagination in medicine, and the emerging psychoneuroimmunological research — arguing that mental imagery is not a complement to healing but its original and most fundamental vehicle, and providing empirical evidence that imagery of cancer and the immune system predicts disease course with clinical accuracy.

**Sources**:
- [Achterberg, J. (1985). *Imagery in healing: Shamanism and modern medicine*. Shambhala.](imagery-in-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Overview

*Imagery in Healing* is structured as both historical survey and research report. Achterberg argues that imagination — the capacity to form internal representations that cross sensory boundaries — is the primary healing mechanism in all cultures and all eras of medicine. The book moves from Paleolithic shamanism (20,000 years of documented practice) through Greek medicine, medieval women healers, Renaissance Paracelsus, and modern clinical applications (hypnosis, biofeedback, childbirth preparation, nursing, oncology), then presents Achterberg's own laboratory findings linking mental imagery to immune cell function and cancer prognosis.

The collaboration with psychologist and statistician [G. Frank Lawlis](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md) is central throughout: Achterberg contributes the conceptual synthesis and clinical observation; Lawlis contributes the research design and statistical analysis of the cancer imagery studies.

## Two Types of Imagery in Healing

Achterberg distinguishes two fundamentally different assumptions about how imagery heals:

**Self-directed imagery** (Type 1): The patient's own imagination affects their own body — through neurological, endocrine, and immune pathways that are now partially mapped. The mechanism is intra-personal: imagery activates the same neural circuits as actual sensory experience, triggering corresponding physiological responses. This is the domain of biofeedback, relaxation training, Simonton visualisation, and Achterberg's own Image CA tool.

**Transpersonal imagery** (Type 2): The assumption that information or healing intention can be transmitted from one consciousness to another without physical contact — the domain of the shaman's healing journey, intercessory prayer, and remote healing. This cannot currently be explained by the pathways that account for Type 1 imagery. Achterberg does not dismiss it; she argues it requires a different evidentiary standard (historical, anthropological, and phenomenological rather than laboratory-experimental) and a different ontological framework.

## The Shaman as Prototype Healer (Chapter 1)

Achterberg places the shaman at the origin point of all healing using imagination. Shamanism predates all other recorded healing traditions by at least 15,000 years (cave paintings at Les Trois Frères, France, ~17,000 BCE show a figure interpreted as a shaman). It is found on every inhabited continent; its core features recur across cultures with no documented contact.

**Core features of shamanic practice**:
- Entry into an altered state of consciousness (the Shamanic State of Consciousness, SSC) — voluntarily and with the intent to heal
- Access to a "spirit world" or "non-ordinary reality" (Harner's terminology) in which diagnostic and therapeutic information is obtained
- Return from that state with specific healing knowledge applicable to the patient in ordinary reality
- Spirit guides or power animals as sources of knowledge and protection
- Community participation: healing is a communal event, not a private transaction

**The SSC and modern research**: Michael Harner's work (and Charles Tart's consciousness classification) identifies the SSC as a voluntarily entered and exited state distinct from sleep, dreaming, hypnosis, and psychosis. Key distinction: the schizophrenic is overwhelmed by non-ordinary experience and cannot re-enter ordinary reality at will; the shaman masters the boundary between realities. EEG research on out-of-body states and sensory deprivation (REST: Restricted Environmental Stimulation Therapy) suggests theta wave activity (4–7 Hz, associated with creativity, imagery, and reverie) is a correlate of SSC-like states.

**Ritual induction methods**: Sweat lodges and saunas (heat-induced altered states), fasting and sleep deprivation, drumming at 4–7 beats/second (driving theta rhythms — documented by EEG studies), plant medicines (ayahuasca, peyote, datura, Psilocybe mushrooms), sensory deprivation (darkness, isolation), and intense physical exertion (spirit dancing, as in the Salish Indians' ceremonial).

LeShan's "reality 2" (the consciousness framework used by healers and mystics across traditions) is identified as the secular-scientific description of the same state. See [Shamanic Healing](../concepts/shamanic-healing.md).

## History of Imagery in Medicine (Chapter 2)

Achterberg traces the "golden thread" of imagination through Western medical history:

- **Asclepius and dream incubation**: Greek healing temples (Asclepia) used incubation sleep — fasting, ritual preparation, sleeping in the temple to receive diagnostic and therapeutic images from the god — as primary medicine. Cures documented on stone votives include vision restored, paralysis reversed, infertility resolved.
- **Aristotle, Hippocrates, Galen**: All used imagery diagnostically. Galen documented the circularity of humours and images — images influence humoral states which influence further image production.
- **Medieval women healers and the witch trials**: Achterberg argues that the persecution of women healers (estimated 100,000–9 million killed between 1500–1650) represented the systematic suppression of an imagination-based healing tradition that competed with the emerging male-guild medicine and the Church's monopoly on legitimate healing. Women practising herbal medicine with incantations and ritual were the direct successors of the shamanic tradition.
- **Paracelsus (1493–1541)**: Recovered the principle of imagination as healing agent from women healers and folk medicine. "Man is his own doctor. The physician finds healing herbs in his own garden." Stated explicitly that imagination could produce and cure disease.
- **Cartesian split**: Descartes' assertion of mind-body dualism gave medicine permission to dissect the body without fear of damaging the soul — but severed the healing tradition from its imagination-based core.

## Modern Clinical Uses (Chapter 3)

The "shaman scientists" — practitioners who combine ancient wisdom with modern science:

**Childbirth**: Grantly Dick Read (1942) argued that pain in childbirth was amplified by fear-induced muscle tension and that imagery could interrupt the fear-pain cycle. Lamaze extended this as "psychoprophylaxis." Dick Read's evidence: blood flow to the uterus is restricted by fear-induced vasoconstriction, causing ischaemic pain; relaxation and breathing imagery restore blood flow.

**Autogenic training** (Schultz and Luthe, 7-volume series): Six standardised imagery-relaxation exercises progressively covering arms, legs, heartbeat, breathing, solar plexus, and forehead. Documented effects on asthma, headache, diabetes, arthritis, dental procedures. Changes measured include muscle tension, skin temperature, blood sugar, blood pressure, hormone secretion, and brain waves.

**Nursing** (Jean Johnson): Providing "sensory information" — imagery of what procedures will feel and sound like — before surgery reduced hospital stays and reduced use of pain medication in cholecystectomy, cast removal, and endoscopy patients.

**Therapeutic Touch** (Delores Krieger, New York University): Achterberg notes research showing changes in blood chemistry and haemoglobin following TT. [Pat Heidt](../concepts/mental-imagery-healing.md) combined TT with imagery; [Gretchen Randolph](../concepts/mental-imagery-healing.md) integrated biofeedback.

**Biofeedback**: Instrumentation that provides real-time feedback on physiological states (brain waves, muscle tension, skin temperature, heart rate) — allowing voluntary learning of processes thought to be beyond conscious control. Achterberg treats biofeedback as essentially a technology for learning to communicate with the body via imagery: the instrument teaches the patient what the desired state feels like, then imagery sustains and generalises that state without instrumentation.

**The physicians**: Simonton and Simonton (cancer imagery), Dean Ornish (imagery in cardiac rehabilitation), Joe Goldstritch, Larry Dossey, Michael Samuels (*Seeing with the Mind's Eye*), Irving Oyle (incubation sleep adapted to modern clinical settings), Norman Shealy.

**Burn unit research** (Achterberg and Kenner, NIH-funded): 149 burned patients assigned to relaxation, relaxation + imagery, relaxation + imagery + biofeedback, or control. Imagery groups required less pain medication, less sedative, less sleep medication, and showed lower muscle tension (EMG) than control or relaxation-only groups. The relaxation + imagery combination was most effective; biofeedback added limited benefit beyond imagery alone.

## The Physiology and Biochemistry of Imagery (Chapter 4)

**Images are physiologically real**: Edmund Jacobsen (1929) demonstrated via EMG that imagining a movement produces measurable muscle activation in the relevant muscle group. Imagining eating a lemon produces salivation. Athletes who mentally rehearse performance show physiological changes identical to those produced by actual practice. The motor cortex cannot distinguish between vividly imagined and actually performed movement.

**The holographic brain model** (Karl Pribram): Memory is not stored in specific locations but distributed holographically across the brain. This explains why damage to one area does not eliminate specific memories — each part of the brain contains enough information to reconstruct the whole. Achterberg uses this model to explain how imagery changes physiology: an image activates a distributed pattern across the brain, which then reverberates through the autonomic nervous system to target organs.

**Hemisphere specialisation**: The right hemisphere specialises in imagery storage and retrieval. The left hemisphere processes language and sequential logic. Achterberg proposes that imagery is the right hemisphere's language — the medium through which emotional and physiological information is processed before it reaches the language-based left hemisphere. Cutting the corpus callosum (split-brain studies) disrupts this translation, producing alexithymia-like symptoms: emotions are felt but cannot be expressed in language and instead express through the body.

**The stress pathway (Selye)**: Perceived threat → hypothalamic activation → ACTH release → cortisol and corticosterone → immune suppression. Prolonged stress produces sustained immune compromise, creating conditions for cancer, infection, and autoimmune disorders. The hypothalamus serves as the convergence point for emotional (cortical) input and autonomic (systemic) output.

**Endorphins and enkephalins**: Discovered 1973; produced by the body in response to pain, stress, and — notably — positive emotional states. Beta-endorphin enhances T cell proliferation (Gilman et al.); enkephalins increase T cell percentages and invigorate their activity against cancer cells (Plotnikoff, reported 1982). Goldstein (1980): music produces "thrills" (tingling, goosebumps) associated with endorphin release, and these thrills are attenuated by naloxone (an opioid blocker) — suggesting music-induced imagery produces real endorphin activity.

## The Immune Odyssey (Chapter 6)

**Ader and Cohen's conditioned immune suppression**: Robert Ader (University of Rochester, 1975) paired cyclophosphamide (an immunosuppressive drug) with saccharin solution in rats. After conditioning, saccharin alone suppressed immunity — and rats that continued drinking it died at higher rates than expected. The immune system can be trained via Pavlovian conditioning; it "learns" context-dependent responses exactly as other physiological systems do. This established that the immune system is not autonomous but responds to learned psychological associations.

**Brain → immune system pathways**: Karen Bulloch (1981) identified projections from the brainstem and spinal cord to the thymus gland in mice and rats — direct neural innervation of the primary immune organ. Bardos et al.: hemisphere cortex lateralisation controls NK cell reactivity. The hypothalamus → pituitary → immune axis provides the biochemical pathway.

**Cancer as immune disorder**: Achterberg develops the surveillance theory of cancer: T killer cells normally identify and destroy mutant cells before they multiply. When the immune system is chronically suppressed (by stress, cortisol, learned helplessness), surveillance fails and cancer is allowed to establish itself. This is not a speculative theory but the basis of current immunotherapy approaches.

**The Achterberg-Lawlis Image CA studies**: See [Mental Imagery and Healing](../concepts/mental-imagery-healing.md) for full detail. Brief summary: 200 cancer patients' imagery (of their cancer, their immune cells, and their treatment) was assessed via structured interview and drawing. Imagery scores predicted two-month outcomes at 93% accuracy. Twelve correlations were found between imagery dimensions and blood chemistry / haematology variables.

**Hall hypnosis study**: Howard Hall (Pennsylvania State University) used hypnosis to ask 20 male subjects to visualise their white blood cells as "sharks attacking germs." T cell function increased significantly in young subjects (under 50) at one week post-procedure — effect not sustained in the older group, attributed to age-related immune suppression. Study limitation: small N, no randomisation.

**Peavey biofeedback study** (1982, award-winning at Biofeedback Society of America): 16 stressed subjects assigned to biofeedback-assisted relaxation program or control. Biofeedback subjects showed improved neutrophil phagocytic function; controls did not. Neutrophil count was unchanged — the effect was on function, not number, suggesting quality rather than quantity of immune response was altered.

**Smith and Schneider imagery study** (Michigan State University, 1983): 16 subjects asked to imagine neutrophils leaving the blood vessel walls and exiting the bloodstream. Blood neutrophil count dropped from 8,200 to 6,400 per ml (significant at p<0.0001); 60% of the circulating neutrophils left the bloodstream during the imagery session. In a follow-up, subjects asked to image neutrophils staying in the bloodstream showed increased adherence (from 28% to 56%). This is the most direct demonstration in the literature that specific imagery directly controls specific immune cell behaviour.

## The Achterberg-Lawlis Cancer Imagery Research

### The Image CA Tool

Developed from interviews with 200 cancer patients at Simonton's Cancer Counseling and Research Center (Fort Worth) and the Cancer Rehabilitation Clinic at Parkland Memorial Hospital (Dallas). Assessment procedure:

1. Patient is guided through a relaxation induction (20 minutes)
2. Asked to describe (via interview and drawing) three things: the cancer cells; the white blood cells and immune system fighting the disease; the treatment and what it is doing
3. Images are scored on 14 dimensions: vividness, activity, and strength of cancer cells; vividness, activity, and strength of white blood cells; relative size and number of cancer vs. immune cells; strength and effectiveness of treatment; symbolism used; integration of the three imagery components; regularity of imagery practice; and outcome prognosis ventured by the patient

### Key Findings

- 93% accuracy predicting two-month outcomes (death vs. deterioration vs. remission) from imagery scores alone
- 100% accuracy for identifying patients who would die within two months
- Imagery scores predicted outcome independently of blood chemistry variables (which measured current state, not trajectory)
- Trestman's analysis found 12 correlations between Image CA dimensions and haematology/blood chemistry variables
- Imagery used for laryngectomy rehabilitation, mastectomy recovery, and cancer prognosis confirmed predictive validity across populations

### What the Imagery Revealed

Achterberg analyses the symbolic content:
- **Archetypal fighter imagery** (knights, Vikings, Sir Richard): associated with best outcomes
- **Predatory animal imagery** (sharks, bears, mean dogs): associated with best immune response
- **Peaceful, gentle imagery** (snowflakes, clouds): not associated with positive prognosis
- **Cancer images**: when cancer was symbolised by crabs, ants, or underwater creatures (blood-cell-scale representations), outcomes were better than when cancer was seen as vast or overwhelming
- The imagery must tell a coherent story: the treatment must be seen as effective against the specific cancer being imaged; the immune cells must be clearly larger, more numerous, and more powerful than the cancer cells

Achterberg is explicit that forcing "killer" imagery (if it feels incongruent or is dismissed as "gore") is counter-productive. The imagery must arise authentically from the patient's own symbolic system.

## Spontaneous Remission and Attitude

Citing Eric Peper and Ken Pelletier's bibliography of 400+ documented spontaneous remission cases, Achterberg identifies the single most consistent factor: **a change in attitude** — described variably as hope, renewed will to live, renewed sense of purpose, or entry into prayer or contemplative practice. This parallels what Pelletier independently identified in his survivor interviews and what LeShan argues is the re-activation of the patient's "life meaning connection." The immune mechanism that translates attitude change into tumour regression is not fully specified but is consistent with the endorphin-immunity and conditioned-immunity findings: attitude change alters the neuroendocrine-immune communication that either suppresses or enables surveillance.

## Critique

**The text quality and compression**: The raw text is heavily compressed (63% compression ratio per the metadata), removing punctuation, articles, and some sentence structure. This affects quotation reliability; the ideas are intact but specific quotes should be verified against the original.

**Study sizes and designs**: The Hall hypnosis study (N=20), Peavey biofeedback study (N=16), and Smith/Schneider neutrophil study (N=16) are small and should be regarded as preliminary. The Image CA studies at Parkland and Simonton's centre are more substantial (200 patients) but are correlational, not randomised. Achterberg does not oversell the evidence; she presents it as "promising" and calls for replication.

**The transpersonal claim**: Achterberg distinguishes Type 2 (transpersonal) imagery from Type 1 (self-directed) and declines to dismiss it, but does not provide laboratory evidence for it. This is methodologically honest but potentially frustrating to readers seeking a unified evidential framework.

**Causal direction**: Imagery predicting cancer outcomes does not establish that changing imagery causes outcome improvement. Patients who feel they are recovering may generate more positive imagery; the imagery may track the immune state rather than cause it. Achterberg is aware of this and treats the imagery as a diagnostic window, not a proven intervention variable.

**Note**: The mainstream scientific consensus supports conditioned immune suppression (Ader replication across species including humans), neural innervation of the thymus (Bulloch, confirmed subsequently), and the role of stress hormones in immune suppression (Selye, extensively replicated). The specific Image CA predictive findings and the Michigan State neutrophil imagery experiments have received limited independent replication and should be regarded as suggestive rather than established.
