# Love, Medicine and Miracles

**Summary**: Siegel's 1986 account of his surgical oncology practice and the ECaP (Exceptional Cancer Patients) group therapy programme he founded; argues that patients' attitudes, will to live, love, and emotional expression measurably influence cancer outcomes, grounded in psychoneuroimmunology research and illustrated by hundreds of clinical cases.

**Sources**:
- [Siegel, B. S. (1986). *Love, medicine and miracles: Lessons learned about self-healing from a surgeon's experience with exceptional patients*. Harper & Row.](love-medicine-miracles.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Overview

*Love, Medicine and Miracles* was published by Harper & Row in 1986. It is Siegel's first book — a synthesis of his clinical experience as a Yale surgical oncologist, his transformation from detached surgeon to emotionally engaged healer, and the observations about cancer survival that led him to found ECaP (Exceptional Cancer Patients) in 1978.

The book's central claim is that exceptional patients — those who defy statistical prognoses — share identifiable psychological traits: they refuse victimhood, assert their needs, express emotion, and engage love as a biological force. Siegel grounds this claim in psychoneuroimmunology research (the immune system is controlled by the brain via the hypothalamus and responds to emotional states), case studies from his practice, and the research of colleagues including O. Carl Simonton, Lawrence LeShan, Elida Evans, Caroline Bedell Thomas, Leonard Derogatis, David McClelland, and Jeanne Achterberg.

The book became an international bestseller and contributed significantly to the mainstreaming of mind-body medicine and patient empowerment in oncology.

## Structure

### Introduction

Siegel opens with his surgical training — his expectation that good outcomes meant technical perfection — and the disillusionment that began when he could not save everyone regardless of skill. The turning point was a 1978 workshop with oncologist O. Carl Simonton and psychologist Stephanie Matthews-Simonton, who introduced psychological techniques (meditation, imagery) for cancer patients. Siegel discovered that his colleagues — doctors — were the only ones not learning at the workshop. He realised medicine had trained him to treat diseases, not people.

He describes founding ECaP with his wife Bobbie and a nurse: they sent letters to 100 patients offering group therapy. Twelve showed up. He began observing three types of patient.

### Part I: Minding the Body (Chapters 1–4)

**Chapter 1 — The Privileged Listener**: Siegel recounts his journey from surgeon-mechanic to what Bobbie Siegel called "the privileged listener" — a physician who hears patients' fears, dreams, premonitions, and emotional truths. Key elements: moving his desk out of the consulting room so patient and doctor sat as equals; encouraging patients to call him by his first name; the ECaP discovery that three distinct patient types exist (see [Exceptional Cancer Patients](../concepts/exceptional-cancer-patients.md)).

**Chapter 2 — The Healing Partnership**: The Mr. Wright/Krebiozen case — a paradigm of meaning-as-medicine, in which a patient's tumours melted with Krebiozen, returned when he read it was worthless, vanished again after saline injections presented as "superrefined Krebiozen," and led to his death within days of the AMA's final condemnation of the drug. Siegel draws out the implications: the placebo response is not a nuisance to control but the healing force medicine should harness. The doctor-patient relationship is the primary vehicle for mobilising this force. Trust, hope, information-sharing, and humour all matter physiologically.

**Chapter 3 — Disease of the Mind**: The biology of the mind-body connection. The hypothalamus regulates body maintenance processes and receives nerve fibres from higher brain regions — meaning psychological events reach the body via the hypothalamus. The immune system (T cells, B cells, natural killer cells) is networked with the brain via nerves to the thymus and spleen, and responds to neuropeptides. Evidence: Ader and Cohen's conditioning of immune suppression; Riley's mouse experiments (stress-raised cancer rates from 7% to 92%); animal studies showing psychological state determines whether implanted tumour cells take hold.

Stress (Selye's 1936 framework) releases cortisol and adrenalin that suppress immune function. This is adaptive for acute physical threats but destructive when sustained chronically by psychological despair, grief, or loss of meaning. Siegel reviews the literature on social factors: divorced people have dramatically elevated cancer and heart disease rates compared to married people; men who smoke have one third the lung cancer rate of men who smoke and lose their jobs.

The chapter also presents Siegel's cancer personality profile (derived from LeShan, Evans 1926, Caroline Bedell Thomas's Johns Hopkins 30-year study): a person who lacked emotional closeness in childhood, came to rely on external validation, invested meaning in a single relationship or role, and when that meaning disappeared experienced quiet desperation — unexpressed grief and loss rather than overt depression.

**Chapter 4 — The Will to Live**: Siegel's tool for assessing and strengthening the patient's will to live: four diagnostic questions (see [Exceptional Cancer Patients](../concepts/exceptional-cancer-patients.md)). He discusses the role of denial, stoicism, and quiet desperation as separate failure modes. He reviews Derogatis's 1979 finding that 35 breast cancer long-term survivors were rated by their physicians as difficult — they expressed emotions, asked questions, and formed real relationships, rather than performing compliance. He presents the role of imagery and drawings in revealing the unconscious, citing Susan Bach's work and Achterberg and Lawlis's finding that imagery predicted disease state more accurately than blood chemistry in 126 cancer patients.

### Part II: Embodying the Mind (Chapters 5–9)

**Chapter 5 — Beginning the Journey**: The practical ECaP programme — what patients are asked to do, how the group sessions work, and why Siegel believes therapy supplemented by nutrition, exercise, play, and laughter constitutes a treatment programme. Discussion of how to work with radiation and chemotherapy through belief and imagery, minimising side effects.

**Chapter 6 — Focusing the Mind for Healing**: The techniques — relaxation, meditation, and the relationship between them. Siegel discusses how the alpha state (relaxed brain wave pattern) is the foundation for communicating with deeper levels of the mind. Herbert Benson's research on the relaxation response; Alberto Villoldo's study showing meditation increased immune hormone levels 100%; Joan Borysenko's work on meditation reducing insulin requirements in diabetics.

McClelland's research at Harvard on the immune effects of different motivations: individuals motivated by power have lower salivary IgA than those motivated by love and service to others. This provides the immunological mechanism by which love heals — not as a metaphor but as a measurable endocrine-immune event.

**Chapter 7 — Images of Disease and Healing**: Achterberg and Lawlis's imagery assessment used to predict disease trajectory (95% accuracy in predicting death vs. remission in 200 patients). The history of imagery in healing from Levi-Strauss through the Simontons. Siegel's preference for imagery that heals rather than attacks: most cancer patients are disturbed by warfare imagery; instead, ECaP uses imagery of digestion, love, light, and animals to represent the immune system.

**Chapter 8 — Becoming**: The psychological journey from compliant patient to self-actualised person. Al Siebert's research on survivor personalities (building on Benedict and Maslow): survivors show complexity of character, child curiosity, playfulness, willingness to make mistakes, nonconformity, ability to absorb experiences without being destroyed, and the capacity for serendipity — converting what others call accidents into gifts. Self-respect and creativity; independence and assertiveness; the "problem patient" as the healer's term for the survivor. The survivor personality chapter includes Siegel's four faiths: faith in oneself, one's physician, one's treatment, and one's spiritual path.

**Chapter 9 — Love and Death**: Death is not failure; dying is possible with agency, choice, and love. Siegel presents cases of patients who chose the timing of their deaths and patients who died peacefully having resolved their unfinished business. The unconditional love section: love raises IgA (McClelland), reduces atherosclerosis risk 50% in rabbits (Cornhill and Levesque), predicts angina pectoris better than any other variable in 10,000 Israeli men (Medalie and Goldbourt — "Does your wife show you love?"). The chapter also covers near-death communication, precognition among patients and family members, and Siegel's spiritual framework.

## Key Thinkers Referenced

- **[O. Carl Simonton](../thinkers/o-carl-simonton.md)**: Oncologist who pioneered imagery-based cancer treatment; the 1978 Portland workshop that transformed Siegel; co-author of *Getting Well Again* (1978)
- **[Lawrence LeShan](../thinkers/lawrence-leshan.md)**: Psychologist who conducted depth therapy with 71 cancer patients over three decades; identified the cancer personality profile (lack of closeness in childhood, quiet desperation, loss of meaning); cancer as response to the loss of one's "life meaning connection"
- **Elida Evans**: Jungian-trained psychologist whose 1926 *A Psychological Study of Cancer* first systematically described the cancer personality type; borrowed only six times from Yale Medical Library in fifty years
- **Caroline Bedell Thomas**: Johns Hopkins physician who began profiling medical students in 1946 and tracked them for decades; found that students who suppressed emotional expression were more likely to develop cancer; predicted disease location from students' drawings
- **Leonard Derogatis**: Psychologist whose 1979 study of 35 breast cancer patients found long-term survivors were judged difficult by their physicians — they expressed emotions and formed authentic relationships rather than performing compliance
- **[Hans Selye](../thinkers/hans-selye.md)**: Developed the stress concept in 1936; his own cancer (reticulum cell sarcoma) at age 65 serves as a case study in choosing to fight rather than surrender
- **David McClelland**: Harvard psychologist who found that watching a film of Mother Teresa raised IgA levels in subjects, even those who disliked her; altruistic motivation increases immune function
- **Jeanne Achterberg** and **G. Frank Lawlis**: Developed imagery assessment tools for cancer patients; achieved 95% accuracy predicting death vs. remission
- **Al Siebert**: Psychologist who studied survivor personalities, beginning with Korean War paratrooper veterans; described the hierarchy of needs from safety through self-actualisation to synergy
- **Susan Bach**: Jungian therapist who spent 30 years developing interpretation of patients' drawings as diagnostic and prognostic windows
- **[Viktor Frankl](../thinkers/viktor-frankl.md)**: Psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor; his observation that concentration camp guards spared prisoners with a spark of life in their eyes is cited as evidence that love protects against death
- **[Herbert Benson](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md)**: Harvard cardiologist whose relaxation response research provides the physiological foundation for meditation practice in ECaP
- **[Robert Becker](../thinkers/robert-becker.md)**: Surgeon whose research on bioelectricity provides a mechanism for hypnotic healing — hypnotised patients produce measurable voltage changes in specified body areas, which control chemical healing processes

## Relationship to the Wiki's Core Themes

Siegel's work intersects with the wiki's central frameworks at multiple points:

**With Myss and Shealy**: The foreword to [*The Creation of Health*](creation-of-health.md) established the direct relationship. Siegel's cancer personality profile maps directly onto Myss's insight that "biography is biology." His ECaP survivor characteristics parallel Shealy's account of the fourteen healing attitudes. The shared principle: health is not the absence of disease but the presence of authentic selfhood, love, and meaning.

**With Borysenko**: Siegel's PNI mechanism (hypothalamus → hormones → immune suppression) is the same mechanism Borysenko documented in the Mind/Body Clinic. Both emphasise emotional expression vs. repression as the key variable. Both describe the cancer patient as someone who has lost self-love and lives for external approval.

**With Dossey**: Siegel's will-to-live framework is a clinical expression of Dossey's meaning-as-medicine thesis. Both cite the Krebiozen case; both identify meaning as the active biological variable. Dossey's Black Monday syndrome (meaning of Monday morning killing) and Siegel's cancer personality profile (meaning of a lost relationship causing collapse) describe the same principle at different scales.

**Extension**: Siegel adds specific clinical technologies (ECaP programme, imagery protocols, drawing interpretation, four-question assessment) that the other thinkers describe more theoretically. He also adds Al Siebert's survivor personality taxonomy, which provides the most detailed description in the wiki of what psychological health actually looks like in practice.
