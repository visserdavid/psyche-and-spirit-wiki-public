# The Magus of Strovolos

**Summary**: Kyriacos Markides's first-person ethnographic account of Daskalos (Stylianos Atteshlis), a Cypriot mystic healer, recording several years of teachings on an eclectic esoteric-Christian cosmology — reincarnation, thought-form "elementals," out-of-body travel ("exomatosis"), and hands-on healing — alongside eyewitness reports of possession-exorcism, materialization, and dematerialization.

**Sources**:
- [Markides, K. C. (1985). *The magus of Strovolos: The extraordinary world of a spiritual healer*. Routledge & Kegan Paul.](magus-of-strovolos.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

---

## Overview

*The Magus of Strovolos* is sociologist Kyriacos Markides's account of Spyros Sathi — known to his students as "Daskalos" (Greek for "Teacher") — a retired Cypriot civil servant who led a lay spiritual circle called the Researchers of Truth from his home in Strovolos, a suburb of Nicosia. Markides, a University of Maine professor doing ethnographic fieldwork on Cypriot folk religion, became Daskalos's student and chronicler between 1978 and 1983. The book is the first of what became a trilogy (followed by *Homage to the Sun* and *Fire in the Heart*, neither yet in this vault).

Markides frames his method explicitly as participant-observation rather than parapsychological testing: he states at the outset that the book does not attempt to "ascertain the validity" of the phenomena described, but to present the world as Daskalos's students experienced it. This stance — bracketing rather than adjudicating the truth-claims — runs through the entire book and is itself a methodological choice worth noting alongside the content.

## Daskalos and His Circle

Daskalos (1912–1995) taught a syncretic cosmology combining Eastern Orthodox Christian mysticism, Gnosticism, Kabbalah-inflected symbolism, and reincarnation doctrine, transmitted orally to students in his "Stoa" (a converted room behind his house) and through a network of affiliated circles ("Researchers of Truth") in Cyprus and, via annual visits, Greece. His inner circle in the book includes Iacovos (a former political prisoner and Daskalos's closest disciple), Kostas, and Theophanis of Paphos, another healer with decades-long ties to Daskalos.

Daskalos claimed his teaching came through discarnate "Masters" — principally "Father Yohannan," identified as John the Evangelist — channeled or consulted in trance, and traced his own spiritual authority to a lineage of teachers going back, in his account, thousands of years and multiple incarnations.

## Healing, Materialization, and Eyewitness Claims

Markides documents dozens of healing episodes attributed to Daskalos — spinal realignment, tumor removal via "dematerialization," diagnosis by photograph, and cures Markides personally witnessed (including the case of "Mrs. Katina," corroborated, in Markides's account, by before/after X-rays). Daskalos frames all cures as contingent on the patient's karma ("no healing is permitted that is not allowed by Holy Providence") and insists healers who take on a patient's Karma directly ("bearing" it) suffer physical consequences themselves — Daskalos attributes his own 1983 leg wound and near-amputation to assuming a family member's karmic debt.

The book's most extraordinary claims are chapters on **materialization and dematerialization**: Daskalos narrates transmuting a gold ring into silver by entering its atomic structure in an exomatic state, turning living plant matter into brass, and — during the July 1979 Skylab re-entry — claims to have joined "guardian" extraterrestrial intelligences in nudging the falling space station's trajectory away from populated areas. Markides reports these as Daskalos's own first-person narration, witnessed in real time by Markides and Iacovos monitoring Daskalos's pulse and breathing during the trance state, but explicitly states there is "no way of testing the reality" of the experience.

## Possession, Exorcism, and "Invisible Helpers"

Chapter 11 ("Tales of Possession") recounts several cases Daskalos frames as possession — including an exorcism-style intervention for an Israeli patient ("Hadas") possessed, in Daskalos's diagnosis, by the vengeful spirits of two deceased Nazi war criminals, treated with a mixed Cabbalistic-Christian ritual (the Star of David, "Shalom Alehem," the "Unpointed Sword," and the concept of *Erevos* — a purgatorial forgetting-state rather than punishment). See [Karma and Possession (Daskalos)](../concepts/karma-and-possession-daskalos.md) and, for the wiki's other possession framework, [Martin's Stages of Possession](../concepts/possession-process-martin.md).

Daskalos's cosmology also includes benevolent discarnate guides ("Masters," "helpers") who assist living Researchers of Truth — a structural opposite to [Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md)'s possessing entities, see below.

## Critique

**Evidentiary status**: As with [*Hostage to the Devil*](hostage-to-the-devil.md), the extraordinary claims here — materialization, dematerialization, atomic transmutation, extraterrestrial contact — rest entirely on first-person narrative testimony (Daskalos's own account, relayed by Markides) with no independent instrumentation, controlled observation, or third-party corroboration beyond Markides's own presence as an interested participant-observer. Markides is candid about this limitation throughout and does not claim scientific verification.

**Methodological stance**: Markides's explicit decision to write as a participant rather than a skeptical investigator produces a rich phenomenological record of what it is like to be inside Daskalos's world, but forecloses the kind of testing (blinded prediction, instrumentation, replication) that would let external readers evaluate the healing and paranormal claims independently. This is a different methodological choice than, for example, [Norman Shealy](../thinkers/norman-shealy.md)'s EEG measurements of healer Ostad Hadi Parvarandeh in [*Sacred Healing*](sacred-healing.md), which at least attempted instrumented before/after comparison.

**Cultural and syncretic construction**: Daskalos's cosmology freely blends Orthodox Christian liturgical elements, Hindu/Buddhist-inflected reincarnation doctrine, Kabbalah-derived symbolism (the six-pointed star, "Cabbala" exorcism ritual), and Gnostic ideas about matter and spirit, in a manner not derived from any single lineage's internal consistency — a pattern common to twentieth-century Western esoteric syncretism (Theosophy, the Golden Dawn tradition) rather than a transmission from a single unbroken tradition, notwithstanding Daskalos's own claims to ancient lineage.

**Note**: Mainstream physics holds that the atomic transmutation claims (gold to silver, plant to brass) described in Chapter 13 are impossible under known chemistry and nuclear physics — element identity is fixed by proton number and cannot be altered by any means resembling meditation or will. Mainstream psychiatry treats the possession cases described in Chapter 11 as consistent with dissociative and conversion presentations, following the same differential-diagnosis logic detailed in [Malachi Martin](../thinkers/malachi-martin.md)'s critique section.
