# Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism

**Summary**: Gershom Scholem's foundational historical-critical survey of Jewish mysticism — from early Merkabah/Hekhalot visionary ascent, through the Zohar (which Scholem demonstrates was composed by Moses de León in 13th-century Spain, not transmitted from antiquity), Lurianic Kabbalah's tzimtzum/shevirah/tikkun cosmology, and the 17th-century Sabbatai Zevi messianic movement, to 18th-century Hasidism.

## Overview

Delivered as the 1938 Hilda Stroock Lectures at the Jewish Institute of Religion, New York, and first published in 1941, *Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism* is the founding text of the modern academic study of Kabbalah. See [Gershom Scholem](../thinkers/gershom-scholem.md) for the author's biography and methodological stance. The vault file is the widely used Schocken Books 3rd revised edition (1995 printing; the 3rd revised edition itself dates to 1954).

## Structure and Key Content

### General Characteristics of Jewish Mysticism (Lecture 1)

Scholem opens with a general theory of mysticism as a historical stage of religious consciousness: a "romantic" recovery of unity following the classical religious stage's opening of an "abyss" between transcendent God and finite man. He argues Jewish mysticism, like all mysticism, works within the specific positive content of its parent tradition (Torah, the commandments, the doctrine of divine unity) rather than existing as generic, tradition-free "mystical experience." He also notes the near-total absence of personal, autobiographical writing among Kabbalists — a marked contrast with Christian and Islamic mystical literature — which he attributes to a deep-seated Jewish reticence about intimate self-exposure even in the context of describing union with God.

### Merkabah Mysticism and Early Kabbalah (Lectures 2–4)

Scholem traces the earliest stratum of Jewish mysticism to Talmudic-era Merkabah ("Chariot") and Hekhalot ("Palaces") literature: visionary ascent through seven celestial palaces to view the divine throne-chariot described in Ezekiel, with close affinities to contemporary Gnostic speculation. He follows this through medieval German Hasidism (Hasidei Ashkenaz) and the 13th-century emergence of Kabbalah proper in Provence and Spain, including Abraham Abulafia's "Prophetic Kabbalism" — an ecstatic technique of letter permutation and combination aimed at inducing prophetic states, illustrated in the book with a lengthy translated excerpt from an anonymous disciple's autobiographical account of Abulafia's method.

### The Zohar: Authorship and Doctrine (Lectures 5–6)

Scholem's most philologically detailed and historically consequential argument: against the traditional attribution to the 2nd-century sage Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, he demonstrates through close linguistic analysis that the bulk of the *Zohar* was composed by the 13th-century Spanish kabbalist **Moses de León**, in an artificial literary Aramaic built on his own medieval Hebrew habits of thought, complete with systematic grammatical errors and citations of invented, non-existent source books. He distinguishes three compositional layers — the *Midrash ha-Ne'elam* (earliest, 1275–1281), the main body of the Zohar (1281–1286), and the later, inferior *Ra'aya Meheimna* and *Tikkunim* (c. 1300, by a different, lesser imitator) — and treats Moses de León not as a simple forger but as someone who experienced himself as a channel for genuine tradition even while composing in his own hand. See [Zohar: The Book of Enlightenment](zohar-book-of-enlightenment.md) and [Moses de León](../thinkers/moses-de-leon.md), both already in this wiki via Daniel Chanan Matt's 1983 anthology, which cited this same thesis secondhand; this ingest grounds it in Scholem's original argument. Scholem also gives the fullest account in the book of the **Sefirot** doctrine as developed across the Zohar's theosophical system — see [Sefirot](../concepts/sefirot.md).

### Isaac Luria and Safed Kabbalah (Lecture 7)

Following the trauma of the 1492 expulsion from Spain, a new Kabbalistic school emerged in 16th-century Safed, Ottoman Palestine, under Isaac Luria. Scholem gives the first systematic modern account of **tzimtzum** (God's self-contraction to make room for creation), **shevirat ha-kelim** (the "Breaking of the Vessels" — a primordial catastrophe scattering divine sparks throughout material reality and constituting the origin of exile and evil), and **tikkun** (the cosmic repair achieved through human ethical action, gathering the scattered sparks) — a doctrine of Exile and Redemption that Scholem reads as "the great myth of Exile" giving mystical form to the lived historical experience of Spanish Jewry's dispersion. See the new page [Lurianic Kabbalah](../concepts/lurianic-kabbalah.md) and the existing, Zohar-derived [Tikkun](../concepts/tikkun.md), now deepened with Luria's fuller cosmological elaboration.

### Sabbatianism: Mystical Heresy (Lecture 8)

Scholem's most extended and psychologically detailed case study: the meteoric rise and catastrophic collapse of the **Sabbatai Zevi** messianic movement (1665–1666), in which a Kabbalistically-literate but almost certainly bipolar ("manic-depressive," in Scholem's period terminology) young man was proclaimed the Messiah by his self-appointed prophet Nathan of Gaza, drawing much of the entire Jewish Diaspora into a mass movement of penitence and ecstatic expectation — before Sabbatai Zevi's forced conversion to Islam under threat of execution by the Ottoman Sultan produced a theological crisis Scholem treats as the origin of a genuinely new, heretical current within Judaism: the **doctrine of the "holy sinner"** and, in its most radical (Frankist) form, a doctrine of the "holiness of sin" — deliberate transgression as a paradoxical means of hastening cosmic redemption. See the new page [Sabbatianism](../concepts/sabbatianism.md).

### Hasidism as the Latest Phase (Lecture 9)

Scholem closes with 18th-century Polish/Ukrainian Hasidism, founded by Israel Baal Shem Tov, reading it as history's third and most successful attempt to popularize Kabbalistic mysticism to a mass audience — succeeding, where Lurianic Kabbalism and Sabbatianism both eventually failed or turned destructive, by deliberately stripping out the dangerous Messianic-apocalyptic charge that had driven Sabbatianism's collapse, while retaining the mystical intensity of its predecessors in a domesticated, community-centered form built around the figure of the Zaddik (righteous leader/saint).
