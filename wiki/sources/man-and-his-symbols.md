# Man and His Symbols

**Summary**: The only work in which Jung and four close associates explain Jungian psychology to a general audience — covering symbols vs signs, the unconscious, dreams, archetypes, the hero myth, individuation, shadow, anima/animus, the Self, mandala symbolism, and the symbolic dimensions of modern art.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G., von Franz, M.-L., Henderson, J. L., Jacobi, J., & Jaffé, A. (1964). *Man and his symbols*. Doubleday.](man-and-his-symbols.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-22

## Overview

*Man and His Symbols* was conceived by Jung and his closest collaborators as an introduction to analytical psychology for the general reader. Jung wrote Part 1 shortly before his death in 1961; the remaining four parts were written by Marie-Louise von Franz, Joseph L. Henderson, Jolande Jacobi, and Aniela Jaffé. The book combines theoretical exposition with abundant illustrations drawn from art, mythology, dreams, and cultural history.

## Contents by Section

### Part 1: Approaching the Unconscious (C. G. Jung)

Jung's opening essay establishes the book's foundational distinction: **symbols are not signs**. A sign stands for a known thing (a traffic light); a symbol carries a meaning that exceeds what can be stated rationally. Symbols arise where language and logic cannot fully express the content of an experience — they point toward something that remains partially unknown.

The unconscious, Jung argues, is not merely a repository of repressed material (Freud's view) but a vast creative source that **compensates** the one-sidedness of conscious life. Every dream is a communication from this deeper layer — not a disguised wish, but a counterbalance to whatever the waking ego is overclaiming or ignoring. Dreams speak in symbols and require **amplification** (connecting the dream image to parallel cultural, mythological, and historical material) rather than free association (which drifts away from the image).

Jung introduces the **four psychological functions** — thinking, feeling, intuition, and sensation — and the personality attitudes of introversion and extraversion. No individual develops all four functions equally; the **inferior function** remains most deeply connected to the unconscious and is therefore the gateway through which the unconscious most powerfully erupts.

He illustrates the unconscious's **prospective function** with the case of a ten-year-old girl who told him twelve dreams in succession — all containing advanced initiatory symbolism (death, rebirth, cosmic imagery). She died shortly after. The dreams had anticipated what her ego could not consciously face.

### Part 2: Ancient Myths and Modern Man (Joseph L. Henderson)

Henderson examines how the [hero myth](../concepts/hero-myth.md) — the universally recurring narrative of departure, trial, transformation, and return — functions as a psychological map. The same pattern appears in Winnebago tribal mythology (four sequential hero figures: Trickster, Hare, Red Horn, Twin Heroes), in Greek tragedy, and in modern dream series.

The [Trickster](../concepts/trickster-archetype.md) is the most primitive form of hero — amoral, appetite-driven, lacking a coherent self — who through repeated misadventure gradually develops the very qualities (purpose, moral awareness, social bonds) that the hero must possess. Successive hero cycles show the progressive maturation of consciousness from instinctual (Trickster) to purposeful (Twins). Henderson's account draws on [Paul Radin](../thinkers/paul-radin.md)'s Winnebago fieldwork, published in full as *[The Trickster](the-trickster.md)* (1956) alongside Jung's own commentary essay and a companion essay by Karl Kerényi comparing the Trickster to the Greek god Hermes.

Henderson traces [initiation rites](../concepts/initiation-rites.md) across cultures as the social enactment of the same process: the ritual death of the boy's ego-identity and the rebirth of the man. Beauty and the Beast is interpreted as the archetypal narrative of feminine initiation — the transformation of the inner masculine from threatening beast to true partner. Orpheus, Dionysus, and Christ represent successive refinements of the mystery religion archetype (dying god, dismemberment, resurrection), each one deepening the integration of spiritual transformation into human consciousness.

### Part 3: The Process of Individuation (Marie-Louise von Franz)

Von Franz provides the most systematic account of [individuation](../concepts/individuation.md) in the book, illustrated with extended dream series and fairy tale analyses.

The [Self](../concepts/the-self.md) is the organizing centre of the psyche — the whole person that both the ego and the unconscious serve. It manifests in dreams through specific images: the mandala (circle with a centre), the philosopher's stone, the wise old man or woman, the divine child, and various personifications depending on the dreamer's gender and stage of development.

The **Naskapi Great Man** (Mista'peo) is introduced as an Indigenous parallel to the Self: a tribe of Eastern Canadian hunters who live in isolation and are guided by an inner voice — the "big man" — accessible through attentive dreaming. This illustrates that what Jungian psychology calls the Self corresponds to what various cultures have called the inner guide, the guardian spirit, or the divine inner spark.

Von Franz traces the shadow, anima/animus, and Self through concrete dream examples:
- The **unexplored house** dream (a person discovers unknown rooms in a familiar house) — the shadow as unlived potential
- The **French desperado** dream — the shadow as dangerous, amoral energy that must be neither enacted nor repressed, but dialogued with
- The **Moses and Khidr** story from the Koran — the ethical paradox of the shadow: apparent evil in service of a larger pattern of meaning

The **anima** in men develops through four stages: Eve (purely biological, instinctual), Helen of Troy (romantic, aestheticized), the Virgin Mary (spiritual, elevated), and Sapientia/Sophia (wisdom, transcendence). Each stage represents a deeper integration of the feminine principle, from sensual relatedness to wisdom-bearing guide.

The **animus** in women develops correspondingly: from raw physical power (the athlete, the muscle man), to initiative and action (the hero), to the bearer of the word (the professor, the preacher), to the guide to meaning (the sacred teacher, Gandhi-type figure). Integrated, the animus gives a woman firmness, courage, objectivity, and access to spiritual depth.

Von Franz presents the **Bath Badgerd** story (a Persian fairy tale) as an illustration of the Self's dual nature: the First Man, the diamond hidden at the centre, the parrot that causes petrification — all are symbols of the Self's simultaneous creative and destructive potential. The hero (Hatim) must approach the centre not by imitating a teacher but by developing his own authentic relationship to the mystery.

The section concludes with a detailed case study of a young man identified as "Henry," whose dreams over nine months of analysis reveal the progressive stages of masculine individuation — from avoidance of instinct and sexuality, through encounter with shadow figures and the anima, to a climactic confrontation with Eastern wisdom figures (Chinese gatekeepers) and the I Ching oracle, leading to his eventual marriage and entry into adult life.

### Part 4: Symbols in the Visual Arts (Aniela Jaffé)

Jaffé traces three recurring symbolic motifs across the history of art: stone, animal, and circle.

**Stone**: From Jacob's stone at Bethel to the megalithic alignments at Carnac to modern sculpture (Max Ernst, Hans Aeschbacher), stone carries the numinosity of what is permanent, primordial, and spiritually inhabitable. The tendency to animate stone — to carve faces into menhirs, to set stone circles as sacred boundaries — reflects the projection of unconscious content onto the material world.

**Animal**: Ice Age cave paintings (Lascaux, Altamira, Trois Frères) reveal a sustained and sophisticated engagement with animal symbolism over 50,000 years. The man-in-animal-disguise figure at Trois Frères is interpreted as a shaman — a figure who crosses the boundary between human consciousness and animal instinct. Animal gods across world religions (Egyptian, Hindu, Greek, Norse, Christian) preserve this boundary-crossing quality. The animal in dreams represents the instinctual ground of the psyche — the part of human nature that must be honoured rather than suppressed.

**Circle/Mandala**: The mandala appears not only in Tibetan thangkas and Navajo sand paintings but in the ground plans of ancient cities (Rome as *Roma quadrata*, Washington D.C., Angkor Wat), in cathedral rose windows, and — in disguised form — throughout the history of modern painting (Kandinsky, Klee, Mondrian, Pollock). Jaffé analyses modern art's turn from figurative representation to abstraction as a psychological event: the collapse of the centre (the death of God, the loss of shared symbols) drives artists toward both the abyss (Chirico's metaphysical emptiness, Marini's disintegrating horse rider) and toward the unconscious search for a new centre (Chagall's warmth, Klee's penetration of nature, Kandinsky's cosmic harmonics). UFO sightings are interpreted — following Jung's earlier essay — as projections of the mandala archetype onto the sky during a time of maximum cultural disorientation.

### Conclusion: Science and the Unconscious (Marie-Louise von Franz)

Von Franz examines the convergence between Jungian psychology and microphysics. Wolfgang Pauli (Nobel laureate in physics and Jung's collaborator) identified a structural parallel: just as the physicist's measurement apparatus cannot be excluded from the description of quantum phenomena, the observer's consciousness cannot be excluded from the psyche's contents. The principle of **complementarity** (Niels Bohr) — that two mutually exclusive descriptions may both be necessary — applies in psychology as much as in physics.

The concept of **[synchronicity](../concepts/synchronicity.md)** — Jung's term for meaningful coincidence, events connected not by causation but by meaning — is proposed as a bridge between the domains of psyche and matter. The Darwin/Wallace parallel discovery and similar cases in the history of science are offered as examples of synchronistic events that suggest a deeper ordering principle connecting inner (psychic) and outer (physical) events.

Von Franz argues that archetypes function analogously to what physicists call "possibilities of reaction" — they are not laws governing what must happen, but predispositions toward certain patterns, which manifest in synchronistic arrangement when activated.

## Key Arguments

1. **Symbols carry surplus meaning** that signs cannot — they are the natural language of the unconscious and must be approached through amplification, not reductive analysis.
2. **Dreams compensate** the ego's one-sidedness and carry prospective (anticipatory) as well as retrospective meaning.
3. **The hero myth** is universal because it maps the development of consciousness from instinctual (Trickster) to purposeful and spiritually mature (Twin Heroes, dying-and-rising god).
4. **The Self** — the totality of the psyche — expresses itself through mandala imagery, fairy tale heroes, and spontaneous dreams; the ego's task is to align with, not master, the Self's direction.
5. **Modern art's abstraction** is not mere aesthetic fashion but a cultural response to the loss of shared symbols — an unconscious search for new images adequate to the totality of human experience.
6. **Physics and psychology** are approaching the same boundary from different directions: the unus mundus, the one world in which matter and psyche are two aspects of a single underlying reality.

## Tensions and Critiques

- **Empirical status**: Like *CW 9.1*, the claims in *Man and His Symbols* are not falsifiable in the scientific sense. The book's method is interpretive and analogical, not experimental.
- **Gender essentialism**: Jung's account of anima/animus presupposes a binary and is treated as universal; contemporary psychology regards gender as far more fluid and culturally constructed.
- **Eurocentric framing**: Non-Western materials (Navajo, Naskapi, Egyptian, Hindu) are used to illustrate Jungian categories rather than understood on their own terms — a form of appropriation that post-colonial scholars have critiqued.
- **Modern art interpretation**: Jaffé's psychologising of 20th-century art movements is suggestive but reductive; artists' actual intentions and social contexts are largely bypassed in favour of archetypal interpretation.
