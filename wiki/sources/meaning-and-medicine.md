# Meaning & Medicine: A Doctor's Tales of Breakthrough and Healing

**Summary**: Dossey's 1991 collection of clinical stories and philosophical essays demonstrating that perceived meanings — how patients interpret events in their lives — produce direct biological effects, sometimes causing illness and death, sometimes catalysing astonishing recoveries.

**Sources**:
- [Dossey, L. (1991). *Meaning & medicine: A doctor's tales of breakthrough and healing*. Bantam Books.](meaning-and-medicine.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

---

## Overview

*Meaning & Medicine* is Larry Dossey's third book, published by Bantam Books in December 1991. It consists of 22 chapters in three parts, structured as a progression from breakdown (how meaning causes illness) through a philosophical interlude (what the body is) to breakthrough (how meaning enables healing).

The book's thesis, stated in the prologue (the "Talking Out Worms" case), is that perceived meanings — the interpretations patients attach to events in their lives — are biologically active. They enter the body and alter it in measurable ways. Dossey draws on case histories from his own practice and from the research literature to show that meanings can kill and meanings can heal, and that a medicine that ignores meaning is practicing half a science.

The book is dedicated to Professor D. S. Kothari, the Indian physicist who corresponded with Dossey and introduced him to the idea that the concept of the atom itself requires the concept of the self — a convergence of physics and consciousness that shapes Dossey's theoretical framework.

## Structure

### Prologue: Talking Out Worms

The book opens with two cases: Maria the curandera, who heals a dying calf by "talking to the worms" (addressing the meaning of the infection to the parties concerned), and Frank, a heart patient who discovers he can control his heart rate monitor by controlling what chest pain means to him. Both cases illustrate Dossey's central insight: the body is a meaning meter.

### Part I: Breakdown (Chapters 1–10)

Ten chapters showing how perceived meanings — particularly shame, despair, helplessness, isolation, and dread — directly impair health and can cause death.

**Chapter 1 — Asthmatic Intern: Shame Can Be Fatal**: Jennie, a 27-year-old with asthma, wishes to die aloud while being pressed to relive her trauma history — and dies within minutes. Shame, Dossey argues (citing Borysenko's [*Guilt Is the Teacher*](guilt-is-the-teacher.md)), severs the bridge between self and others, creating a physiological cascade.

**Chapter 2 — Caught in the Act of Forbidden Play: Cardiac Arrest**: A male teacher goes into ventricular fibrillation after roughhousing with his daughters triggers unconscious guilt and forbidden impulses. Bernard Lown's Harvard research identifies three factors in lethal arrhythmia: underlying heart disease, a pervading sense of despair, and a trigger event.

**Chapter 3 — Siege of Shame: Multiple Personality Disorder**: MPD as the psyche's coping strategy for unmanageable childhood abuse. The Inner Self Helper — found in MPD patients as an entity that has no fear, guilt, or hatred, knows the patient's history, and serves as a conduit for healing — is discussed as an expression of the psyche's self-healing capacity (following Allison and Wilber).

**Chapter 4 — Halloween Helplessness: What Does It Mean to Die?**: John's death following Halloween property vandalism illustrates Lown's three-factor model of sudden cardiac death. George Engel's six-year collection of 170 press reports of sudden death shows that the "giving-up, given-up complex" — the feeling of being trapped with no exit — is the predominant emotional context.

**Chapter 5 — Voodoo Death: The No-Exit Syndrome**: Walter Cannon's documentation of voodoo death; the mechanism is the meaning assigned to the event, not the event itself. Modern equivalents: the nursing home placement, forced retirement, and the physician's prognostic curse. The antidote to voodoo is restoration of meaning (Langer and Rodin's nursing home study: giving patients a plant to care for and choices to make cut the mortality rate in half over 18 months).

**Chapter 6 — Black Monday Syndrome: Dread Means Dead**: Heart attacks cluster on Mondays at 9 a.m. The finding is confirmed across 14 published studies. The 1972 Massachusetts study found that job dissatisfaction was a better predictor of first heart attack than any traditional risk factor. Dossey argues that Black Monday syndrome demonstrates that the meaning of Monday — dread of returning to a despised job — is the primary causal agent. Kobasa's three-Cs hardiness model (commitment, control, challenge) is the antidote.

**Chapter 7 — Johnny Can't Read, Johnny Has Heart Disease**: William Ruberman's study of 2,320 male heart attack survivors shows that isolation and life stress (both correlated with low education) quadrupled the risk of death. The Alameda County longitudinal study (7,000 people, 9 years): social connectedness predicted mortality across all ages and both sexes.

**Chapter 8 — Patient, Patient: Hazards of the Medical Experience**: Words can kill. Mrs. S. died in heart failure after her cardiologist said "TS" (meaning tricuspid stenosis, which she interpreted as "terminal situation"). Catheterization patients have died after offhand remarks by physicians. Anthony Lalli's study of 248 fatalities during X-ray contrast procedures attributes many to the meaning of the fear and entrapment patients experience — not to the chemical agent.

**Chapter 9 — Getting, Getting Cancer: Problems of the Medical Student**: Caroline Thomas's 30-year Johns Hopkins study showed that students who suppressed emotional expression developed cancer at higher rates. Glaser's Ohio State research found natural killer cell activity dropped at exam time. Medical students' situation mirrors the Black Monday patient: trapped, unable to express, no exit.

**Chapter 10 — Broken Hearts: The Toxicity of Bereavement**: An estimated 35,000 Americans die from grief each year (20% of bereavement deaths, per Schleifer). Schleifer's Mount Sinai study: bereaved husbands showed profoundly reduced lymphocyte function. The Llanidloes, Wales study: bereaved relatives were 5–10 times more likely to die in the year following a spouse's death. Dossey introduces the question of non-local connectedness between spouses — minds as not fully bounded — citing Schrödinger's observation that mind is singular.

### Part II: Meaning and the New Body (Chapters 11–13)

**Chapter 11 — Meaning Links Mind and Matter: The Theory of Physicist David Bohm**: Dossey presents [Bohm's](../thinkers/david-bohm.md) soma-significance theory, communicated to him directly by Bohm, as the philosophical foundation for the book's central thesis. See [*Wholeness and the Implicate Order*](wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md) for Bohm's own published framework, of which soma-significance is a related but distinct formulation. "Soma" (body) and "significance" (mind/meaning) are not two substances — they are two aspects of one whole, connected by meaning as a third entity. The analogy: a magnet's north and south poles are encompassed by the magnetic field — mind, body, and meaning form an analogous whole.

**Chapter 12 — The Body as Machine**: A critical history of the machine metaphor in medicine — from Graves and Corrigan's mechanical metaphors (water hammer pulse, pistol shot pulse) through military metaphors (the body at war) to computer metaphors. Dossey argues that these metaphors are not neutral; they determine what treatments are tried and what healing events are permitted to occur. The "tomato effect" (rejecting therapies that work because they don't fit the current model — like Americans refusing to eat tomatoes for centuries because they were nightshade) and the "snakeroot effect" (continuing therapies that don't work) are identified as consequences of entrapment by body-as-machine thinking.

**Chapter 13 — Body as Music**: Susumu Ohno (Beckman Research Institute) assigned musical notes to DNA bases and transcribed living organisms' genomes into musical compositions — producing pieces that listeners compared to Bach and Brahms without knowing the source. Running the process in reverse, Ohno found that Chopin's Funeral March translates into the DNA code of a cancer gene. Dossey argues that the body-as-music metaphor is not merely poetic but supported by biochemical evidence of sound's effects on physiology (TM mantras reducing cholesterol; silence producing health; ICU noise affecting recovery).

### Part III: Healing Breakthroughs (Chapters 14–22)

**Chapter 14 — The Case of the Fishskin Boy: Genes and Memes**: In the 1950s, Dr. A. A. Mason (British Medical Journal) hypnotised a boy he believed had warts. The boy had congenital ichthyosis — a genetic skin disease with no known treatment. The hypnosis produced 60–70% improvement. Lewis Thomas (Sloan-Kettering): the case suggests a "superintelligence" within the organism that can be activated. Dawkins's meme concept (bits of consciousness that parallel genes) is introduced: the boy's meaning/belief about his skin — operating at hypnotic depth — altered the expression of his genetic programme. Cairns and Barry Hall's bacterial mutation experiments suggest organisms respond to the significance of a situation, directing mutations toward survival.

**Chapter 15 — The Power of Belief: Psychic Healing at the Harvard Health Service**: David McClelland's experiment: a Boston healer told 13 Harvard students they were healed; 9 of 13 felt better with measurably increased IgA. When health service physicians told students the same thing, nobody improved. McClelland's conclusion: belief permeates the body. Myrin Borysenko's anecdotal cure by a wine-guzzling healer (she laughed for an hour in the bathtub and her flu vanished). Lawrence LeShan: healers create the conditions for the patient's own self-healing capacity to operate — the healer does not do the healing. McClelland's Mother Teresa film experiment: watching the film raised IgA levels even in students who professed to dislike her — because the act of love registered in the body before the critical mind could object.

**Chapter 16 — Helper High: Healing by Helping Others**: Allan Luks's survey of 1,700 women volunteers: 88% reported a physical "helper high" (warmth, increased energy, reduced pain) from helping others. 82% said the sensation recurred when they remembered the helping. Luks proposed endorphin release as the mechanism. McClelland's IgA studies support this: altruistic motivation produces measurable immune enhancement. Watching film of Attila the Hun dropped IgA levels.

**Chapter 17 — Healing at a Distance: Era III Medicine**: Dossey presents his Three Eras of Medicine framework in full (the framework first published in *Recovering the Soul*, 1989). Era I (mechanical/local), Era II (mind-body), Era III (non-local consciousness). Evidence for Era III: Byrd's CCU prayer study (prayed group used 5x fewer antibiotics, had no patients on ventilators vs. 12 in control group, fewer arrests); Wirth's double-blind wound healing study (13 of 23 treated subjects had zero wound size by Day 16, none of 21 controls); TM group Jerusalem study (war deaths in Lebanon decreased 70% on peak meditation days); Grinberg-Zylberbaum EEG synchrony experiments (meditating pairs developed correlated brain wave patterns through walls); Braud and Schlitz's transpersonal imagery experiments. Dossey explicitly cites *The Creation of Health* (Shealy & Myss, 1988) and describes Myss's 93% accuracy in intuitive diagnosis from 1,200 miles as documentation of Era III medicine in clinical practice.

**Chapter 18 — Place of Emptiness: Return of Miracles**: De Vaca (16th-century explorer, healed others after a spiritual crisis of shipwreck) and Keizo Funatsu (buried in an Antarctic blizzard, experienced a womb-like peace and survived) as illustrations of the paradox that emptiness — stopping the effort to control — creates space for healing. Lewis Thomas: Americans are obsessed with health as something to manage and control; this may prevent the emptiness that healing requires. Lourdes as an institutional site of this same phenomenon.

**Chapter 19 — Standing in the Void: Paradoxical and Rational Healing**: Dossey arranges healing approaches on a spectrum from "rational" (surgery, medication, biofeedback) to "paradoxical" (prayer, miracles, spontaneous remission). He presents the Krebiozen case (a cancer patient's tumours "melted like snowballs on a stove" after injection, then recurred when he read the drug was worthless, then disappeared again after injection with water) as a paradigm case of meaning-as-medicine. Delizia Cirolli's Lourdes healing (neuroblastoma/Ewing tumour of the knee, certified by the International Medical Committee of Lourdes) is presented as an unexplained miraculous cure. Dossey does not attribute these events to divine intervention but emphasises that the rational framework cannot account for them. The domain of paradox requires a different stance: alignment, acceptance, not-doing.

**Chapter 20 — The Helper Within: Invisible Power**: Walter Dent, a 97-year-old patient who refused all medications and tests but survived a hip fracture without pain or complications, embodies what Aldous Huxley called the "Not-I" — the healing intelligence within the organism that functions best when the ego's efforts are suspended. MPD research's Inner Self Helper is its most dramatic clinical expression. Shelley Taylor's positive illusions research: cancer patients who used denial or fighting spirit survived longer than those who realistically accepted poor prognoses.

**Chapter 21 — The Hospital Experience: Survivor**: Roger Ulrich's study: gallbladder surgery patients with rooms overlooking trees recovered faster and needed less pain medication than patients whose rooms faced a brick wall. Guzzetta's research showing three 20-minute music sessions in the CCU reduced complications. Egbert's Massachusetts General study: anesthesiologists who briefed surgical patients on what to expect and taught relaxation techniques reduced morphine requirements by half and shortened hospital stays by three days. The Planetree unit at San Francisco Pacific Presbyterian (13 beds, founded 1985 by Angelica Thieriot): patients can read their own charts, choose room décor, cook in the unit kitchen, and have unlimited visitors. Dossey presents these as evidence that the hospital environment carries meaning — and that meaning, for better or worse, is always biologically active.

**Chapter 22 — Pictures in the Mind: Using Imagination for Healing**: Stephen, a truck driver facing amputation, visualised blood flowing through his leg as though it were steel pipe — and his nurse measured the Doppler signal return to his foot within 15 minutes. Anna, with Raynaud's disease, learned biofeedback temperature training; her hands warmed from 65°F to the mid-90s; her symptoms abated and her self-image transformed. Both cases illustrate the body-as-music metaphor in practice: the mind's images are the fingers playing the instrument.

**Afterword**: Dossey narrates his own migraine headaches and how a faculty adviser's dismissive phrase ("just relax") — delivered without explanation — paradoxically freed him from terror about the problem. The meaning assigned to the headaches (threat to career, sign of pathology) was the active ingredient; when the meaning changed, the course of the illness changed.

## Key Concepts Introduced or Extended

- **[Meaning and Health](../concepts/meaning-and-health.md)**: The central framework — perceived meanings are biologically active; they enter the body through the same pathways that Bohm's soma-significance theory describes
- **[Voodoo Death](../concepts/voodoo-death.md)**: Walter Cannon's research and its modern equivalents — the No-Exit Syndrome, the prognostic curse, Black Monday
- **[Spontaneous Remission](../concepts/spontaneous-remission.md)**: Paradoxical healing, the spectrum from rational to miraculous, Krebiozen, Lourdes
- **[Era III Medicine](../concepts/prayer-and-healing.md)**: Dossey's Three Eras framework (first published in *Recovering the Soul*, 1989); non-local consciousness as the theoretical home of prayer, therapeutic touch, and intuitive diagnosis
- **[Hardiness](../concepts/hardiness.md)**: Kobasa's 3 Cs as the cure for Black Monday syndrome
