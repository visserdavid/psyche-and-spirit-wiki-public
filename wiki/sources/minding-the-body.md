# Minding the Body, Mending the Mind

**Summary**: Borysenko & Rothstein (1987) — the clinical manual of Harvard's Mind/Body Clinic: psychoneuroimmunology as the scientific foundation, the relaxation response as the core tool, and mindfulness, reframing, and forgiveness as the path from chronic stress to healing wholeness.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J., & Rothstein, L. (1987). *Minding the body, mending the mind*. Addison-Wesley.](minding-the-body.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

---

## Publication and Author

*Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* was first published by Addison-Wesley in March 1987. [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md), PhD, is a former cancer cell biologist who co-founded the Mind/Body Clinic at Boston's Beth Israel Hospital under the mentorship of [Herbert Benson](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md), MD. Larry Rothstein collaborated on the writing. The foreword is by Benson.

The book documents the ten-week Mind/Body Program delivered at the clinic, which had treated approximately 2,000 patients by the time of publication — patients referred by physicians for conditions ranging from hypertension and migraine to cancer and AIDS.

## Structure

The book follows the Mind/Body Program arc:

- **Chapter 1 — Science of Healing**: [Psychoneuroimmunology](../concepts/psychoneuroimmunology.md), the [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md), faith and belief as health factors
- **Chapter 2 — Getting Control**: Meditation, the paradox of control, conditioning, helplessness vs. [hardiness](../concepts/hardiness.md)
- **Chapter 3 — Breaking the Anxiety Cycle**: Breathing, stretching, the anxiety feedback loop, and its two cycle-breakers
- **Chapter 4 — Mindfulness**: [Mindfulness](../concepts/mindfulness.md) as present-moment awareness; the Witness stance; desire and suffering
- **Chapter 5 — Mind Traps**: Cognitive patterns that generate unnecessary suffering (awfulizing, control-need, blame, denial)
- **Chapter 6 — Reframing and Creative Imagination**: Affirmations, dreams, hypnosis, creative imagination as healing tools
- **Chapter 7 — Healing Emotions**: Emotions as teachers; finishing unfinished business; forgiveness as accepting the core being
- **Chapter 8 — Sam's Story**: A physician dying of AIDS who achieves healing — wholeness and peace — without being cured
- **Epilogue — Twelve Brief Reminders**: Practical distillation of the program

## The Scientific Foundation: Psychoneuroimmunology

Borysenko presents [psychoneuroimmunology (PNI)](../concepts/psychoneuroimmunology.md) as the scientific grounding for the entire Mind/Body program. The key claim: the brain, nervous system, and immune system communicate bidirectionally via neuropeptides, making the mind and the body functionally inseparable.

Key research cited:
- **Robert Ader and Nicholas Cohen** (University of Rochester): conditioned immune suppression in rats — saccharin (the bell) associated with immunosuppressive drug (cyclophosphamide) until saccharin alone suppresses immunity. The body learns to suppress its own defenses.
- **Myrin Borysenko and Herbert Benson**: injection of adrenaline in volunteers produced a decline in helper T-lymphocytes — demonstrating that stress hormones directly suppress immune function.
- **Jemmott, McClelland, Benson et al.** (Harvard): exam stress reduced IgA antibody levels in saliva; students with a high need for power showed the steepest immune drops.
- **Kiecolt-Glaser and Glaser** (Ohio State): exam stress decreased natural killer cell function and interferon production.
- **G. Richard Smith and Sandra McDaniel**: conditioned immune suppression in humans — patients given saline instead of tuberculin eventually failed to respond even to real tuberculin.

Two hormones mediate stress-immune suppression: **adrenalin** and **cortisol**, both released in the fight-or-flight response.

## The Relaxation Response

[Herbert Benson](../thinkers/herbert-benson.md)'s [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md) is the physiological counterpart to the fight-or-flight response, documented through his meditation research at Harvard. When elicited:
- Heart rate and blood pressure drop
- Breathing rate and oxygen consumption decline
- Brain waves shift from beta to alpha rhythm
- Blood flow moves from muscles to brain and skin
- The body shifts from sympathetic to parasympathetic dominance

The relaxation response can be elicited by any technique that focuses attention and reduces verbal thinking: meditation, breathing exercises, repetitive prayer, yoga, progressive muscle relaxation. The ten-week program teaches patients to elicit it reliably.

Borysenko notes that twenty minutes of meditation produces a deeper metabolic drop than eight hours of sleep — making it a potent tool for nervous system recovery.

## Hardiness and Learned Helplessness

Chapter 2 contrasts two psychological orientations that determine how people respond to life stress:

**Learned helplessness** (Seligman): when events feel uncontrollable, motivation decreases, the mind fixates on failure, and immunity drops. Jay Weiss's rat experiments showed that uncontrollable shock produced ulcers while identical controllable shock did not. Seligman showed this generalises from one situation to many.

**Hardiness** (Kobasa): three attitudes that protect against stress-induced illness — commitment (engagement with life rather than alienation), control (belief in one's ability to influence events), and challenge (seeing change as opportunity rather than threat). Kobasa's studies of business executives and lawyers demonstrated that these attitudes buffered illness even under high stress loads. The Roseto, Pennsylvania community study showed that social cohesion and family closeness reduced heart disease to below-predicted rates regardless of other risk factors.

George Vaillant's thirty-year Harvard alumni study (*Adaptation to Life*, 1977): men with mature coping styles were four times healthier by their fifties than men with immature styles.

## Mindfulness

[Mindfulness](../concepts/mindfulness.md) — present-moment awareness without judgment — is the fourth chapter's subject and the conceptual bridge between the breathing/meditation skills and the emotional healing work. Borysenko draws on Buddhist meditation practice (Jon Kabat-Zinn's Stress Reduction and Relaxation Program at UMass Medical School; Thich Nhat Hanh's *Miracle of Mindfulness*) and on Ram Dass / Richard Alpert's teaching to present mindfulness as the antidote to the mind's chronic tendency to "awfulize" — to catastrophise present events using past conditioning.

The **Witness** (also Inner Self, Self) is the stable observational stance from which the mind's activity can be observed without being swept away by it. This stance, accessed through meditation and breathing, is presented as the source of the body's self-healing capacity.

## Mind Traps and Awfulizing

Chapter 5 (Mind Traps) identifies the cognitive patterns that generate unnecessary stress and illness:
- **Awfulizing**: catastrophising future events using fight-or-flight circuits, making the body respond to imagined threats as real ones
- **Control-need**: trying to control uncontrollable situations, producing chronic arousal when control is not achieved
- **Blame**: assigning exclusive responsibility for suffering to others, preventing action
- **Denial**: not acknowledging what is happening, making fear grow in the dark

The mind-body mechanism: the body cannot distinguish between imagined threats and real ones. Mental imagery of a lemon produces real salivation. Exam worry produces real immune suppression. The mind's tendency to awfulize therefore directly activates the fight-or-flight response and its immune-suppressing hormones.

## Healing Emotions and Forgiveness

Chapter 7 distinguishes between two emotional categories:
- **Fear-based emotions** (anger, resentment, guilt, sadness): associated with bodily holding, muscle tension, elevated heart rate — the fight-or-flight signature
- **Love-based emotions** (joy, compassion, peace, humour): associated with relaxation, openness, letting go

Forgiveness is the central healing emotion. Borysenko defines it not as condoning behaviour but as "accepting the core being — the divine consciousness — in the other person, not judging the person." Forgiveness starts with the self. Near-death experience research by Kenneth Ring (*Heading Toward Omega*, 1984) is cited: survivors consistently report a life review oriented entirely around love — whether they gave and received it — not around accomplishments. This reframes life's meaning around the emotion that produces healing.

"Finishing unfinished business" — completing things left unsaid with people from the past, through letters or conversations — is presented as a specific technique for releasing the energy trapped in resentments and regrets.

## Sam's Story: Healing Without Curing

The book's final chapter narrates Borysenko's year-long relationship with Sam, a physician in his thirties dying of AIDS in 1983. Sam was not cured — he died. But he achieved what the book defines as healing: a state of wholeness, peace, and connection to his inner life and to the people he loved.

Key elements of Sam's healing:
- Meditation and the relaxation response reduced his fear enough to allow connection
- He completed his unfinished business with family and friends
- He achieved forgiveness — of himself and others
- At death, he led a spontaneous group meditation from his hospital bed, calling each person to speak from the heart
- His family later cast in gold the yantra (geometric symbol of the Self) Borysenko had given him

Sam's story illustrates the [healing vs. curing](../concepts/healing-vs-curing.md) distinction that is implicit throughout the book: healing is the restoration of wholeness — a state of peace, connection, and alignment with one's deepest nature — which can occur even in the presence of terminal disease and imminent death.

## Critique

**Scientific grounding**: The PNI framework is the best-supported area of mind-body medicine. The stress-immune links Borysenko describes are well-replicated. The relaxation response is documented and replicable.

**Clinical claims**: The claim that 75% of physician visits are for stress-related conditions (cited in the introduction) is widely repeated but difficult to verify precisely — estimates vary significantly by definition. The symptom-reduction data from the Mind/Body Clinic are not from randomised controlled trials.

**Forgiveness and healing**: The near-death experience research (Ring) and case studies (Sam) that Borysenko uses to argue for love and forgiveness as healing forces are compelling narratively but are not controlled evidence. They demonstrate that healing without curing is possible and meaningful; they do not demonstrate that forgiveness reliably improves prognosis.

**The conditioning model**: The conditioned-immune-suppression finding (Ader/Cohen) is one of the most replicated findings in PNI. Borysenko's application to clinical practice — that patients can learn to condition healing responses through meditation — is reasonable extrapolation but not yet proven.
