# On Death and Dying

**Summary**: Kübler-Ross's 1969 landmark text documenting the five stages of dying through interviews with terminally ill patients — the foundational text of modern death studies and the direct precursor of Brennan's seven-stage healing model.

**Sources**:
- [Kübler-Ross, E. (1969). *On death and dying*. Macmillan.](on-death-and-dying.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Overview

*On Death and Dying* grew from an interdisciplinary seminar that Elisabeth Kübler-Ross established at the University of Chicago in 1965 with four theology students looking for a research project. They chose to study "death as a crisis in life" and decided the only valid data source was dying patients themselves. Over two years, the seminar grew from four students to fifty regular attendees — physicians, nurses, chaplains, social workers, and theologians — and the interviews with dying patients became its centrepiece.

The book is not a textbook or a psychological manual. It is an account of what terminally ill patients said when someone sat down, listened without anxiety, and asked them to be teachers. What emerged was a pattern — five stages through which most patients moved as they came to terms with dying — and a sustained critique of the way modern medicine denies and mechanises the dying process.

## The Five Stages

Kübler-Ross identifies five stages that most dying patients pass through, in some order, as they assimilate the reality of death. She emphasises that these are not strictly sequential, that patients can revisit earlier stages, that stages can coexist, and that individual variation is enormous. **Hope** runs as an undercurrent through all five stages — the last thing patients relinquish.

### Stage 1: Denial and Isolation

"No, not me." The first response to a terminal diagnosis is almost universally denial. Of the over 200 patients Kübler-Ross interviewed, nearly all passed through some period of denial. She argues it is a necessary buffer — it gives the psyche time to mobilise its defences and come to readiness for what cannot yet be fully confronted.

Denial takes forms: requests for second opinions, conviction that X-rays were mixed up, shopping for doctors who will contradict the diagnosis, and maintaining a "make-believe world" for a period. It is not pathological; maintained denial beyond initial shock is rare. What is essential is that the denial belongs to the *patient's* schedule, not the physician's or family's comfort.

> "Denial is usually a temporary defense and will soon be replaced by partial acceptance."

Clinically: the physician's and family's *own* anxiety about death is the primary obstacle to allowing the patient to move through denial at their own pace. When caregivers rush patients out of denial, they serve themselves, not the patient.

### Stage 2: Anger

When denial can no longer be maintained, it is replaced by feelings of rage, resentment, and envy. "Why me? Why not the man across the hall who is eighty-two and no use to anyone?" The anger is often displaced — projected onto physicians ("they don't know what they're doing"), nurses (who get blamed for every discomfort), visiting family, God.

Kübler-Ross's extended cases in this chapter — including Sister I., a nun with Hodgkin disease, and Mr. O., a controlling businessman — illustrate that the displaced anger is not capricious: it is the howl of a person who has been robbed of every form of control. The therapeutic response is not to become defensive or withdraw, but to understand the function of the anger and create space for it to be expressed.

The worst responses from staff are avoidance, shortening visits, and defensiveness — all of which confirm the patient's sense of abandonment and increase the anger. The most helpful response is dignified acknowledgment: "You have every reason to be angry."

### Stage 3: Bargaining

Less discussed and shorter-lived, bargaining is the stage of conditional acceptance and hidden deals. Patients bargain with God, with doctors, with themselves: "If I can just see my son's wedding, I'll accept the rest." "If I promise to dedicate my life to God, perhaps He will grant me more time."

Kübler-Ross's paradigm case is an opera singer with a malignancy disfiguring her jaw and face. She could not perform on stage; she asked to perform just *once more*, attended Kübler-Ross's own seminar, and made arrangements with the hospital chaplain. She performed her lifetime performance — and then bargained again. None of her patients kept their bargains; the pattern repeated like a child's "Just one more story."

Bargaining serves as a transitional structure, a tentative engagement with change that preserves the illusion of control. Many promises involve guilt and are kept secret from family; the chaplain's role is to hear them without judgement and help patients release the guilt that enforces the bargain.

### Stage 4: Depression

Depression arises when the patient can no longer deny the illness, when the cumulative losses become real: loss of role, of function, of body image, of the future once planned. Kübler-Ross distinguishes **two types**:

**Reactive depression** is grief about losses already suffered — the woman who no longer feels like a woman after a mastectomy, the breadwinner who can no longer work, the mother whose children are being cared for by others. This depression responds to active intervention — reassurance, problem-solving, addressing the practical concerns.

**Preparatory depression** is grief about impending losses — the process of decathecting from the world, of saying goodbye not in words but in the inner preparation for separation. This is *not* a depression to lift; attempts to cheer the patient at this stage interfere with necessary mourning. The right response is silent, compassionate presence. "It is a tool to prepare for the impending loss of all the love objects."

> "If the patient is allowed to express his sorrow he will find a final acceptance much easier and will be grateful to those who can sit with him during this stage of depression without constantly telling him not to be sad."

### Stage 5: Acceptance

Acceptance is not happiness. Kübler-Ross describes it as "almost devoid of feelings" — a time of quiet contemplation and expectation. The struggle is over; the patient who has worked through the preceding stages reaches a point of waiting, of readiness. Circle of interest narrows; the patient may prefer brief visits, silence, and the presence of one or two trusted people rather than stimulation.

Acceptance is distinguished from **resignation** (passive giving up, despair) and **depression** (stage 4 preparatory grief). It has a quality of dignity and of completion. Family members often find this stage more difficult to accept than the patient does: they experience the patient's detachment as rejection or giving up, and may push for more treatment or more hope precisely when the patient has found peace.

Mrs. W.'s case is the most painful illustration: a patient who had reached acceptance and asked to be allowed to die peacefully was subjected to a planned surgery against her expressed wishes (because her husband and the surgical team could not let go), was subsequently hospitalised against her will, and expressed her protest through an episode of apparent psychosis that was in fact her only available means of resistance.

### Hope: The Constant Thread

Hope runs through all five stages and is the last thing to be relinquished. Kübler-Ross found that nearly all patients, even those in final acceptance, maintained a small reserve of hope — for an experimental drug, for a remission, for a miracle. "I think they never totally give up the possibility that there is a slight chance that something might work, a new drug, a last-minute success in a research project."

When a patient stops expressing hope entirely, Kübler-Ross found it was often a sign that death was imminent — within 24 hours. The physician's role is not to maintain false hope, but not to extinguish hope either: to be honest about prognosis while leaving open the possibility of "What if?"

> "It is the physician's task not to take away hope, but to mean by hope what the patient needs it to mean."

## The Interdisciplinary Seminar

The book's material came from the Death and Dying Seminar that Kübler-Ross ran at the University of Chicago Billings Hospital from 1965. The seminar was initially resisted by attending physicians, who refused permission for patient interviews on the grounds that such conversations would traumatise patients. Every week, Kübler-Ross and her colleagues spent hours obtaining one patient who was willing to be interviewed.

The format: a dying patient was interviewed in a small room with a one-way mirror, in front of an audience of 50 medical, nursing, and theological students. The interview was open-ended; the patient was told that the hospital staff wanted to learn from people facing serious illness. After the interview, the patient was returned to their room and the group processed their reactions.

What the seminar discovered, against the expectations of the protesting physicians, was that patients were *grateful* to be asked. The most consistent patient complaint was not about the interview but about the isolation and evasion they experienced on the wards — the staff who changed the subject when death was mentioned, the families who maintained cheerful pretence rather than allowing honest conversation, the physicians who made treatment decisions without consulting the patient.

The seminar served as desensitisation for staff and students who had never been trained to sit with dying patients. It demonstrated that communication about death is possible, valuable, and not harmful to patients.

## The Dehumanisation of Dying

*On Death and Dying* is simultaneously a clinical study and a social critique. Kübler-Ross argues that modern technological medicine has transformed dying from a human event into a medical problem — and in doing so has stripped the dying person of dignity, autonomy, and meaningful connection.

The dying person, she observes, is:
- Rushed from their home to an emergency room, surrounded by technicians
- Treated as an "object of great concern and investment" rather than as a person
- Subjected to tests, procedures, infusions, and machines without being consulted
- Unable to get one person to stop for a minute and ask a question
- Surrounded by staff who are "too busy with the heart rate, pulse, and electrocardiogram" to notice the person

> "I wonder if we shall always race the machines to prevent the simple death of a human being with dignity."

Kübler-Ross contrasts this with her own childhood in rural Switzerland, where the dying farmer lay at home, distributed his belongings to his family, received friends and neighbours, and was visible to the children of the community as part of the natural order. That model is presented not as nostalgia but as evidence that dying with dignity is possible — and that modern medicine has systematically undermined it.

Her critique extends to the emotional environment: families who lie to patients about their prognosis ("Mother is on a trip"), physicians who use euphemisms, hospitals that exclude children from dying parents' rooms. All of these, she argues, leave the dying person isolated in their truth, unable to complete their unfinished business or say their goodbyes.

## Therapy for the Terminally Ill

The book's final chapter addresses what therapeutic help is actually needed by dying patients. Kübler-Ross is explicit that this is *not* about long-term psychotherapy but about presence, listening, and the "door opening" interview.

Most dying patients, she found, need:
- Someone to sit with them without anxiety about the word "dying"
- Permission to express anger, guilt, grief, and fear
- Help completing unfinished practical business (care of children, financial arrangements)
- Reassurance that they will not be abandoned

The "door opening" interview is the first encounter that signals to the patient: *This person is not afraid of what I have to say.* Once that signal is received, the patient opens — often with an outpouring of concerns that had been held in check for weeks or months.

Mr. E.'s case illustrates this economy: an eighty-three-year-old man with multiple losses (wife died, daughter died, relatives not visiting) who had developed intractable pain and constipation that disappeared within 24 hours of a single interview in which he was allowed to express his rage, grief, and guilt. The physical symptoms were the body's language for emotions that had nowhere else to go.

The group therapy section documents a spontaneous network of patient-to-patient support that developed from the seminar: patients helping each other, teaching students, receiving the dignity of being treated as people with something worth saying.

> "Watching a peaceful death of a human being reminds us of a falling star; one of a million lights in a vast sky that flares up for a brief moment only to disappear into the endless night forever."

## Connections Within the Wiki

**The direct source for Brennan's seven-stage model**: [Seven Stages of Healing](../concepts/seven-stages-of-healing.md) explicitly builds on the five stages here, adding rebirth and creation as post-acceptance phases specific to healing (rather than dying).

**The psychology of illness**: Kübler-Ross's work is the foundational empirical study of the emotional experience of illness and dying — the precursor of the entire field that Siegel, Borysenko, Myss, and Achterberg would develop. See [The Psychology of Illness](../concepts/the-psychology-of-illness.md).

**Healing vs. curing**: The acceptance stage — a patient who has found peace with dying — is one of the clearest illustrations of [Healing vs. Curing](../concepts/healing-vs-curing.md): a person can be healed without being cured.

**The five stages and crisis**: Kübler-Ross's five stages are one of the richest empirical maps of what [Crisis as Initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) looks like from the inside.

**Near-death experiences**: *On Death and Dying* predates Kübler-Ross's NDE work (she became a major NDE researcher and advocate following this book, co-founding the hospice movement and corresponding extensively with Moody). Her later work is directly relevant to [Near-Death Experiences](../concepts/near-death-experiences.md).

**Dehumanisation and meaning**: The critique of technological medicine parallels Dossey's [Meaning and Health](../concepts/meaning-and-health.md) and Harman's [Consciousness Paradigm Shift](../concepts/consciousness-paradigm-shift.md).

## Critique

**Stage model validity**: Subsequent research has consistently failed to confirm that the five stages occur universally, sequentially, or distinctly. Psychologists such as Wortman and Silver (1989) found that many people never reach "acceptance" and that this does not indicate a failure of grieving. The stages are now understood as a *descriptive* heuristic rather than a normative sequence.

**Sample limitations**: All of Kübler-Ross's patients were from a single university hospital in the 1960s. Cultural, religious, socioeconomic, and demographic variation was not systematically studied.

**Stage models as prescriptions**: The model has been widely misapplied as a prescription — patients who grieve "out of order" or do not reach "acceptance" have sometimes been made to feel they are failing. This is the opposite of Kübler-Ross's intent, which was to remove shame from any emotional response to dying.

**The dehumanisation critique**: Valid and widely accepted in retrospect, but the book's idealised portrait of pre-modern dying does not account for the suffering, the lack of pain control, and the medical powerlessness that accompanied "dying at home with dignity" in earlier eras.
