# Other Lives, Other Selves

**Summary**: Roger Woolger's 1987 Jungian account of past-life regression therapy — arguing that "past-life" imagery, whatever its ontological status, carries genuine psychic and somatic truth that can be processed to resolve phobias, body symptoms, and repetitive patterns that resist conventional therapy.

**Sources**:

## Overview

[Roger Woolger](../thinkers/roger-woolger.md) is a Jungian analyst trained at Oxford and the Jung Institute in Zürich who stumbled into past-life regression work in Vermont in 1976. The book traces his reluctant conversion from Oxford empiricist to practitioner of a therapy he himself found initially absurd, and presents the resulting clinical framework.

The book's central argument: past-life images — whatever their ultimate metaphysical origin — carry *psychic truth*. A "memory" of being stabbed in the throat in a past life, whether or not historically real, holds the emotional and somatic charge of a real wound. That charge can be released through the same cathartic process that releases any other buried trauma. The literal reality of reincarnation is therapeutically irrelevant; the reality of the psychic pattern is not.

## Structure

**Part I — Introductory Encounters with Past Lives**: Woolger's personal evolution; his own first regression (as a papal soldier in the Albigensian Crusade massacres); the typical session with Peter (a twentieth-century migrant worker); the distinction between past-life memory and fantasy; three explanatory frameworks (parapsychological, psychological, Theosophical).

**Part II — An Emerging Synthesis**: The psychotherapist's approach — blending Jungian active imagination, hypnotic regression, and psychodrama; the multidimensional psyche (COEX systems, the lotus wheel diagram — past-life, birth, childhood, and present layers of one complex); karma as the psychology of the complex; the Symbolic Resonance concept.

**Part III — Key Aspects of Past-Life Therapy**: The subtle body and how specific body regions carry past-life themes (throat → beheadings/silencing; belly → courage/gutlessness; genitals → sexual violation); the "archetype of the wounded feminine" and its clinical expression in women's reproductive and sexual difficulties; the drama of psychic opposites across lives (victim/perpetrator polarity).

**Part IV — Perspective**: The wheel of birth — the perinatal interface between life and past-life work (Grof's matrices); the wheel of death — dying thoughts as the mechanism that transmits samskaras; three levels of past-life therapy as a form of Jungian individuation; conclusion on the limits and scope of the method.

## Key Concepts

### Past-Life Complexes and Samskaras

Woolger's central theoretical move: adapting Jung's concept of the complex to include what he calls the *past-life complex* or *samskara* (Sanskrit: soul impression). A samskara is formed when intense dying thoughts, emotions, or unresolved conflicts at death become embedded in the subtle body and reactivate across lifetimes. They operate exactly as Jungian complexes do — autonomously, below consciousness, triggered by present-life resonances — except that their root lies deeper than the current biography. See [Past-Life Complexes](../concepts/past-life-complexes.md).

### The Subtle Body

The vehicle through which past-life memories are stored and transmitted between lives. Specific body regions carry specific past-life themes: throat and neck accumulate unspoken words, betrayals, and beheadings; the belly holds issues of courage and gutlessness; the genitals carry histories of sexual violation and shame. This body-region symbolism parallels the chakra system (throat chakra → self-expression; second chakra → sexuality/creativity) and Keleman's somatic layering. See [Subtle Body](../concepts/subtle-body.md).

### The Deep Memory Process (Three-Level Therapy)

Woolger's method integrates Jungian active imagination, Gestalt body awareness, Reichian energy work, and Moreno's psychodrama into a three-level process:

1. **Realistic/Cathartic stage**: Take the past-life story at face value; allow the emotional charge to release. Mountains are mountains.
2. **Symbolic/Archetypal stage**: De-literalise; find the meaning in the pattern; engage the shadow figure (victim becomes perpetrator and vice versa). Mountains are not mountains.
3. **Integral/Mystical stage**: Full acceptance of the pattern as one's own karma; compassion for all roles; identification with the witnessing Self behind the drama. Mountains are mountains again.

See [Deep Memory Process](../concepts/deep-memory-process.md).

### The Three Levels Map as Individuation

Woolger explicitly maps his three-level therapy onto Jung's individuation process. The sequence — catharsis (Stage 1), shadow integration (Stage 2), Self-realisation (Stage 3) — mirrors the journey from ego to Self. Dürckheim's account of the tension of opposites as the necessary condition for growth is quoted at length as the philosophical foundation of Stage 2.

### Karma as Psychology

Woolger does not argue for reincarnation metaphysically. His preferred position: karmic patterns (samskaras) are real psychological structures that operate as if they originated in previous lifetimes, regardless of the ultimate truth of reincarnation. The therapeutic task is to process and dissolve the samskara — whether it records a genuine past life, an archetypal pattern from the collective unconscious, or a genetic-cultural memory — and thereby free the current personality from compulsive repetition.

### The Archetype of the Wounded Feminine

A recurring clinical finding: women carry accumulated cultural misogyny in their bodies as past-life samskaras of rape, torture, infanticide, and sacrifice. Woolger argues this is not individual pathology but a collective wound — two thousand years of civilisational suppression of the feminine, encoded in the subtle body and inherited across lifetimes. Healing requires reclaiming body sovereignty and confronting the internalized perpetrator as shadow.

The two concepts are structurally identical; Woolger operates at the psychic/imaginal level, Brennan at the energy-field level.

**[Individuation](../concepts/individuation.md)** (Jung): Woolger's three-level therapy is a reformulation of individuation for a multi-lifetime psyche — shadow integration extended to the drama of oppressor and victim across lifetimes.

**[Hara: Vital Centre](../concepts/hara-as-vital-centre.md)** (Dürckheim): Woolger met Dürckheim twice in Germany and credits him with the philosophical foundation for Stage 3 of his therapy — the mystical acceptance of opposites described in *The Way of Transformation*. ## Critique

**Falsifiability**: Past-life memories cannot be verified against historical records with any reliability. Ian Stevenson's children's cases are the strongest empirical evidence for past-life memory and remain contested. Woolger's own position — that verification is therapeutically irrelevant — sidesteps but does not resolve this.

**Suggestibility**: Trance and relaxation states increase susceptibility. Woolger is aware of this and explicitly distinguishes his technique from directed hypnosis; nonetheless, the therapist's presence and questioning inevitably shape what surfaces.

**Cultural specificity**: The "past lives" produced in regression are disproportionately from Western historical settings (medieval Europe, ancient Rome, colonial America). Critics argue this reflects the cultural imagination of predominantly Western clients more than universal soul history.

**Woolger's acknowledged limitations**: He explicitly declines to claim his work proves reincarnation; he notes the method is contraindicated for psychotic clients and those addicted to past-life fantasy as ego-enhancement; he emphasises that three-level integration requires ongoing work, not a single dramatic session.
