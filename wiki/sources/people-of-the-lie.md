# People of the Lie: The Hope for Healing Human Evil

**Summary**: Peck's 1983 psychiatric case study of human evil, proposing that evil is a diagnosable personality disorder rooted in malignant narcissism, scapegoating, and the compulsive refusal to acknowledge one's own sinfulness — with chapters on possession and exorcism, and a case study of group evil in the MyLai massacre.

**Sources**:

## Overview

*People of the Lie* is M. Scott Peck's companion to *The Road Less Traveled* (1978), applying the psychiatrist's lens to a subject most scientists had avoided: the nature of human evil. Peck argues that evil is not a theological abstraction but a clinically identifiable condition — a severe personality disorder characterised by malignant narcissism, scapegoating, and an absolute refusal to tolerate self-examination. The book blends case studies, theological reflection, and a startling account of Peck's two witnessed exorcisms.

**APA citation**: Peck, M. S. (1983). *People of the lie: The hope for healing human evil*. Simon & Schuster. (First Touchstone edition 1985.)

## Key Arguments

### Evil as Illness
Peck proposes that evil should be recognised as a personality disorder — a variant of narcissistic personality disorder distinguishable by its characteristic scapegoating behaviour, pathological lying, and the compulsive refusal to acknowledge imperfection or sinfulness. He argues that psychiatry has neglected this category partly from squeamishness about mixing moral judgment with clinical diagnosis, and partly because evil people are experts at maintaining a facade of social respectability.

### The Central Mechanism: Narcissism and the Refusal of Self-Knowledge
Evil, Peck argues, arises not from the absence of conscience but from the unwillingness to tolerate the discomfort of conscience. Evil people feel the call of conscience; they just refuse to submit to it. The core dynamic is narcissism — an absolute dedication to maintaining the self-image of perfection — combined with the projection of acknowledged fault onto others (scapegoating). Evil people lie constantly: not primarily to deceive others, but to maintain their self-deception. Hence the title: *People of the Lie*.

### People of the Lie
The designation "people of the lie" does not mean habitual liars in the ordinary sense. It means people whose entire psychological life is organised around the avoidance of self-knowledge. Layers of rationalisation, self-justification, and projection insulate them from any awareness of their own destructiveness. When confronted with evidence of their harm to others, they escalate the lie.

## Case Studies

**George and the pact with the devil**: George presents with obsessive-compulsive neurosis — intrusive thoughts compelling him to return to the site of each thought to "prove" it didn't happen. Peck traces the symptoms to George's deep terror of death and his cowardly avoidance of the hard work of living authentically. When George announces he has made a "pact with the devil" to stop the compulsions (he will not act on thoughts in exchange for not dying), Peck confronts the immorality of this bargain — and in that confrontation opens the door to genuine therapy. The case introduces the two models Peck uses throughout: medical model and mythic/moral model.

**Bobby and his parents**: Bobby, fifteen, is admitted following theft after his older brother Stuart's suicide. He is listless, self-mutilating, depressed. The identified patient is not the real patient. His parents gave Bobby, for Christmas, the same type of rifle his brother had used to kill himself. They are well-dressed, church-going, articulate — and utterly without empathy or capacity for self-examination. When Peck raises the Christmas gift, they rationalise with skill. He feels revulsion (which he identifies as the appropriate diagnostic signal for evil) and refers Bobby to an aunt who becomes his saving relationship. Bobby's parents represent the most unambiguous case of evil in the book — destroying a child's spirit while maintaining an unbroken facade of respectability.

**Roger and his parents**: An even subtler case. Roger's parents are sophisticated professionals who consistently override Roger's expressed wishes, block his therapy, reframe Peck's recommendations as attacks on their parenting, and finally send Roger to a punitive boarding school while writing to Peck that they have "followed his advice." The lies are numerous, each individually defensible. The pattern is unmistakable.

**Hartley and Sarah**: An adult case: Hartley is enslaved to his wife Sarah's domination. Sarah's withering contempt has reduced him to an infant; every suicide attempt is answered by tighter control. Peck identifies the "thralldom" dynamic — adults who become partners in evil through symbiosis and dependency.

**Angela and the voodoo dream**: Angela cannot speak in relationships with authority figures — a moat of silence protecting her from her intrusive mother. Through analysis of a recurring dream about a voodoo ritual, Peck helps her name her mother's behaviour as evil. Naming is therapeutic: "She who names the demon gains power over it."

**Billie and the spider phobia**: Billie's spider phobia is identified as a displacement of her terror and revulsion at her clinging mother — a masterful case illustrating the clinical utility of naming evil, and the long, painstaking therapeutic work of helping a person achieve genuine separation from a possessive parent.

**Charlene — the teaching case**: The centrepiece of the book. Charlene enters treatment complaining of depression, but becomes the most comprehensive illustration of evil Peck encountered in practice: 400 sessions, in which she systematically attempts to seduce, control, confuse, and ultimately defeat the therapeutic relationship. She withholds information by design, leaves sessions at will, refuses regression, demands to be loved "as an infant" while refusing to relinquish adult control, and finally terminates unilaterally in a brilliant display of timing and power. Peck ultimately concludes that Charlene may be "two minds" — part desiring conquest, part desiring healing — and that his inability to heal her was partly a failure to wield the authority love requires against evil. He uses Charlene as a teaching case for what evil looks like, how it operates, and how therapy fails against it without appropriate authority and self-purification.

## Possession and Exorcism

In Chapter 5, Peck describes his investigation of demonic possession — beginning as a sceptic, becoming a qualified believer after witnessing two exorcisms. Key findings:

- Possession appears to be a process, not a sudden event: the person "sells" themselves (often through loneliness) to what begins as an apparent companion.
- The exorcisms Peck witnessed involved large teams, lasted ten–twenty hours across multiple days, required physical restraint, and concluded with the patient's deliberate choice to trust God over the demonic voice.
- The demonic entity in each case was characterised by extreme narcissism, lies, and inability to understand love — qualities Peck identifies as the spiritual extreme of what ordinary evil people exhibit.
- Satan, in Peck's account, is the "Father of Lies" — a spirit whose weakness is that lies require effort and whose strength is that fear operates so effectively on isolated humans.

Peck calls for rigorous scientific study of possession and exorcism, and for the establishment of a research centre to accumulate case data.

## Group Evil: MyLai

Chapter 6 analyses the 1968 MyLai massacre as a case study in group evil, tracing it through multiple levels of causal responsibility:

- **Individual stress**: Combat stress induces regression; numbing of moral sensitivity is a survival mechanism that becomes a generator of atrocity.
- **Group dynamics**: Dependency on leaders, group narcissism (esprit de corps built on enemy-hatred), fragmentation of individual conscience through specialisation — each person responsible only for their piece of the machine.
- **Task Force Barker's composition**: Self-selected individuals (those who could not avoid military service) plus career soldiers who needed the war for status and meaning.
- **The American public**: Complicit through laziness and narcissism — letting specialists fight a war they did not understand and did not want to know about, until the draft brought it home.
- **The Johnson administration**: Knowing deception of Congress (the Gulf of Tonkin fraud) as national-scale evil in Peck's definition — using power to destroy truth in order to protect self-image.

Peck's prescription is not abolition of specialisation but citizen service — mandatory participation that prevents the insulation of any subgroup from accountability for collective violence.

## The Methodology of Love

The book concludes with Peck's proposal for studying and treating evil through the methodology of love. Evil cannot be defeated by force (which creates more evil) or by anger (which contaminates the healer). The healer must allow themselves to become a "battleground" — absorbing evil into themselves without being destroyed by it, as Christ absorbed it on the cross. This is not passive: it requires authority, discernment, and courage. But its motive is never destruction of the evil person — always their healing.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **Evil as narcissistic personality disorder**: a clinical category, not merely a theological one
- **Scapegoating as the mechanism**: projecting unacknowledged fault onto others, especially children
- **The revulsion response as diagnostic signal**: the therapist's appropriate response to encountering evil is not empathy but revulsion — a saving instinct
- **Naming evil**: the therapeutic necessity of calling what is happening by its true name
- **Thralldom**: the adult equivalent of the child's entrapment — partners in evil through symbiotic dependency
- **Group evil**: the amplification of individual evil through specialisation, dependency, and fragmentation of conscience
- **Father of Lies**: the theological characterisation of evil at its extreme (Satan)
- **Methodology of love**: the only effective counter to evil

## Connections to the Wiki

*People of the Lie* stands at an unusual angle to the rest of the wiki. Where most sources focus on **healing** (and on the illnesses that result from accumulated biography), Peck focuses on what happens when a person consistently *refuses* the healing available to them — when the refusal to tolerate self-knowledge becomes so entrenched that it begins to destroy others rather than merely the self.

This connects deeply to Myss's [biography-is-biology](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md) thesis — but adds the dimension of *perpetrators* rather than patients. Bobby's parents, Roger's parents, Charlene, and Sarah are not ill in the sense of having somatised their biography: they are destructive to others. Peck asks: what does the psychology of illness look like from the perspective of those whose illness radiates outward rather than inward?

The [shadow](../concepts/shadow.md) work required — and deliberately avoided — by Peck's evil patients is exactly the shadow integration Jung, Myss, and Borysenko identify as the key to healing. Peck's evil people are, in Jung's terms, people for whom the shadow is entirely projected outward and never met face to face.

Peck's account of group evil at MyLai parallels Borysenko's and ACIM's treatments of collective shadow — the way entire cultures project their darkness onto enemies and engage in collective scapegoating. His call for citizen participation resonates with Dossey's Three Eras of Medicine framework and Ring's Omega Hypothesis — the idea that individual evolution of consciousness has collective stakes.
