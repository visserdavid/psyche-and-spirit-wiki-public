# Psychological Types

**Summary**: Jung's 1921 theory of introversion and extraversion combined with four psychological functions (thinking, feeling, sensation, intuition), developed during his post-Freud "fallow period" partly to explain why Freud and Adler reached opposite theoretical conclusions from similar clinical material; the direct historical source of the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1971). *Psychological types* (H. G. Baynes, Trans., rev. R. F. C. Hull; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 6). Princeton University Press. (Original work published 1921 as *Psychologische Typen*; first English translation 1923)](psychological-types.md)

## Overview

*Psychological Types* (German: *Psychologische Typen*) appeared in 1921, marking the end of the "fallow period" following Jung's 1913 break with Freud — the same years of inner turmoil documented in *[Memories, Dreams, Reflections](memories-dreams-reflections.md)* and *[The Red Book: Liber Novus](the-red-book.md)*. Jung says the book "sprang originally from the need to define the ways in which my outlook differed from Freud's and Adler's" — his central claim is that Freud (who reduces neurosis to repressed sexuality located in the object) and Adler (who reduces it to the ego's will to power over the object) were not simply right or wrong but were each describing psychic reality from within a different fundamental psychological type: Freud's theory is characteristically **extraverted** (oriented to the object), Adler's characteristically **introverted** (oriented to the subject). See [Eros and the Will to Power](../concepts/eros-and-will-to-power.md) for this argument's earlier, more compressed statement in *[Two Essays on Analytical Psychology](two-essays-on-analytical-psychology.md)* (1917) — this book is where Jung works the same insight into a full, systematic theory.

The book has three parts. **Chapters I–IX** (roughly two-thirds of the text) are a lengthy historical survey tracing the "type problem" through classical and medieval thought (Tertullian vs. Origen as, respectively, introverted-thinking and extraverted-thinking exemplars in the early Church; the medieval nominalism/realism controversy as a projection of the same opposition), Schiller's *Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man* (to whom Jung devotes an entire chapter, reading Schiller's own introverted-thinking type into his aesthetic theory of play as reconciliation of opposites), the Apollonian/Dionysian distinction, and further case studies drawn from poetry, psychopathology, aesthetics, modern philosophy, and biography. **Chapter X, "General Description of Types,"** gives Jung's mature systematic exposition: the two **attitude-types** (introversion, extraversion) crossed with **four functions** — two "rational" or judging functions (**thinking**, **feeling**) and two "irrational" or perceiving functions (**sensation**, **intuition**) — yielding eight functional types (extraverted thinking, introverted thinking, extraverted feeling, introverted feeling, extraverted sensation, introverted sensation, extraverted intuition, introverted intuition), each described in detail along with its characteristic unconscious compensation and neurotic vulnerability. **Chapter XI, "Definitions,"** is an alphabetical glossary of Jung's own technical vocabulary (abstraction, affect, archaism, assimilation, attitude, collective, compensation, concretism, differentiation, ego, enantiodromia, extraversion, fantasy, feeling, and more) and is historically important as the clearest single statement of many core Jungian terms in his own words.

## The Type System

**The two attitudes**: In the **introverted** attitude, libido flows toward the subject — the inner, subjective factor is given priority over the object. In the **extraverted** attitude, libido flows toward the object — external, objective conditions determine action and value. Jung stresses these are not fixed identities but relatively predominant *mechanisms*; every psyche possesses both, and the habitual attitude of consciousness is compensated by the opposite attitude in the unconscious.

**The four functions**: **Thinking** and **feeling** are the two "rational" (judging) functions — thinking judges via logical connection ("what does this mean?"), feeling judges via value ("what is this worth, accept or reject?"); Jung is explicit that "feeling" here is not emotion but a mode of evaluation. **Sensation** and **intuition** are the two "irrational" (perceiving) functions — sensation perceives via the concrete, immediate sense-datum, intuition perceives via unconscious hunches about possibilities not yet actual. In any individual, one function is habitually dominant (the **superior function**); its opposite remains least developed and most bound up with the unconscious (the **inferior function**), where it takes on infantile, archaic, and compulsive qualities and becomes the main channel through which the unconscious erupts into conscious life — an idea Jung develops function by function in Chapter X (e.g., the extraverted-thinking type's repressed feeling surfaces as secret over-sensitivity and moralistic rigidity; the extraverted-feeling type's repressed thinking surfaces as unconsciously destructive, "hideous" obsessive ideas about the very people most valued).

**Eight types**: crossing the two attitudes with the four functions yields eight types (e.g., extraverted thinking — exemplified, per Jung, by a doctrinaire reformer who subordinates all of life to an objective intellectual formula; introverted intuition — the visionary artist or mystic oriented entirely to inner images). Jung explicitly declines to rank the types or treat any as healthier than another; each has characteristic strengths, blind spots, and a predictable form of neurosis when carried to extremes.

## Historical Origins and the MBTI

Jung credits Friedrich Schiller as the first writer to consciously grasp the type problem at scale (in the 1795 *Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man*), and draws further precedent from the Gnostic threefold typology of pneumatikoi/psychikoi/hylikoi, Nietzsche's Apollonian/Dionysian distinction, and William James's "tender-minded"/"tough-minded" temperaments. Jung's own typology, in turn, became the direct historical basis for the **Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI)**, developed decades later by Katharine Cook Briggs and Isabel Briggs Myers — a psychometric instrument Jung himself never created, endorsed, or lived to see. See [Four Psychological Functions](../concepts/four-psychological-functions.md) for the wiki's fuller treatment of the function model and the MBTI's critical reception.

## Critique

Jung's introversion/extraversion distinction has some support in mainstream trait psychology — it loosely maps onto the Big Five model's "Extraversion" dimension, which does show reasonable measurement reliability and predictive validity. However, mainstream personality psychology treats traits as **continuous dimensions** along which people vary by degree, not as **discrete categories** a person either belongs to or does not; Jung's eight-type system, and the derivative MBTI, impose a categorical either/or structure (e.g., "introvert" vs. "extravert") that most trait researchers consider a poor fit to the actual, continuously graded distribution of these traits in the population. This is the same criticism raised against the MBTI specifically: modest test-retest reliability (a meaningful fraction of people are re-classified into a different type on retaking the test weeks later) and no consistent evidence that the sixteen MBTI types correspond to real, discontinuous personality clusters. Jung's own historical-comparative method in Chapters I–IX — reading his own typology into figures like Tertullian, Origen, and Schiller — is also open to the same "confirmation via selection" critique already noted elsewhere in this wiki for [Frazer](the-golden-bough.md)'s and [Campbell](the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md)'s comparative methods (see the critique sections of those pages): Jung acknowledges, with unusual candor for the genre, that his own psychological type shapes which historical readings feel most persuasive to him.
