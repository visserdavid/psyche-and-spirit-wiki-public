# Quantum Reality

**Summary**: Nick Herbert's 1985 survey of the interpretive crisis in quantum physics — the measurement problem, the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen paradox, and John Stewart Bell's 1964 theorem — cataloguing eight competing "quantum realities" that all fit the experimental facts, while carefully separating Bell's proven non-locality from the speculative consciousness/healing claims later popularizers built on it.

**Sources**:
- [Herbert, N. (1985). *Quantum reality: Beyond the new physics*. Anchor Books/Doubleday.](quantum-reality.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-22

---

## Overview

Herbert opens from his own graduate-school bewilderment: quantum theory predicts experimental results with total success, yet supplies no agreed picture of what is actually happening when no one is measuring. He surveys eight distinct, mutually incompatible interpretations physicists have proposed — all experimentally indistinguishable from one another — before showing how John Stewart Bell's 1964 theorem constrains, without settling, the question.

## The Eight Quantum Realities

1. **Copenhagen I** (Bohr) — there is no deep reality, only the abstract quantum description.
2. **Copenhagen II** — reality is created by observation (Wheeler: "no elementary phenomenon is a real phenomenon until it is an observed phenomenon").
3. **Undivided wholeness** (Bohm, Heitler) — the world is a seamless, inseparable whole; see [Implicate Order](../concepts/implicate-order.md).
4. **Many-worlds** (Everett) — every measurement splits reality into all possible outcomes, each equally real.
5. **Quantum logic** — quantum facts require abandoning ordinary Boolean reasoning for a non-classical logic.
6. **Neorealism** (Einstein, de Broglie, Bohm) — the world is made of ordinary objects with attributes of their own, even unobserved; a minority, dissident position among physicists.
7. **Consciousness creates reality** (von Neumann, Wigner) — only a conscious observer collapses the wave function.
8. **Heisenberg's duplex world** — an unmeasured "potentia" (real tendency, not yet actuality) becomes actual only through measurement.

## The EPR Paradox and Bell's Theorem

Herbert devotes his most important chapters (11–12) to reconstructing the argument in detail. Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (1935) proposed a thought experiment — two particles emitted in a correlated "twin state," their properties perfectly matched however far apart they later travel — to argue that quantum theory must be *incomplete*: since no signal can travel faster than light (the "locality assumption"), the correlated particles must already carry hidden, definite properties quantum theory simply fails to describe.

**John Stewart Bell** (1964) showed mathematically that any theory obeying the locality assumption makes a specific numerical prediction (the Bell inequality) that contradicts quantum theory's own prediction for these correlated pairs. When John Clauser (Berkeley, 1972) and later Alain Aspect (Paris, 1982) ran the actual experiment, the Bell inequality was violated exactly as quantum theory predicted. The conclusion is not optional: **reality is non-local**. Distant, correlated events are linked "unmediated, unmitigated, and immediate" — not through any signal, field, or force, but through a connection Bell's proof demonstrates must exist.

Herbert is explicit about what this does and does not mean: non-locality is proven; faster-than-light *signaling* is not possible (a separate result, Eberhard's proof, shows quantum randomness itself blocks any usable message riding on the non-local correlation). Herbert's own closing speculation — whether ordinary human consciousness might itself be "a type of quantum knowledge," non-locally connected to everything it has ever interacted with — is offered explicitly as speculation, not as an established implication of Bell's theorem.

## Reception and Use in This Wiki's Literature

*Quantum Reality* is cited directly, and by name, in [Larry Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md)'s [Healing Words](healing-words.md) and [Michael Talbot](../thinkers/michael-talbot.md)'s [The Holographic Universe](the-holographic-universe.md), both of which quote Herbert's statement that "Bell's theorem requires our knowledge of the world to be non-local" as physical grounding for [Non-local Consciousness](../concepts/non-local-consciousness.md) — the theory that prayer, distant healing, and intuitive diagnosis operate through a non-local field of mind rather than an energy signal that would have to weaken with distance.

Herbert's own chapter 12 epigraph quotes [James George Frazer](../thinkers/james-george-frazer.md) directly: "Contagious magic [is] based on the assumption that substances once joined together possess a continuing linkage, thus... an act carried out on the smaller unit [will] affect the larger unit even [when] physically separated" — Herbert's deliberate framing of Bell's theorem as a physics-grade vindication of the logical structure (though not the causal claims) of [sympathetic magic and taboo](../concepts/sympathetic-magic-and-taboo.md).

## Balance and Critique

Bell's theorem and its experimental confirmation (Clauser 1972, Aspect 1982) are uncontested, mainstream physics — this is not a fringe claim. The critique this wiki must note concerns the *extension* of that physics, made by later, less careful writers than Herbert himself:

- **The scale problem**: Bell's theorem is proven for subatomic particles in tightly controlled, decohered-free laboratory conditions. No accepted physical mechanism bridges this to macroscopic biological systems (a human brain, a healer's intention) — the "scaling up" argument is unproven, not merely undecided.
- **Non-locality without signaling**: Herbert's own Eberhard-proof discussion establishes that the non-local correlations cannot carry usable information. This directly undercuts the popular inference (made in some of the literature this wiki covers, though not by Herbert) that Bell's theorem licenses distant healing or telepathic communication — those claims would require exactly the kind of signal-carrying non-locality Bell's theorem forbids.
- Herbert himself models the right degree of caution here, flagging his own final chapter's consciousness speculation as unconfirmed rumor rather than physics. See [Non-local Consciousness](../concepts/non-local-consciousness.md)'s own critique section for the fuller mainstream-neuroscience objection to scaling quantum non-locality up to mind.
