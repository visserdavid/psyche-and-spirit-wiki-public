# Re-Visioning Psychology

**Summary**: James Hillman's 1975 manifesto for archetypal psychology, delivered as the 1972 Terry Lectures at Yale, which argues that psychology should orient itself around soul-making rather than ego, health, or development — reading imagination, pathology, and ideation as autonomous activities of a plural, polytheistic psyche rather than problems to be solved by a unifying, monotheistic consciousness.

**Sources**:
- [Hillman, J. (1975). *Re-visioning psychology*. Harper & Row.](re-visioning-psychology.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-14

## Overview

*Re-Visioning Psychology* is the foundational theoretical statement of [archetypal psychology](../thinkers/james-hillman.md), the school Hillman founded after breaking with mainstream Jungian analytical psychology. The vault text is the 1975 Harper & Row first edition (266 pages; the book's later HarperPerennial reprints add only a new preface, not new content — the argument itself is unchanged). It began as the 1972 Dwight Harrington Terry Lectures at Yale University and is organized as four essays rather than a linear argument: **Personifying**, **Pathologizing**, **Psychologizing**, and **Dehumanizing**.

Hillman's method is deliberately non-systematic — aphoristic, etymological, mythological, and clinical by turns, drawing on Greek myth, Renaissance Neoplatonism, alchemy, and the case histories of Freud and Jung rather than building a stepwise thesis. He states this explicitly: the book "has no beginning and no ending... is not written as an argument to a conclusion." The organizing thread across all four chapters is *soul-making* — a term Hillman borrows from Keats's letters ("Call the world if you please 'The vale of Soul-making'") and reworks into a technical concept: psychology's task is not curing, developing, or unifying the psyche but deepening its capacity for image, meaning, and reflection.

## Key Takeaways

- **[Soul-Making and the Imaginal](../concepts/soul-making.md)**: Hillman distinguishes soul (deepening, particularizing, image-making, bound to death) from spirit (ascending, unifying, abstracting) and argues psychology has collapsed the two, then reduced soul to ego. Personifying — treating psychic contents (moods, symptoms, complexes) as autonomous figures rather than mechanisms — is the primary technique for recovering soul's plurality.
- **[Pathologizing](../concepts/pathologizing.md)**: The psyche's capacity to fall into disorder, symptom, and morbidity is not a malfunction to be treated but a necessary, meaning-bearing activity of soul-making. Hillman rejects the medical model of psychopathology (inherited from nominalist diagnostic taxonomy) while also rejecting the New Age/humanist denial of pathology's necessity (the "peak experience" alternative).
- **[Polytheistic Psychology](../concepts/polytheistic-psychology.md)**: Hillman's most consequential and controversial move — the psyche is inherently plural, animated by many archetypal/mythic patterns (Apollo, Dionysus, Hermes, Hera, Saturn) rather than unified under one dominant principle. This is an explicit break from [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s individuation-toward-[the Self](../concepts/the-self.md), which Hillman reads as a "crypto-monotheism" imposed on a psyche that is actually polytheistic. It also grounds his critique of humanist psychology's ego-centred model of the person ("Dehumanizing" — the deliberate decentring of psychology from the human ego toward soul, myth, and image).

## Structure

1. **Personifying: Imagining Things** — the history of depersonification (Mersenne, nominalism, the Enlightenment, the Reformation's iconoclasm) and its recovery through Freud's and especially Jung's practice of treating complexes, the shadow, and the anima as autonomous persons.
2. **Pathologizing: Falling Apart** — the three styles of psychiatric "denial" of pathologizing (nominalism, nihilism, transcendence), the critique of both the medical model and the humanist "peak experience" alternative (Maslow), and the case for reading symptoms as mythic language (Saturn/depression, Dionysus/hysteria, Pan/panic).
3. **Psychologizing: Seeing Through** — the recovery of ideation (against a psychology of pure action/behaviour), archetypal premises underlying all systems of thought (including science itself), and the discipline of "seeing through" literalism without collapsing into psychologism.
4. **Dehumanizing: Soul-Making** — the explicit statement of polytheistic psychology against monotheistic consciousness, the critique of humanism's ego-inflation, and the Renaissance (especially Marsilio Ficino) as the historical model for a psychology centred on soul rather than man.

Prior to this ingest, Hillman was present in the wiki only through his later, more accessible works — [*The Soul's Code*](souls-code.md) (1996) and his citation within Myss's [*Sacred Contracts*](sacred-contracts.md) (2001) — both of which assume the theoretical framework this book lays out. This ingest grounds [wiki/thinkers/james-hillman.md](../thinkers/james-hillman.md) in its primary source and supplies the explicit Jung-critique argument (polytheism vs. monotheism, soul vs. Self) that the later books reference but do not develop at length.

## Critique

Hillman's rejection of the medical model of psychopathology and his anti-systematic, literary method have been criticized by mainstream clinical psychology and psychiatry as unfalsifiable and resistant to empirical evaluation — the book makes no claims that could be tested, disconfirmed, or operationalized, and Hillman treats this as a virtue rather than a weakness (see [Pathologizing](../concepts/pathologizing.md) for the fuller discussion). Note: mainstream clinical psychiatry continues to use diagnostic-nosological classification (DSM/ICD) of exactly the kind Hillman calls "nominalism" and regards as a fallacy, precisely because reliable diagnostic categories, however imperfect, are prerequisite to treatment research, epidemiology, and insurance/medical systems — a practical constraint archetypal psychology does not have to answer to.
