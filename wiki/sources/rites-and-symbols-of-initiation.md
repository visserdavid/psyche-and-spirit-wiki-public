# Rites and Symbols of Initiation

**Summary**: Mircea Eliade's 1958 comparative study of puberty rites, tribal secret societies, and shamanic vocation across cultures, arguing that all initiation enacts a symbolic death of the profane, childhood self and rebirth into a sacred, adult mode of existence.

**Sources**:
- [Eliade, M. (1958). *Rites and symbols of initiation: The mysteries of birth and rebirth* (W. R. Trask, Trans.). Harper & Row. (Original work published 1958 as *Birth and rebirth*)](rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-20

## Overview

Originally delivered as the 1956 Haskell Lectures at the University of Chicago under the title "Patterns of Initiation," this book is [Mircea Eliade](../thinkers/mircea-eliade.md)'s single most systematic treatment of initiatory ritual. First published by Harper & Brothers in 1958 as *Birth and Rebirth*, it was reissued in 1975 under the expanded title *Rites and Symbols of Initiation: The Mysteries of Birth and Rebirth* (Harper Colophon), the edition this wiki cites.

Eliade's central thesis: initiation, in whatever cultural form it takes, is a decisive alteration of religious and social status accomplished through the symbolic death of one mode of existence (childhood, ignorance, the profane condition) and rebirth into another (adulthood, sacred knowledge, the transcendent condition). See [Symbolic Death and Rebirth](../concepts/symbolic-death-and-rebirth.md) for the wiki's full treatment of this motif.

## Structure of the Book

Eliade distinguishes **three broad categories** of initiation, present in varying combinations across the ethnographic record:

1. **Puberty initiations** (tribal/age-group initiations): collective, obligatory rites marking the transition from childhood to adulthood, documented from Australia, Tierra del Fuego, and Africa to North America.
2. **Initiation into secret societies or confraternities**: voluntary, more restricted rites of entry into closed groups (men's societies, women's societies, mystery cults), building on but intensifying the puberty pattern with greater secrecy, more elaborate ordeals, and a shift from Supreme Sky Beings toward Ancestor- and mask-centered mythologies.
3. **Shamanic/mystical initiations**: individual, often involuntary vocational callings marked by "initiatory sickness" — visionary dismemberment, reduction to a skeleton, and reconstitution with new organs — the most intensely personal and dramatic of the three categories.

The book proceeds chapter by chapter through Australian Aboriginal puberty ceremonies (bull-roarers, circumcision, subincision, the "swallowed monster" motif); the symbolism of ritual return to the womb (Vedic *diksha* and *upanayana*, the Indian *hiranyagarbha* "golden embryo" rite, Taoist embryonic breathing, Western alchemy); heroic and shamanic initiations (Germanic berserkers, Siberian and Central Asian shamanic dismemberment, Australian medicine-man initiations); and finally "Patterns of Initiation in Higher Religions" — Vedic and Tantric India, the Eleusinian and Dionysian mysteries, and the survival of initiatory motifs in Christianity, alchemy, and Arthurian romance.

## Puberty Initiation: The Basic Pattern

Eliade's Australian material (Kurnai, Yuin, Wiradjuri, Arunta) establishes the recurring scenario: **separation** of the novice from the mother (sometimes deliberately violent, to impress on both mother and child that a death has occurred); **segregation** in the bush under prohibitions of speech, sight, food, and sleep (each interpreted as symbolically equating the novice with the dead, or with a fetus/newborn); **ordeals** (circumcision, subincision, knocking out a tooth, scarification) understood not as arbitrary cruelty but as ritual killing performed by a Supernatural Being (voiced through the bull-roarer); and **revelation** of the tribe's sacred myths and traditions, which is the actual point of the ordeal — access to the tribe's sacred history, not the physical trial itself.

## The Swallowed-Monster and Return-to-Womb Motifs

Two image-clusters recur across virtually every culture Eliade surveys:

**Swallowed by the monster**: the initiatory hut or cabin is repeatedly represented as the body of a devouring monster (a snake, crocodile, or constructed effigy) that the novice is said to enter and be digested by, later to be disgorged/reborn. Documented from Australia (the Kunapipi cult's Great Snake Julunggul) to New Guinea (the Barlun monster-house) to West Africa (the Ngakola secret society).

**Return to the womb**: a parallel and sometimes overlapping image-cluster in which initiatory seclusion, burial, or ritual enclosure represents not death by digestion but gestation — a symbolic re-entry into the Great Mother's or Mother Earth's womb. Eliade traces this from Vedic India (the *diksha* rite, in which the sacrificer is ritually "made an embryo again"; the *hiranyagarbha* "golden embryo" ceremony) through Taoist embryonic-breathing meditation to Western alchemy (Paracelsus: "in order to enter the Kingdom of God ... first enter [into] the body of [the] mother [and] die there").

Eliade stresses that both motifs are **ambivalent rather than purely negative**: entering the monster's belly or the Mother's womb is dangerous, but it is preparatory to rebirth, not mere annihilation — the same ambivalence [Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md) identifies in the [World Navel](../concepts/world-navel.md) as a fixed cosmic center that is simultaneously life-giving and threatening.

## Shamanic Initiation

Chapter V, "Heroic and Shamanic Initiations," previews the material Eliade would develop at book length in *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* (1951; not yet in this wiki). Siberian and Central Asian shamanic vocation typically begins with an "initiatory sickness" — visions or psychotic-seeming episodes in which the future shaman is dismembered by ancestral spirits or demons, has his flesh stripped and bones cleaned, and is given new organs and new blood before waking with the power to heal. Eliade explicitly distinguishes this from ordinary mental illness: the future shaman's crisis is resolved, not perpetuated, by the initiatory pattern, and shamanic vocation is marked by successful integration of the crisis into a new, functioning personality — the opposite trajectory from psychosis. He also documents a strikingly parallel pattern among Australian medicine men (dismemberment, insertion of magical quartz crystals, renewal of organs), which he treats as either evidence of a very ancient, possibly diffused hunter-culture complex or of a universal psychological substrate independent of cultural contact.

## Patterns in Higher Religions

Eliade's final chapter argues that the same initiatory pattern persists, transformed, in Vedic Brahmanism (*upanayana*, "twice-born" status), Tantric yoga and Buddhism (ritual visualization of one's own dismemberment, the *tchöd* rite), the Eleusinian and Dionysian mystery religions of Greece (symbolic descent to Hades, revelation of "the reaped ear of grain," ritual death and rebirth as *nata... sacrum*, "sacred birth"), early Christianity (baptism as literal death-and-rebirth symbolism, though Eliade argues this derives from Christianity's own theological logic rather than direct borrowing from the mystery religions), medieval alchemy (the *nigredo* stage as symbolic death, homologized to return to the womb), and even the Arthurian Grail romances and modern literature (Joyce, Eliot), where initiatory motifs survive as unconscious structure rather than lived ritual. Eliade closes by invoking [Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s concept of individuation directly, reading the psychological crises of modern secular life as unconsciously patterned survivals of the initiatory death-and-rebirth structure.

## Connections Across the Wiki

- [Arnold van Gennep](../thinkers/arnold-van-gennep.md)'s [tripartite schema](../concepts/rites-of-passage-schema.md) (separation/transition/incorporation) is Eliade's explicit structural starting point, cited in his first chapter's notes.
- [Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md)'s [Belly of the Whale](../concepts/hero-myth.md) stage in *[The Hero with a Thousand Faces](the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md)* is the mythological correlate of Eliade's swallowed-monster initiatory motif — Campbell's Jonah parallels Eliade's Ngakola and Kunapipi material directly.
- [Victor Turner](../thinkers/victor-turner.md)'s [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md) elaborates the same "betwixt and between" transitional state Eliade treats as the site of symbolic death, though Turner's ethnographic base (Ndembu fieldwork) is narrower and more empirically grounded than Eliade's wide, largely secondhand comparative net.
- [Jeanne Achterberg](../thinkers/jeanne-achterberg.md)'s [Shamanic Healing](../concepts/shamanic-healing.md) already cites Eliade's later *Shamanism* (1951) secondhand for the "technician of ecstasy" formulation; this ingest gives the wiki Eliade's own primary-sourced account of shamanic initiatory sickness for the first time.

## Critique

**Comparative overreach**: As with Campbell's monomyth and van Gennep's schema, critics argue Eliade's morphological method — assembling structurally similar motifs from historically unconnected cultures — risks flattening genuine cultural and historical difference into a universal template drawn largely from secondhand ethnographic sources rather than direct fieldwork.

**Romanticization of archaic religiosity**: Eliade's evident nostalgia for what he calls "archaic ontology" — his repeated claims that modern secular man has lost something modern religion cannot restore — is itself an interpretive stance, not a neutral description, and has been criticized as reflecting Eliade's own conservative, anti-modernist cultural politics.
