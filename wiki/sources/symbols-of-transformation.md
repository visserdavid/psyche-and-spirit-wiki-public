# Symbols of Transformation

**Summary**: Jung's 1912 book (revised 1952) analyzing a young American woman's published fantasy material — the "Miller fantasies" — as mythological rather than sexual in origin; the text whose theoretical break with Freud's sexual theory of libido directly precipitated the end of their friendship in 1913.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1967). *Symbols of transformation* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 5). Princeton University Press.](symbols-of-transformation.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-22

---

## Overview

Originally published in 1912 as *Wandlungen und Symbole der Libido* (translated into English in 1916 as *Psychology of the Unconscious*), this book grew out of Jung's analysis of a body of fantasy material published by the Genevan psychologist Théodore Flournoy in 1906: the poems, visions, and waking fantasies of a young American woman known only by the pseudonym "Miss Frank Miller," whom Jung never met or treated directly. Jung substantially revised the book in 1952 (republished as *Symbole der Wandlung*), softening some of its earlier, more explicitly Freudian vocabulary while preserving its core argument; this English translation (Hull, 2nd ed. 1967) follows the revised text.

The book's publication is historically inseparable from Jung's break with [Sigmund Freud](../thinkers/sigmund-freud.md): Jung completed its climactic chapter, "The Sacrifice," knowing in advance it would cost the friendship, since it directly contradicted Freud's insistence that libido was fundamentally sexual. *[Memories, Dreams, Reflections](memories-dreams-reflections.md)* gives Jung's own first-person account of the 1913 rupture that followed; this book is the theoretical document that precipitated it.

## Two Kinds of Thinking

Jung opens by distinguishing **directed thinking** (logical, verbal, effortful, adapted to external reality — the thinking of science and communication) from **fantasy thinking** (image-driven, effortless, guided by unconscious motives, and structurally continuous with dream, myth, and the thought of children and "primitives"). He argues fantasy thinking is not a regression to something pathological but a normal, universal mode of psychic activity that surfaces whenever directed thinking relaxes — and that myths are the great, collectively-elaborated products of fantasy thinking, just as dreams are its individual products.

## The Miller Fantasies

Jung reads Miss Miller's fantasy sequence — including a hypnagogic vision of an Aztec/Peruvian hero-figure she names **Chiwantopel** — as spontaneous productions of mythological material she could not have consciously known in that form. He traces the Chiwantopel figure and its accompanying **Sphinx** vision through comparative mythology: the Sphinx as a **Terrible Mother** figure (echoing Oedipus), Chiwantopel as a masculine **animus** figure (a term Jung develops further here — the personification of a woman's unconscious masculine component), and the hero's solar, wandering character (Khidr, Gilgamesh, the Wandering Jew, Mithras) as symbolic self-representations of libido's cyclical death and renewal.

**Methodological caveat**: Jung's central case material was analyzed entirely from a secondary published source, without any direct clinical contact with Miller or ability to verify her actual psychological state — a significant limitation even by the clinical standards of 1912, and one later commentators (including within the Jungian tradition) have noted makes the book's interpretive claims harder to test than case material drawn from Jung's own patients.

## Libido Redefined

The book's central theoretical move is to redefine **libido** as general psychic energy — the intensity or "value" attaching to any psychic content or activity — rather than Freud's specifically sexual libido. Jung treats sexuality as one important channel of libido among several (hunger, the drive to create, spiritual longing), a redefinition that let him read mythological material (hero-birth, incest, sacrifice) as expressing the *transformation* of general psychic energy across life's stages, rather than disguised infantile sexual wishes. This is the conceptual root of the "attitude-types" and function-typology Jung develops fully six years later in *[Psychological Types](psychological-types.md)*.

## The Hero, the Mother, and Sacrifice

Later chapters ("The Origin of the Hero," "Symbols of the Mother and of Rebirth," "The Battle for Deliverance from the Mother," "The Dual Mother," "The Sacrifice") develop, for the first time in full, the archetypal pattern of the hero's incestuous pull back toward the devouring/nourishing mother, the necessity of sacrificing or renouncing that regressive pull, and symbolic rebirth achieved through the sacrifice — material Jung draws substantially from [Frazer](../thinkers/james-george-frazer.md)'s comparative mythology of dying-and-rising vegetation gods (Attis, Osiris, Adonis) alongside Mithraic and Gnostic sources. This is the first developed statement of the hero-mother-rebirth pattern that becomes central to Jung's mature archetypal theory and, through Jung, to [Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md)'s monomyth.

## Critique

Beyond the single-case evidentiary weakness noted above, the book's comparative method — assembling mythological parallels from Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Islamic, Greek, and Christian sources to support a single psychological reading — shares the same "armchair" vulnerability already noted on this wiki regarding [Frazer](../thinkers/james-george-frazer.md) and [Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md): assembling resemblances across unrelated traditions risks manufacturing the very universality it claims to discover. Jung's own later work is more clinically grounded in his own case material; this early book is best read as the founding theoretical document of analytical psychology's break with Freud rather than as an empirically rigorous study.
