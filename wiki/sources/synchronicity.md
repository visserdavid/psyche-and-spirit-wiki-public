# Synchronicity: An Acausal Connecting Principle

**Summary**: Jung's fullest theoretical statement of synchronicity — meaningful, acausal coincidence between psychic and physical events — developed in dialogue with physicist Wolfgang Pauli and grounded in the scarab case, an astrological statistics experiment on 483 marriage horoscopes, J. B. Rhine's ESP card experiments, and a survey of Taoist, alchemical, and mantic forerunners of the idea.

## Bibliographic Note

Jung first floated the term "synchronicity" publicly in a 1930 memorial address on Richard Wilhelm, but developed the full theory only in the early 1950s, in close intellectual partnership with Nobel physicist Wolfgang Pauli — himself Jung's analysand in the 1930s. The essay was originally published in German in 1952 in the joint volume *Naturerklärung und Psyche* (Rascher Verlag, Zurich), pairing Jung's essay with Pauli's own study of archetypal influences on Kepler's astronomy. The English translation by R. F. C. Hull appeared in 1955 (first UK edition), incorporated into Volume 8 of the Collected Works, *The Structure and Dynamics of the Psyche* (1960), and later issued as a standalone Ark/Routledge paperback. The vault file is the standalone Routledge edition (first published in this form 1955, this printing 2003–2006).

## Overview

Jung's essay argues that causality — connection through cause and effect — is not the only ordering principle available to explain how events relate. He proposes a second, **acausal connecting principle**: **synchronicity**, "the simultaneous occurrence of a certain psychic state with one or more external events which appear as meaningful parallels to the momentary subjective state." Two events are synchronistic when they share a *meaning* but have no discoverable causal link.

## Structure and Key Content

### The Scarab Case and Other Clinical Examples

The book's most famous example — later the concept's standard illustration across this wiki (see [Synchronicity](../concepts/synchronicity.md)) — recounts a patient recounting a dream of a golden scarab at the exact moment a real scarabaeid beetle (a rose-chafer) tapped at the consulting-room window. Jung links this to the archetypal symbolism of the scarab as an Egyptian rebirth symbol, arguing the coincidence broke through the patient's rigid rationalism and released a stalled analysis. He gives several parallel cases: a wife's premonition of her husband's death coinciding with birds gathering at the house (echoing an old folk belief linking birds and death), and correspondence-timed dreams of distant deaths (the Krakatoa dream, the Fortgibu plum-pudding coincidence, borrowed from earlier psychical-research literature).

### The Rhine ESP Experiments

Jung leans heavily on J. B. Rhine's card-guessing and dice experiments (Duke University, 1930s) as statistically rigorous evidence for acausal correlation independent of both space and time — subjects scoring significantly above chance regardless of distance from the experimenter (up to 4,000 miles), and even when "guessing" cards not yet shuffled. Jung reads Rhine's finding that scores decline with subject boredom and rise with renewed interest as evidence that **emotional/affective activation of an archetype** is the precondition for synchronistic phenomena — connecting synchronicity structurally to his theory of the **psychoid archetype** (an archetype's irrepresentable, quasi-physical aspect, straddling psyche and matter) and the **collective unconscious**.

### The Astrological Marriage Experiment

Jung's own empirical contribution: a statistical test of the old astrological claim that marriage partners' horoscopes show characteristic correspondences. Using birth data for up to 483 married couples (966 horoscopes) gathered from astrologer contacts, he tested the frequency of classical "marriage aspects" (Sun-Moon and Moon-Moon conjunctions, Mars-Venus aspects) against a much larger pool of randomly paired, unmarried horoscopes. Across three successive batches, the traditional lunar conjunctions did turn up as the statistical maxima — but at steadily declining and individually unremarkable significance levels (calculated with mathematician Markus Fierz's help), a pattern Jung reads not as proof of astrology but as itself a second-order synchronistic phenomenon: an improbable-but-not-impossible coincidence between his own long-standing interest in the outcome and the result obtained. He is candid about errors discovered late in the analysis that had inflated the apparent significance, and about the corrective statistical experiment (reshuffling the batches) that further dissolved the effect — a notable instance of a thinker publishing his own null-result correction rather than suppressing it.

### Forerunners: Tao, Alchemy, and Correspondence Theories

Jung surveys historical precedents for acausal, meaning-based cosmologies: the Chinese concept of **Tao** (via Richard Wilhelm's translations of Lao-tzu and Chuang-tzu) as an ordering principle prior to and encompassing causality; the medieval and classical doctrine of universal **sympathy/correspondence** (Hippocrates, Philo, Theophrastus, Plotinus, Pico della Mirandola); and the **I Ching** as a divinatory technique explicitly built on the premise that a randomly generated hexagram and the questioner's psychological situation are connected by meaning rather than cause — the same logic Jung applies to astrology and to his own clinical material.
