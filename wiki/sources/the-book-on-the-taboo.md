# The Book: On the Taboo Against Knowing Who You Are

**Summary**: Alan Watts's 1966 popular synthesis of Vedantic non-dualism, cybernetics, and ecology, arguing that Western culture suppresses the recognition that the separate "skin-encapsulated ego" is a hallucination — that the individual self is, at root, the whole cosmos playing at being an individual.

**Sources**:
- [Watts, A. W. (1966). *The book: On the taboo against knowing who you are*. Pantheon Books.](the-book-on-the-taboo.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-22

---

## Overview

Written at the height of Watts's public career and dedicated to his children and grandchildren, *The Book* is his most direct and systematic popular statement of the thesis running through his whole body of work: that the felt sensation of being an isolated ego "inside a bag of skin," confronting an external and alien world, is not a fact about human nature but a culturally and linguistically reinforced hallucination — one Watts calls a societal "taboo" because it is tacitly enforced, rarely examined, and treated as dangerous or absurd to question directly.

The book draws primarily on Vedanta (the Hindu Upanishadic teaching that the individual self, *atman*, is ultimately identical with the universal ground of being, *Brahman* — *tat tvam asi*, "thou art that"), presented in a deliberately non-technical, non-devotional, Western idiom rather than as an introduction to Hindu doctrine as such. Watts explicitly states that his purpose is "cross-fertilization" between Western science and Eastern intuition, not textbook exposition.

## Structure and Key Content

The book is organized into six chapters, each developing a facet of the same argument:

- **"Inside Information"**: states the taboo directly — the suppressed recognition that the separate ego is a temporary role, and the mythic "hide-and-seek" image of God/the Self playing at being all things, forgetting its own identity in order to have the game of finding it again.
- **"The Game of Black-and-White"**: uses Gestalt figure/ground perception, cybernetics, and the physics of vibration (all experience as alternating "on/off" pulses that consciousness selectively attends to) to argue that apparent opposites (self/other, life/death, solid/space) are interdependent poles of a single process rather than separate, warring realities — see [The Skin-Encapsulated Ego](../concepts/skin-encapsulated-ego.md) for the "Game of Black-and-White" and its resolution into Vedanta's non-dualism (*advaita*).
- **"The Fake Genuine, the Genuine Fake"**: a phenomenology of where people locate the felt "I" (head, chest, solar plexus, across cultures) and how memory and narrowed conscious attention manufacture the sensation of a persisting, separate observer.
- **"The World Is Your Body"**: extends the argument from the individual organism to the organism-environment relationship, using ecology, the rainbow (which exists only as a relation between sun, moisture, and observer, not as a free-standing object), and quotations from physicists David Bohm and Erwin Schrödinger to argue that organism and environment are mutually defining poles, not separate things.
- **"So What?"**: addresses the practical objection that dissolving the ego-illusion would undercut moral effort and social reform, arguing instead for acting from present spontaneity ("play" rather than anxious future-directed striving) rather than from guilt or compulsive control.
- **"IT"**: closes with the non-dual (*advaita*) resolution proper — the "IT" that cannot be captured in dualistic language because language itself works by contrast and classification, ending (in Watts's own account) with laughter rather than a final doctrine.

## Key Takeaways

- **[The Skin-Encapsulated Ego](../concepts/skin-encapsulated-ego.md)**: Watts's central thesis — the separate self is a societal hoax maintained by language, the "double-bind" of being simultaneously commanded to be free and to conform, and the narrow "spotlight" mode of conscious attention that ignores the vastly larger field (bodily, ecological, cosmic) any individual is actually inseparable from.
- **The hide-and-seek myth of God/Self**: Watts's own retelling of a Vedantic-style children's story — God/the Self plays hide-and-seek with itself by pretending to be every person, animal, plant, and rock in the universe, deliberately forgetting its own identity so the game of re-discovery remains fun; this is his most quoted and most frequently anthologized passage.
- **The "double-bind"**: society simultaneously commands the individual "be a free, responsible, independent agent" and "be loving, spontaneous, and sincere only if you truly want to" — a logically self-contradictory pair of demands (drawing on Gregory Bateson's double-bind concept from communications theory) that Watts argues is the psychological mechanism producing chronic guilt and the compulsive, future-oriented "Game of Black-and-White" (order versus chance, technological control versus surrender).
- **Physics as corroboration, carefully qualified**: Watts cites quantum theory (via David Bohm) and Erwin Schrödinger's own philosophical writing on the unity of consciousness to support the organism/environment non-separability thesis — a rare case in the popular non-dualist literature of citing physicists directly and relatively carefully, rather than through loose analogy alone.
- **Ecology and "world body"**: extends the thesis to a proto-ecological argument that the individual/environment split underlies (and needs to be corrected to address) the environmental and social crises of industrial technological civilization.

## Reception and Critique

*The Book* remains Watts's most reprinted work and a foundational text of the popular consciousness/human-potential literature of the later 1960s and 1970s, cited directly or indirectly across a wide swath of the psychedelic, Esalen, and New Age literature that followed. Its bibliography (Watts's own list of "books that expand these themes") explicitly names [William James's *The Varieties of Religious Experience*](the-varieties-of-religious-experience.md), [Jung's *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*](memories-dreams-reflections.md), and [Teilhard de Chardin's *The Phenomenon of Man*](the-phenomenon-of-man.md) — all three already primary sources in this wiki, confirming Watts's direct engagement with material already central here.

**From scholars of Asian religion**: see [Alan Watts](../thinkers/alan-watts.md) for the fuller critique — Watts's synthetic "Eastern non-dual wisdom" blends distinct traditions (Vedantic *atman*-*Brahman* identity, Buddhist *anatta*/no-self, Taoist *wu wei*) that specialists treat as philosophically non-equivalent.

**From mainstream psychology and neuroscience**: the claim that the separate-self sensation is a "hallucination" in a strong sense is a philosophical/contemplative claim, not an empirically settled one; cognitive science broadly agrees the sense of a unified, continuous self is constructed rather than a simple given (consistent with Watts's account of narrowed attention and memory), but does not thereby establish the further metaphysical claim that the self is identical with the undifferentiated cosmic totality — a claim Watts himself, following Vedanta, holds as a matter of contemplative realization rather than laboratory demonstration.
