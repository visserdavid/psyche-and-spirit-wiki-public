# The Golden Bough

**Summary**: Sir James George Frazer's monumental comparative study of magic, myth, and religion — begun from the puzzle of the priest-king of Diana's grove at Nemi and expanded into a worldwide survey of sympathetic magic, dying-and-rising vegetation gods, sacred kingship, and the scapegoat ritual — one of the most influential and most contested books of modern anthropology.

**Sources**:
- [Frazer, J. G. (1994). *The golden bough: A study in magic and religion* (R. Fraser, Ed.). Oxford University Press. (Original work published 1890; new abridgement of the 2nd and 3rd eds., 1900/1906–1915)](the-golden-bough.md)

## Overview

*The Golden Bough* began in 1889 when Frazer, reading Virgil's *Aeneid* and Pausanias's *Description of Greece*, became fascinated by a strange rite recorded at the shrine of Diana at Nemi, near Rome: the priesthood there — the "King of the Wood" — was held by a runaway slave who could only obtain the office by killing his predecessor in single combat, having first broken a branch (the "golden bough," identified by Frazer with mistletoe) from a sacred tree, and who himself lived under permanent threat of being killed by the next challenger. Frazer set out to explain this single bizarre custom and ended up building, across three editions (1890, two volumes; 1900, expanded; 1906–1915, twelve volumes), a comparative theory of the evolution of human thought from magic through religion to science.

The vault file is the Oxford World's Classics edition (first published 1994, reissued 1998; ISBN 978-0-19-283541-3) — Robert Fraser's one-volume "new abridgement of the second and third editions," which restores material Frazer himself abridged out of his own popular 1922 one-volume abridgment (notably most of the book's original extended discussion of the Crucifixion as an instance of the scapegoat-king pattern, which Frazer progressively softened, then relegated to an appendix, then cut outright, after criticism from Andrew Lang). The wiki cites the work under its original 1890 first-edition year per its established convention for classic texts read in a later critical edition — see [Mysterium Coniunctionis](mysterium-coniunctionis.md) and [The Rites of Passage](the-rites-of-passage.md) for the same treatment.

## Key Takeaways

- **[Sympathetic Magic and the Evolution of Belief](../concepts/sympathetic-magic-and-taboo.md)**: Frazer's foundational distinction between homeopathic ("like produces like") and contagious (things once in contact retain a connection) magic, and his three-stage scheme of cultural development — Age of Magic, Age of Religion, Age of Science — in which magic and science share a common assumption of a fixed order of causes and effects, while religion alone appeals to the goodwill of personal supernatural agents.
- **[Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md)**: Frazer's account of Adonis, Attis, Osiris, Dionysus, and the Corn-Mother/Corn-Spirit as a single vegetation-death-and-return pattern underlying sacred kingship, agricultural ritual, and — in Frazer's most controversial and most heavily revised argument — the Crucifixion itself.
- **[The Scapegoat Ritual](../concepts/scapegoat-ritual.md)**: the periodic ritual transference of a community's accumulated evil, sin, or ill fortune onto a person, animal, or object that is then expelled or killed — from ancient Near Eastern and Mexican rites to the Roman Saturnalia and (in Frazer's reading) the mock-king humiliation preceding the Crucifixion.
- **Sacred kingship and ritual regicide**: kings as living vessels of a fertility-force whose vitality is bound to the health of the land, who therefore cannot be permitted to age or weaken in office — deposed, sacrificed, or replaced by a temporary substitute ("mock king") at the first sign of decline. The Nemi priesthood is Frazer's central (though empirically thin) exhibit.
- **Taboo**: Frazer treats taboo as an early, undifferentiated form of the sacred/profane distinction — the holy and the abhorrent are not yet separated categories but both are simply set apart from ordinary contact.
- **The Balder myth and fire festivals**: the closing books use the Norse myth of Balder — killed by a sprig of mistletoe, the one thing that had not sworn not to harm him, and burned on a ship-pyre — to connect the dying-god pattern to European seasonal fire festivals, and finally to identify the Golden Bough of the opening puzzle with the mistletoe itself: a plant that, growing without roots in the earth, was thought to house the "external soul" of the oak on which it grew.

## Structure

The Oxford abridgement follows Frazer's own four-part architecture: **Book I, "The King of the Wood"** (Nemi, priestly kings, the magic/religion distinction, taboo); **Book II, "Killing the God"** (the mortality of gods, temporary and substitute kings, Adonis, Attis, Osiris, Dionysus, Demeter and Persephone, the Corn-Mother, and the eating of the god); **Book III, "The Scapegoat"** (transference of evil, ancient scapegoats, the Mexican and Saturnalian material, and — in this restored edition — the Crucifixion of Christ); and **Book IV, "The Golden Bough"** (Balder, fire festivals, the external soul, and death and resurrection, closing with Frazer's return to and resolution of the Nemi puzzle).

## Reception and Critique

*The Golden Bough* was, on publication in 1890, an instant sensation and a scandal in equal measure: Robert Fraser's introduction notes it was read "under the bedsheets by torchlight" by Victorian readers who understood exactly what its comparative method implied — that the sacrificial and mythological structures Frazer found in "primitive" religion the world over were structurally indistinguishable from those at the heart of Christianity itself. Frazer never states this conclusion outright ("I make no reference to the parallelism, leaving readers to draw their own conclusions," he told his publisher), but the book's entire architecture, in its most expanded (1900, 1906–1915) editions, builds toward it — a design so unsettling to Frazer himself, after Andrew Lang's public attack, that he progressively excised and finally cut the explicit Crucifixion material from his own later one-volume abridgment. The Oxford World's Classics edition used here restores it.

The book was immensely influential on twentieth-century literature and thought: T. S. Eliot cited it directly as a structural source for *The Waste Land*; it shaped Freud's *Totem and Taboo*; it is the direct methodological ancestor of [Joseph Campbell](../thinkers/joseph-campbell.md)'s comparative mythology (Campbell cites Frazer explicitly — see [The Hero with a Thousand Faces](the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md)) and of [Mircea Eliade](../thinkers/mircea-eliade.md) and [Arnold van Gennep](../thinkers/arnold-van-gennep.md)'s comparative ritual studies (see [The Rites of Passage](the-rites-of-passage.md), which cites Frazer extensively).

**Mainstream anthropological critique**: Frazer's method — an "armchair" synthesis of secondhand travelers', missionaries', and colonial administrators' reports about cultures Frazer never visited, arranged into a single universal evolutionary sequence (magic → religion → science) — is now considered fundamentally outdated by mainstream anthropology, which since Malinowski and Boas has insisted on direct fieldwork, cultural relativism, and skepticism toward grand unilinear evolutionary schemes. Frazer's comparative habit of lifting similar-looking customs out of their specific cultural contexts and treating them as interchangeable instances of one underlying pattern is precisely the method modern ethnography rejects. J. Z. Smith's influential essay "Dying and Rising Gods" (*Encyclopedia of Religion*, 1987) goes further, arguing that the entire "dying-and-rising god" category — Frazer's central organizing concept — is a modern scholarly construction imposed on traditions whose actual death-and-return narratives differ far more than Frazer's synthesis allows; see the Critique section of [Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md) for the fuller discussion, which already flagged this exact tension before this ingest.

Frazer's status is thus a clean case, within this wiki, of a text whose influence and whose empirical/methodological soundness diverge sharply: essential as a source for understanding an entire generation of Jungian, mythological, and ritual-studies thinking (Campbell, Eliade, van Gennep), while itself resting on a comparative method modern anthropology has largely abandoned.
