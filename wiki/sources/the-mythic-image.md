# The Mythic Image

**Summary**: Campbell's magnum opus — a 572-page illustrated journey through world mythology, arguing that myths are public dreams arising from the same unconscious source as private dreaming, with Part IV providing the most detailed comparative psychological reading of the seven-chakra system in the English language.

**Sources**:

## Structure and Argument

The book is organised in six parts, each moving from the outer world of image toward the inner world of consciousness:

**Part I — The World Dream**: The universe as Vishnu's dream; comparative dream-mythology across India, Egypt, Mesopotamia, and the Christian West; the Mighty Goddess as embodiment of [Maya's three powers](../concepts/maya-three-powers.md).

**Part II — The Idea of Cosmic Order**: The Bronze Age civilisational matrix that Campbell argues underlies all literate mythologies; the World Mountain (ziggurat, pyramid, pagoda) as universal temple; the calendric number 432,000 appearing identically in Hindu, Norse, and Babylonian sources; the precession of the equinoxes (25,920-year cycle; 432,000 = 25,920 × 16.67; one zodiac degree = 72 years).

**Part III — The Lotus and the Rose**: The lotus as the East's primary symbol of consciousness arising from dark waters; its Western counterpart the rose; Prajnaparamita ("Wisdom of the Yonder Shore") as Buddhist parallel to the Christian Madonna; the Shakti as divine feminine energy; Dante's Paradiso as a chakra ascent.

**Part IV — Transformations of Inner Light**: The book's climax and its most original contribution — a detailed comparative psychological analysis of the seven-chakra system, reading the Kundalini Yoga tradition alongside Western art, philosophy, and mysticism. See [Chakra Psychology (Campbell)](../concepts/chakra-psychology-campbell.md).

**Part V — Sacrifice**: The universal figure of the willing victim — the Bodhisattva who refuses liberation to serve others; the dying-and-rising god across cultures (Osiris, Attis, Adonis, Dionysus, Tammuz, Krishna, Christ); pig sacrifice mythology linking Egypt, Greece, Mesoamerica, and Melanesia; the principle that sacrifice transfers the power of the victim to the sacrificer.

**Part VI — Waking**: The myth of the Buddha's death by "boar flesh" as the final dying-god episode; the fire sermon; "Tathagata" (One Come / One Waked) as the paradox of the enlightened being who remains in the world; Schopenhauer's universe-as-dream; James Joyce's synthesis; the goal of all myths as a waking from the dream of multiplicity to the knowledge of unity.

## The Central Thesis: Myths as Dreams

Campbell opens with a quotation from Shakespeare ("We are such stuff as dreams are made on") and a Kalahari bushman's question: is a man dreaming he is a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming it is a man? His central argument, developed across all six parts, is that:

- **Myths are public dreams** — they arise from the same unconscious source as private dreaming
- **Dreams are private myths** — they structure experience through the same archetypal patterns that myths use collectively
- **The universe itself is a dream** — Vishnu's dreaming on the cosmic ocean is India's way of saying that all phenomenal existence is a projection of consciousness

This thesis directly supports [C. G. Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md)'s hypothesis of the collective unconscious and is the philosophical ground of Myss's [Symbolic Sight](../concepts/symbolic-sight.md): if myth and dream share a source, then learning to read mythological symbols is learning to read the signals the unconscious sends through the body.

## The Comparative Mythology Method

Campbell's method is not Freudian (symbol = repressed impulse) or Lévi-Straussian (myth = structural logic) but **amplificatory** in Jung's sense: each symbol is expanded by setting it in its widest comparative context, allowing its surplus meaning to appear. A lotus found in Hindu iconography is amplified by its appearance in Egyptian, Orphic, and Christian contexts — the common element across all settings is what carries the universal import.

Campbell's key evidence for a shared Bronze Age matrix:
- The number **432,000 years** (the Hindu Mahayuga) appears in the Poetic Edda (540 doors × 800 warriors = 432,000) and in the Babylonian priest Berossos's record of 432,000 years from the first king to the Flood — three independent traditions, same figure
- The **caduceus** (two entwined serpents on a central staff) appears on Sumerian vessels (2000 BC), Indus Valley seals, Mesoamerican monuments (feathered serpent/Quetzalcoatl), and in Greek Hermes/Asklepios imagery — identical symbol, widely separated cultures
- **World mountain** architecture (ziggurat, step pyramid, pagoda, Aztec temple) reproduces the seven-sphere cosmos in stone across every literate civilisation

## Maya's Three Powers

A central concept of the book is the Vedantic teaching on **maya** — the goddess-power that creates, sustains, and dissolves the illusion of multiplicity. Maya has three powers:

1. **Veiling Power** (*avarana*): hides the Self; the root of ignorance
2. **Projecting Power** (*vikshepa*): generates the appearance of a world of separate objects and persons
3. **Revealing Power** (*prakasha*): found in art, scripture, and meditation — the power that dissolves the veil and allows the Self to shine

The Goddess (Lakshmi, Tara, the Madonna, the rose window of Chartres) is maya's Revealing Power in embodied form. This frames the entire book: its images are vehicles of Revealing Power.

See [Maya: Three Powers](../concepts/maya-three-powers.md).

## The Chakra System as Psychological Map

Part IV is the longest and most technically demanding section. Campbell uses the seven-chakra framework of Kundalini Yoga — transmitted through Sri Ramakrishna's first-person accounts and Sir John Woodroffe's translations — as a master map of psychological development, then demonstrates that Western art, poetry, and mysticism can all be read through this lens.

The three lower chakras (Muladhara, Svadhishthana, Manipura) describe consciousness bound to the world — materialism, sexuality, and power. The heart chakra (Anahata) is the transition: Dante's "La Vita Nuova" begins here, the Wish Fulfilling Tree is reached, and the sound of *om* heard. The three upper chakras (Vishuddha, Ajna, Sahasrara) describe the progressive dissolution of ego consciousness into ground awareness.

See [Chakra Psychology (Campbell)](../concepts/chakra-psychology-campbell.md).

## The Dying-and-Rising God

Part V surveys the most widely distributed mythological motif after the hero's journey: the god who dies and rises. Campbell traces this archetype through:

- **Egypt**: Osiris dismembered by Seth, reassembled by Isis, resurrected as Lord of the Netherworld; Horus avenges and restores
- **Mesopotamia**: Tammuz/Dumuzi, "Son of the Abyss," killed and resurrected annually
- **Greece**: Adonis gored by a boar (his own divine aspect); Dionysus/Zagreus dismembered by Titans
- **Phrygia**: Attis, beloved of Cybele, self-castrated and dying under a pine tree; resurrected
- **India**: Krishna, avatar of Vishnu the world-dreamer, born amid miracles and killed by an arrow; the cosmic boar who rescues Earth-goddess from the ocean of dissolution
- **Christianity**: Christ as the fullest Western expression — voluntary sacrifice, descent, resurrection

The key insight: in each case the god who is sacrificed is an aspect of the deity who receives the sacrifice. Seth is Horus; Adonis is Aphrodite's own; the boar is Finn's aspect; the pig is Seth. The dismemberment is the creative act — the one becomes many; the re-membering (resurrection) is the return to unity. This parallels Myss's principle that illness is a form of dismemberment — a crisis of fragmentation — that can become the occasion for re-integration.

See [Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md).

## The Caduceus as Universal Symbol

One of Campbell's most striking arguments is the universal appearance of the caduceus — two serpents entwined around a central staff — as a symbol of the life-force mediating between polarities:

- **Sumer, 2000 BC**: Libation cup of King Gudea of Lagash — serpent god Ningishzida shown as a pair of copulating vipers rising from a staff
- **Indus Valley, 2000 BC**: Seals showing entwined serpents parallel to the caduceus
- **Greece**: Hermes/Mercury's caduceus (guide of souls); Asklepios's single serpent staff (healing)
- **Mesoamerica**: The feathered serpent Quetzalcoatl; Mayan and Aztec serpent-column temples
- **Moundville, Alabama**: Engraved stone disk showing hand with eye of mercy framed by paired rattlesnakes (1200–1600 AD)

Campbell identifies the caduceus with the Indian diagram of Kundalini yoga: the two serpents are **ida** (lunar, cooling) and **pingala** (solar, heating), spiralling around the central channel **sushumna** — the spine. The central staff is the axis of consciousness; the intertwined serpents are the polarities that Kundalini must balance and transcend on its ascent.

## Ramakrishna's First-Person Account

Campbell quotes extensively from Sri Ramakrishna (1836–1886), the Bengali saint whose reported experiences of Kundalini arising provide the most vivid first-person account of the chakra system in operation:

> "The Spiritual Current rises along the spine, crawling like an ant... The soul swims in the ocean of ecstasy like a fish... There is a current like a monkey, jumping from one branch to another, one place to another... It hops like a bird from branch to branch... [It moves] like a snake, going zigzag, and as it reaches the head, I go into samadhi."

Ramakrishna's five movements of Kundalini (ant/fish/monkey/bird/serpent) correspond to the five elements of the lower chakras traversed in ascent. This experiential account is offered alongside Campbell's comparative-mythological analysis as converging evidence that the chakra system maps a real psychological process — even if its ultimate nature remains open.

## Connections Across the Wiki

- **Myss and the chakra system**: This book is Myss's primary scholarly reference for the chakra model she uses in *Anatomy of the Spirit* — Campbell's psychological reading supports her interpretation of each chakra as a stage of biographical development
- **Jung**: Campbell quotes Jung extensively; the book treats Jungian psychology and Kundalini yoga as converging maps of the same inner territory
- **Johari**: Johari's *Chakras* (2000) provides the traditional Hindu attribute system; Campbell provides the comparative mythology and Western art readings — the two books are complementary
- **Brennan**: Campbell's chakra-as-psychological-development-level provides a framework for understanding why each auric layer (Brennan) encodes the concerns of its corresponding chakra
- **Hillman**: The acorn theory of soul's calling parallels Campbell's "world dream" — the individual's myth is their portion of the cosmic dream
