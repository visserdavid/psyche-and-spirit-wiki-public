# The Phenomenon of Man

**Summary**: Pierre Teilhard de Chardin's posthumously published synthesis of evolutionary science and Christian theology, arguing that cosmic evolution has a built-in directionality — increasing "complexity-consciousness" — moving matter through the biosphere and into a planetary "noosphere" of collective thought, ultimately converging on a final, personal, unifying state he calls the **Omega Point**.

**Sources**:
- [Teilhard de Chardin, P. (1959). *The phenomenon of man* (B. Wall, Trans.; J. Huxley, Intro.). Harper & Brothers. (Original work published 1955 as *Le phénomène humain*)](the-phenomenon-of-man.md)

## Overview

Pierre Teilhard de Chardin (1881–1955) was a French Jesuit priest, geologist, and paleontologist — a member of the team that unearthed the Peking Man fossils in China. He wrote *Le Phénomène Humain* between 1938 and 1940, but the Catholic Church's Holy Office, wary of his unorthodox treatment of evolution and original sin, refused him permission to publish it (or his other major philosophical works) throughout his lifetime; he left the manuscript with a friend to be published only after his death, and it appeared in French in 1955, the year he died, from Éditions du Seuil. Bernard Wall's English translation, with an introduction by the evolutionary biologist Sir Julian Huxley, appeared in 1959 from Harper & Brothers (Collins in the UK). The vault edition is the Harper Perennial Modern Thought reissue.

Teilhard frames the book explicitly as science, not metaphysics or theology: "not a work of metaphysics, still less... a sort of theological essay, but purely and simply a scientific treatise." His method is to place man at the centre of a coherent, evolutionary narrative and trace the "experimental law of recurrence" running through it — while acknowledging in the same breath that a study of the *whole* phenomenon of man cannot help but open onto philosophy and religion, since "the meridians of... science, philosophy and religion... are bound to converge."

## Structure and Key Argument

The book is organized as four "books" tracing a single ascending curve: **Life Came** (the stuff of the universe, and its hitherto-neglected "Within"), **Life** (the cellular revolution and its expansion), **Thought** (the emergence of reflective consciousness in man and the birth of the noosphere), and **Survival** (the convergence of the noosphere toward the Omega Point) — followed by an Epilogue ("The Christian Phenomenon"), a Postscript, and an Appendix explicitly addressing the place of evil in the scheme.

### The Within and Without of Things

Teilhard's starting move is to insist that every observable "without" (external, physical, measurable) has a corresponding, if often imperceptible, "within" (interior, psychic, proto-conscious) — a claim close to **panpsychism**. Matter and consciousness are not two separate substances requiring a bridge, but two faces — **tangential energy** (linking an element to others of the same complexity) and **radial energy** (drawing it toward greater complexity and centredness, i.e. forward) — of a single underlying psychic stuff. This grounds his central empirical claim: **the Law of Complexity-Consciousness** — that as matter becomes more intricately organized (atom → molecule → cell → organism → society), its interior "consciousness" correspondingly deepens. See [Noosphere and the Law of Complexity-Consciousness](../concepts/noosphere-and-complexity-consciousness.md) for the full treatment.

### Life, the Cellular Revolution, and Orthogenesis

Book Two traces life's "expansion" through reproduction, multiplication, conjugation, association, and what Teilhard calls **controlled additivity** — a directional accumulation of complexity he terms (following existing biological usage, though controversially) **orthogenesis**. He is careful to distinguish this from a claim that evolution is goal-directed in a crude teleological sense at the level of individual mutations; the direction he asserts operates at the level of the whole ascending curve of life, not of any single lineage's fitness.

### Hominisation and the Noosphere

Book Three describes the "threshold of reflection" — the moment a creature (man) not only knows, but *knows that it knows* (a distinction Teilhard renders as "non seulement connaître, mais se connaître"). This threshold is a qualitative, not merely quantitative, leap — the birth of thought as such — after which evolution's main line of advance shifts from biological/genetic change to cultural/psychosocial change. Humanity's increasing planetary interconnection (through population growth, migration, and especially communication and shared thought) is building a global mental layer Teilhard names the **noosphere** — his own 1925 coinage, layered over the [biosphere](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md) as the biosphere was layered over the lithosphere.

### Convergence, Love-Energy, and the Omega Point

Book Four is the book's most theologically explicit section — see [Omega Hypothesis § Teilhard de Chardin and the Omega Point](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md) for the concept's use elsewhere in this wiki. Teilhard argues the noosphere's convergence culminates not in an impersonal dissolution of individuals into a collective mass, but in **hyper-personalisation** — a Center of centers he calls the **Omega Point**, which paradoxically personalizes and universalizes at once ("union differentiates"). He gives Omega four attributes: **autonomy** (it is not merely the sum of evolution's parts but an independent, "already existing" pole drawing evolution toward itself), **actuality** (it must be really present now, not merely a future ideal, because love — his proposed mechanism of convergence — "dies at the touch of the impersonal and anonymous"), **irreversibility** (once reached, a state beyond the reach of entropy — Teilhard's account of what personal immortality would mean cosmologically), and **transcendence** (Omega must in the end escape the confines of space-time that it gathers together, rather than merely being their terminus). He proposes **love** itself as a form of cosmic energy — "a direct trace marked in the heart of the element" — the psychic-attraction analogue of the "curvature" that draws matter together as gravity.

### The Epilogue: The Christian Phenomenon

Teilhard identifies the Omega Point theologically with the Cosmic Christ, arguing that the convergent, personalizing structure he derives from evolutionary science independently confirms — rather than merely accommodates — the Christian claim of a "Great Presence" already operative within the noosphere.

### The Appendix: The Place of Evil

Anticipating the objection that his account reads as naively optimistic, Teilhard closes with a short, explicit treatment of evil, distinguishing four forms inherent to the very structure of an evolving, groping, converging universe rather than external to it: **evil of disorder or failure** (the statistical by-product of "groping" — many failures for every success, "many days of misery for one hour of joy"); **evil of decomposition** (death itself, the "regular, indispensable condition" of one generation's replacement by the next); **evil of solitude and anxiety** (the mind's dread on first waking to a universe it does not yet understand); and **evil of growth** ("the pangs of childbirth" — the cost, in effort and suffering, exacted by every synthesis toward greater unity). He states plainly: "the universe... labours, sins, suffers" precisely because it is in the business of arrangement and centration — but leaves open, without resolving, "the possibility of... a primordial deviation" beyond what ordinary evolutionary "groping" would explain, deferring to theology for that further question. See [Human Evil](../concepts/human-evil.md#a-sixth-account-teilhards-cosmic-evolutionary-evil) for how this compares with the wiki's other accounts of evil.

## Reception and Critique

Julian Huxley's introduction (itself an important document, written after a near-decade of friendship and correspondence with Teilhard) praises the book's scope while flagging, even in 1958, that "the biologist may consider that *Phenomenon of Man* pays insufficient attention to genetics and the possibilities and limitations of natural selection," and that Teilhard's treatment of sin and suffering is, from a secular scientific standpoint, "unorthodox" territory Huxley himself did not follow him into (Teilhard's identification of Omega with a personal Deity being, for Huxley, an unnecessary theological overlay on an otherwise defensible naturalistic thesis of psychosocial evolution).

**Mainstream evolutionary biology's critique** is sharper: the modern evolutionary synthesis holds that evolution has no built-in direction or goal — natural selection acts locally and opportunistically on random variation, with no mechanism by which the process as a whole could be "aimed at" a future state. Teilhard's central empirical claim — that complexity and consciousness increase together as a law of the whole evolutionary process — is, on this view, a metaphysical/theological commitment dressed in scientific vocabulary rather than a testable hypothesis; the biologist and prominent atheist Peter Medawar's scathing 1961 review in *Mind* ("The Phenomenon of Man") remains the best-known statement of this line of attack, accusing Teilhard of building an argument that reads as profound chiefly because it is evasively written. Note: this wiki treats Teilhard's law of complexity-consciousness as a theological-philosophical synthesis inspired by, but not equivalent to, accepted evolutionary science — distinct from his genuinely primary-source paleontological fieldwork, which is uncontroversial.

**The Vatican's theological critique** was, ironically, the opposite complaint: the Holy Office's 1962 *monitum* (formal warning) cautioned against the book's ambiguity on original sin, the historicity of Adam, and other points of Catholic doctrine — the same tension that had kept it unpublished during Teilhard's lifetime.

Alan Watts lists this book in his own bibliography for [*The Book: On the Taboo Against Knowing Who You Are*](the-book-on-the-taboo.md) (1966) as a work expanding the same themes; Watts's own biosphere/"world body" argument (organism and environment as inseparable poles) explicitly echoes Teilhard's biosphere concept.
