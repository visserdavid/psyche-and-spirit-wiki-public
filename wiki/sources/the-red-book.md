# The Red Book: Liber Novus

**Summary**: The unmediated primary document behind Jung's 1913–1930 "confrontation with the unconscious" — his private, calligraphed, self-illustrated record of deliberately induced waking fantasies, published only in 2009, 48 years after his death, with an extensive scholarly introduction by Sonu Shamdasani reconstructing its composition and its direct line into Jung's entire later theoretical system.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (2009). *The red book: Liber novus* (S. Shamdasani, Ed.; M. Kyburz, J. Peck, & S. Shamdasani, Trans.). W. W. Norton. (Composed 1913–1930)](the-red-book.md)

## Overview

The vault file is the 2009 "Reader's Edition" — a text-only edition (without the facsimile's page images and calligraphy) prepared alongside the full deluxe facsimile, both published by W. W. Norton in October 2009. Jung began the underlying self-experiment in December 1913, shortly after his break with [Sigmund Freud](../thinkers/sigmund-freud.md), recording deliberately induced waking fantasies first in a series of black notebooks (the *Black Books*), then transcribing, elaborating, and illuminating them into a large red leather-bound folio he called *Liber Novus* ("New Book"). He worked on it intermittently until around 1930, when his growing engagement with alchemy gave him, in his own words in the book's unfinished 1959 epilogue, a way to "arrange the whole" that made further work on the book itself unnecessary. He never published it, and left only the instruction that it "remain in the family" — the Society of the Heirs of C. G. Jung held it privately for 48 years before releasing it for publication under historian **Sonu Shamdasani**'s editorship.

For the full account of the book's content — Philemon, Salome and Elijah, the descent, the *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos*, the mandalas, and their direct theoretical afterlife in individuation, the persona/anima distinction, and the Self — see [Active Imagination and the Red Book](../concepts/active-imagination-and-the-red-book.md), which this ingest substantially deepened beyond what *[Memories, Dreams, Reflections](memories-dreams-reflections.md)* describes secondhand.

## Structure

- **Liber Primus** ("The Way of What Is to Come"): the opening book, framed with epigraphs from Isaiah and the Gospel of John as a self-consciously prophetic work — Jung's "refinding of the soul" after the break with Freud.
- **Liber Secundus** ("Images of the Erring"): the main sequence of descent-fantasies, encounters with Philemon, Salome, Elijah, and others, and the *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos*.
- **Scrutinies** (also called *Liber Tertius*): a later (1917-onward) manuscript revisiting and reinterpreting the earlier material, in which Jung comes to recognize that much of what he had earlier attributed to himself had in fact been "given" by Philemon — a moment of critical self-differentiation from his own inner authority figure.

Word-for-word comparison (Shamdasani's introduction reports) shows the *Liber Novus* draft faithfully reproduces the *Black Books* fantasies with only minor editing; the second layer — Jung's own interpretive commentary threaded between the fantasies — is where his later general psychological theory first takes shape, applying what he called the "constructive method" (reading fantasy material for its prospective, meaning-making function) rather than the causal-reductive method he associated with Freud.

## Why This Book Matters to the Rest of Jung's Work

Jung stated repeatedly, including in *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, that every later theoretical concept — archetypes, the collective unconscious, individuation, active imagination itself, the anima/animus, the Self, the mandala as a symbol of psychic wholeness — was derived from this material. Shamdasani's introduction traces this directly: Jung's 1916 lecture "The Structure of the Unconscious" (the earliest published theoretical residue of this period) introduces the personal/collective unconscious distinction and the danger of "godlikeness" from over-identifying with collective material, worked out in real time as Jung tried to differentiate his own experience from psychosis. The 1916 *Systema Munditotius* mandala anticipates, in image, the argument about good and evil as a *complexio oppositorum* that Jung would only make discursively decades later in *[Aion](aion.md)*.

## Balance and Critique

See [Active Imagination and the Red Book](../concepts/active-imagination-and-the-red-book.md) for the fuller discussion. In brief: whether this period represents disciplined self-experimentation or a self-managed psychotic episode later rationalized as method is a genuine, unresolved scholarly controversy (Richard Noll's critical books on one side; Shamdasani's own framing of it as a *shared*, not solitary, clinical technique on the other). Jung's specific claim that certain 1913–1914 fantasies were precognitive of the First World War is also worth treating skeptically on its own terms — pre-war Europe's arts and culture were already saturated with apocalyptic imagery, which weakens the evidentiary force Jung himself placed on the "precognition."
