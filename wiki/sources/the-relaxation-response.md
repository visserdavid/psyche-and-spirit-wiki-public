# The Relaxation Response

**Summary**: Benson & Klipper (1975) — the founding scientific document of clinical mind-body medicine: the physiological discovery that every human possesses an innate counter-mechanism to the fight-or-flight response, elicitable through meditation, prayer, or focused attention, with measurable benefits for hypertension and stress-related disease.

**Sources**:
- [Benson, H., & Klipper, M. Z. (1975). *The relaxation response*. William Morrow.](the-relaxation-response.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

---

## Overview

*The Relaxation Response* is Herbert Benson's primary scientific statement — the book that established the relaxation response as a measurable physiological state and proposed it as a clinical intervention for hypertension and stress-related disease. It was preceded by Benson's 1974 article in the *Harvard Business Review* and written in collaboration with Miriam Z. Klipper for a general audience.

The book unfolds in four movements:
1. The problem: hypertension as a modern epidemic linked to the fight-or-flight response
2. The discovery: TM research demonstrating a measurable counter-state
3. The historical survey: cross-cultural evidence that all contemplative traditions independently discovered the same mechanism
4. The technique: a secular four-element method for clinical use

## The Problem: Hypertension as Modern Epidemic

Benson opens with the epidemiological case. At the time of writing, hypertension afflicted between 23 and 44 million Americans (15–33 percent of the adult population) and contributed to two million deaths per year — through atherosclerosis, heart attack, and stroke. Ninety to ninety-five percent of cases were "essential hypertension" — no identifiable anatomical cause.

Benson's hypothesis, developed with [Hans Selye](../thinkers/hans-selye.md)'s stress framework and the Holmes-Rahe Social Readjustment Rating Scale as theoretical ground, was that repeated elicitation of the [fight-or-flight response](../concepts/fight-or-flight-response.md) in situations that do not permit physical action was the primary driver. Urban adjustment, racial disadvantage, occupational mobility, and life-event accumulation all raised blood pressure — not through identifiable tissue pathology, but through repeated stress-activation.

Supporting studies cited:
- Texas City explosion (1947): elevated blood pressures in surrounding patients in the days following the blast
- Puerto Rico → city migration: blood pressure correlated with degree of urbanisation/Westernisation
- Fijian Islanders: BP tracked degree of Westernisation
- African Zulu tribe: BP rose with urban migration
- University of Michigan (E. Harburg): blacks in high-stress Detroit ghetto neighbourhoods had higher BP than class-matched blacks in less-stressed areas
- J. Brod (Czech): mental arithmetic (subtract 17 from 1,194 to a metronome) produced the full integrated fight-or-flight response — increased BP, heart rate, and muscle blood flow — in laboratory subjects

## The TM Research

In 1968, practitioners of Transcendental Meditation approached Benson's laboratory at Harvard's Thorndike Memorial Laboratory (Beth Israel Hospital, Boston) claiming meditation lowered blood pressure. Benson initially declined to investigate, but the practitioners persisted. After obtaining Human Studies Committee approval, Benson and R. Keith Wallace began a systematic programme.

Wallace had completed his doctoral thesis at UCLA on TM physiology; he then joined the Harvard team. Together they recruited and studied TM practitioners — ages seventeen to forty-one, practising from one month to nine years — measuring three periods: thirty minutes sitting, twenty to thirty minutes meditating, and twenty to thirty minutes post-meditation.

**Findings**: meditation produced a measurable state distinct from sleep and ordinary waking:
- Oxygen consumption decreased 10–20 percent within three minutes (vs. an 8 percent drop over four to five hours of sleep)
- Heart rate decreased approximately three beats per minute
- Respiration rate decreased
- Skin resistance increased
- Brain waves shifted to increased alpha rhythm
- Blood lactate fell dramatically (lactate is produced by muscular metabolism and had been linked to anxiety states by Pitts and McLure, 1967)

The combination — decreased metabolism, decreased sympathetic activation, alpha waves — constituted a distinct fourth state of consciousness: not waking, not sleep, not dreaming. Benson called it the **relaxation response**.

## Physiological Mechanism

The complementary nature of the relaxation response and the fight-or-flight response was established by [Walter R. Hess](../thinkers/hans-selye.md), Nobel Prize-winning physiologist, who demonstrated in cat brain stimulation experiments that the hypothalamus controls two opposed states: a sympathetic (ergotropic, activating) state and a parasympathetic (trophotropic, restorative) state. Activating one suppresses the other.

Benson proposed that the repeated elicitation of the trophotropic/relaxation response counteracts the effects of chronic fight-or-flight activation — that this is the mechanism by which meditation, prayer, and contemplative practice produce their health effects.

Supporting animal evidence: Folkow and Rubinstein (University of Göteborg) implanted electrodes in rat hypothalami and electrically stimulated the fight-or-flight area. Rats receiving repeated stimulation developed persistent hypertension; unstimulated controls maintained normal blood pressure.

## The Cross-Cultural Survey: Four Elements Everywhere

Benson's most ambitious chapter surveys the world's contemplative traditions to show that every major tradition independently discovered a method for eliciting the relaxation response. All methods share four structural elements:

1. **Quiet environment** — withdrawal from sensory stimulation; a room, a place of worship, nature
2. **Mental device** — a word, phrase, or sound repeated, or a fixed object of gaze; the device occupies attention and prevents distracting thoughts
3. **Passive attitude** — when distracting thoughts arise, they are disregarded rather than resisted; a relaxed, receptive stance rather than concentration or effort
4. **Comfortable position** — a posture maintained for twenty minutes without tension; sitting preferred over lying (to prevent sleep)

Traditions documented:
- **Christian**: St. Augustine's "recollection" (Confessions); the 14th-century *Cloud of Unknowing* (use of a single syllable — "God" or "Love"); Martin Luther's *Man Pray* (Lord's Prayer repeated with concentrated heart); Fray Francisco de Osuna's *Third Spiritual Alphabet* (gaze fixed to prevent wandering, repeating "No"); St. Teresa of Ávila's *Way of Perfection*; Hesychasm / Prayer of the Heart (Mt. Athos — "Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me," timed to breathing rhythm)
- **Jewish**: Merkabolism (2nd century AD); Rabbi Abulafia's system of meditating on Hebrew letters constituting God's name — Gershom Scholem called this "a Jewish equivalent of Yoga"
- **Islamic/Sufi**: Dhikr — the worshipper sits in a corner, repeating "Allah, Allah" until the word flows from tongue to heart; described by Al Ghazali
- **Taoist**: Chuang Tzu's "sitting forgetting" (*zuowang*) — "I cast aside my limbs, discard my intelligence, detach from body and mind, and become one with the Great Universal Tao"
- **Buddhist**: Siddhartha Gautama's *anapanasati* (breath counting to ten, repeated); Ashvagosha's *Awakening Faith* — five stages of practice, the first being "preventing thoughts" through posture, environment, and returning the mind when it wanders
- **Hindu/Yoga**: Patanjali's system of *ekagrata* (one-pointedness of mind) — concentration on a single object to dam the stream of mind; Maharishi Mahesh Yogi's Transcendental Meditation as a simplified, Westernised Yoga technique
- **Poetry**: Emily Brontë, Wordsworth (*Tintern Abbey*), and Tennyson all described states consistent with the relaxation response as the ground of their visionary experience

Benson's thesis: the state that mystics across cultures have named — union with God, satori, nirvana, the Tao — has a measurable physiological correlate. He does not reduce the spiritual to the physiological; he argues instead that every tradition has independently found the same body-based door.

## Clinical Applications

### Hypertension Study

Benson's team recruited eighty-six hypertensive subjects. After a six-week baseline measurement period, thirty-six subjects who had not altered medications were taught to elicit the relaxation response through Transcendental Meditation. Blood pressure was measured while not meditating.

Results (systolic/diastolic, mmHg):
- Baseline: 146 / 93.5
- After weeks of twice-daily practice: 137 / 88.9
- A drop from borderline hypertensive to near-normal range, without medication change

Of three to ten subjects who chose to stop meditating, blood pressure returned to pre-study levels within four weeks — confirming that the effect was maintenance-dependent, not curative.

Benson was clear: the relaxation response does not *cure* hypertension, but regular practice maintains reduced blood pressure and may reduce medication dosage as an adjunct to medical care.

### Drug Abuse Study

R. K. Wallace surveyed 1,862 TM practitioners (teachers in training, colleges recruited by Shelley at University of Kansas) on drug use before and after beginning meditation:
- Marijuana/hashish: 78% were users pre-meditation → 37% at six months → 12% at twenty-two months (66% decrease)
- LSD: 50% were users pre-meditation → 97% of users had stopped by twenty-two months
- Hallucinogens, amphetamines, narcotics, alcohol, and cigarettes: similar dramatic decreases
- Cigarettes: 48% smoked pre-meditation → 16% at twenty-one months

Methodological limitations acknowledged: self-report, motivated sample (meditation teachers), no randomisation. A controlled follow-up study in Massachusetts and Michigan high schools (Shelley, University of Kansas) — with matched schools receiving TM and not — supported the findings.

## The Secular Technique

Benson distilled the four elements into a non-denominational method for clinical use:

1. Sit in a comfortable position
2. Close your eyes
3. Relax your muscles, starting from your feet and progressing to your face
4. Breathe through your nose; become aware of your breathing
5. As you breathe out, say the word **ONE** silently
6. Continue for ten to twenty minutes
7. Do not use an alarm; when finished, sit quietly for a few minutes before standing
8. Practice once or twice daily, not within two hours of a meal

Any word or short phrase can substitute for "ONE" — including a prayer word or phrase from one's own tradition. The content is secondary to the act of focusing and passively returning when distracted.

## Significance for the Wiki

*The Relaxation Response* is the foundational scientific text behind:
- **Joan Borysenko's Mind/Body Clinic**: explicitly built on Benson's work; *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* (1987) is the clinical elaboration
- **[Centering Prayer](../concepts/centering-prayer.md)**: Thomas Keating's practice is situated in Benson's four-element framework; Borysenko explicitly compares the two
- **[Psychoneuroimmunology](../concepts/psychoneuroimmunology.md)**: the relaxation response's suppression of cortisol and adrenalin reverses the immunosuppression of chronic stress — the bridge from Benson to the PNI field
- **[Biography Is Biology](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md)**: Benson's hypertension data provided the first controlled clinical evidence that psychological stress produces measurable organic disease outcomes — and that psychological intervention reverses them
- **The entire wiki's meditation/prayer literature**: every tradition Benson surveys appears elsewhere in the wiki — the Cloud of Unknowing (centering prayer), Buddhist breathing meditation (mindfulness), Yoga (chakra tradition), Taoist sitting forgetting — and his four-element framework unifies them

## Critique

**Clinical scope**: Benson's 1975 evidence for the relaxation response's effects was strongest for hypertension and anxiety. Subsequent research has confirmed these and extended to pain, immune function, and depression. Claims for cancer or other serious disease remain more contested.

**Reductionism debate**: Spiritual practitioners have questioned whether Benson's mapping of mystical states onto the trophotropic response reduces the sacred to the somatic. Benson explicitly denies this — he is not saying God is a physiological state, but that the body has a reliable physiological response that practices across traditions have learned to elicit. The spiritual meaning of that state is a separate question.

**Technique specificity**: Benson's generic "one technique fits all" model has been challenged by subsequent research showing that different contemplative practices (TM, mindfulness, compassion training) produce different neurological and psychological effects beyond basic parasympathetic activation. The relaxation response may be a necessary but not sufficient description of meditative states.

**Drug abuse evidence**: The Wallace studies were methodologically weak (self-report, motivated sample, retroactive recall). The controlled Kansas/Michigan study was stronger but still small. Causal claims about meditation reducing addiction require more controlled evidence.
