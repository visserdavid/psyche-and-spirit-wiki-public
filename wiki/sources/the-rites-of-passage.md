# The Rites of Passage

**Summary**: Arnold van Gennep's founding 1909 text of ritual theory — a vast cross-cultural survey (territorial crossings, pregnancy, birth, initiation, betrothal, marriage, funerals, and the calendar) demonstrating that ceremonies marking any change of place, state, or social position share a universal three-phase structure: separation, transition (margin/limen), and incorporation.

**Sources**:
- [van Gennep, A. (1960). *The rites of passage* (M. B. Vizedom & G. L. Caffee, Trans.). University of Chicago Press. (Original work published 1909 as *Les rites de passage*)](the-rites-of-passage.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-17

---

## Overview

*The Rites of Passage* is the founding text of ritual and liminality theory in anthropology — the direct theoretical ancestor of [Victor Turner](../thinkers/victor-turner.md)'s *[The Ritual Process](the-ritual-process.md)* (1969), which elaborates its middle phase into [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md) and [Communitas](../concepts/communitas.md). Where Turner's book is a focused theoretical elaboration built from years of intensive Ndembu fieldwork, van Gennep's is a comprehensive comparative taxonomy: he surveys an enormous range of ethnographic material — his own French folklore fieldwork plus reports from dozens of cultures across Africa, Australia, the Pacific, the Americas, and classical antiquity — to argue that a single underlying pattern organizes ceremonies as different as crossing a national frontier, a girl's puberty rite, a wedding, and a funeral.

See [The Rites of Passage Schema](../concepts/rites-of-passage-schema.md) for the full account of the tripartite structure, van Gennep's theory of sacred/profane "pivoting," and his concluding "energetics" argument about the rhythm of transition.

## Structure of the Book

The book proceeds through the life cycle and its parallel social passages: territorial passage (Ch. II — crossing frontiers, thresholds, and doors), the relation of individuals and groups (Ch. III — reception of strangers), pregnancy and childbirth (Ch. IV), birth and childhood (Ch. V), initiation rites (Ch. VI — by far the longest chapter, covering circumcision, tribal initiation societies, the Eleusinian and Orphic mysteries, and Christian baptism as convergent instances of the same pattern), betrothal and marriage (Ch. VII), funerals (Ch. VIII), and other types of rites — seasonal and calendrical ceremonies, pilgrimage, enthronement (Ch. IX) — before a concluding theoretical chapter (Ch. X).

## Key Arguments

**The tripartite schema.** Van Gennep's central and most famous contribution: rites of passage comprise **rites of separation** (*préliminaire*), **rites of transition** (*liminaire* — he coined "liminal" from the Latin *limen*, threshold), and **rites of incorporation** (*postliminaire*). He is explicit that the three phases are not equally developed in every rite: funeral rites tend to emphasize the transitional phase most heavily (the deceased and the mourners linger in an ambiguous, unresolved state), while marriage rites tend to emphasize incorporation. See [The Rites of Passage Schema](../concepts/rites-of-passage-schema.md).

**Territorial grounding.** Van Gennep argues that social passage is frequently modeled on, and often literally enacted through, physical passage — crossing a boundary stone, a threshold, a bridge, a sacred zone. He surveys threshold worship, boundary deities, and portal architecture across dozens of cultures as evidence that the spatial and the social "threshold" are experienced as the same kind of event.

**Sacred/profane pivoting.** Van Gennep proposes that sacredness is not an intrinsic, fixed property of persons or things but a relative, situational quality: a person is sacred (or impure/taboo) *in relation to* a particular group or state, and profane in relation to another. Rites of passage are the mechanism by which this relative sacredness is transferred, activated, or dissolved as a person moves between states.

**Initiation as the widest category.** The initiation chapter treats an enormous range of material — Australian totemic initiation, Ndembu-region tribal ceremonies, the Eleusinian Mysteries, Orphic and Dionysian mysteries, the cults of Attis, Adonis, and Osiris, and Christian baptism — as structurally convergent instances of the same separation-transition-incorporation pattern, each dramatizing death and rebirth. Van Gennep draws this dying-god material directly from [James George Frazer](../thinkers/james-george-frazer.md)'s *[The Golden Bough](the-golden-bough.md)*, one of his most heavily cited sources throughout the book, though van Gennep's tripartite structural analysis (separation/transition/incorporation) is a distinct theoretical contribution layered on top of Frazer's comparative-mythological raw material — see [Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md). Van Gennep is careful to separate the sociological category of "initiation rite" from the biological fact of puberty, marshaling comparative demographic data (age of menarche across dozens of populations) to show that initiation ceremonies are only loosely, and often not at all, timed to physiological puberty — a corrective still cited in the literature against the older assumption that all "puberty rites" are about puberty as such.

**The concluding "energetics."** In his final chapter, van Gennep frames the whole schema in near-physical terms: life is organized as a "series of stages... with similar ends and beginnings," structured like the alternation of rest and motion in mechanics — energy is expended and must be periodically renewed. Rites of passage are society's device for cushioning the disturbance these transitions inevitably cause to both the individual and the group.

## Reception and Influence

Van Gennep's schema was, by his own translators' account, only sporadically taken up within anthropology for decades after its 1909 publication (the book was not translated into English until 1960) — noted by name mainly by a handful of scholars (Radcliffe-Brown, Chapple and Coon) before [Victor Turner](../thinkers/victor-turner.md) made the middle, liminal phase the centerpiece of his own theoretical program in *[The Ritual Process](the-ritual-process.md)* (1969), crediting van Gennep explicitly and repeatedly throughout that book.
