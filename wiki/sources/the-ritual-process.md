# The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure

**Summary**: Victor Turner's foundational anthropological text (based on his 1966 Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures), which builds on his Ndembu fieldwork in Zambia to establish liminality and communitas as general theoretical concepts describing how ritual, and social life more broadly, oscillates between structure (differentiated, hierarchical, role-bound organization) and anti-structure (undifferentiated, egalitarian communion).

**Sources**:
- [Turner, V. (1969). *The ritual process: Structure and anti-structure*. Aldine Publishing.](the-ritual-process.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-14

## Overview

*The Ritual Process* is Victor Turner's most influential book, source of the two concepts — **liminality** and **communitas** — that have since spread far beyond anthropology into performance studies, theology, literary criticism, and popular psychology (via, in this wiki, [Joan Borysenko](../thinkers/joan-borysenko.md)'s [Crisis as Initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md)). The book collects five originally separate essays, unified by a single argument: society is not a static structure but a dynamic process, alternating between two complementary modalities of human relatedness.

The book opens with dense ethnographic case studies from Turner's fieldwork among the Ndembu of Zambia (Chapters 1–2: the Isoma fertility ritual and Wubwang'u twin ceremony), then generalizes into the book's core theoretical chapters (3–5): [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md), the "betwixt and between" state at the heart of any rite of passage; and [Communitas](../concepts/communitas.md), the direct, egalitarian bond that liminality generates, set against its dialectical partner, structure.

## Ndembu Fieldwork: Isoma and Wubwang'u

Turner grounds his theory in years of fieldwork among the Ndembu, a matrilineal, hunting-and-cultivating people of what was then Northern Rhodesia. Two rituals anchor the book's opening chapters:

- **Isoma**: a women's fertility ritual performed for women suffering repeated miscarriage or infant death, believed caused by a shade (ancestor spirit) offended by the woman's neglect of her matrilineal obligations. The ritual's structure — passing through a symbolic tunnel between two "ikela" (holes), one representing death and witchcraft, the other life and health — stages the same death/rebirth structure Turner later generalizes as liminality.
- **Wubwang'u**: a ritual performed for women who have borne, or are expected to bear, twins — a condition Ndembu culture treats as a *paradox* (twinship both threatens the normal one-child-per-birth-slot kinship structure and is treated as a blessing requiring communal celebration). The ritual's "contest of sexes" episode, with its ribald antiphonal songs between men and women, stages the tension between the two principles that structure Ndembu society (matriliny and virilocal marriage) and resolves it through licensed, ritualized obscenity.

Turner reads these rituals via Ndembu exegesis (asking practitioners what the symbols mean) combined with structural analysis of the ritual's spatial and object symbolism (a method indebted to, but going beyond, Lévi-Strauss).

## Liminality and the Rite of Passage

Building on [Arnold van Gennep](../thinkers/arnold-van-gennep.md)'s three-phase model of rites of passage (separation, margin/limen, reaggregation — see *[The Rites of Passage](the-rites-of-passage.md)*, now ingested directly), Turner elaborates the middle, liminal phase in depth — the "threshold" condition of ritual subjects who are "betwixt and between" ordinary social categories, structurally invisible, stripped of status, and marked by symbols of death, gestation, or formlessness. See [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md) for the full account, including Turner's famous table of oppositions between liminality and normal status, his analysis of the Ndembu Kanongesha chieftainship installation rite, and his extension of the concept to status-reversal rituals like Halloween.

## Communitas and the Structure/Anti-Structure Dialectic

Turner's second and most widely borrowed concept: the intense, direct, egalitarian bond — **communitas** — that arises among people who pass through liminality together. He sets this against **structure**, the normal, differentiated, hierarchical organization of society, arguing the two are not opposed values (one good, one bad) but complementary phases of an inescapable social process. See [Communitas](../concepts/communitas.md) for the distinction between spontaneous, normative, and ideological communitas, Turner's historical case studies (the Franciscan poverty movement, the Bengali Sahajiya-Vaishnava devotional movement), and his extension of the concept to 1960s hippie counterculture.

## Reception and Influence

The book has been cited across history of religions, literary theory, theology, drama studies, and semiotics. Turner himself, in a 1977 preface added for the Cornell Paperbacks edition, acknowledges that the book "produced its share of controversy" and that critics have accused him of overgeneralizing the concepts of liminality and communitas beyond the small-scale societies from which he derived them — a critique addressed in the [Critique](../concepts/liminality.md#critique) sections of the concept pages below.
