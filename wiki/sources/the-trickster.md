# The Trickster: A Study in American Indian Mythology

**Summary**: Paul Radin's 1956 compilation and translation of the Winnebago (Ho-Chunk) Trickster cycle — the mythological adventures of Wakdjunkaga — paired with two independent interpretive essays: Karl Kerényi's comparative-mythological reading against Hermes, and C. G. Jung's psychological reading of the Trickster as an archaic forerunner of the shadow.

**Sources**:
- [Radin, P. (1956). *The trickster: A study in American Indian mythology* (K. Kerényi & C. G. Jung, Commentaries). Philosophical Library.](the-trickster.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

---

## Overview

*The Trickster* assembles three independent, unharmonized voices around a single body of material: the Winnebago Trickster cycle collected and translated by anthropologist [Paul Radin](../thinkers/paul-radin.md), plus supplementary Winnebago Hare-cycle material and summarized Assiniboine and Tlingit trickster myths. Radin adds his own extensive analytical essay ("The Nature and Meaning of Myth") situating Wakdjunkaga within Winnebago history, culture, and comparative North American trickster cycles. The volume then gives the same material to two outside interpreters — [Karl Kerényi](../thinkers/karl-kerenyi.md), writing as a classicist, and [Carl Jung](../thinkers/carl-jung.md), writing as a psychologist — without reconciling their readings. The 1956 edition (Philosophical Library, New York) is the version ingested here; a Schocken Books paperback reprint followed in 1972 and is the edition most commonly cited today.

Jung's essay, "On the Psychology of the Trickster Figure," was later reprinted as part of [*The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious*](collected-works-9-1.md) (CW 9.1) — the wiki's existing secondary source for the Trickster archetype. This ingest replaces that second-hand summary with Radin's actual ethnographic material and the full text of both commentary essays, neither of which survived into CW 9.1 or into Henderson's chapter in [*Man and His Symbols*](man-and-his-symbols.md).

## The Winnebago Trickster Cycle (Radin)

Radin's translation presents Wakdjunkaga ("the tricky one") across roughly fifty episodes: he abandons a war party to cohabit with a woman; his own hand fights his other hand; he kills his adoptive "brother's" children through carelessness and disguised violence; he loses his penis (kept in a box and dispatched independently to seduce a chief's daughter) piece by piece to a chipmunk; he is fooled into believing his own anus is a separate, disobedient companion; he is caught by his own trickery inside an elk skull; and he is finally driven, exhausted, transformed, and slowly acquiring rudimentary awareness of consequence, to the edge of the world, where he eats a final meal and ascends to heaven. See [Trickster Archetype](../concepts/trickster-archetype.md) for the psychological reading of this arc.

Radin's own analysis (Part Three) argues Wakdjunkaga is not conceived by the Winnebago as evil or as a deity properly speaking, but as a figure logically and chronologically prior to any fixed order — without a fixed bodily form, without social role, without memory of his own mistakes, and therefore without guilt. Radin reads the cycle as, in part, deliberate satire of specific Winnebago institutions (the warpath feast, puberty-fasting rites, the Thunderbird/Bear clan chieftainship contest), and situates the Winnebago material comparatively against Northwest Coast Raven cycles, Algonquian and Siouan variants, and cases (Oglala Iktomi, "Spider") where a trickster figure is demoted from an earlier divine status by later systematizing mythographers.

## Kerényi's Commentary: The Trickster and Hermes

Kerényi's essay ("Trickster in Relation to Greek Mythology") reads Wakdjunkaga against the Greek trickster-god Hermes — see [Karl Kerényi](../thinkers/karl-kerenyi.md) — and against the picaresque tradition running from Petronius through Rabelais to Thomas Mann's *Felix Krull*. His central argument is that Wakdjunkaga and Hermes share a family resemblance (phallic emphasis, boundary-crossing, comic reversal, association with theft and cunning) but occupy structurally different positions: Hermes remains a god within a pantheon — a psychopomp, a "spirit of life" who "opens ways" and ranges across the whole span of experience from the phallic to the spiritual — while Wakdjunkaga's wanderings simply end, unredeemed by any settled divine office, when he departs the earth. Kerényi calls this Hermes's "limitlessness" against Wakdjunkaga's more bounded "spirit of disorder": Hermes is disorder that still serves order; Wakdjunkaga is disorder without portfolio. He explicitly declines to reduce the Trickster to a single explanatory theory (contra Kristensen's "divine deceiver" hypothesis or W. F. Otto's boundary-stone reading of Hermes), preferring — consistent with his method elsewhere in [*Essays on a Science of Mythology*](essays-on-a-science-of-mythology.md) — to let mythology remain "a mode of speech" that resists full translation into theory.

## Jung's Commentary: The Trickster as Shadow

Jung's essay extends well beyond the brief summary already in CW 9.1. He situates Wakdjunkaga among a wide range of parallel figures: Mercurius in alchemy, the medieval Church's own "Feast of Fools" (*festum fatuorum*) and "Feast of the Ass" (*festum asinorum*) — clergy electing a mock bishop, braying liturgy, masquerade and license within the church itself, suppressed by ecclesiastical decree only in the 1440s–1580s — Carnival, the folklore simpleton (Hanswurst, Tom Thumb), and reported poltergeist phenomena, which Jung reads as displaying the same low, mischievous, "trickster-level" intelligence. He proposes a rough correspondence between stages of the Trickster's bodily disintegration/reintegration and the chakra sequence (citing Arthur Avalon's *The Serpent Power*) as a further cross-cultural marker of an archaic level of consciousness.

Jung's central claim is that the Trickster preserves, in mythic form, a stage of consciousness that individual and collective development have since "outgrown" but never fully erased: undifferentiated, appetite-driven, without memory of its own errors, without guilt, and without a fixed bodily boundary. This is precisely the definition of the [shadow](../concepts/shadow.md) at its most primitive: not yet personal, not yet even fully separated from the animal and the divine. Because civilization proceeds by *forgetting* this level rather than resolving it, Jung argues, it does not disappear — it persists underground, breaking out in individual pathology, in mass political "monkey tricks," and in the mobilized collective shadow of whole nations. The Trickster's story ends, however, on a note Jung treats as significant rather than incidental: hints of the saviour figure emerge from the same material, suggesting that the recognition and integration of shadow content — not its suppression — is the route by which "disaster" is converted into liberation.

## Critique

**Single-case universalism**: Both Kerényi's and Jung's essays draw sweeping cross-cultural and psychological conclusions from a single ethnographic case — Radin's translation of one narrator's (Sam Blowsnake's) rendition of the Winnebago cycle. Mainstream anthropology treats Radin's fieldwork as historically important but cautions against the move, made by both commentators, from "this cycle exhibits pattern X" to "the Trickster archetype universally means X." Radin's own comparative chapter is more cautious than either commentary built on top of it, explicitly noting that trickster cycles vary considerably in content, moral valence, and divine status across tribes, and that the "pure" trickster-type plot he isolates required him to strip out large amounts of culture-hero and creation material that many tribes integrate directly into their trickster narratives.

**Jung's comparative method**: Jung's citation of Church "Feast of Fools" customs, alchemical Mercurius, chakra correspondences, and reported poltergeist phenomena within a few pages exemplifies the amplification method that is generative for depth psychology but not falsifiable as historical or anthropological claim; the wiki notes this pattern already for CW 9.1 and *Aion*.

**Kerényi vs. Jung**: The two commentaries were solicited independently and are not reconciled by the publisher or by each other — Kerényi's classicist reading treats Hermes as retaining a "spirit of life" that ranges into the sublime (poetry, love, guidance of souls) which the Winnebago material, on his own account, never fully reaches; Jung's reading treats the same gap as evidence of an earlier, less differentiated stage of psychic development. Both readings can be held simultaneously without contradiction, since they operate at different levels of description (comparative mythology vs. depth psychology), but the book does not itself attempt the synthesis.
