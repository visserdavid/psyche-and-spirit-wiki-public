# Two Essays on Analytical Psychology

**Summary**: Jung's foundational statement of analytical psychology in two essays spanning three decades of revision — "On the Psychology of the Unconscious" (contrasting his views with Freud's and Adler's) and "The Relations Between the Ego and the Unconscious" (introducing the persona, the dangers of dissolving it, and the technique of differentiating the ego from anima/animus and the mana-personality); the clearest single source for the *machinery* of individuation, as distinct from *Aion*'s account of its structure and goal.

**Sources**:
- [Jung, C. G. (1966). *Two essays on analytical psychology* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 7). Princeton University Press.](two-essays-on-analytical-psychology.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-09

## Bibliographic Note

The volume collects two essays with long, separate revision histories. "On the Psychology of the Unconscious" began as the short 1912 lecture "Neue Bahnen der Psychologie" ("New Paths in Psychology"), was substantially rewritten and expanded in 1917 as *Die Psychologie der unbewussten Prozesse*, and reached its final form in 1943 as *Über die Psychologie des Unbewussten*. "The Relations Between the Ego and the Unconscious" began as a 1916 lecture, "La Structure de l'inconscient," and was rewritten in 1928 as *Die Beziehungen zwischen dem Ich und dem Unbewussten*, with a further revision in 1935. The two early-draft versions — "New Paths in Psychology" (1912) and "The Structure of the Unconscious" (1916) — are reproduced as appendices in this Collected Works edition, letting the reader see Jung's thinking in its earlier, less systematic form. Translated throughout by R. F. C. Hull; editors Sir Herbert Read, Michael Fordham, Gerhard Adler, and William McGuire.

## Overview

Where [*Aion*](aion.md) gives the wiki its clearest statement of individuation's *structure* (the [Ego–Shadow–Syzygy–Self](../concepts/shadow.md) sequence) and [*Mysterium Coniunctionis*](mysterium-coniunctionis.md) gives its alchemical *symbolism*, this volume gives the clinical *mechanics*: how a person actually gets from persona-identification to a working relationship with the unconscious, illustrated throughout with Jung's own case material (the philosophy student, the woman who dreams of crossing a river guarded by a crab, the businessman felled by retirement, "Henry"-style dream sequences predating the *Man and His Symbols* case by decades).

### Essay 1: On the Psychology of the Unconscious

Jung opens by triangulating his own position against [Sigmund Freud](../thinkers/sigmund-freud.md) and [Alfred Adler](../thinkers/alfred-adler.md). See [Eros and the Will to Power](../concepts/eros-and-will-to-power.md) for the full treatment: Freud reduces neurosis to repressed sexuality (Eros) and reads dreams as disguised wish-fulfilment; Adler reduces it to a masculine protest, a compensatory will to power, and reads the same symptoms as strategies for domination. Jung's own contribution is not a third reduction but a **typological** resolution: Freud and Adler each see correctly what their own attitude-type (extraverted, object-oriented for Freud; introverted, subject-oriented for Adler) predisposes them to see. Neither theory is false; each is a partial description depending on where the investigator stands.

From this triangulation Jung develops the **personal / collective / transpersonal unconscious** distinction (deepening [Collective Unconscious](../concepts/collective-unconscious.md)): psychic material transferred onto the doctor in analysis eventually exceeds anything traceable to personal biography (father, mother) and reveals **archetypal** content — Jung's clinical example is a patient's dream imagery of God as wind, structurally identical to the Greek *pneuma* and Hebrew *ruah*, without the patient having any exposure to either language.

The essay's central case (a woman patient whose recurring dream of crossing a river guarded by a crab, worked through both causal-reductive and "level-B" symbolic interpretation) introduces the **synthetic-constructive method** — reading dream symbols not only backward (to infantile memory) but forward, as anticipations pointing toward a still-unrealized psychic development. This is [Individuation](../concepts/individuation.md)'s clearest clinical demonstration of its own [Janus-faced](../concepts/individuation.md) claim about the unconscious.

The essay also gives the wiki its fullest primary-source statement of **[compensation](../concepts/compensation.md)**: the unconscious as a self-regulating system that produces, in dreams and symptoms, exactly the corrective the conscious attitude's one-sidedness requires.

### Essay 2: The Relations Between the Ego and the Unconscious

Part One traces what happens when analysis lifts repressions and the ego begins assimilating unconscious material: first a state of **inflation** (grandiosity or its mirror-image, engulfing sense of inferiority), then the discovery that the **[persona](../concepts/persona.md)** — the social mask, "a segment of the collective psyche" mistaken for the whole personality — must be relativized rather than either clung to or abandoned. Jung names the two failure modes precisely: **regressive restoration of the persona** (retreating to the old mask, diminished) and **identification with the collective psyche** (inflation into prophet, guru, or "mana personality," discussed below).

Part Two, "Individuation," gives the technique itself: **active imagination** as the method of engaging fantasy material without either dismissing it as "mere" fantasy or concretizing it (mistaking the symbolic content for literal fact); the systematic treatment of [anima and animus](../concepts/anima-animus.md) as functions of relationship to be differentiated from the ego rather than lived out unconsciously; and, as the final stage before Self-relationship proper, the **[mana-personality](../concepts/mana-personality.md)** — the archetype of magician, hero, or saint that the ego risks identifying with once anima/animus has been substantially assimilated.

## Critique

**Retrofitted coherence across three decades of revision**: Because the two essays were each revised repeatedly (1912→1917→1943 and 1916→1928→1935), the volume reads less like a single argument than a palimpsest — earlier formulations (preserved in the appendices) sometimes sit uneasily against the final, more systematic 1943/1928 versions. Jung's own prefaces acknowledge this; the *Aion* material five/eight years later is considerably more architecturally clean.

**The Freud/Adler triangulation as self-serving typology**: Jung's resolution — that Freud and Adler are each partially correct because of their own attitude-type — is elegant but also conveniently positions Jung's own theory as the superordinate, type-transcending one. Critics both contemporary (Freud himself) and later have noted that this move lets Jung avoid directly refuting either predecessor's clinical claims.

**Case material as anecdote**: As with all of Jung's clinical writing, the illustrative cases (the crab-dream patient, the businessman, "Henry"-precursor material) are single-case narratives interpreted retrospectively by the analyst who also proposes the theory they illustrate — not independently verifiable data. **Note**: this is a general limitation of psychoanalytic case-study methodology, not unique to Jung, but it bears directly on how much evidential weight the mana-personality and persona material can carry.
