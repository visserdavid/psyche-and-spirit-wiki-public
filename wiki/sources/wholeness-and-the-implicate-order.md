# Wholeness and the Implicate Order

**Summary**: David Bohm's 1980 collection of essays arguing that fragmentary thought — not merely a scientific error but the root of social, ecological, and psychological crisis — must give way to a physics and a mode of perception grounded in undivided wholeness; introduces the implicate/explicate order, the holomovement, and the experimental "rheomode" language.

**Sources**:
- [Bohm, D. (1980). *Wholeness and the implicate order*. Routledge & Kegan Paul.](wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

---

## Overview

*Wholeness and the Implicate Order* collects essays written by David Bohm across roughly two decades (the earliest, on hidden variables in quantum theory, dates to the early 1950s; the latest, on consciousness, to the late 1970s), unified by an introduction written for the 1980 volume. The edition read for this ingest is the 2002 Routledge Classics reissue, unchanged in content from the 1980 Routledge & Kegan Paul original. The book is Bohm's most accessible statement of the physics and philosophy that [Brennan](../thinkers/barbara-ann-brennan.md), [Talbot](../thinkers/michael-talbot.md), and [Dossey](../thinkers/larry-dossey.md) — all already in this wiki — draw on at second hand.

Two things distinguish the book from its later popularisations. First, its opening diagnosis is not physics at all but a social and psychological argument: that fragmentary thought is the *cause*, not merely a symptom, of the twentieth century's compounding crises. Second, roughly a third of the book (the "rheomode" chapter) is a concrete linguistic experiment — a proposed verb-centred mode of English intended to stop language itself from encoding false divisions. Neither theme survives intact in the secondary literature that cites Bohm for the implicate order alone; both are covered here for the first time. See [Fragmentation and Wholeness](../concepts/fragmentation-and-wholeness.md).

## Structure

1. **Introduction and Chapter 1 (Fragmentation and Wholeness)** — the social/psychological diagnosis: dividing thought into nations, professions, and "self vs. other" produces the world's crises because thinkers mistake convenient divisions in *thought* for divisions in *reality itself*.
2. **Chapter 2 (Rheomode)** — an experimental verb-based grammar ("levate," "vidate," "dividate," "ordinate," each conjugated as re-/irre- forms) meant to demonstrate that subject-verb-object syntax itself manufactures the illusion of separate, static "things."
3. **Chapter 3 (Reality and Knowledge as Process)** — thought and perception as one continuous process, not a subject observing an external, separate reality.
4. **Chapters 4–5 (Hidden Variables; Order in Physics)** — Bohm's causal ("pilot wave") reinterpretation of quantum mechanics, and a historical account of how physics' notion of "order" changed from Newtonian to relativistic to quantum.
5. **Chapter 6 (The Implicate Order and Its Law)** — the implicate/explicate distinction, the holomovement, and the hologram and ink-in-glycerine analogies (see [Implicate Order](../concepts/implicate-order.md) for the full treatment already in this wiki).
6. **Chapter 7 (Enfolding-Unfolding Universe and Consciousness)** — extends the implicate order to cosmology (a "sea of energy" at the Planck scale, ~10⁻³³ cm, from which the ordinary vacuum and matter both emerge as ripples) and to consciousness and memory, citing [Karl Pribram's](../thinkers/karl-pribram.md) holographic brain research directly as evidence that memory is enfolded rather than localized.

## Key Content Not Previously in the Wiki

**The fragmentation diagnosis** (Chapter 1): Bohm argues that the habit of treating theoretical divisions (nations, disciplines, the separate ego) as if they were divisions in reality itself — rather than as convenient, provisional "forms of insight" — is the single generative mechanism behind ecological destruction, social conflict, and individual neurosis alike. See [Fragmentation and Wholeness](../concepts/fragmentation-and-wholeness.md) for the full argument, including the rheomode experiment this diagnosis motivates.

**Cosmology of the sea of energy**: Bohm proposes that beneath the implicate order lies a "sea of energy" filling apparently empty space, with an estimated energy density (extrapolating quantum field zero-point calculations to the Planck length) vastly exceeding that of ordinary matter — matter and the Big Bang itself being a "ripple" on this background. This deepens [Implicate Order](../concepts/implicate-order.md)'s existing cosmology material.

**Mind and matter as "projections of a common ground"**: Bohm explicitly declines to say mind causes matter or matter causes mind; both are projections from an implicate ground that is itself neither. He cites Leibniz's monads and Whitehead's "actual occasions" as historical precedents, and Piaget's developmental psychology as empirical support for the claim that our sense of a world of separate objects in space, time, and causality is *constructed*, not given.

**Direct citation of Pribram**: In Chapter 7, Bohm cites [Karl Pribram's](../thinkers/karl-pribram.md) holographic model of brain memory storage directly as the neuroscientific indication that the implicate order applies to consciousness — the primary-source version of a connection this wiki previously only had via Talbot's popularisation. See [Holonomic Brain](../concepts/holonomic-brain.md).

## Critique

**A physicist's philosophy, not a physics result**: The implicate order, the pilot-wave interpretation, and their extension to cosmology and consciousness are Bohm's own philosophical synthesis. The pilot-wave (hidden-variables) interpretation of quantum mechanics is empirically indistinguishable from the standard (Copenhagen) interpretation — it makes no different testable predictions — so accepting it is a matter of philosophical preference, not experimental necessity. See [Implicate Order § Critique](../concepts/implicate-order.md#critique).

**The rheomode did not take**: Bohm's proposed verb-based grammar was a serious experiment but had no influence on linguistics or philosophy of language outside readers of this book; it remains a personal thought-experiment rather than an established critique of natural language.

**The fragmentation thesis is a diagnosis, not a demonstrated causal mechanism**: Bohm asserts that fragmentary thought *causes* social and ecological crisis, but the book offers no empirical test of this causal claim — it is argued philosophically, by analogy with physics, rather than established sociologically or psychologically.
