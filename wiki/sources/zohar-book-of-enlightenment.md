# Zohar: The Book of Enlightenment

**Summary**: A selected anthology from the Zohar — the central text of Jewish Kabbalah, composed by Moses de León in 13th-century Spain — translated and introduced by Daniel Chanan Matt; provides the primary-source Kabbalistic grounding for Myss's use of the sefirot and establishes key concepts (Ein Sof, Shekhinah, Tif'eret, tikkun) that cross-link to virtually every tradition in this wiki.

## Overview

The *Zohar* (Hebrew: זֹהַר, meaning "Splendor" or "Radiance") is the foundational text of Jewish Kabbalah. Written in a distinctive pseudo-Aramaic, it takes the form of a mystical commentary on the Torah organized around the wanderings and teachings of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and his circle of disciples in second-century Galilee. Modern scholarship — led by [Gershom Scholem](../thinkers/gershom-scholem.md), whose own primary argument is now directly in this wiki via [*Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism*](major-trends-in-jewish-mysticism.md) — has established through detailed philological analysis (an artificial Aramaic built on Moses de León's own medieval Hebrew habits, systematic grammatical errors, and citations of invented source books) that the bulk of the text was actually composed in 13th-century Spain by Moses de León (c. 1240–1305), who circulated it in booklets while claiming to be copying an ancient manuscript. Scholem distinguishes three compositional layers (the earliest *Midrash ha-Ne'elam*, the main body, and a later, inferior *Ra'aya Meheimna*/*Tikkunim* stratum by a different, lesser imitator), so "sole authorship" is a simplification even Scholem himself qualifies. The work ranks alongside the Bible and the Talmud in traditional Kabbalistic communities.

This volume, translated and introduced by Daniel Chanan Matt for Paulist Press's Classics of Western Spirituality series, comprises approximately 2% of the full Zohar — selected passages from Bereshit (Genesis), Shemot (Exodus), and Vayikra/Bemidbar/Devarim (Leviticus–Deuteronomy), preceded by an extensive introduction and accompanied by detailed notes occupying nearly half the volume.

Caroline Myss cites this specific edition (Matt, 1983) as her primary Kabbalistic reference in [*Anatomy of the Spirit*](anatomy-of-the-spirit.md).

## Historical Context

Moses de León composed the Zohar during a period of severe pressure on Spanish Jewry. The Reconquista had reduced Jewish usefulness as colonisers; Christian burghers saw Jews as competition; Alfonso X's law code codified anti-Jewish attitudes. The Jewish community faced a deeper internal threat: the spread of rationalism among the educated class, who studied Maimonides' *Guide for the Perplexed* and used Aristotelian philosophy to justify the neglect of tradition and commandments.

Moses de León was incensed by this rationalism — kabbalists who "kiss words of Greek assistants!" — and composed the Zohar to restore the living, mythological depth of Torah. By attributing his work to the revered Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, he secured both audience and authority. The pseudepigraphic strategy succeeded: within a century the Zohar was venerated as a third sacred text alongside Torah and Talmud.

The Zohar also responds to the Jewish condition of exile (galut). Israel in exile = the Shekhinah in exile = a rift in the divine unity. The kabbalistic project of tikkun (repair) through prayer and Torah observance is simultaneously personal, communal, and cosmological.

## The Kabbalistic System

### Ein Sof: The Infinite

God's ultimate nature is *Ein Sof* — literally "Without End" or "Infinite." Ein Sof is utterly unknowable, beyond all attributes, beyond thought and speech. The Zohar rarely names Ein Sof directly; focus falls instead on the sefirot, the divine emanations through which the Infinite makes itself known and through which creation is sustained.

The kabbalists distinguished Ein Sof from the sefirot in order to resolve the paradox of divine transcendence and immanence: Ein Sof cannot be grasped, but the sefirot — as aspects of the divine personality — provide the human soul with ten gates through which to approach and know God.

See: [Ein Sof](../concepts/ein-sof.md)

### The Ten Sefirot

The ten sefirot are stages/aspects/emanations of the divine personality, arranged in three triads plus a final manifestation:

**The Head Triad (transcendent)**
- **Keter** (Crown): The interface between Ein Sof and manifestation; pure Will/Nothingness; beyond differentiation
- **Hokhmah** (Wisdom): The first point of Thought; the seed of creation; the masculine divine
- **Binah** (Understanding/Intelligence): The Divine Mother; womb from which the seven lower sefirot are born; *Teshuvah* (Return)

**The Body Triads (active/ethical)**
- **Hesed** (Love/Mercy/Greatness): Right arm; Divine love and abundance; Abraham's quality
- **Gevurah/Din** (Judgment/Power): Left arm; divine rigor and limitation; Isaac's quality
- **Tif'eret** (Beauty/Compassion): Heart of the Tree; the Sun; balances Hesed and Din; Jacob's quality; the primary name of the Blessed Holy One

- **Nezah** (Endurance): Right leg; source of prophecy; channels inspiration
- **Hod** (Majesty): Left leg; paired with Nezah; integrity; source of vision
- **Yesod** (Foundation): The Righteous One (Tzaddik); the phallus; channels all the flow of the upper sefirot into manifestation; foundation of the world

**The Manifestation**
- **Malkhut/Shekhinah** (Kingdom/Divine Presence): Daughter of Binah, Bride of Tif'eret; the feminine earth-presence of God; the mirror reflecting all sefirot; the gate through which one enters the divine realm; in exile with Israel

See: [Sefirot](../concepts/sefirot.md), [Shekhinah](../concepts/shekhinah.md)

### Tif'eret and Shekhinah: The Sacred Marriage

The central dramatic tension of the Zohar is the relationship between Tif'eret (the masculine divine aspect, the Blessed Holy One) and Shekhinah (the feminine divine presence). Their union generates soul and sustains creation. Human sin ruptures this union by empowering the Sitra Ahra (the Other Side); Torah observance, prayer, and ethical action restore it. The Sabbath is the weekly celebration of this sacred marriage (hieros gamos).

The Shekhinah accompanies Israel in exile — "Wherever Israel went into exile, the Shekhinah went with them" (Talmud, cited by Zohar). The King yearns for the Queen: "He climbs roofs, runs stairs, scales walls, peers through holes to see her." This is God's love for Israel, even in punishment.

### The Sitra Ahra: The Other Side

The demonic realm (Sitra Ahra) is rooted in the sefirot themselves — specifically in Gevurah/Din (Judgment) untempered by Hesed (Love). Lilit and Samael are its personifications. The Sitra Ahra is not an independent force but a consequence of divine imbalance. Abraham's descent into Egypt is read as a heroic journey through the Abyss; Jacob's wrestling with the angel is an encounter with the demonic that, survived, produces the name "Israel" (one who wrestled with God and prevailed).

### Torah as Garment, Body, Soul

The Zohar teaches that Torah has four levels: the garment (the narrative surface), the body (the commandments), the soul (the hidden divine wisdom), and the soul of the soul (the innermost mystery). "Fools see only the garment. One who knows looks not at the garment but at the body inside." This four-level hermeneutic parallels the Jungian distinction between sign and symbol and the Tibetan Buddhist four-kaya teaching.

## Key Passages in This Anthology

- **Creation of Elohim**: The primordial blinding spark (zohar) flashing from the Concealed of Concealed; palace of Binah birthing creation
- **Hidden Light**: The primordial light of Day One, hidden by God and stored for the righteous; Moses saw from Sinai, now hidden in Torah
- **Look at Torah**: The three-garment teaching (garment/body/soul); Torah's outer story as protective husk for the inner light
- **Shekhinah passages**: The exile of the Divine Presence; God peering through lattices at Israel's synagogues; the pledge God gave Israel
- **God, Israel, and Shekhinah**: The King/Queen/son dynamic; Israel as the heart of the world
- **Threshing Secrets**: The secret gathering of Rabbi Shimon's circle; souls departing during the transmission; the dangerous holiness of revealed wisdom
- **Rabbis Encounter Child**: A schoolboy stuns the rabbis with Torah wisdom revealed from the soul-level; his father is the soul of Rav Hamnuna Sava
- **Wedding Celebration**: The hieros gamos (sacred marriage) of Tif'eret and Shekhinah at the Sabbath

## Relation to Other Wiki Sources

**Myss and the Three Traditions**: Myss's [Seven Sacred Truths](../concepts/seven-sacred-truths.md) integrates the sefirot as a third parallel to the chakra system and Christian sacraments. This volume provides the primary-source grounding for her Kabbalistic column.

**ACIM parallels**: The Zohar's *Ein Sof* parallels ACIM's "God" — utterly beyond form, known only through its emanations. The Shekhinah's exile maps onto ACIM's [Separation and Atonement](../concepts/separation-and-atonement.md): a primordial split that human perception maintains but divine love continuously seeks to heal. Tikkun parallels Atonement: the recognition that the separation need not be permanent.

**Jung and archetypes**: The ten sefirot function as ten archetypal qualities of the Divine — universal patterns that the soul is mandated to embody and refine. This is structurally identical to Jung's collective unconscious populated by archetypes. The Zohar's Patriarchs-as-sefirot (Abraham = Hesed, Isaac = Gevurah, Jacob = Tif'eret) prefigures Jungian archetypal biography.

**The divine feminine**: The Shekhinah as the exile/reunion feminine divine parallels Northrup's [Female Energy System](../concepts/female-energy-system.md), Campbell's Maya/Goddess as Revealing Power, and the Tibetan Buddhist Dakini. The restoration of the Shekhinah through tikkun parallels Myss's healing model: illness arises from soul-energy disconnection; healing restores the flow.

**Ein Sof as Ground**: The incomprehensible Infinite that underlies and generates all sefirot parallels Brennan's [Core Star](../concepts/core-star.md), the Tibetan Buddhist [Rigpa](../concepts/rigpa-nature-of-mind.md) and [Ground Luminosity](../concepts/ground-luminosity.md), and Jung's [Self](../concepts/the-self.md) as the unknowable ground of the psyche.

## Critique

**Scholarly debate on authorship**: Modern scholarship (Scholem, Tishby, Matt himself) has established Moses de León as the primary author, not the ancient Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. The pseudepigraphic framing is deliberate — Scholem treats it as a legitimate, if unusual, category of religious literature rather than simple fraud, noting Moses de León appears to have experienced himself as a genuine vessel for tradition even while composing in his own hand. Traditionalists defend the book's authority on other grounds; academic study enhances rather than diminishes appreciation.

**Myss's use of the sefirot**: Myss uses the sefirot selectively and synthetically, filtered through her own framework. Her specific seven-level compression (pairing Hokhmah/Binah, Hesed/Gevurah, Nezah/Hod) departs from traditional interpretations in ways Kabbalistic scholars would critique. Her overall alignment, however, has clear textual basis in the Zohar's own structure.

**Spiritual appropriation**: Non-Jewish use of Kabbalistic material — detached from the Jewish legal and liturgical context in which it developed — raises ongoing concerns about integrity. The Zohar was created to strengthen Jewish identity and practice in a time of persecution; its adoption as a universalist framework is a historical irony Moses de León could not have anticipated.
