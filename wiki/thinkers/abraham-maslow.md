# Abraham Maslow

**Summary**: American psychologist (1908–1970); founder of humanistic psychology; developer of the hierarchy of needs and the concept of self-actualization; his idea of "metapathology" — suffering caused by a thwarted need for meaning — is structurally parallel to Frankl's existential vacuum and informs Borysenko's crisis-as-initiation framework.

## Biography

Abraham Harold Maslow was born in Brooklyn, New York, on April 1, 1908. He completed his PhD in psychology at the University of Wisconsin in 1934, studying under Harry Harlow. He held positions at Brooklyn College and, from 1951, at Brandeis University, where he chaired the psychology department. He died on June 8, 1970.

Maslow is best known for his 1943 paper "A Theory of Human Motivation," which introduced the hierarchy of needs, and for his later concept of self-actualization. He is regarded as the father of humanistic psychology, a "third force" distinct from both Freudian psychoanalysis and Skinnerian behaviourism.

## Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow's hierarchy organises human needs into five tiers, from most basic to most advanced:

1. **Physiological** — food, water, sleep, warmth
2. **Safety** — security, stability, freedom from fear
3. **Love and belonging** — social connection, affection, community
4. **Esteem** — competence, achievement, recognition
5. **Self-actualisation** — realising one's full potential; becoming what one is capable of becoming

The hierarchy is commonly depicted as a pyramid, though Maslow himself resisted overly rigid interpretations. Higher needs become salient only when lower needs are substantially met.

## Self-Actualisation and Peak Experiences

Maslow studied "self-actualising" individuals — people who were living at their full capacity — through biographical research on historical figures (Lincoln, Jefferson, Einstein, Eleanor Roosevelt, Thoreau, Beethoven, William James). He identified a cluster of common characteristics including spontaneity, problem-centred orientation, independence, deep human connections, and democratic character structure.

Self-actualising people regularly reported **peak experiences** — transient states of profound joy, completeness, and loss of self-consciousness in which ordinary categories of time and space seem suspended. Maslow saw these as the most direct human encounters with psychological health and with what earlier traditions called mystical experience.

## Metapathology and the Existential Vacuum

Beyond the five-level hierarchy, Maslow proposed a set of **meta-needs** (or Being-values, B-values): truth, beauty, justice, goodness, wholeness, vitality, uniqueness, completeness, simplicity, aliveness, and meaning. These are not drives toward deficiency-reduction but drives toward realisation and growth.

When meta-needs are chronically frustrated, the person suffers **metapathology** — a specific form of existential suffering distinct from neurotic anxiety or physical pain. Symptoms include meaninglessness, alienation, cynicism, anhedonia, and a pervasive sense of not living. Maslow's patient Jack — who had every material comfort and yet felt "I'm not living" — is a clinical illustration.

[Viktor Frankl](viktor-frankl.md)'s "existential vacuum" is structurally equivalent: both describe a specific suffering caused not by unresolved inner conflict but by a thwarted need for meaning. [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) links both to the [crisis-as-initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) framework: the metapathology patient needs not therapy in the conventional sense but initiation into a larger purpose.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers in the Wiki

**[Viktor Frankl](viktor-frankl.md)**: Maslow and Frankl are parallel figures — both identified meaning as the level of human motivation above pleasure and power; both described a distinct form of existential suffering; both contributed to the humanistic psychology tradition. The main difference: Frankl developed his insight from surviving Auschwitz; Maslow developed his from studying healthy people at their peak.

**[Carl Jung](carl-jung.md)**: Maslow's peak experiences closely resemble Jung's numinous experiences — encounters with the Self that dissolve the ego's boundary. Both saw these states as central to psychological health rather than as exceptional aberrations.

**[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)**: Borysenko invokes Maslow's metapathology to explain a form of suffering that conventional psychotherapy cannot address — the person who has everything and yet is empty, who needs not repair but purpose.

## Critique

**The pyramid structure**: The hierarchical model implies that higher needs only activate when lower ones are satisfied — which contradicts documented cases of people prioritising meaning, love, or beauty under conditions of physical deprivation (Frankl's camp observations being the most extreme example). Maslow himself acknowledged this problem in later work.

**Sampling bias**: His study of self-actualising individuals drew on historical figures and personal acquaintances, with no systematic methodology. Critics have noted the sample was largely Western, male, and historically exceptional.

**Cultural specificity**: The hierarchy's emphasis on individual self-actualisation reflects Western (particularly American) cultural values. Cross-cultural psychology has documented different orderings of needs, and some cultures place group belonging or spiritual transcendence above individual fulfilment.
