# Alfred Adler

**Summary**: Austrian physician and founder of individual psychology, whose theory that neurosis is a "masculine protest" — a compensatory will to power against felt inferiority — gave Jung his other main theoretical counterpoint (alongside Freud's Eros theory) in developing analytical psychology's typological resolution of the two schools.

- [Jung, C. G. (1966). *Two essays on analytical psychology* (R. F. C. Hull, Trans.; 2nd ed.; Collected Works of C. G. Jung, Vol. 7). Princeton University Press.](../sources/two-essays-on-analytical-psychology.md)

## Overview

Alfred Adler (1870–1937) was an early member of Freud's Vienna Psychoanalytic Society who broke with Freud in 1911 over the primacy of sexuality in neurosis, founding **individual psychology**. Adler's central claim: neurosis is not a conflict over repressed sexual instinct but a strategy — however self-defeating — for securing power, safety, or superiority against a felt sense of organic or social inferiority (the "masculine protest"). Symptoms, on this reading, are unconscious devices of domination: Adler's own clinical example (discussed in Jung's parallel treatment) is a woman whose asthma and night terrors secure her household's constant attention and submission.

## Role in *Two Essays on Analytical Psychology*

[Jung](carl-jung.md) treats Adler, alongside Freud, as one of two valid-but-partial theories of neurosis — see [Eros and the Will to Power](../concepts/eros-and-will-to-power.md) for the full triangulation. Where Jung reads Freud's Eros theory as characteristically **extraverted** (object-oriented), he reads Adler's will-to-power theory as characteristically **introverted** (subject-oriented): Adler's attention runs toward the subject's own security and self-assertion rather than toward the object of desire. Jung's own resolution is not to choose between the two theories but to argue each theorist has generalized his own typical attitude into a universal law — the earliest primary-source statement in this wiki's Jung material of the introversion/extraversion distinction later systematized in *Psychological Types* (1921).

## Critique

Adler's individual psychology, like Freud's psychoanalysis, is not treated by mainstream contemporary psychiatry as empirically validated in the modern evidence-based sense; it belongs to the history of psychodynamic theory. Adler's concept of the "inferiority complex" entered popular usage well beyond its original clinical context.
