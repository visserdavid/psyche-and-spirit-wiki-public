# Andrew Greeley

**Summary**: American Catholic priest and sociologist of religion (1928–2013), professor at the University of Chicago and research associate at the National Opinion Research Center, whose national surveys documented that a large minority of Americans report genuine mystical experiences yet keep them secret — the empirical foundation for Joan Borysenko's "closet mystics" thesis.

## Biography

Andrew Moran Greeley (1928–2013) was ordained a Catholic priest of the Chicago archdiocese in 1954 and earned a doctorate in sociology from the University of Chicago in 1962, going on to a career as professor of sociology (University of Arizona and University of Chicago) and research associate with the National Opinion Research Center (NORC). Alongside prolific sociological work on American Catholicism and the Irish-American experience, Greeley conducted influential national surveys on the incidence of mystical and paranormal experience in the general population, published in works including *The Sociology of the Paranormal* (1975).

## Key Finding: Mystical Experience Is Widespread and Concealed

Greeley's surveys, conducted in 1973 and repeated in the mid-1980s, found that roughly 35% of Americans reported having experienced "a force that seemed to lift them out of themselves," with about 5% reporting the more intense experience of being "bathed in light." He found — importantly for how the wiki uses his work — that these experiences correlated with *above-average* mental health, education, and general life satisfaction, not with psychopathology, credulity, or social instability, directly countering the assumption that mystical-experience reports are markers of disturbance.

## Use in the Wiki: Closet Mystics

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) uses Greeley's survey data as the empirical backbone of her concept of "a nation of closet mystics": people who have had profound mystical or paranormal experiences but conceal them out of fear of ridicule or psychiatric pathologising. See [Closet Mystics](../concepts/closet-mystics.md) for the full treatment, and [Near-Death Experiences](../concepts/near-death-experiences.md), where Greeley's adult survey data sits alongside [Melvin Morse](melvin-morse.md)'s pediatric NDE research as complementary evidence for the same underlying phenomenon across age groups.

## Critique

Greeley's surveys rely on self-report and are vulnerable to social-desirability effects in both directions (over-reporting to please a surveyor interested in the topic, or under-reporting to avoid appearing credulous), and "mystical experience" is not consistently operationalised across the studies Borysenko cites. The data documents that people report these experiences; it does not by itself adjudicate what the experiences are — a materialist account (temporal lobe activity, culturally primed interpretation of ordinary emotional peaks) remains consistent with the same survey findings as a genuinely transcendent account. Borysenko treats this ambiguity as open rather than resolved.
