# Andrew Weil

**Summary**: American physician, author, and pioneer of integrative medicine; best known for his argument that health means wholeness and that healing is an innate biological capacity of the organism that medicine should support rather than replace.

**Sources**:
- [Weil, A. (1983). *Health and healing: Understanding conventional and alternative medicine*. Houghton Mifflin.](../sources/health-and-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Biography

Andrew Weil (b. 1942, Philadelphia) graduated from Harvard College (1964) and Harvard Medical School (MD, 1968). He went on to study traditional healing systems worldwide — Ayurveda in India, curanderismo in Latin America, traditional Chinese medicine, shamanism among indigenous peoples in North America and the Amazon — before developing his concept of integrative medicine.

He founded and directed the Arizona Center for Integrative Medicine at the University of Arizona, where he trained physicians in combining conventional clinical medicine with evidence-based complementary practices: nutrition, herbal medicine, mind-body techniques, acupuncture, osteopathy, and psychospiritual support.

His 1983 *Health and Healing* was the most philosophically systematic of his early works, written from lectures at the University of Arizona before "integrative medicine" existed as a term. Later works — *Natural Health, Natural Medicine* (1990), *Spontaneous Healing* (1995), *Eight Weeks to Optimal Health* (1997), *Healthy Aging* (2005) — extended the framework into clinical application.

Weil also researched altered states: his 1968 double-blind study of marijuana in *Science* (with Zinberg and Nelsen) was the first controlled study of cannabis in humans, and he wrote *The Natural Mind* (1972) on the relationship between altered states of consciousness and health.

## Core Ideas

### Health as Wholeness

Weil argues that Western medicine cannot define health because it has abandoned the philosophical and religious roots of the concept. The Proto-Indo-European root *kailo* gives simultaneously **health**, **holy**, **whole**, **hale**, and **heal** — concepts that Western modernity treats as separate but that share a single origin in the intuition that wholeness is both the condition of health and a spiritual goal.

See [Health as Wholeness](../concepts/health-as-wholeness.md).

### Nine Principles of Health and Illness

A framework of empirical-philosophical principles derived from clinical observation: that perfect health is transient; that illness need not be calamity; that all bodies have innate healing capacity; that agents (germs, toxins) are not causes (susceptibility is); that all illness involves mind and body; that illness progresses through stages; that bodies differ individually; that everyone has a structural weak point; and that blood carries the principal healing energy.

See [Nine Principles of Health and Illness](../concepts/nine-principles-of-health.md).

### The Innate Healing System

Healing is not given by medicine but is a biological property of living organisms, composed of three processes: reaction (inflammation, fever), regeneration (cellular replacement), and adaptation (return to equilibrium). Medicine's job is to remove obstructions to healing, not to provide something the body lacks.

See [Innate Healing System](../concepts/innate-healing-system.md).

### Mind-Mediated Healing and the Placebo Response

Weil gives the most systematic analysis of placebo response in this wiki. He identifies five misconceptions physicians hold about placebo, argues that wart cures are the clearest evidence for a mind-directed immune mechanism, and documents the S.R. Hodgkin's case as a "documented miracle cure" in which a conversion experience triggered complete remission of Stage III cancer.

See [Placebo Effect](../concepts/placebo-effect.md) and [Spontaneous Remission](../concepts/spontaneous-remission.md).

### Critique of Holistic Medicine

Despite helping found the American Holistic Medical Association, Weil is sharply critical of holistic medicine in practice: it lacks a coherent philosophy, accepts unproven modalities (applied kinesiology, Bach flower remedies, colour therapy) uncritically, and tends toward a "fantasy of sweetness and light" that avoids the shadow dimension of illness. His model: a pragmatic integrative physician who uses whatever the case requires — allopathic, complementary, or psychospiritual — without ideological commitment to any system.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers

| Weil's position | Converges with | Diverges from |
|---|---|---|
| Health as wholeness | [Myss's biography-is-biology](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md); [Northrup's feminine principle of health](christiane-northrup.md) | Allopathic medicine's disease-centred definition |
| Healing as innate capacity | [Siegel's exceptional cancer patients](../concepts/exceptional-cancer-patients.md); [Achterberg's imagery research](../concepts/mental-imagery-healing.md) | Standard pharmacological model |
| Agents ≠ causes | [Dossey's meaning as primary variable](../concepts/meaning-and-health.md) | Germ theory (Koch's postulates as applied clinically) |
| Wart cures as key evidence | [Talbot's holographic interpretation](michael-talbot.md) | Dermatology's treatment of wart cures as anecdote |
| Critique of holistic medicine | [Harman's need for coherent philosophy](willis-harman.md) | New Age medicine's uncritical enthusiasm |

Weil read the same literature as Talbot, Dossey, and Harman — Pribram's holonomic brain theory, Bohm's implicate order, quantum non-locality — but characteristically moves immediately to clinical application. Where Talbot builds a cosmology, Weil builds a consultation model.
