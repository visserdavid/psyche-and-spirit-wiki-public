# Aniela Jaffé

**Summary**: Swiss Jungian analyst (1903–1991) and Jung's personal secretary; edited *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*; wrote the visual arts section of *Man and His Symbols*, tracing stone, animal, and circle symbolism from cave paintings to modern abstraction.

## Overview

Aniela Jaffé (1903–1991) was a Swiss Jungian analyst who served as Jung's secretary and collaborator from the 1950s until his death. She is best known for editing *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (1962) — the memoir she compiled from Jung's autobiographical dictations — and for her later work on occult phenomena and the symbolism of the visual arts.

## *Man and His Symbols*, Part 4: Symbols in the Visual Arts

Jaffé's contribution to [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) examines three recurring motifs in the history of human art-making — stone, animal, and circle — as expressions of the archetypal dimension of the psyche. She argues that art, like dreams and myth, is a medium through which the unconscious speaks in symbolic form.

### Stone Symbolism

The use of stone as a sacred material appears across cultures and millennia: Jacob's stone at Bethel (animated by God's presence, it becomes a pillar and an altar), the megalithic alignments at Carnac in Brittany, the stone circles of Stonehenge, Zen Buddhist rock gardens, and modern sculpture (Max Ernst, Hans Aeschbacher, James Rosati).

Jaffé argues that the urge to work stone, to give it shape, or to arrange it in meaningful patterns expresses the projection of unconscious content onto matter. Stone embodies what is permanent, primordial, and supra-personal — the Self as impervious to time. The tendency to carve faces into stones (menhirs, herms), to invest specific stones with numinosity, or to treat stone circles as boundary markers between the sacred and the profane reflects the same psychological dynamic as dream symbolism: the inner world externalises itself in the material world.

### Animal Symbolism

The Ice Age cave paintings of Lascaux, Altamira, and Trois Frères — spanning 60,000 to 10,000 BCE — represent humanity's most sustained engagement with animal symbolism. Jaffé traces the interpretive trajectory:
- Cave animals were not merely decorative: target marks suggest hunting magic (killing the image kills the animal); mating scenes suggest fertility ritual
- The shaman figure at Trois Frères — a man wrapped in animal hide with antlers and bear paws — represents the initiatory crossing of the boundary between human consciousness and animal instinct
- This boundary crossing is preserved in the animal attributes of gods across world religions: Egyptian (Hathor-cow, Amon-ram, Thoth-baboon), Hindu (Ganesha-elephant, Vishnu-boar, Hanuman-ape), Greek (Zeus-swan/bull/eagle), Norse (Wotan-raven/horse)

The animal in art represents the instinctual ground of the psyche — the part of human nature that exists prior to, and beneath, the elaborations of culture and reason. Jaffé argues that dreams in which a person is pursued by an animal represent the instinctual life pressing for integration. The animal is not the enemy; it is the shadow of civilisation's over-cultivated persona.

### Circle/Mandala in Art History

The [mandala](../concepts/mandala.md) as artistic form appears in:
- Navajo sand paintings (healing rituals in which the patient sits at the centre)
- Tibetan thangkas (cosmological mandalas used for meditation)
- Hindu yantras (geometric meditation objects, especially the Sri Yantra combining nine triangles)
- Buddhist wheel of life (eight directions from the centre, corresponding to the Noble Eightfold Path)
- Medieval cathedral rose windows
- City planning: Rome (*Roma quadrata*, divided by two arteries meeting at the *mundus*), Washington D.C., Angkor Wat, Palmanova (Italy, 1593)

The shift from mandala-based architecture (circular or radially symmetrical) to elongated basilica/Latin cross plans in Gothic cathedrals reflects, for Jaffé, the medieval theology of transcendence — the aspiration toward heaven over the grounding in earth. The Renaissance's return to circular church plans (Bramante and Michelangelo's St. Peter's) reflects the re-centering on the human.

### Modern Art as Symptom and Search

Jaffé's most original contribution is her psychological reading of 20th-century abstract art. She traces the sequence:
- **Chirico** (metaphysical painting): empty city squares, headless mannequins, marble fragments — the void left by the "death of God" (Nietzsche), the loss of shared symbols
- **Kandinsky**: the separation from the world of objects, the search for the "inner necessity" of pure colour and form — spirituality without dogma, the cosmos without a shared God-image
- **Klee**: penetration of the appearance of things to the ground of life — art as spiritual investigation, not representation
- **Mondrian**: the reduction to pure vertical/horizontal tension — the search for unchanging reality beneath the flux of appearances
- **Pollock**: painting in a trance state — the submission of the conscious will to the unconscious process; *prima materia* as creative source
- **Schwitters**: the exaltation of rubbish (bus tickets, wire, nails) — the alchemical investment of matter with spirit
- **Marini**: the horse-and-rider series tracking from peaceful rider (post-WWII hope) to petrified, falling figure (the death of the heroic individual in the mass age)

Jaffé reads this arc as a cultural parallel to the individual's shadow encounter in dreams: the progressive dissolution of the centres of meaning (God, reason, the human figure) forces a confrontation with the void — and through the void, with the unconscious substrate from which new symbols might emerge. UFOs, discussed briefly following Jung's earlier essay on the subject, are interpreted as mandala projections: circular luminous objects appearing in the sky at a moment of maximum collective disorientation.

## Broader Significance

Jaffé's section exemplifies the Jungian approach to culture: art, like dreams, is read as symbolic communication from the unconscious. The art historian's question (what did the artist intend? what social forces shaped this work?) is supplemented — not replaced — by the psychological question: what archetype is this image an expression of? What stage of psychic development does this symbol represent?
