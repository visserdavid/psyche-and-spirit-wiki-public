# Anne Wilson Schaef

**Summary**: American clinical psychologist and author (1934–2020) whose *When Society Becomes an Addict* (1987) reframed patriarchal culture as a self-perpetuating "addictive system" — the concept Christiane Northrup adopts and applies specifically to women's health in *Women's Bodies, Women's Wisdom*.

**Sources**:
- [Northrup, C. (1994). *Women's bodies, women's wisdom: Creating physical and emotional health and healing*. Bantam Books.](../sources/womens-bodies-womens-wisdom.md)

## Biography and Key Contribution

Anne Wilson Schaef (1934–2020) was an American clinical psychologist and organizational consultant whose *When Society Becomes an Addict* (1987) argued that patriarchal Western society functions structurally like an addiction: it numbs authentic feeling, demands denial of its own harmful consequences, assigns authority externally rather than to the individual's own inner guidance, and reproduces itself by transmitting its beliefs through those it has already harmed. The book became a *New York Times* bestseller and was nominated for Best Political Book of the Year.

## Use in the Wiki

[Christiane Northrup](christiane-northrup.md), in the opening chapter of *[Women's Bodies, Women's Wisdom](../sources/womens-bodies-womens-wisdom.md)*, adopts Schaef's addictive-system framework and applies it specifically to women's health, identifying five core beliefs of the addictive system that Northrup argues are directly destructive to women's physical wellbeing (disease as enemy, medical science as omnipotent, the female body as abnormal, women as second-class citizens, and experts as more authoritative than a woman's own body knowledge). Northrup's stated reason for adopting Schaef's reframing over the term "patriarchy" is strategic: "addictive system" describes a pattern that both men and women perpetuate and from which both suffer, rather than assigning blame in a way Northrup felt would provoke defensiveness rather than change. See [Addictive System](../concepts/addictive-system.md) for the full treatment.

## Critique

Schaef's framework has been influential in feminist and recovery-movement writing but is a metaphorical extension of clinical addiction concepts to societal structures, not an empirically operationalised or independently tested model. Applying addiction language to an entire culture risks over-generalising: not all patriarchal cultural norms that harm women plausibly operate through the specific psychological mechanisms (denial, numbing, external attribution of authority) that define individual addiction. The wiki's own critique of Northrup's adoption of the concept notes the same limitation, alongside the specific claim that these cultural beliefs produce identifiable physiological effects on women's health — a claim consistent with general psychoneuroimmunology research on chronic stress but not demonstrated for the addictive-system framework as a distinct category.
