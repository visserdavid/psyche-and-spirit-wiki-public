# Arnold van Gennep

**Summary**: French-Dutch ethnographer and folklorist (1873–1957) who coined the term "rites of passage" and identified their universal three-phase structure (separation, margin/limen, aggregation) in his 1909 work *Les rites de passage* — the direct theoretical foundation for [Victor Turner](victor-turner.md)'s theory of liminality and communitas.

**Sources**:
- [Turner, V. (1969). *The ritual process: Structure and anti-structure*. Aldine Publishing.](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)

## Biography and Contribution

Arnold van Gennep was a French ethnographer whose 1909 book *Les rites de passage* (*The Rites of Passage*) first identified that ceremonies marking a change of place, state, social position, or age share a common underlying structure across cultures, regardless of their specific content. He divided this structure into three phases:

1. **Separation** — rites detaching the individual from a previous fixed point in the social structure.
2. **Margin** (or **limen**, Latin for "threshold") — the intervening, ambiguous period in which the subject has none of the attributes of the past or coming state.
3. **Aggregation** (or reincorporation) — rites marking the return, in a new state, with new rights and obligations.

Van Gennep's book was known but not widely developed within anthropology until [Victor Turner](victor-turner.md) took up the middle, liminal phase as the centerpiece of his own theory in *[The Ritual Process](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)* (1969), elaborating it into the concepts of [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md) and [Communitas](../concepts/communitas.md). Turner credits van Gennep explicitly and repeatedly throughout the book as the source of the basic schema he builds on.
