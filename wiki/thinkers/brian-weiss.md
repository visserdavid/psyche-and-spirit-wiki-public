# Brian Weiss

**Summary**: American psychiatrist (b. 1944), chairman of the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Sinai Medical Center in Miami, whose *Many Lives, Many Masters* (1988) documented a patient's spontaneous past-life regressions during hypnotherapy and became one of the most widely read clinical accounts of past-life therapy — cited by Joan Borysenko as an example of clinically documented, if metaphysically unresolved, therapeutic benefit from the practice.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

## Biography

Brian Leslie Weiss (b. 1944) trained in psychiatry and went on to chair the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Sinai Medical Center in Miami. He had neither training nor prior interest in past-life therapy when, in the early 1980s, a patient under conventional hypnotherapy for anxiety began spontaneously describing apparent memories of previous lives. Over some eighteen months of continued treatment, the regression work resolved phobias that years of conventional therapy had not touched. Weiss published the case as *Many Lives, Many Masters* (1988), a decision [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) explicitly credits as professionally courageous, given the reputational risk a chairman of psychiatry took in publishing material so far outside psychiatric orthodoxy.

## Use in the Wiki

Borysenko, in *[Fire in the Soul](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)*, cites Weiss as one of the clinical pioneers of past-life therapy alongside [Roger Woolger](roger-woolger.md), whose more theoretically developed framework (past-life material as archetypal rather than necessarily literal-historical) the wiki treats as the more rigorous complement to Weiss's case-based approach. See [Past-Life Therapy](../concepts/past-life-therapy.md) for the fuller treatment, including the four explanatory frameworks (literal reincarnation, genetic/cultural memory, imaginal archetypes, therapeutic metaphor) Borysenko weighs against each other without requiring a resolution to achieve therapeutic benefit.

## Critique

Weiss's case reports document clinical outcomes (phobia resolution) that are real and observed, but — as with all past-life therapy — no method exists to verify that the "memories" surfaced under hypnosis are literal historical records rather than products of suggestion, hypnotic confabulation, or therapeutic expectation. The [False Memory Syndrome](../concepts/past-life-therapy.md#critique) controversies of the 1990s established that hypnotic states can generate vivid, emotionally compelling but factually false memories; Weiss's methodology (case narrative rather than controlled study) cannot rule this out. The therapeutic benefit he documents is consistent with several non-literal explanations (metaphor, archetype, suggestion-assisted catharsis) that do not require accepting reincarnation as historical fact — a caveat the wiki applies uniformly to this literature.
