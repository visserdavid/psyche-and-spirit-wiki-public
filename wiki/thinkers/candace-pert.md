# Candace Pert

**Summary**: American neuroscientist whose research on neuropeptides provided a biochemical basis for the mind-body connection, cited by Myss as scientific grounding for the idea that emotions are stored in the body's cells.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J., & Rothstein, L. (1987). *Minding the body, mending the mind*. Addison-Wesley.](../sources/minding-the-body.md)
- [Myss, C. (1996). *Anatomy of the spirit: The seven stages of power and healing*. Harmony Books.](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Overview

Candace B. Pert (1946–2013) was an American neuroscientist and pharmacologist best known for her discovery of opiate receptors in the brain (1972) and her subsequent research on neuropeptides — chemical messenger molecules that transmit information between the brain and every system of the body.

## Significance in Myss's Framework

[Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md) cites Pert's work as the scientific foundation for a core claim of [biography is biology](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md):

> *"Dr. Candace Pert has proven that neuropeptides — the chemicals triggered by emotions — are thoughts converted into matter."*

Pert's research demonstrated that:
- Neuropeptides are manufactured not only in the brain but throughout the body — immune cells, gut cells, and other tissues produce and receive the same chemical signals as brain cells.
- As Pert told journalist Bill Moyers (in the documentary *Healing and the Mind*): "The mind is in the body" — emotions have a molecular substrate distributed throughout all the body's systems.
- The mind-body split is, at the biochemical level, an artificial division. What we experience as emotion corresponds to real chemical changes in tissue.

For Myss, this provides a scientific bridge between the intuitive claim (emotions are stored in the body) and a mainstream physiological mechanism.

## Pert's Own Framing

Pert herself became an advocate for mind-body medicine and was not averse to spiritual interpretations of her work. She described neuropeptides as a form of energy not fully understood, and suggested that consciousness might persist in some form after physical death. This put her at odds with colleagues who preferred a strictly materialist reading of her findings.

## Critique

While Pert's research on neuropeptides and opiate receptors is well-established and significant, the interpretive leap from "emotions have biochemical correlates" to "specific biographical experiences directly cause specific diseases via energy field dynamics" is not supported by her data. Mainstream psychoneuroimmunology (PNI) confirms that chronic stress affects immune function and increases disease risk, but does not confirm the specific illness-emotion mappings Myss proposes. Pert's more speculative claims about consciousness and energy were not part of her peer-reviewed scientific work.

## Pert and Psychoneuroimmunology

Pert's neuropeptide work is the molecular foundation of [psychoneuroimmunology](../concepts/psychoneuroimmunology.md) — the field that [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) operationalised clinically at Harvard's Mind/Body Clinic. Borysenko invokes neuropeptides explicitly as the messengers linking mind and immune system: neuropeptides produced in brain areas governing emotion bind to receptors in immune cells, creating two-way signalling that makes psychological states physiologically potent. Pert's discovery that these receptors exist throughout the body — not only in the brain — established that the "molecules of emotion" are distributed across all the body's systems, providing the anatomical substrate for the entire mind-body medicine claim.
