# Daniel Chanan Matt

**Summary**: American scholar of Jewish mysticism (b. 1950) whose translation and introduction in *Zohar: The Book of Enlightenment* (1983) is the primary academic source for the Kabbalistic dimension of this wiki; his contextual scholarship makes the Zohar's system accessible to the cross-traditional audience of holistic health readers.

**Sources**:
- [Matt, D. C. (Ed. & Trans.). (1983). *Zohar: The book of enlightenment* (Classics of Western Spirituality). Paulist Press.](../sources/zohar-book-of-enlightenment.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Biography

Daniel Chanan Matt received his Ph.D. from Brandeis University and has taught at Boston University, the University of Texas at Austin, and the Center for Judaic Studies at the Graduate Theological Union in Berkeley, California. He is one of the foremost American scholars of Kabbalah and Jewish mysticism.

His 1983 Paulist Press edition of the Zohar — part of the prestigious Classics of Western Spirituality series — was among the first serious English translations of the Zohar aimed at a general academic audience. The volume includes a 40-page scholarly introduction reconstructing the Zohar's authorship, historical context, and theological system, followed by selected passages and nearly 200 pages of annotated notes.

Matt subsequently produced the monumental *The Zohar: Pritzker Edition* (Stanford University Press, beginning 2004) — a definitive scholarly translation and annotation of the complete Zohar in 12 volumes, representing decades of work. This edition is considered the standard scholarly reference.

---

## Significance for This Wiki

Caroline Myss cites the 1983 Matt edition as her primary Kabbalistic source in [*Anatomy of the Spirit*](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md). Matt's accessible introduction and selection directly shaped which aspects of the Zohar's system entered the holistic health literature through Myss's synthesis.

Matt's contribution to this wiki is primarily as a scholarly mediator: he makes [Moses de León](moses-de-leon.md)'s 13th-century mystical vision available to 20th-century readers, and his critical introduction — distinguishing what the Zohar actually says from what Theosophical and syncretic readers have projected onto it — is an important counterweight to selective or decontextualised use.
