# Elisabeth Kübler-Ross

**Summary**: Swiss-American psychiatrist (1926–2004) whose 1969 *On Death and Dying* established the five-stage model of dying and founded modern death studies; later a major near-death experience researcher and hospice advocate.

**Sources**:
- [Kübler-Ross, E. (1969). *On death and dying*. Macmillan.](../sources/on-death-and-dying.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Biography

Elisabeth Kübler-Ross was born in Zürich, Switzerland on 8 July 1926, one of triplets. She trained as a psychiatrist at the University of Zürich, receiving her MD in 1957. After emigrating to the United States, she worked at the Colorado General Hospital and then at the University of Chicago Billings Hospital, where she established the interdisciplinary Death and Dying Seminar in 1965.

*On Death and Dying* (1969) grew directly from that seminar, in which terminally ill patients were interviewed before an audience of medical and theological students. The book transformed both psychiatry and medicine: it made death a legitimate subject of clinical inquiry and education, and its five-stage model became one of the most cited frameworks in psychology.

Following *On Death and Dying*, Kübler-Ross became deeply interested in near-death experiences and life after death. She co-founded the hospice movement in the United States alongside Cicely Saunders, advocating consistently for home-based, dignity-centred dying. She died on 24 August 2004 in Scottsdale, Arizona.

## Core Contributions

### The Five Stages of Dying

Kübler-Ross's central contribution is the empirically observed pattern of emotional response to terminal diagnosis: denial and isolation, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance, with hope as a constant undercurrent. These are described fully in [Five Stages of Dying](../concepts/five-stages-of-dying.md).

The stages were derived from interviews with over 200 dying patients and represent a qualitative map of the territory, not a normative prescription. Kübler-Ross was explicit that patients move through stages non-linearly, revisit earlier stages, and that individual variation is enormous.

### The Seminar as Method

Kübler-Ross's methodological innovation was treating dying patients as teachers rather than subjects. The seminar at the University of Chicago broke with the prevailing medical convention of protecting patients from honest conversations about their dying. She demonstrated empirically that such conversations were not harmful but were, in most cases, actively desired by patients who felt isolated by the conspiracy of silence around them.

### Communication with the Dying

The book is a sustained argument for honest, presence-based communication with dying patients. Kübler-Ross documents the damage done by avoidance, euphemism, and the exclusion of patients from decisions about their own care. Her "door opening" interview — the encounter that signals to the patient that honest speech is safe — is a foundational technique of palliative care.

### Critique of Technological Dying

*On Death and Dying* launched the modern critique of hospital-based dying as dehumanising. Kübler-Ross argued that the technological imperative — to treat death as a medical failure to be fought at all costs — strips dying patients of dignity, autonomy, and meaningful connection. This critique was foundational to the hospice movement and to the emergence of palliative medicine as a distinct specialty.

### NDE Research (later career)

After *On Death and Dying*, Kübler-Ross became one of the earliest and most prominent researchers and advocates for near-death experiences. She wrote the foreword to both of [Raymond Moody's](raymond-moody.md) founding NDE books, *[Life After Life](../sources/life-after-life.md)* (1975) and *Reflections on Life After Life* (1977); Moody credits her independent, parallel research with dying patients as decisive corroboration that the phenomenon he was documenting was not an isolated coincidence of his own case-finding. Her work with Moody and others helped establish NDE research as a serious field and contributed to the evidence base surveyed by [Kenneth Ring](kenneth-ring.md) in *Heading Toward Omega* (1984).

## Connections Within the Wiki

**Brennan's seven stages**: [Barbara Ann Brennan](barbara-ann-brennan.md) explicitly builds the [Seven Stages of Healing](../concepts/seven-stages-of-healing.md) on Kübler-Ross's five stages, adding rebirth and creation as post-acceptance phases. Brennan's model is the direct clinical extension of Kübler-Ross's dying model into the healing context.

**Borysenko and crisis**: [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) in [*Fire in the Soul*](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md) uses the NDE evidence (including Kübler-Ross's later work) as a key pillar of [Crisis as Initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) — the life review as evidence that love and wisdom are the only measures that survive death.

**Siegel and dying patients**: [Bernie Siegel](bernie-siegel.md) in [*Love, Medicine and Miracles*](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md) was deeply influenced by Kübler-Ross's work; ECaP's understanding of the dying patient as teacher and the importance of emotional honesty both derive directly from her work.

**Healing vs. curing**: Kübler-Ross's acceptance stage — a patient at peace with dying — is one of the clearest empirical examples of [Healing vs. Curing](../concepts/healing-vs-curing.md): wholeness achieved without physical recovery.

**Palliative care and dignity**: The critique of dehumanised dying in modern hospitals connects to [Meaning and Health](../concepts/meaning-and-health.md) (Dossey) and [Conscientious Psychology](../concepts/conscientious-psychology.md) (Shealy).

## Critique

**Empirical limitations of the stage model**: Subsequent grief research found the five stages are not universal, sequential, or distinct categories. George Vaillant's longitudinal work and other studies suggest grief follows highly individual patterns. The model's staying power is as a heuristic, not as an empirically validated sequence.

**The appropriation problem**: The five stages were specifically developed from patients facing *death* and have been widely overapplied to grief, job loss, divorce, and every form of loss and change — often in ways that pathologise non-sequential responses.

**Death as taboo-breaking**: Kübler-Ross was culturally positioned as the person who broke a massive cultural taboo, which partly explains the outsized response to her work. Critics from within psychiatry argued her clinical data was thin and the methodology insufficiently systematic.

**Later career controversies**: Kübler-Ross's later embrace of channelling, spirit guides, and claims about life after death was criticised within the scientific community and complicated her legacy, though she remained consistent in applying her core therapeutic principles.
