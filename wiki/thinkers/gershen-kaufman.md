# Gershen Kaufman

**Summary**: American clinical psychologist and professor emeritus at Michigan State University, author of *Shame: The Power of Caring* and *The Psychology of Shame*, whose concept of the "interpersonal bridge" — the bond of love and mutual recognition that shame fractures — is the central structural metaphor in Joan Borysenko's account of childhood shame and inner-child work.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1990). *Guilt is the teacher, love is the lesson*. Warner Books.](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

## Biography and Key Contribution

Gershen Kaufman was educated at Columbia University and received his Ph.D. in clinical psychology from the University of Rochester, going on to a long career as professor emeritus at Michigan State University's Counseling Center and Psychology Department, where he spent over three decades in clinical practice, supervision, and research on shame. His shame theory synthesises object relations theory, interpersonal theory, and — distinctively — Silvan Tomkins's affect theory, presented most fully in *The Psychology of Shame: Theory and Treatment of Shame-Based Syndromes* and in the more accessible *Shame: The Power of Caring*.

Kaufman's central structural metaphor is the **interpersonal bridge**: the bond of trust, love, and mutual recognition connecting two selves, along which genuine emotional contact flows. He also distinguishes shame as a **passing emotion** — a valuable, momentary survival signal (the blush of exposure) — from shame as **identity**: an enduring conviction of worthlessness that forms when shaming experiences accumulate and the bridge is repeatedly broken.

## Use in the Wiki

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) adopts Kaufman's interpersonal-bridge metaphor as the central structural device in her account, in *[Guilt Is the Teacher, Love Is the Lesson](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md)*, of how childhood shame forms: when a parent's inconsistency, contempt, or withdrawal of love communicates that a child's authentic self is unacceptable, the bridge fractures, and the child's near-universal conclusion — "this must be my fault" — becomes the seed of what Borysenko (following William James) calls "soul sickness." See [Inner Child](../concepts/inner-child.md) and [Guilt and Soul Sickness](../concepts/guilt-and-soul-sickness.md) for the fuller treatment, including how Kaufman's passing-emotion/identity distinction underlies Borysenko's own distinction between healthy guilt and unhealthy shame.

## Critique

Kaufman's shame theory is a respected and clinically influential framework within psychotherapy, but "interpersonal bridge" and "shame identity" remain clinical-theoretical constructs rather than standardised, independently measured diagnostic categories — there is no agreed instrument for quantifying bridge integrity or shame-identity severity, and Borysenko's application of the framework rests on clinical observation rather than controlled outcome research.
