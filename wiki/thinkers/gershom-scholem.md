# Gershom Scholem

**Summary**: German-Israeli historian and philologist (1897–1982), founder of the modern academic study of Jewish mysticism as a historical-critical discipline; established that the Zohar was composed by Moses de León in 13th-century Spain rather than transmitted from antiquity, and gave the first systematic historical account of Kabbalah, Lurianic cosmology, and Sabbatianism.

**Sources**:
- [Scholem, G. (1995). *Major trends in Jewish mysticism* (3rd rev. ed.). Schocken Books. (Original work published 1941)](../sources/major-trends-in-jewish-mysticism.md)

## Biography

Gershom (Gerhard) Scholem was born in Berlin in 1897 to an assimilated German-Jewish family. He turned to Zionism and to the study of Hebrew and Jewish mysticism as a young man, partly in reaction against his family's assimilationism, and emigrated to Palestine in 1923. He became the first professor of Jewish mysticism at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, a position that did not exist as an academic discipline before him — his own life's work created the field. He died in Jerusalem in 1982.

*Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism* originated as the 1938 Hilda Stroock Lectures at the Jewish Institute of Religion, New York, and was first published in 1941; the book is dedicated to the memory of his close friend Walter Benjamin, who died in 1940 attempting to flee occupied France.

## Method: Historian, Not Mystic

Scholem's foundational methodological stance — stated explicitly in the book's first lecture — is that Jewish mysticism must be studied as a **historical phenomenon**, with the same philological rigor applied to any other body of texts, rather than approached devotionally or dismissed wholesale. He is sharply critical of both extremes that preceded him: the 19th-century "Science of Judaism" scholars (Graetz, Zunz, Geiger, Steinschneider), who treated Kabbalah with open contempt as an embarrassing irrationalist deviation from "true" rational Judaism, and non-Jewish occultist popularizers (Éliphas Lévi, Aleister Crowley) whose treatments of Kabbalah he considered "highly coloured humbug." Scholem's own approach — treat the sources philologically and historically while taking their content and psychological seriousness fully seriously — became the template for the entire subsequent academic field.

## Core Contributions

### The Zohar as 13th-Century Composition, Not Ancient Text

Scholem's single most consequential and, at the time, most controversial finding: the *Zohar*, traditionally attributed to the 2nd-century sage Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, was substantially composed nearly a thousand years later by the Spanish kabbalist Moses de León (c. 1240–1305). Scholem reaches this conclusion through close philological analysis — the Zohar's Aramaic is not the Aramaic actually spoken in 2nd-century Palestine but an artificial literary language built on Moses de León's own medieval Hebrew habits of mind, complete with systematic grammatical errors, invented words, and misreadings of the Babylonian Talmud; its geography of Palestine is fantastical and derived from literary sources rather than lived knowledge; its citations of "ancient" authorities are frequently to invented, non-existent books. See [Zohar: The Book of Enlightenment](../sources/zohar-book-of-enlightenment.md) and [Moses de León](moses-de-leon.md) for how this wiki's existing pages already documented this thesis (via Matt's 1983 introduction) and how Scholem's own fuller argument now grounds it directly.

Scholem's account is more nuanced than a simple fraud narrative: he argues Moses de León experienced himself as a genuine vessel for received tradition even while composing in his own hand, distinguishes an earliest layer (the *Midrash ha-Ne'elam*, written 1275–1281) from the main body of the Zohar (1281–1286) and the latest layer (*Ra'aya Meheimna* and *Tikkunim*, c. 1300, which Scholem argues is by a different, lesser writer imitating the original), and treats the pseudepigraphic form itself as "a legitimate category of religious literature" rather than mere forgery.

### Lurianic Kabbalah: Tzimtzum, Shevirah, Tikkun

Scholem gives the first systematic historical account of the cosmology developed by Isaac Luria and his circle in 16th-century Safed, Ottoman Palestine, in the traumatic aftermath of the 1492 expulsion of Jews from Spain — see [Lurianic Kabbalah](../concepts/lurianic-kabbalah.md) for the full treatment of **tzimtzum** (divine self-contraction to make room for creation), **shevirat ha-kelim** (the primordial catastrophe of the Breaking of the Vessels, the origin of evil and exile), and **tikkun** (the cosmic repair effected through righteous human action) — deepening the wiki's existing, Zohar-derived treatment of [tikkun](../concepts/tikkun.md).

### Sabbatianism as Mystical Heresy

Scholem's account of the 17th-century Sabbatai Zevi messianic movement — a mass movement across the Jewish Diaspora that collapsed into crisis when its self-proclaimed Messiah converted to Islam under Ottoman pressure in 1666 — is his most psychologically vivid historical case study, and the subject of a dedicated new page: see [Sabbatianism](../concepts/sabbatianism.md).

## Relation to Other Thinkers in This Wiki

Scholem's historical-critical method stands in instructive contrast to [Carl Jung](carl-jung.md)'s treatment of alchemy and Gnosticism: Jung reads historical esoteric material psychologically, as externalized projections of universal archetypal processes, largely bracketing questions of textual authorship and historical transmission; Scholem insists on exactly those questions as the necessary foundation before any psychological or theological reading can be responsibly built on top of the material. Both scholars, working in roughly the same mid-20th-century period, share a conviction that mystical/esoteric material deserves serious intellectual engagement rather than either credulous acceptance or rationalist dismissal — but they reach that seriousness by opposite routes.
