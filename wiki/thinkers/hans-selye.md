# Hans Selye

**Summary**: Hungarian-Canadian endocrinologist (1907–1982) who introduced the scientific concept of stress and the General Adaptation Syndrome — establishing that chronic stress causes progressive physiological damage and underlies a wide range of illnesses.

## Biography

Hans Selye (1907–1982) was born in Vienna and spent most of his career at the University of Montreal. He is widely credited with introducing the concept of "stress" into biology and medicine. His research, beginning in the 1930s, demonstrated that a wide variety of physical and psychological challenges produce a stereotyped physiological response — the General Adaptation Syndrome — that, when sustained over time, leads to organ damage and disease.

## General Adaptation Syndrome

Selye's core finding, developed across two decades of endocrinological research and set out in full in his own primary account [*The Stress of Life*](../sources/the-stress-of-life.md) (1956), is a stereotyped triphasic bodily response to any sufficiently strong demand — see [General Adaptation Syndrome](../concepts/general-adaptation-syndrome.md) for the full treatment (the operational definition, the stressor/stress distinction, adaptation energy, and "diseases of adaptation"). In brief, the three stages are:

1. **Alarm reaction**: The immediate response to a stressor — adrenaline release, elevated blood sugar, increased heart rate. This is adaptive in the short term.
2. **Stage of adaptation (resistance)**: The body adapts to the stressor, and the alarm reaction subsides. However, adaptation has a cost: the threshold for new stressors is lowered. Over time, previously tolerable stimuli (a single cigarette, a cup of coffee, a teaspoon of sugar) can each individually trigger the alarm reaction.
3. **Stage of exhaustion**: If stressors continue, adaptation fails. The body enters a state it cannot sustain, and disease becomes increasingly likely. Selye called this maladaptation — the feature underlying most chronic illness.

## Altruistic Egoism: Selye's Philosophy of Gratitude

*The Stress of Life*'s closing chapters extend the biological findings into an explicit ethic, which Selye calls **altruistic egoism**. He traces an evolutionary line from single-cell self-preservation, through the cooperation of cells within a multicellular body (where one cell's "altruism" toward the organism is simultaneously its own best strategy for survival), to human social cooperation. His practical conclusion: because interpersonal stress is driven by the twin poles of **gratitude** and **revenge**, and because gratitude is cumulative and self-reinforcing while revenge only breeds further revenge, consciously working to earn others' goodwill is not mere altruism but the most biologically sound form of long-term self-interest. Selye offers this "philosophy of gratitude" as the ultimate practical lesson of the entire stress research program — a rare case of a laboratory endocrinologist drawing an explicit ethical conclusion from his own physiological data.

## Relevance to The Relaxation Response

[Herbert Benson](herbert-benson.md) cites Selye explicitly as part of the theoretical foundation for linking stress to hypertension. In *The Relaxation Response* (1975), Benson positions the fight-or-flight response within the broader context Selye established — that chronic physiological stress activation produces cumulative organ damage. Benson's hypertension epidemic argument (essential hypertension as primarily stress-driven) rests on Selye's framework; his clinical solution — the [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md) as the counterpart — is his answer to Selye's problem.

Where Selye described the problem (what stress does to the body), Benson described a practical intervention (how to reverse it). The two bodies of work are complementary: Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome provides the disease model; Benson's relaxation response provides the clinical tool.

## Relevance to Sacred Healing

[Shealy](norman-shealy.md) builds on Selye's framework in developing his concept of [Electromagnetic Dysthymia](../concepts/electromagnetic-dysthymia.md) (EMD) — a modern syndrome of cumulative stress overload from environmental, chemical, and electromagnetic sources. EMD is essentially Selye's exhaustion stage produced by the specific stressors of contemporary life. Shealy cites Selye's work to argue that stress — not specific pathogens or genetic predispositions — is the common denominator of most modern chronic disease.

Selye's model also informs the [attitude and health](../concepts/attitude-and-health.md) framework: emotional states such as anger, fear, guilt, and resentment are potent stressors that drive the alarm reaction and, if chronic, lead to the exhaustion stage.

## Critique

Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome, while foundational, has been substantially refined. Modern stress research recognises that the stress response is far more nuanced than Selye's model suggested — it varies by stressor type, individual appraisal, social context, and biological predisposition. The concept of "stress" has also become so broad as to risk explanatory vagueness: almost any adverse condition can be labelled a stressor, making specific causal claims difficult.

Nevertheless, the core insight — that chronic physiological activation (sustained elevation of cortisol, adrenaline, and other stress hormones) damages organs and systems — is solidly supported by decades of research and underpins the field of psychoneuroimmunology.
