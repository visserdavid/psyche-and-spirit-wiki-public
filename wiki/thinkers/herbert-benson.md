# Herbert Benson

**Summary**: American cardiologist and Harvard professor (b. 1935); discoverer of the relaxation response — the physiological counterpart to the fight-or-flight response, elicitable through meditation and breathing; founder of the Behavioral Medicine unit at Boston's Beth Israel Hospital; mentor to Joan Borysenko and pioneer of clinical mind-body medicine.

## Biography

Herbert Benson, MD (b. 1935) is a cardiologist who trained at Harvard Medical School and spent his career at Harvard and Boston's Beth Israel Hospital. He is best known as the discoverer and populariser of the [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md) — the physiological counterpart to the [fight-or-flight response](../concepts/fight-or-flight-response.md), accessible through meditation, focused breathing, or repetitive prayer.

In the late 1960s, TM practitioners approached his laboratory at Harvard's Thorndike Memorial Laboratory (Beth Israel Hospital) and persuaded him to study meditation scientifically. Working with R. Keith Wallace (who had completed his UCLA doctoral research on TM physiology), Benson documented that meditation produced a measurable, reproducible state distinct from both sleep and ordinary waking. He published this as *The Relaxation Response* in 1975 (William Morrow), with co-author Miriam Z. Klipper.

Benson directed the Behavioral Medicine unit at Beth Israel Hospital, where he created a structure for integrating mind-body research into clinical medicine. In September 1981 he enabled [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) and Ilan Kutz, MD, to found the Mind/Body Clinic within his unit — the institutional home for the clinical program that *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* documents. He wrote the foreword to Borysenko's book.

His collaborative research included Harvard biofeedback studies (demonstrating that blood pressure can be voluntarily controlled through conditioning — Shapiro, Tursky, Schwartz), monkey blood pressure conditioning studies (with Herd, Morse, Kelleher), and the clinical hypertension trial documenting that regular elicitation of the relaxation response reduces blood pressure in essential hypertension patients without medication change.

## The Relaxation Response

Benson's core discovery: just as the body has an evolutionarily ancient [fight-or-flight response](../concepts/fight-or-flight-response.md) (activating the sympathetic nervous system for action against danger), it also has a complementary [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md) (activating the parasympathetic system for rest and restoration). Walter Hess (Nobel laureate physiologist) had identified both states in cat brain stimulation experiments; Benson documented the relaxation response in meditating humans.

Physiological changes when the relaxation response is elicited:
- Oxygen consumption decreases 10–20 percent (more than after four to five hours of sleep)
- Heart rate decreases approximately three beats per minute
- Respiration rate decreases
- Blood lactate decreases (lactate elevation is associated with anxiety states)
- Brain waves shift toward increased alpha rhythm
- Blood pressure decreases

Any technique that focuses attention on a repetitive stimulus (a word, sound, breath, movement) while passively disregarding other thoughts can elicit the response. Benson identified this as the common denominator across diverse contemplative traditions — expressed through four structural elements present in every tradition he surveyed: quiet environment, a mental device (word/phrase/mantra), a passive attitude, and a comfortable position.

## The Cross-Cultural Survey

The most historically ambitious section of *The Relaxation Response* documents that every major contemplative tradition — Christian mysticism (St. Augustine, the *Cloud of Unknowing*, St. Teresa of Ávila, Hesychasm), Jewish mysticism (Merkabolism, Rabbi Abulafia), Sufi Islam (Dhikr, Al Ghazali), Taoism (Chuang Tzu's "sitting forgetting"), Buddhism (anapanasati breath meditation, Ashvagosha), and Yoga (Patanjali) — independently discovered methods embodying the same four structural elements. The variation is in cultural content; the mechanism is universal.

Benson does not reduce the spiritual to the physiological. His argument is that the body reliably enters a measurable restorative state through these practices — and that this state is part of what the traditions have discovered — without claiming to explain away the spiritual dimension.

## Significance for the Wiki

Benson's relaxation response is the clinical mechanism underlying many of the healing practices described across the wiki:

- **Shealy's electrotherapy**: TENS and cranial electrical stimulation aim to shift the nervous system from sympathetic to parasympathetic dominance — the same physiological shift as the relaxation response, achieved electrically rather than through meditation.
- **Shealy's Five Sacred Rings**: GigaTENS stimulation of acupuncture circuits aims to raise neurochemicals (DHEA, oxytocin) that are depleted by chronic sympathetic activation.
- **Myss's practice**: Though Myss does not use relaxation response language, her clinical observation that prayer, contemplation, and symbolic perception shift a person's relationship to their illness aligns with the parasympathetic-dominant state Benson describes.
- **Brennan's healing work**: The chelation and laying-on-of-hands sessions that Brennan describes induce deep relaxation as a prerequisite for energy healing — consistent with the relaxation response as the physiological ground of healing.

## Critique

**Scientific consensus**: The relaxation response is one of the best-established findings in mind-body medicine. It is reproducible, measurable, and now widely accepted in mainstream medicine. Its usefulness for hypertension, anxiety, and stress-related symptoms is well-supported.

**Mechanism debates**: The precise mechanisms by which the relaxation response produces its effects — which neurotransmitters, which neural pathways, which immunological changes — continue to be refined. Benson's original descriptions have been substantially extended by subsequent PNI research.

**Clinical application**: Benson's claim that the relaxation response can be elicited by any focusing technique, regardless of its spiritual content (secular word vs. prayer), has been both confirmed and contested. Some researchers argue that the specific content of meditative practice (compassion training, mindfulness, TM) produces different neurological and psychological effects beyond what a generic relaxation response accounts for.
