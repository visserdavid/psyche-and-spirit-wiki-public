# J. Krishnamurti

**Summary**: Indian philosopher and speaker (1895–1986) who taught that the psychological "self" is itself a fragmenting construct maintained by thought, and that freedom comes only through direct, choiceless self-observation rather than the adoption of any doctrine, teacher, or organized system — a teaching that directly shaped David Bohm's diagnosis of fragmentary thought.

## Biography

Jiddu Krishnamurti (1895–1986) was born in India and, as a child, was proclaimed by the Theosophical Society to be the vehicle for a coming "World Teacher." Groomed for this role for over a decade, he dissolved the organization built around him in 1929, declaring that "truth is a pathless land" and could not be reached through any organization, creed, or teacher — including himself. He spent the rest of his life travelling and speaking independently, without founding a religion or formal system, on the nature of thought, fear, conditioning, and freedom.

## Key Teaching

Krishnamurti's central claim is that the psychological "self" — the sense of a separate, continuous "I" that thought constructs and defends — is not a discovery about reality but a fragmenting *activity* of thought itself. Thought divides experience into observer and observed, self and world, "what is" and "what should be," and then mistakes these divisions for the way things actually are. This is the same movement of fragmentary thought that [David Bohm](david-bohm.md) later generalized, in *[Wholeness and the Implicate Order](../sources/wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)*, into a full critique of fragmentary thinking across science, society, and psychology — see [Fragmentation and Wholeness](../concepts/fragmentation-and-wholeness.md).

Krishnamurti held that this fragmentation cannot be corrected by more thought (a new belief, discipline, or system) but only by choiceless, direct awareness of the movement of thought as it happens — a form of observation without the observer separating itself from what is observed.

## Connection to David Bohm

Krishnamurti and Bohm held decades of recorded public dialogues, beginning in the 1960s, in which Bohm found that Krishnamurti's description of thought as a fragmenting, self-perpetuating process closely paralleled his own conclusions from physics about the illusory nature of rigid, mechanical divisions in nature. Bohm cites Krishnamurti directly in the opening chapter of *Wholeness and the Implicate Order* as a source for the argument that inquiry into the nature of thought and "the field of measure" must take priority over inherited habits of ordering experience. See [David Bohm](david-bohm.md) and [Fragmentation and Wholeness](../concepts/fragmentation-and-wholeness.md) for the fuller treatment of how this dialogue shaped Bohm's published argument.

## Critique

Krishnamurti's teaching resists conventional evaluation on its own terms, since he explicitly rejected doctrine, method, and institutional authority — there is no system to test empirically. Critics have noted a tension between this anti-authoritarian stance and Krishnamurti's own decades-long role as a singular authoritative speaker to large audiences. His claim that direct awareness dissolves psychological fragmentation is a first-person phenomenological claim, not one that has been operationalized or tested by mainstream psychology.
