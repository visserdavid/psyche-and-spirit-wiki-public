# James Fowler

**Summary**: American developmental psychologist and theologian (1940–2015); professor at Emory University; developed the six-stage model of faith development — from pre-reflective magical thinking through literal-mythic belief, social conformism, critical individuation, paradox-tolerance, and universalising compassion — used by Borysenko to explain why some people deepen through crisis while others are shattered by it.

**Sources**:

## Biography

James W. Fowler III was born on October 12, 1940. He completed his doctorate at Harvard Divinity School and spent most of his academic career at Emory University in Atlanta, where he was Professor of Theology and Human Development and director of the Center for Ethics. He died on October 16, 2015.

Fowler's major work is *Stages of Faith: The Psychology of Human Development and the Quest for Meaning* (Harper & Row, 1981), developed from more than 350 in-depth interviews. It applies developmental stage theory — drawing on Piaget's cognitive stages, Erikson's psychosocial stages, and Kohlberg's moral stages — to the domain of faith: how people find and maintain meaning, value, and connection to the transcendent.

## The Six Stages of Faith

Fowler distinguishes "faith" from religion or belief. Faith is the activity of finding meaning, constructing a sense of ultimate value, and being related to what one treats as worthy of one's commitment — whether that involves a God, a community, a set of values, or a cause.

**Stage 1 — Intuitive-Projective** (early childhood): Faith shaped by fantasy, imagination, and the powerful images of parents and environment. God as powerful protector or terrifying destroyer — not yet rationally organised.

**Stage 2 — Mythic-Literal** (childhood, but often persisting into adult life): Stories, beliefs, and symbols are taken literally; the world is experienced through a narrative of fairness and retribution. Borysenko's Jay (AIDS as divine punishment for homosexuality) and the Texan Christian counsellor operate here.

**Stage 3 — Synthetic-Conventional** (adolescence, widespread in adults): Faith is deeply felt but unreflected; identity is constituted by group membership and conformity to the community's belief system. The person's faith organises their whole world coherently but has not been critically examined.

**Stage 4 — Individuative-Reflective** (young adulthood or later): The person takes responsibility for their own beliefs through critical examination. Previous Stage 3 certainties are often disrupted or abandoned. This transition is frequently painful and may feel like loss of faith.

**Stage 5 — Conjunctive** (rarely before midlife): The person learns to live with the paradoxes and tensions that Stage 4's critical reason cannot resolve — acknowledging the truth in opposing perspectives, reclaiming the power of myth and symbol, moving beyond demythologising. Borysenko's Leslie operates here.

**Stage 6 — Universalising** (rare; the fulfilment of faith development): The rare individuals who have committed their lives wholly to the realisation of love, justice, or the Kingdom of God — Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., Mother Teresa, Thomas Merton. Stage 6 people are often regarded by the surrounding community as dangerous or extremist because their commitment to the transcendent overrides the compromises that maintain social order.

## Relevance to *Fire in the Soul*

Fowler's model provides [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) with an explanation for why the same crisis shatters some people and deepens others: the outcome depends partly on the stage of faith development from which the person encounters the crisis.

- A Stage 2 person encounters illness as divine punishment — confirming or destroying their literal-mythic framework.
- A Stage 3 person experiences crisis as challenge to community membership and group belief.
- A Stage 4–5 person has the cognitive and spiritual resources to hold the paradox that bad things happen to good people without needing a punishing God or a New Age "you create your reality" doctrine to explain it.

Crucially, a therapist, pastor, or counsellor operating from Stage 2 or 3 cannot help a client who is being called toward Stage 4–5. The helper's own framework actively blocks the growth if they treat their client's Stage 4 questioning as faithlessness rather than development.

## Critique

**Hierarchical structure**: Critics have questioned whether the stages represent genuine developmental progress or merely reflect Western, liberal, Protestant assumptions about what mature faith looks like. Stage 6's exemplars (Gandhi, King, Merton) are predominantly male, middle-class, and from major Western or Indian religious traditions.

**Empirical base**: The interview methodology, while rich, is not readily replicable. Stage assignments require trained raters and are subject to interpretive variability.

**The "universalising" endpoint**: The assumption that Stage 6's dissolution of self-protective commitment is universally desirable has been challenged by feminists and others who argue that Stage 3's communal embeddedness may be healthier for many people than Stage 4's individuation.
