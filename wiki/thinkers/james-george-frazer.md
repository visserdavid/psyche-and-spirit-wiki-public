# James George Frazer

**Summary**: Scottish social anthropologist (1854–1941) whose comparative, "armchair" study of magic, myth, and religion — above all *The Golden Bough* — founded much of twentieth-century comparative mythology and ritual studies, while establishing a method (worldwide comparison of decontextualized customs, synthesized into a single evolutionary sequence) that mainstream anthropology has since largely rejected.

## Life and Work

Frazer was born in Glasgow in 1854 to a devout Free Church of Scotland family, studied classics at Glasgow University and then Trinity College, Cambridge, and remained a Cambridge fellow for most of his career (with a period at Liverpool). His early exposure to David Hume's skeptical epistemology, to classical languages and literature, and to the physics of his teacher Lord Kelvin all shaped *The Golden Bough*'s method: a Humean interest in the psychological sources of belief, combined with the comparative and inductive habits of Victorian natural science applied to religion and myth.

Reading Edward Burnett Tylor's *Primitive Culture* in 1883 converted Frazer to the emerging discipline of anthropology; a subsequent collaboration with the biblical scholar William Robertson Smith (for whom Frazer wrote the *Totem* and *Taboo* entries in the ninth edition of the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*) gave him his first sustained anthropological subject matter, expanded into *Totemism* (1887) and later the vast *Totemism and Exogamy* (1910). *The Golden Bough* itself grew from a chance convergence, in March 1889, of a passage in Virgil's *Aeneid* (the golden bough that permits Aeneas to enter the underworld) with a scholiast's note connecting it to the priesthood of Diana at Nemi, and with a report of a strikingly similar succession-by-combat kingship rite in Malabar, India. Frazer never visited Nemi or conducted ethnographic fieldwork of any kind; his method throughout was to synthesize secondhand reports — missionary accounts, colonial administrators' observations, travelers' journals, and a questionnaire he circulated to workers in the field — into a single comparative argument.

The book grew across three editions: two volumes in 1890, an expanded edition in 1900, and a twelve-volume third edition (1906–1915), before Frazer produced his own one-volume popular abridgment in 1922 — a text from which he had, by then, progressively cut his own most controversial material (the extended argument that the Crucifixion of Christ instantiates the same dying-scapegoat-king pattern he traced worldwide), after public criticism from the folklorist Andrew Lang. See [The Golden Bough](../sources/the-golden-bough.md) for the fuller publication history and the Oxford World's Classics edition (ed. Robert Fraser) that restores this excised material.

Frazer went blind in 1930 and continued to work by dictation until his death in May 1941; his wife, Lilly (herself a folklorist), died a few hours after him.

## Core Contributions

- **[Sympathetic Magic and Taboo](../concepts/sympathetic-magic-and-taboo.md)**: the homeopathic/contagious distinction in magical thinking, and the three-stage Magic → Religion → Science scheme of cultural evolution.
- **[Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md)**: the comparative synthesis of Osiris, Adonis, Attis, Dionysus, and the Corn-Spirit into a single vegetation-death-and-return archetype — the direct methodological ancestor of [Joseph Campbell](joseph-campbell.md)'s later, Jungian-inflected version of the same pattern in *The Mythic Image*.
- **[The Scapegoat Ritual](../concepts/scapegoat-ritual.md)**: the ritual transference of communal evil onto an expelled victim, object, or animal.
- Sacred kingship, ritual regicide, and the taboos surrounding sacred persons.

## Influence

Frazer's comparative method shaped an entire generation of thinkers this wiki already covers: [Joseph Campbell](joseph-campbell.md) cites Frazer directly and extensively in *[The Hero with a Thousand Faces](../sources/the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md)*; [Arnold van Gennep](arnold-van-gennep.md) draws heavily on Frazer's material throughout *[The Rites of Passage](../sources/the-rites-of-passage.md)*; and [Mircea Eliade](mircea-eliade.md)'s history-of-religions approach in *[Rites and Symbols of Initiation](../sources/rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md)* works the same comparative terrain with a more historically disciplined method. T. S. Eliot drew on *The Golden Bough* for the structure of *The Waste Land*, and Freud's *Totem and Taboo* engages Frazer's totemism scholarship directly.

## Critique

Mainstream anthropology, from Bronisław Malinowski and Franz Boas onward, has largely rejected Frazer's "armchair" method: assembling similar-looking customs from unrelated, unvisited cultures into a single global evolutionary narrative, without fieldwork or attention to local cultural context. J. Z. Smith's essay "Dying and Rising Gods" (*Encyclopedia of Religion*, 1987) argues that Frazer's central organizing category — the dying-and-rising god — imposes false unity on traditions whose actual death-and-return narratives differ far more than his synthesis suggests. See the Critique section of [Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md) for the fuller discussion. Frazer nonetheless remains historically indispensable: nearly every later comparative-mythology and ritual-studies thinker in this wiki (Campbell, Eliade, van Gennep) worked in direct, acknowledged dialogue with his synthesis, even while superseding his method.
