# James Hillman

**Summary**: American post-Jungian psychologist (1926–2011), founder of archetypal psychology, whose foundational manifesto *Re-Visioning Psychology* (1975) argues that the psyche is inherently polytheistic and that soul-making — not ego integration — is psychology's true task, and whose later *The Soul's Code* (1996) argues every person is born with a unique image of their calling, guided by a daimon (guardian spirit).

## Life and Career

James Hillman (1926–2011) was an American psychologist trained in the Jungian tradition at the C. G. Jung Institute in Zurich, where he later became Director of Studies. He broke significantly from mainstream Jungian psychology in the 1970s with his development of *archetypal psychology*, a school that emphasises the primacy of imagination and soul over ego, individuation as a goal, and clinical interpretation.

His major works span five decades. *Re-Visioning Psychology* (1975) is the theoretical foundation of archetypal psychology. *The Dream and the Underworld* (1979) reframes dreams as belonging to the underworld of the psyche rather than the ego's interpretive agenda. *A Hundred Years of Psychotherapy — and the World's Getting Worse* (1992, with Michael Ventura) is his most accessible polemic. *The Soul's Code* (1996) is his widest-audience work and the most directly relevant to this wiki.

## Archetypal Psychology

Hillman founded the school of *archetypal psychology*, which extends Jungian depth psychology by emphasising imagination and soul over ego and individuation. It takes as its central problem not the healing of the ego but the deepening of soul — *soul-making*, a term borrowed from John Keats.

Key tenets of archetypal psychology:

- The psyche is inherently **polytheistic** — animated by multiple, sometimes conflicting, archetypal presences that resist reduction to a single integrating ego or Self
- **Soul-making** (Keats: "a world of pains and troubles to school an intelligence and make it a soul") is the fundamental purpose of psychological life — not happiness, integration, or health, but the deepening of imaginal capacity
- **Pathology is not to be cured** but understood as the psyche's own form of meaning-making. Symptoms are not problems to be eliminated but communications to be heard
- Depth psychology requires engagement with **image, myth, and symbol** rather than explanation and technique. The clinical hour should proceed by amplification (following the image) rather than interpretation (reducing the image to a cause)
- **Beauty** is a psychological necessity — its neglect in psychology is a clinical and cultural failure

This approach contrasts with the more prescriptive self-development orientation of Myss's work, while sharing the commitment to archetypal frameworks as the deepest language of the psyche.

## Re-Visioning Psychology (1975)

[*Re-Visioning Psychology*](../sources/re-visioning-psychology.md) — delivered as the 1972 Terry Lectures at Yale — is Hillman's founding theoretical statement, organized as four essays rather than a linear argument:

- **Personifying**: the recovery of [soul-making](../concepts/soul-making.md) through treating psychic contents (moods, symptoms, complexes) as autonomous imaginal figures, against the "nominalist" reduction of image to mechanism
- **Pathologizing**: the argument, developed at length in [Pathologizing](../concepts/pathologizing.md), that psychic disorder and symptom are not malfunctions to be cured but necessary, meaning-bearing activities of soul — read as mythic language (Saturn/depression, Dionysus/hysteria) rather than diagnostic labels
- **Psychologizing**: the recovery of ideation and reflection as soul-activity, and the identification of the archetypal premises (Apollonic, Saturnine, Hermetic) underlying every system of thought, including science itself
- **Dehumanizing**: the explicit statement of [Polytheistic Psychology](../concepts/polytheistic-psychology.md) against monotheistic consciousness, and the critique of humanism's ego-centred model of the person, with Renaissance Neoplatonism (especially Marsilio Ficino) as the historical model for a soul-centred rather than man-centred psychology

This book is the primary source for material the wiki previously knew only second-hand via *The Soul's Code* and Myss's citations in *Sacred Contracts* — including the polytheism-vs-monotheism critique of Jung (below), which this book states far more directly than the later, more popular works.

## The Soul's Code: The Acorn Theory

*The Soul's Code* (1996) is Hillman's most sustained argument against causal psychology and for a mythic understanding of biography. Its central concept is the [Acorn Theory](../concepts/acorn-theory.md):

Every person is born with a unique *image* — the acorn — that contains the pattern of who they are destined to become. This image is not a potential waiting to be shaped by experience; it is a fully formed blueprint requiring expression, not construction. An accompanying [daimon](../concepts/daimon.md) (guardian spirit) carries this image throughout the life, working through attractions, compulsions, accidents, and decisive encounters to bring it into expression.

Hillman draws on:
- **Plato's Myth of Er** (*Republic*, Book 10): each soul selects a *paradeigma* (pattern, image) before birth, is assigned a daimon, and descends through Lethe (forgetting) into embodiment
- **Plotinus**: the daimon accompanies the soul from its lot in the invisible world into body, parents, place, and circumstances
- Cross-cultural evidence of the accompanying spirit (Roman *genius*, Christian guardian angel, Egyptian *ka/ba*, Amerindian personal spirit)

### The Parental Fallacy

Chapter 3 of *The Soul's Code* — Hillman's most polemical — demolishes the assumption that parents are the primary architects of their children's character and calling. Hillman reviews the biographies of dozens of eminent figures and finds no consistent pattern: devoted mothers, hostile mothers, absent fathers, and neglectful parents all appear in the childhoods of the eminent and the ordinary alike. The daimon *selected* the parents as the circumstances suited to the soul's image — not the reverse.

He supports this with behavioral genetics research (Plomin, Dunn) showing that shared family environment accounts for remarkably little of the variance in sibling and twin outcomes.

### Esse Percipi — Being Seen

Chapter 5 introduces the [Esse Percipi](../concepts/esse-percipi.md) principle: "to be is to be perceived." The soul's image needs a witness — a mentor, teacher, or significant other who sees the unique pattern and names it. Washington perceiving Hamilton, Roosevelt perceiving Lyndon Johnson, Darwin's schoolmaster Henslow perceiving the young naturalist, Miss Shank perceiving Elia Kazan. Perception is ontologically enabling; the mentor creates the conditions under which calling becomes visible.

### Nature Nor Nurture

Chapter 6 reviews behavioral genetics research on twins to show that neither genetics nor shared environment can account for individuality. Emergenesis (traits that emerge from unique genetic configurations rather than running in family lines) and epistasis (interaction effects between genes) suggest the irreducibility of individual configuration — the pattern that Hillman identifies as the image.

### Fate, Telos, and Accident

Chapter 9 distinguishes fate (*moira* — one's portion or image) from fatalism (everything is predetermined). Teleology gives value to events without imposing a plan. Accidents serve the image without being engineered. The Bergman case study shows how the obsessions and accidents of childhood (a circus woman on a horse, a cinematograph) serve the image of a filmmaker without being caused by a developmental sequence.

### Bad Seed

Chapter 10 confronts destructive calling. Hillman's extended analysis of Hitler argues that the demonic is the daimon stripped of the limiting conditions (eros, time, body, other) that temper calling into human form. Evil is not the absence of calling but the calling's possession by a force that recognizes no limitation.

## Relationship to Myss

Myss cites Hillman's reading of Plato's Myth of Er as the closest philosophical parallel to her Sacred Contracts framework. Both argue:
- Pre-birth soul selection of a life pattern
- A guardian/companion who carries and guides the fulfilment of that pattern
- The insistence that apparently negative life events serve the soul's development

**Where they differ**:
- Hillman's framework is psychological and imaginal; Myss's is theological and covenantal
- Hillman's daimon is morally neutral (it can produce Hitler); Myss's Sacred Contract is oriented toward divine potential
- Hillman is primarily analytical (biography-reading); Myss is primarily practical (twelve archetypal companions, wheel casting, symbolic sight)
- Hillman explicitly rejects the idea that calling is a purposively arranged divine curriculum; Myss explicitly affirms this

## Relationship to Jung

Hillman revises rather than extends Jung. Key differences:

- **Individuation**: Jung's individuation is a developmental process moving through phases toward the integration of shadow, anima/animus, and the Self. Hillman's acorn theory denies linearity — the image is present from birth and asserts itself throughout life, not in sequence.
- **The Self**: Jung's Self is an integrating archetype aiming at wholeness. Hillman's acorn is a specifying principle aiming at particularity — not wholeness but *thisness* (haecceitas).
- **Polytheism vs. monotheism**: Hillman's archetypal psychology is explicitly polytheistic — animated by multiple divine presences. Jung's individuation tends toward a single goal (the Self). Hillman argued that this was a crypto-monotheism that distorted the actual pluralism of the psyche.
