# Jeanne Achterberg

**Summary**: American psychologist, researcher, and author (1942–2012) who pioneered the scientific study of mental imagery as a healing mechanism, developed the Image CA cancer prognosis tool, and wrote the foundational synthesis linking shamanism, the history of Western medicine, and modern psychoneuroimmunology.

**Sources**:
- [Achterberg, J. (1985). *Imagery in healing: Shamanism and modern medicine*. Shambhala.](../sources/imagery-in-healing.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Biography and Institutional Context

Jeanne Achterberg was a research psychologist at the Cancer Rehabilitation Clinic, Parkland Memorial Hospital (Dallas, Texas) and at the University of Texas Health Science Center, where she collaborated with O. Carl Simonton's Cancer Counseling and Research Center in Fort Worth. Her primary research partner was psychologist and statistician G. Frank Lawlis, with whom she co-authored *Bridges of the Bodymind* (1980), which presented the statistical foundations of the Image CA research.

Achterberg later taught at Saybrook Graduate School in San Francisco, a leading institution for humanistic and transpersonal psychology. She served as president of the Association for Transpersonal Psychology and contributed to the field of integrative medicine through research, teaching, and writing. Her later works include *Woman as Healer* (1990), a historical study tracing women's healing traditions from prehistory to the present, which expanded the historical material in Chapter 2 of *Imagery in Healing*.

She died in 2012 in Hawaii, where she had relocated with her partner.

## Core Contributions

### The Image CA Tool

Achterberg's most concrete clinical contribution is the Image CA assessment — a structured 14-dimension evaluation of a cancer patient's mental imagery of their disease, their immune system, and their treatment. Developed with G. Frank Lawlis from interviews and drawings by approximately 200 cancer patients, the tool predicted two-month disease outcomes (remission, deterioration, or death) at 93% accuracy, and identified all patients who would die within two months (100% accuracy for terminal cases).

The 14 scoring dimensions assess: vividness, activity, and strength of the cancer cells; vividness, activity, and strength of the immune response; relative size and number comparisons between cancer and immune cells; the perceived strength and effectiveness of treatment; the symbolism employed; the integration and coherence of all three imagery components; the regularity of the patient's imagery practice; and the patient's own prognosis prediction.

Achterberg distinguishes the Image CA from Simonton-style visualisation: Image CA is a *diagnostic* tool, not a treatment. It reads the patient's implicit beliefs and expectations about their illness, which then predict how the disease will progress. The therapeutic implication is that changing those images may change the outcome — but the causal pathway is not proven by the predictive correlation alone.

See [Mental Imagery and Healing](../concepts/mental-imagery-healing.md) for full detail.

### Typology of Imagery

Achterberg's framework distinguishes two fundamentally different mechanisms claimed for healing imagery:

**Type 1 (Self-directed imagery)**: The patient's own imagination influences their own body — through neurological, endocrine, and immune pathways now partially mapped by psychoneuroimmunology. The mechanism is intra-personal and physiologically explicable. Most clinical research (biofeedback, hypnosis, relaxation, Simonton visualisation) operates in this domain.

**Type 2 (Transpersonal imagery)**: The assumption that consciousness or healing intention can be transmitted from one person to another without physical mediation — the domain of shamanic healing, intercessory prayer, and remote healing (e.g., distant diagnosis as practised by medical intuitives such as [Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md)). Achterberg neither endorses nor dismisses this claim; she argues it requires different evidence and different explanatory frameworks. Her own transpersonal healing research — published after *Imagery in Healing* — involved attempting to predict when a healer 4,000 miles away was sending healing intention, using fMRI to detect correlated brain activations in the recipient.

### Shamanism as First Medicine

Achterberg places shamanism at the origin of all healing that uses imagination. She argues that shamanic practice (20,000+ years of evidence) establishes the anthropological baseline: across cultures with no documented contact, healers enter altered states of consciousness, access non-ordinary information, and return with specific healing knowledge. This universality suggests shamanic practice taps a real human capacity rather than cultural accident.

See [Shamanic Healing](../concepts/shamanic-healing.md).

### History of Women Healers

Achterberg's historical narrative (developed further in *Woman as Healer*, 1990) argues that the witch trials of 1500–1650 represent the systematic suppression of an imagination-based healing tradition carried by women. The practitioners who were persecuted were the direct successors of the shamanic tradition — herbalists, midwives, and ritual healers who combined plant medicine with incantation and imagery. Their elimination created the conditions for the exclusively mechanistic-reductive model of medicine that emerged from the male physician guilds and eventually became the biomedical paradigm.

## Key Collaborators

**G. Frank Lawlis**: Psychologist and statistician; co-developer of the Image CA scoring system; co-author of *Bridges of the Bodymind* (1980). His statistical rigour provided the methodological credibility for the imagery-outcome correlations.

**[O. Carl Simonton](o-carl-simonton.md)**: Radiation oncologist whose cancer visualisation programme provided the clinical context in which Achterberg developed the Image CA. Simonton's Cancer Counseling and Research Center was one of the primary research sites.

**[Norman Shealy](norman-shealy.md)**: Neurosurgeon and holistic medicine pioneer who provided institutional support and shared the frameworks linking imagery, consciousness, and healing.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers in This Wiki

- **[Lawrence LeShan](lawrence-leshan.md)**: LeShan's "reality 2" (the consciousness mode used by mystic healers) is Achterberg's secular equivalent of the Shamanic State of Consciousness. Both are describing the same altered-state framework from different directions.
- **[Bernie Siegel](bernie-siegel.md)**: Siegel cites Achterberg and Lawlis's Image CA findings in *Love, Medicine and Miracles* — specifically the accuracy figure (he cites 95%; the primary source says 93% for remission prediction, 100% for death prediction). Achterberg's research is the empirical foundation for Siegel's clinical intuitions about imagery and outcome.
- **[Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md)**: Achterberg's Type 2 (transpersonal) imagery and Myss's medical intuition are parallel phenomena — both involve accessing diagnostic information about a patient through non-sensory means. Achterberg provides the scientific framework within which Myss's work can be evaluated.
- **[Norman Shealy](norman-shealy.md)**: Shealy and Myss's collaboration on medical intuition is the clinical practice; Achterberg's Image CA is the laboratory research version of the same question: can inner information reveal disease state?
- **[W. Brugh Joy](w-brugh-joy.md)**: Joy's "Command Therapy" and Achterberg's therapeutic imagery both treat the body as responsive to mental commands — Joy using direct organ-addressed commands, Achterberg using symbolic imagery of immune response.
- **[Louise Hay](louise-hay.md)**: Hay's affirmation practice is an implicit imagery intervention — repetitive belief change through verbal-visual association — operating at the same level (body-mind communication via inner representation) as Achterberg's work, without the clinical research framework.
