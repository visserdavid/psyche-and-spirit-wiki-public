# Joan Borysenko

**Summary**: American cell biologist and psychologist (b. c. 1945); co-founder of the Mind/Body Clinic at Harvard's Beth Israel Hospital; pioneer of psychoneuroimmunology in clinical practice; author of *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* (1987); bridges laboratory immunology, meditation, and spiritual psychology.

## Biography

Joan Borysenko, PhD, graduated from Bryn Mawr College and joined Harvard Medical School as a research assistant in 1968, studying effects of stress on blood pressure in monkeys with [Herbert Benson](herbert-benson.md). She subsequently pursued a research career in cancer cell biology, completing a fellowship in Experimental Pathology and working in the Department of Anatomy and Cellular Biology at Tufts Medical School.

Her own health during this period was poor: she suffered from migraines, stomach pains, recurring bronchitis, and colitis — stress-related conditions she managed unsuccessfully with medications. A chance introduction to meditation during her graduate years transformed her experience: a migraine that would have lasted days dissolved in twenty minutes of her first meditation session. This personal discovery redirected her career.

When Benson invited her to join his Behavioral Medicine unit at Boston's Beth Israel Hospital (c. 1978), she shifted from laboratory cancer research to mind-body medicine. In September 1981 she co-founded the Mind/Body Clinic with psychiatrist Ilan Kutz, MD. She became its director and, over the following years, developed the ten-week Mind/Body Program that *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* documents.

Her husband, Dr. Myrin Borysenko, is an immunologist whose collaborative research on stress and antibody levels provided key data for the book's psychoneuroimmunology (PNI) framework.

## Guilt Is the Teacher, Love Is the Lesson (1990)

*Guilt Is the Teacher, Love Is the Lesson* is Borysenko's second major book. Where *Minding the Body* provided a clinical programme for stress-related illness (meditation, breathing, mindfulness), this book investigates the deeper source of that stress: [guilt and soul sickness](../concepts/guilt-and-soul-sickness.md) — the shame-based personality that generates chronic fear-based thinking and the physiological suffering it produces.

Borysenko introduces William James's term "soul sickness" as the name for the syndrome, distinguishes healthy guilt (conscience: "I made a mistake") from unhealthy shame identity ("I am a mistake"), and documents twenty-one expressions of the guilt-driven personality. The book traces the origins of shame to the broken [interpersonal bridges](../concepts/inner-child.md) of childhood — conditions under which the [Natural Child](../concepts/inner-child.md) is progressively covered by masks (the false self) and the accumulating [shadow bag](../concepts/shadow.md) (Robert Bly's concept).

Part Two addresses the spiritual dimension: the need for a reconnection to the Self and to Spirit that psychological work alone cannot provide. Borysenko draws on Andrew Greeley's survey data documenting millions of Americans as "[closet mystics](../concepts/closet-mystics.md)" and distinguishes [spiritual optimism](../concepts/spiritual-optimism.md) (life as blessing, growth, and return to love) from spiritual pessimism (life as punishment). Matthew Fox's *Via Positiva*, the Prodigal Son parable, and Gnostic wisdom (the Gospel of Thomas) provide the optimistic framework.

Part Three develops [forgiveness](../concepts/forgiveness.md) as the culminating practice: a six-step process for forgiving oneself and another six for forgiving others, grounded in Joseph Campbell's reading of the Grail legend (compassion as "the flower of psychospiritual growth"). Relationships are examined as mirrors and teachers — the locus where shadow projections become visible and the forgiveness work becomes concrete.

## Fire in the Soul (1993)

*Fire in the Soul: A New Psychology of Spiritual Optimism* is Borysenko's third major book and the culmination of her psychospiritual project. Where the first two books addressed how to live more healthily and how to heal the shame-based personality, this book faces the most fundamental challenge: Why do bad things happen, and how is suffering transformed rather than merely endured?

The book opens through two patients — **Leslie** (cancer survivor, faith-based equanimity) and **Jay** (AIDS, fear-based religious guilt) — as archetypes of the two orientations toward crisis. Through Job's story (Stephen Mitchell's translation), the [stages of faith](../concepts/stages-of-faith.md) (James Fowler), and Viktor Frankl's logotherapy, Borysenko argues that the difference between suffering that destroys and suffering that transforms is **meaning** — and that the framework determining whether one finds meaning is the central clinical and spiritual variable.

Part Two extends the fear-and-healing work into territory that earlier books approached only obliquely: trauma and PTSD (Stanislav Grof's four perinatal stages; the Holocaust survivor Ka-Tzetnik 135633's LSD therapy in Leiden); [past-life therapy](../concepts/past-life-therapy.md) and the imaginal realm (Henry Corbin); karma cleaning (Elizabeth Lawrence's Mari El healing; samskaras); and a deepened forgiveness framework that recovers the Aramaic depth of Jesus's teaching (Neil Douglas-Klotz's *Prayers of the Cosmos*) and extends forgiveness across apparent lifetimes.

Part Three develops the practitioner's toolkit: [centering prayer](../concepts/centering-prayer.md) (Thomas Keating's river of presence), loving-kindness meditation, walking meditation (Thich Nhat Hanh), Byrd's CCU prayer study and Spindrift's non-directed prayer research, ten survival tips for crisis, and a chapter of poems and prayers as touchstones of hope.

The book's signature contributions: the [crisis-as-initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) framework (Victor Turner's liminality, the mwadi as threshold dweller), the three stages of courage (willful, psychological, spiritual — including the Stage 3 account of Borysenko's healing relationship with her dying mother, March 1988), and the rich NDE research (Donna Elsten's full account; Melvin Morse's children's NDE studies) as evidence that the universe is a loving place.

## Intellectual Position

Borysenko's work sits at the intersection of three traditions:

**Laboratory immunology**: Her training as a cell biologist gives her precise command of the psychoneuroimmunological mechanisms — stress hormones, neuropeptides, T-lymphocytes, natural killer cells — that mediate between psychological states and physical health. This distinguishes her from practitioners who invoke mind-body connections in vaguer terms.

**Clinical practice**: The Mind/Body Clinic provided 2,000 patient cases before *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* was published. The ten-week program — meditation, breathing, mindfulness, reframing, forgiveness — was field-tested as a clinical intervention, not merely theorised.

**Spiritual psychology**: Borysenko integrates insights from Buddhist meditation (Thich Nhat Hanh, Jon Kabat-Zinn), Hindu teaching (Siddha Yoga; Swami Muktananda), and Christian and Jewish traditions into a framework she calls the Inner Self — the stable, loving core of the person that is accessible through meditation and that constitutes the ground of genuine healing. Ram Dass / Richard Alpert is quoted as a guide to mindfulness practice.

Her distinctive contribution is making PNI clinically operational: not merely demonstrating that stress suppresses immunity (Ader, Cohen) but designing a teachable program that counteracts that suppression through specific practices.

## The Mind/Body Clinic Program

The ten-week program Borysenko developed with Ilan Kutz and Stephen Maurer at Beth Israel covers:

1. The science of healing — PNI and the relaxation response
2. Meditation instruction — learning to elicit the [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md)
3. Breathing and stretching exercises — the anxiety cycle-breakers
4. [Mindfulness](../concepts/mindfulness.md) — present-moment awareness and the Witness stance
5. Mind traps — identifying awfulizing, control-need, and blame patterns
6. Reframing — using creative imagination and affirmations to shift habitual thinking
7. Healing emotions — completing unfinished business, practicing forgiveness
8. Integration — the Twelve Brief Reminders

The program aims at attitude change as a physiological intervention: because the body cannot distinguish imagined threats from real ones (imagining a lemon causes real salivation), shifting cognitive and emotional patterns produces measurable changes in immune function and stress hormones.

## Healing and Sam's Story

The book's most powerful section — Sam's Story — narrates Borysenko's year-long relationship with a physician dying of AIDS in 1983. Sam was not cured. He died. But through meditation, forgiveness, and connection to his deepest self, he achieved what Borysenko defines as healing: a state of wholeness, peace, and genuine presence. On his final day, Sam led a spontaneous group meditation from his hospital bed, calling each person to speak from the heart. His family later cast in gold the yantra (geometric symbol of the Self) Borysenko had given him.

Sam's story defines the [healing vs. curing](../concepts/healing-vs-curing.md) distinction that organises the whole book. The goal of the Mind/Body program is healing in this sense — not the elimination of disease but the restoration of the person's connection to their innermost nature.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers in the Wiki

**Herbert Benson**: Mentor and director. Benson's discovery of the relaxation response provided the scientific and clinical foundation for Borysenko's work. She regards him as the person who made clinical mind-body medicine possible at a major research hospital.

**[Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md)**: Significant convergence, though they work in different registers. Myss reads the energy field to identify the biographical source of illness; Borysenko uses PNI research to explain how that biography becomes physiology. Both articulate [biography is biology](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md). Both emphasise that 75% of illness is stress-related. Both distinguish healing (wholeness) from curing (disease removal).

**[Norman Shealy](norman-shealy.md)**: Parallel trajectory. Shealy and Borysenko both work from mainstream medical training toward integrating mind, body, and spirit. Shealy's [Electromagnetic Dysthymia](../concepts/electromagnetic-dysthymia.md) concept and his [attitude and health](../concepts/attitude-and-health.md) work overlap closely with Borysenko's stress-immunity framework.

**[Rachel Naomi Remen](rachel-naomi-remen.md)**: Listed among valued colleagues in Borysenko's acknowledgments (as "Naomi Remen M.D."). Both work in the intersection of medicine, meaning, and healing.

**[Candace Pert](candace-pert.md)**: Pert's neuropeptide research is the molecular mechanism underlying the PNI framework Borysenko uses. Borysenko invokes neuropeptides explicitly as the messengers linking mind and immune system.

## Critique

**Scientific rigour**: The PNI framework Borysenko uses is well-supported. The relaxation response is documented, replicable, and now widely accepted. These aspects of her work have solid empirical grounding.

**Clinical evidence**: The Mind/Body Clinic's results — symptom reduction, decreased anxiety and depression — are reported as clinical observations rather than randomised controlled trials. Symptom improvement in an uncontrolled program could reflect natural disease course, regression to the mean, or placebo effects alongside genuine technique-specific effects.

**Spiritual claims**: The Inner Self, the healing power of love and forgiveness, and the meaning drawn from near-death experience research (Kenneth Ring) are presented as clinical observations and resonant frameworks, not as empirically established causal claims. Borysenko is consistently careful to distinguish what the research shows from what her clinical experience suggests.
