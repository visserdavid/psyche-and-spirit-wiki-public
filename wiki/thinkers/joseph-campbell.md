# Joseph Campbell

**Summary**: American mythologist (1904–1987) whose magnum opus *The Mythic Image* provides the most sustained comparative psychological reading of the seven-chakra system in the English language, arguing that myths are public dreams arising from the same unconscious source as private dreaming; his hero's journey monomyth is the structural backbone of Myss's chakra model and Sacred Contracts framework.

## Overview

Joseph Campbell (1904–1987) was an American professor of comparative mythology at Sarah Lawrence College, best known for *[The Hero with a Thousand Faces](../sources/the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md)* (1949), which identified a universal narrative structure — the **monomyth** or **hero's journey** — across world mythologies. His work synthesizes mythological traditions from across cultures to reveal common patterns of initiation, death, and rebirth.

Key works: *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949), *The Masks of God* (4 vols., 1959–1968), *The Mythic Image* (1974), and the posthumous *The Power of Myth* (with Bill Moyers, 1988). *The Mythic Image* — the Bollingen Series C volume, ten years in preparation — is considered by many scholars his deepest and most original work, but *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* remains his most widely read and influential book, and the direct textual source of the monomyth schema *The Mythic Image* and later works build on. See [Hero Myth](../concepts/hero-myth.md) for the wiki's full account of the seventeen-stage schema, and [World Navel](../concepts/world-navel.md) for Campbell's account of the fixed cosmic center anchoring the journey.

## Biography

Campbell earned a master's degree in medieval literature at Columbia University, focused on the Old French and Old German Arthurian romances, then went to study at the University of Paris (Sorbonne) and the University of Munich from 1927. There he encountered both the mythically-inflected modernist art of James Joyce, Thomas Mann, Picasso, and Paul Klee, and the "revolutionary psychological teachings" of Freud and Jung — an exposure he later credited as the source of his own formula "myths are public dreams, dreams are private myths." He returned to Columbia in 1929 intending to continue his doctorate but abandoned it after his advisers refused to approve a dissertation spanning Arthurian legend, modernist literature, and depth psychology together; the Wall Street Crash arrived the same year, and he spent much of the Depression unemployed, reading independently across mythology, philosophy, and comparative religion. He joined the faculty of Sarah Lawrence College in 1934 and taught there until retiring in 1972.

*The Hero with a Thousand Faces* originated when a New York publisher asked Campbell to compile a modern collection of world myths "like Bulfinch's Mythology"; Campbell instead spent five years developing the single recurring hero-pattern he found running through Sumerian epic, Pacific and Siberian folktale, the lives of Buddha and Jesus, and modern psychiatric case material — the book that resulted, in his own account, was "a book about how to read a myth" rather than a myth collection itself. George Lucas has described discovering *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* mid-way through drafting the original *Star Wars* screenplay as the moment he first recognized the pattern he had already been working from intuitively.

## The Mythic Image (1974)

*The Mythic Image* (Princeton University Press, 1974; Bollingen Series C) is a 572-page illustrated work arguing that myths are public dreams and dreams are private myths — both arising from the same unconscious source. Across six parts it moves from the outer world of mythological imagery (the universe as Vishnu's dream) through cosmic order (world mountain, calendric cycles, the number 432,000) and the lotus/rose symbolism of East and West, to a detailed psychological reading of the seven-chakra system of Kundalini Yoga, the universal sacrifice archetype, and the final theme of waking from the dream of multiplicity.

### The Central Thesis: Myths as Dreams

Campbell's central claim: the universe is the dream of a divine dreamer (Vishnu on the cosmic ocean), and all human mythological imagery is the waking mind's attempt to remain in contact with that deeper consciousness. See [Myths as Dreams](../concepts/myths-as-dreams.md).

### Maya's Three Powers

The Vedantic teaching on maya — Veiling Power (hiding the Self), Projecting Power (generating apparent multiplicity), and Revealing Power (the function of art, scripture, and meditation) — is central to the book's argument. The Goddess (Lakshmi, Tara, the Madonna) is maya's Revealing Power in embodied form. See [Maya: Three Powers](../concepts/maya-three-powers.md).

### The Chakra System as Psychological Map

Part IV of *The Mythic Image* is the most technically detailed section and Campbell's most original contribution: a comparative psychological reading of the seven chakras as seven modes of motivation, using Sri Ramakrishna's first-person Kundalini accounts alongside Western art (Dante's *La Vita Nuova* at chakra 4, the razor's edge of Vishuddha, Blake's Job plates as third-chakra god-image, Eckhart's mystical union as Sahasrara). See [Chakra Psychology (Campbell)](../concepts/chakra-psychology-campbell.md).

The caduceus — two serpents entwined around a central staff — is shown to appear identically on Sumerian vessels (2000 BC), Indus Valley seals, Mesoamerican monuments, and in Greek Hermes/Asklepios imagery; Campbell identifies it as the universal symbol of ida and pingala coiling around sushumna.

### The Dying-and-Rising God

Part V surveys the dying-and-rising god across Osiris/Horus (Egypt), Tammuz (Mesopotamia), Adonis/Attis (Syria/Phrygia), Dionysus (Greece), Krishna/Vishnu-as-boar (India), and Christ (Christianity). The key insight: in each case the boar or slaying agent is the god's own shadow — the unredeemed animal power of the divine turning against its personal form. See [Dying-and-Rising God](../concepts/dying-and-rising-god.md).

### Waking

The book closes with the myth of the Buddha's death (eating "succulent boar flesh" from Chunda the blacksmith — the dying-god archetype completing in Buddhist form), the Fire Sermon (all things are on fire with passion, hatred, and infatuation — the three veiling forces), and the insight that the goal of all mythology is not the hero's victory but self-divestiture — the voluntary sacrifice that wakes the dreamer from the dream.

## Significance in Myss's Framework

[Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md) draws on Campbell in two ways:

### 1. The Hero's Journey as Chakra Map
Myss explicitly identifies the seven-chakra system as a map of the hero's journey in Campbell's sense. Each chakra represents a stage of initiation in which the individual confronts specific challenges, integrates specific lessons, and advances from tribal/group-defined consciousness toward individual spiritual authority. The journey is not merely narrative but biological — challenges that are not met show up in the body as illness or energy depletion.

### 2. *The Mythic Image* as Source
Myss cites Campbell's *The Mythic Image* (Princeton University Press, 1974) as one of the accepted interpretations of the chakra system's seven power centres. The book's visual and mythological mapping of chakra symbolism provides a scholarly grounding for her energy model.

### 3. The Hero's Journey in *Sacred Contracts*

In [*Sacred Contracts*](../sources/sacred-contracts.md), Myss explicitly maps the hero's journey onto the five stages of fulfilling a Sacred Contract. The call to adventure, the crossing of the threshold, the ordeal, and the return all reappear as stages in how individuals discover their archetypal companions and live out their soul-level purpose. Campbell's monomyth thus provides the narrative backbone for Myss's understanding of spiritual biography.

## Campbell's Own Framework

Campbell was not a healer or medical intuitive; his concerns were academic and literary. His concept of the hero's journey is about psychological and spiritual transformation, not physical health per se. The application of his framework to illness and energy medicine is Myss's own innovation.

## Critique

Campbell's comparative method is itself directly descended from [James George Frazer](james-george-frazer.md)'s *[The Golden Bough](../sources/the-golden-bough.md)* — the same critique leveled at Frazer's "armchair" synthesis of decontextualized customs into a single evolutionary pattern applies, in modified form, to Campbell's monomyth. Campbell's monomyth has been criticized by scholars for over-universalizing: forcing diverse cultural narratives into a single Western-shaped template can erase important differences. Feminist critics (including some within the holistic health movement) have noted that the hero's journey privileges a masculine archetype of individual adventure and return. Myss's framework partly addresses this by incorporating feminine archetypes (Shekhinah, the partnership chakra's emphasis on relationship), but the underlying Campbell model remains oriented toward individual spiritual heroism.
