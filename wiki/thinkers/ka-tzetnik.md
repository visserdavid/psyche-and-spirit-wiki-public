# Ka-Tzetnik 135633 (Yehiel De Nur)

**Summary**: Polish-born Holocaust survivor and author (1909–2001); Auschwitz prisoner number 135633; wrote under the pen name Ka-Tzetnik 135633 (*Ka-Tzetnik* = concentration camp inmate in camp slang); his five LSD therapy sessions with Professor Jan Bastiaans at Leiden — documented in *Shivitti: A Vision* (1989) — exemplify Borysenko's and Stanislav Grof's frameworks of trauma as initiation and the birth-rebirth passage.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Biography

Yehiel De Nur (also spelled Yehiel Dinur) was born in Sosnowiec, Poland, in 1909. He was deported to Auschwitz in 1943 and survived until the camp's liberation in 1945; he was interned under prisoner number 135633. His parents and family were murdered. He emigrated to Palestine (later Israel) after the war.

De Nur took the pen name **Ka-Tzetnik 135633** — *Ka-Tzetnik* being camp slang for a concentration camp prisoner — and refused to use his own name in his published work, referring to himself in the third person. He described Auschwitz as "another planet": a place so outside human experience that no ordinary language could describe it. His testimony at the Adolf Eichmann trial in Jerusalem in 1961 became internationally known; he collapsed on the witness stand after speaking for several minutes.

De Nur died in Tel Aviv on July 17, 2001.

## Works

His major works, all written under Ka-Tzetnik 135633, include:
- *Salamandra* (1946, later published in English as *House of Dolls*) — documenting the women's forced brothel at Auschwitz
- *Clock Overhead* (*Stella Maris*, 1953)
- *Shivitti: A Vision* (Harper & Row, 1989) — the account of his five LSD therapy sessions

## The LSD Therapy with Bastiaans

After decades of untreated PTSD (insomnia, nightmares, inability to write in the first person, living as though he were not a subject of his own life), De Nur underwent five sessions of LSD therapy with Professor **Jan Bastiaans** at Leiden University in the Netherlands. Bastiaans had used LSD therapeutically with Holocaust survivors and former political prisoners since the 1960s.

In the sessions, De Nur relived the Auschwitz experience at cellular depth — not as a narrative memory but as a re-embodied reality. The crucial turning point in his final session: he encountered the divine name — "Shivitti Adonai l'negdi tamid" ("I set the Lord before me always," Psalm 16:8) — superimposed on the Auschwitz horizon. The S.S. officer and the prisoner were both revealed as participants in a mystery that transcended their individual roles. He experienced what [Stanislav Grof](stanislav-grof.md) describes as Stage 4 of the perinatal process: complete ego death, then a vision of his mother's soul rising from the crematorium flames as a phoenix — the salamander that lives in fire for seven years and is reborn. He wept for the first time since liberation.

The therapy produced a transformation in his writing and selfhood: he was able to write in the first person for the first time and to return his manuscript to Auschwitz as a witness rather than a traumatised prisoner.

## Significance for the Wiki's Frameworks

**[Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)** reads Ka-Tzetnik's case as the most extreme instance of [crisis as initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md): if the initiation framework holds even in the context of Auschwitz, it holds everywhere. The phoenix vision — the salamander reborn from seven years in fire — is the book's central image of what the soul can do with the worst that existence can offer.

**[Grof](stanislav-grof.md)'s framework** maps exactly: the pre-therapy state (locked in Stage 2 No-Exit, living as a third-person object) corresponds to the claustrophobic perinatal matrix; the therapy moved him through Stage 3 (death-rebirth struggle in the sessions) to Stage 4 (ego death and liberation, the phoenix vision).

**The name *Shivitti***: the Hebrew word that became the title of the memoir is from Psalm 16:8 — the act of placing God's presence before one at every moment, including inside the death camp. The book argues that this presence was real and available even there.

## Critique

**Methodological questions about LSD therapy**: Jan Bastiaans's work with LSD was clinically pioneering but methodologically informal. The therapeutic mechanism is not established; the outcomes in individual cases — however transformative — do not constitute evidence that can be systematised.

**The risk of universalising**: Using Ka-Tzetnik's transformation as evidence for the general "crisis as initiation" framework risks trivialising ordinary crisis by implying it is the same kind of thing as surviving Auschwitz — and risks trivialising Auschwitz by fitting it into a redemptive narrative. Borysenko handles this with care but the tension remains.
