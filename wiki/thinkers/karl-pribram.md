# Karl Pribram

**Summary**: American neurophysiologist (1919–2015), professor at Stanford University; author of *Languages of the Brain* (1971); proposed the holonomic brain model — that the brain uses Fourier (holographic) mathematics to store and process information in a distributed way — providing the neuroscience half of the holographic paradigm that Talbot and Brennan build upon.

## Biography

Karl H. Pribram was born in Vienna, Austria, in 1919, and emigrated to the United States. He trained in neurosurgery and worked with Karl Lashley at the Yerkes Laboratory of Primate Biology in Orange Park, Florida. Lashley's thirty-year search for memory engrams — specific locations in brain tissue where memories are stored — had produced puzzling results: no matter how much cortex he removed from trained rats, their memories persisted. Pribram witnessed these results and began seeking an explanation.

In the early 1960s he worked at Yale, where his clinical observations confirmed that patients who lost portions of their brains did not lose corresponding portions of their memories. When he read an article in *Scientific American* describing the construction of holograms in the mid-1960s, the solution crystallised.

He became professor of neuropsychology at Stanford, later at Georgetown and other universities. His commitment to opposing the standard neurosurgical practice of lobotomy cost him a job at Yale in the early 1950s — before he was established enough for his convictions to have currency.

## The Holonomic Brain Model

Pribram's key insight: the brain processes information not by one-to-one correspondence (one input → one output location) but by distributed transformation, using the mathematical principles of holography.

**The puzzle**: Karl Lashley's experiments showed that removing up to 90% of a rat's cortex did not abolish specific memories — it merely degraded them. Visual cortex excision studies showed that removing 98% of optic nerve fibres in cats did not prevent task performance. This was inexplicable if memories or sensory functions were stored in specific locations.

**The holographic solution**: In a hologram, information is stored as an interference pattern distributed across the entire plate; any piece of the plate can reconstruct the whole image. If the brain stored information in a similar distributed way — encoded as interference patterns across large swaths of neural tissue — then partial destruction would reduce quality without eliminating specific content.

**Fourier transforms**: When DeValois and DeValois (Berkeley, 1979) showed that visual cortex cells respond not to patterns but to the Fourier transforms (wave-form representations) of those patterns, Pribram's model was confirmed. The brain uses the same mathematics Dennis Gabor used to invent holography — converting images into frequency-domain representations and back again.

**The frequency domain**: Pribram proposed that underlying the world of objects and events is a "frequency domain" — a realm of interfering waves that the brain converts into the apparently solid world of experience. Reality as perceived is the brain's holographic projection; strip away the brain's processing and you would encounter a blur of interfering frequencies. This is the neuroscientific basis for the claim that "reality" is a holographic construct.

**What the holonomic brain explains**:
- Vast memory storage (up to 10^20 bits in a structure the size of a skull)
- Associative memory (scents and sounds triggering complete scene reconstructions)
- Invariant recognition of faces across years and angles
- Phantom limb pain (the limb's hologram persists in the field even after amputation)
- Photographic memory (access to larger regions of holographic storage)
- Learned skill transfer (motor patterns encoded as Fourier wave forms)

## Pribram and Bohm Together

[Bohm](david-bohm.md) cites Pribram's holographic memory research directly in *[Wholeness and the Implicate Order](../sources/wholeness-and-the-implicate-order.md)* (Chapter 7), treating it as neuroscientific evidence for extending the implicate order into consciousness.

Pribram's observation, in the 1970s, of Bohm's implicate order deepened his model. If the brain is a hologram, and the universe itself is holographic (Bohm), then brain and universe share the same deep structure. Reality as experienced = brain's holographic decoding of the implicate-order frequency domain. Each perceiving system (brain) is a hologram within the greater hologram (universe).

Pribram summarised: "The brain constructs reality from frequencies. Take away the lenses — the brain's holographic processing — and you are left with the interference pattern of the frequency domain itself."

## Critique

**Model vs. mechanism**: The holonomic brain model explains the distribution of memory but does not provide a detailed cellular mechanism for holographic encoding and retrieval. It remains a mathematical analogy rather than a complete biological theory.

**Mainstream neuroscience**: While Pribram's distributed-memory insights were confirmed and his Fourier analysis observations are accepted, the full holographic interpretation is considered speculative by most neuroscientists.
