# Kenneth Ring

**Summary**: American psychologist and NDE researcher (b. 1936); professor of psychology at the University of Connecticut; co-founder and first president of the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS); author of *Life at Death* (1980) and *Heading Toward Omega* (1984) — the two books that established NDE research as a scientific field.

**Sources**:
- [Ring, K. (1984). *Heading toward omega: In search of the meaning of the near-death experience*. William Morrow.](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Biography

Kenneth Ring, PhD, is Professor Emeritus of Psychology at the University of Connecticut in Storrs. He was introduced to Raymond Moody's *Life After Life* (1975) and recognised that the NDE phenomenon demanded systematic scientific investigation. In 1977 he began interviewing near-death survivors, producing the most rigorous early study of the phenomenon.

His first book, *Life at Death* (1980), established the empirical reality of the NDE across a controlled sample of 102 cases, confirming and quantifying Moody's observations: approximately 48% of near-death survivors reported NDEs, and the experiences tended to unfold in five stages (peace, body separation, entering darkness, seeing light, entering light). The book was published by Coward, McCann and Geoghegan.

In December 1980 Ring co-founded the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS), located at the University of Connecticut, and served as its first president. IANDS became the primary research infrastructure for NDE study worldwide, maintaining archives, publishing the journal *Anabiosis*, and coordinating research internationally.

Ring's home in Connecticut — dubbed the "Near Death Hotel" by his partner Norma Kraenzle — became a gathering place for NDErs, researchers, and scholars. The hundreds of in-depth conversations conducted there over six years formed the qualitative foundation of *Heading Toward Omega* (1984).

**Connection to Caroline Myss**: Ring explicitly acknowledges in *Heading Toward Omega*: "I would also like to thank Caroline Myss for reading the manuscript and for her criticisms." He also cites Myss and Lee Sannella in his endnotes, noting their shared observation that "transformation of consciousness does not guarantee that an individual will become a saint, but it does afford one an increased opportunity for growth." Ring and Myss were therefore in direct professional dialogue about the nature and limits of transformative experience in the early 1980s.

**Connection to Elisabeth Kübler-Ross**: Kübler-Ross wrote the foreword to *Heading Toward Omega*, placing Ring's work within her own decades of research on death and dying.

## Life at Death (1980)

Ring's first book established the core empirical facts:
- NDE is a real, reproducible phenomenon (not confabulation or wish-fulfilment)
- ~48% of near-death survivors report NDEs (broadly consistent with Sabom's 42% and Gallup's 35% figures)
- The invariance hypothesis: neither the circumstances of near-death (illness, accident, suicide attempt) nor the person's demographic background (age, sex, religion, race) predict whether or to what degree an NDE occurs
- Five stages of the core NDE: peace, body separation, entering darkness, seeing light, entering light (not all stages occur in every case; intensity varies)

The book confirmed Raymond Moody's observations with a more rigorous methodology and established NDE research as a legitimate scientific inquiry.

## Heading Toward Omega (1984)

Ring's second and more ambitious work — see the [source page](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md) for detailed documentation. Three major contributions:

### 1. Systematic Aftereffects Research (the Omega Study)

Using the Life Changes Questionnaire (LCQ), Behavior Rating Inventory (BRI), Psychic Experience Inventory (PEI), and Religious Beliefs Inventory (RBI), Ring documented consistent and corroborated changes following NDEs:

- **Value shifts**: appreciation for life and others increases; materialism and concern with social approval decrease; quest for meaning and consciousness intensifies
- **Spiritual universalism**: NDErs shift toward a post-denominational, universalist spirituality — felt closeness to God, deemphasis of formal religion, openness to reincarnation, belief in the unity of all religions; 49% of NDErs score as "universalism shifters" vs 15% of those with no death experience
- **Psychic development**: 58% of NDErs in the Omega sample report increases in psi phenomena; confirmed by Greyson's independent study of 80 IANDS NDErs (p < .0001) and Kohr's ARE study

### 2. The Kundalini Hypothesis

Ring proposes that the NDE is biologically mediated by the release of kundalini energy — the dormant bioenergy described in Hindu-Tantric traditions. Post-NDE symptoms in Omega subjects (heat sensations, energy movement up the spine, light phenomena, reduced sleep need, spontaneous healing capacities) parallel clinical descriptions of kundalini awakening. Ring proposes that disruption of breathing during near-death events may trigger a kundalini response. See [Kundalini](../concepts/kundalini.md) and [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md).

### 3. The Omega Hypothesis

Ring argues that NDEs are catalysts in humanity's evolution toward Teilhard de Chardin's Omega Point — a convergent state of heightened consciousness and love. The 8 million American NDErs (Gallup, 1982) represent what John White calls "Homo noeticus" — prototypes of an evolving form of human consciousness. Rupert Sheldrake's morphic resonance provides the mechanism by which a critical mass of transformed individuals could precipitate species-wide change.

## Theoretical Commitments

Ring approaches NDE research from a position of scientific humility: he acknowledges methodological limitations throughout his work, presents findings as hypotheses requiring controlled replication, and distinguishes between what the data shows and what he believes it implies. He explicitly states the Omega hypothesis is not proven by his data but represents "the most plausible interpretation" available.

At the same time, Ring is willing to follow the data where it leads, even when the implications are unconventional. His engagement with kundalini, psychic phenomena, planetary visions, and evolutionary spirituality distinguishes him from more conservative NDE researchers (e.g., Michael Sabom, who focused on verifying NDE perceptions against medical records).

## Key Findings Summary

| Finding | Method | Result |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Value shifts post-NDE | LCQ (26 NDErs) | Consistent increases in appreciation for life, compassion; decreases in materialism, status concern |
| Third-party corroboration | BRI (15+ raters) | Modal 8/11 items confirmed by observers |
| Psychic development | PEI (26 NDErs) | 58% report increase; confirmed by Greyson (p < .0001) and Kohr |
| Spiritual universalism shift | RBI (172 respondents) | 49% NDEr universalism shifters vs 15% controls; χ² p < .0005 |
| Belief in God post-NDE | RBI item 9 | 94% NDErs (non-mainline Christian) agree; 48.5% non-death controls |
| Belief in life after death | RBI item 3 | 90%+ of non-mainline Christian NDErs agree |

## Critique and Scholarly Reception

Ring's work helped legitimise NDE research within academia and led to IANDS's establishment as an institutional home for the field. His *Life at Death* and *Heading Toward Omega* remain foundational references.

Critics point to the methodological limitations Ring himself acknowledges (self-selected samples, absence of controls) and note that his theoretical extensions — kundalini, evolutionary consciousness, morphic resonance — move beyond what the data warrants into what is properly metaphysical speculation. The planetary visions chapter in particular has been the most contested part of *Heading Toward Omega*, partly because the specific predictions (upheaval by 1988) did not occur as described.

Ring's response is that the visions should be understood probabilistically — as statements about possible futures, not fixed destinies — and that the meaning of NDEs ultimately exceeds what any single study can prove or disprove.
