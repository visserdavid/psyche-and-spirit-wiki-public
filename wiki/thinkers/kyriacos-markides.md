# Kyriacos Markides

**Summary**: Cyprus-born American sociologist (University of Maine) whose participant-observer trilogy on the Cypriot mystic Daskalos — beginning with *The Magus of Strovolos* (1985) — became an influential ethnographic bridge between academic sociology and popular literature on esoteric healing and spiritual teaching.

**Sources**:
- [Markides, K. C. (1985). *The magus of Strovolos: The extraordinary world of a spiritual healer*. Routledge & Kegan Paul.](../sources/magus-of-strovolos.md)

## Biography

Kyriacos C. Markides was born and raised in Cyprus, earned his Ph.D. from Wayne State University in 1970, and began teaching sociology at the University of Maine, Orono, in 1972. His early academic work was in the sociology of Cyprus and Greek Cypriot social structures. Beginning with a 1978 sabbatical field trip to Cyprus intended to research folk healing, he became a student of the healer known as [Daskalos](daskalos.md) (Stylianos Atteshlis) and gathered material across four periods of fieldwork (summer 1978; December 1978–September 1979; summers 1981 and 1983) that became *The Magus of Strovolos*.

## Method and Stance

Markides frames his project explicitly as sociological participant-observation, not parapsychological investigation. He states at the outset that the book "is not a study to test or ascertain the validity" of the phenomena Daskalos and his circle describe, but an attempt "to present the world as experienced by the subjects" — meaning Markides deliberately positions himself as a participant who becomes progressively enmeshed in Daskalos's teaching rather than as a detached tester of claims. This choice shapes the entire book: dramatic claims (materialization, extraterrestrial contact, atomic transmutation) are presented largely without external corroboration, alongside Markides's own acknowledgment, repeated at several points, that "there was no way of testing the reality" of what he was told or shown.

Markides continued the study of Daskalos in two further volumes, *Homage to the Sun* (1980) and *Fire in the Heart* (1988) — neither yet in this vault — extending his ethnographic account of Daskalos's circle and teachings.

## Key Contribution

Markides's most significant scholarly move is bringing an academically credentialed sociologist's sustained, multi-year fieldwork to bear on a living folk-mystical tradition, producing one of the most detailed English-language ethnographic records of a non-Western (Greek Orthodox/Cypriot) esoteric healing tradition — comparable in structure, though not content, to Carlos Castaneda's Don Juan books, a comparison Markides's own narrative repeatedly invites by having Daskalos discuss Castaneda directly.
