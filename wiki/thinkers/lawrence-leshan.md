# Lawrence LeShan

**Summary**: American psychologist who spent three decades conducting depth therapy with cancer patients and identifying the psychological profile that predicts cancer development; author of *You Can Fight for Your Life* (1977) and *Cancer as a Turning Point* (1989); a foundational researcher in psycho-oncology and a key influence on Bernie Siegel and O. Carl Simonton.

**Sources**:
- [Siegel, B. S. (1986). *Love, medicine and miracles: Lessons learned about self-healing from a surgeon's experience with exceptional patients*. Harper & Row.](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

## Biography

Lawrence LeShan, Ph.D. (born 1920) is an American experimental and clinical psychologist whose career took a decisive turn in the 1950s when he began studying the psychological dimensions of cancer. Dissatisfied with the prevailing view that cancer was purely a biological disease, he undertook a systematic study of the personalities, life histories, and emotional styles of cancer patients.

Over three decades he conducted depth psychotherapy with 71 cancer patients and personality studies of 455 cases. His findings were published in *You Can Fight for Your Life: Emotional Factors in the Causation of Cancer* (1977) and later expanded in *Cancer as a Turning Point* (1989).

LeShan is also known for his work on psychic healing and non-local consciousness. His *The Medium, the Mystic and the Physicist* (1974) placed parapsychological phenomena within a framework connecting quantum physics and mysticism — an interest that runs parallel to his cancer research but is distinct from it.

## The Cancer Personality Profile

LeShan's most clinically influential contribution is his description of the psychological profile of people who develop cancer — derived from 30 years of depth therapy and personality research.

### The Structure of the Cancer Personality

LeShan identified a characteristic life pattern in 68 of 71 cancer patients he treated in depth therapy (versus only 3 of 88 non-cancer clients):

**Phase 1 — Childhood loss of safety**: The future cancer patient experienced childhood as a place where full emotional expression was dangerous. Love felt conditional — if they were the wrong kind of person, they would lose approval. This produced a fundamental withdrawal of authenticity: the child learned to suppress genuine needs, desires, and emotions in order to secure attachment.

**Phase 2 — Investment in external meaning**: In early adult life, the person found one central meaning: a close relationship, a career, a cause, or a creative project. This "life meaning connection" — one person or thing that made life feel real and worthwhile — became the organising centre of their existence. They functioned well as long as this centre held.

**Phase 3 — Loss of meaning**: The central relationship ended (through death, divorce, or betrayal); the career was terminated; the cause collapsed. The person experienced this loss not as grief but as the final confirmation of a childhood conviction: "I am not worth loving; there is nothing left for me." They could not reconstruct meaning because their emotional life had been suppressed since childhood — they had no internal resource to draw on.

**Phase 4 — Quiet desperation**: After the loss, the person continued to function — maintaining routines, going through the motions of relationships and work — but with the experience of inner emptiness. This is not the depression of a person who feels sadness; it is the hopelessness of a person who does not feel entitled to feel anything at all. The body, LeShan argued, registered this state as an instruction to surrender its defences. Cancer appeared.

### Cancer as Message

LeShan's interpretation of cancer — adopted and extended by Siegel — was that cancer was not a punishment but a message from the body:

*"Cancer is a symbol of the illness going on in the patient's life, and is a warning signal to take a new road."*

Elida Evans (1926), the Jungian psychologist whose work LeShan built on, expressed it similarly: *"Cancer represents in the individual the breaking up of a previous adaptation to environment, a calling back of projected libido which had gone into the outer world, a return to the unconscious."*

In LeShan's therapeutic framework, the goal was not to defeat cancer through willpower but to help the patient find what they had been withholding from themselves — the life they had suppressed — and to begin living it. He observed that when patients found or recovered their authentic "song to sing," some cancers went into remission.

## Key Research

### Personality Studies

LeShan's 455-patient personality study identified the pattern described above — the hopelessness, the childhood loss, the quiet desperation — as a distinguishing feature of cancer patients. The pattern preceded the diagnosis by years or decades.

He also documented what he called the "target organ" phenomenon: the organ affected by cancer corresponded to the emotional significance of that organ in the patient's life. The throat of a singer who had been silenced; the breast of a woman whose nurturing had been rejected; the intestines of a person who could not "digest" his life. (This overlaps with Myss's chakra-based disease mapping and with Shealy's electromagnetic dysthymia organ correspondences.)

### Psychotherapy Results

In his depth therapy with 71 cancer patients, LeShan observed that roughly half showed improvements in their condition — including some remissions — that correlated with the psychological changes achieved in therapy. These results were not published in peer-reviewed form and cannot be evaluated as controlled data, but they were consistent enough to support LeShan's conviction that the psychological dimension was causally relevant, not merely correlated.

### The Gang Leader Case

LeShan describes one patient — a gang leader who developed Hodgkin's disease — whose cancer responded to therapy and then recurred when he was offered a promotion to a desk job. The work that gave his life meaning (the camaraderie and danger of street leadership) was the same work that his wife wanted him to abandon for a respectable desk career. LeShan saw this as a demonstration that meaning — not attitude or positive thinking — was the relevant variable.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers in the Wiki

**With Siegel**: Siegel explicitly credits LeShan as one of the two or three most important influences on his clinical thinking. LeShan's cancer personality profile is the psychological foundation for Siegel's description of the Type 2 patient (the quiet desperado who performs compliance) and for the four questions — especially question 4 ("Why do you need your illness?"), which is LeShan's question in another form: what need is your illness meeting that your life was failing to meet?

**With Myss**: LeShan's insight that cancer represents a loss of "life meaning connection" maps onto Myss's account of second-chakra and fourth-chakra patterns — specifically the depletion that comes from maintaining relationships that violate the self. The "saint" who gives without receiving, whose boundaries are never enforced, whose life is organised around someone else's needs — this is the Myss cancer patient as much as the LeShan one.

**With Dossey**: LeShan's finding that cancer follows the loss of meaning is the individual-biography correlate of Dossey's population-level finding that job dissatisfaction (loss of occupational meaning) predicts heart attack. Both describe the body as registering meaning at a physiological level.

## Critique

LeShan's work shares the critique applicable to all psycho-oncology research of the pre-RCT era:

- Case-series and interview research cannot establish causal direction: cancer itself may cause the hopelessness and withdrawal LeShan identifies
- Self-reported life history in therapy is subject to memory distortion and narrative construction
- The therapy improvement claims are anecdotal and not independently verifiable
- The "cancer personality" construct has been operationalised in subsequent research (by researchers including Temoshok and Greer) with mixed results

**Note**: The Temoshok Type C personality research in the 1980s attempted to operationalise LeShan's cancer personality as "emotional suppression" and found some evidence that this style correlates with poorer outcomes in melanoma, but the findings did not replicate consistently across cancer types. Mainstream oncology does not accept the cancer personality as an established risk factor.
