# M. Scott Peck

**Summary**: American psychiatrist and author (1936–2005); best known for *The Road Less Traveled* (1978); *People of the Lie* (1983) proposes evil as a clinical personality disorder rooted in narcissism and scapegoating, and documents two exorcisms witnessed by Peck himself.

## Biography

Morgan Scott Peck (1936–2005) was born in New York City, educated at Harvard and Case Western Reserve University School of Medicine, and practised psychiatry in Connecticut for most of his career. He served as an Army psychiatrist and later held a private practice.

His first major book, *The Road Less Traveled* (1978), became one of the longest-running bestsellers in American publishing history, spending thirteen years on the *New York Times* bestseller list. It defined mental health as "the process of dedicating oneself to reality, at all costs" — a definition Peck carried into *People of the Lie* as the obverse: evil as the refusal of reality.

Peck converted to Christianity at age forty-three (1980), and his religious commitment is explicit in his work. He was a committed ecumenicist, and his writing consistently attempts to integrate the insights of psychiatry with Christian theological categories — without requiring readers to share his faith.

Later works include *The Different Drum: Community Making and Peace* (1987), *A World Waiting to Be Born* (1993), and *Glimpses of the Devil* (2005), in which he describes his possession cases in full detail (withheld from *People of the Lie* to avoid overbalancing the book).

## Core Contributions

### Discipline, Love, and Grace (The Road Less Traveled, 1978)
Peck's first and best-known book lays the constructive framework later books apply to evil. Its core claims:

- **Discipline** is a set of four techniques (delaying gratification, accepting responsibility, dedication to truth, balancing) for scheduling life's pain and pleasure so problems get solved rather than avoided. See [Discipline as the Four Tools of Growth](../concepts/discipline-four-tools.md).
- **Love** is redefined as "the will to extend one's self for the purpose of nurturing one's own or another's spiritual growth" — an act of will, not a feeling, sharply distinguished from falling in love, cathexis, dependency, and self-sacrifice. See [Love as an Act of Will](../concepts/love-as-will.md).
- **Grace** is Peck's name for a force, evidenced in dreams, serendipity, and unexplained survival, that supports human growth against a countervailing entropic pull whose psychological form is **laziness** — his candidate for the functional meaning of original sin, and the seed of his later definition of evil. See [Entropy, Laziness, and Grace](../concepts/entropy-and-grace.md).

### Evil as Clinical Reality
Peck's central contribution is the insistence that evil is not merely a theological category but a clinically observable, diagnosable condition. He proposes that evil constitutes a severe variant of narcissistic personality disorder, distinguished by:

- The absence of guilt in the face of harm caused to others
- The *presence* of a functional conscience that is consistently overridden — evil people are not psychopaths (who lack conscience) but people who refuse to tolerate the pain conscience produces
- Pathological lying designed primarily to maintain self-deception
- Consistent scapegoating of others — projecting unacknowledged faults onto children, partners, or subordinates
- A characteristic concern with maintaining appearances of respectability and goodness

The defining diagnostic signal, Peck argues, is the therapist's **revulsion** response — not empathy or pity, but a visceral instinct to escape the presence. He argues this is not countertransference but an evolved protective mechanism.

### The Refusal of Self-Knowledge
Peck distinguishes evil from ordinary sinfulness on a single axis: the *willingness to tolerate awareness of one's own imperfection*. All humans sin; the evil person is one who has made a consistent series of choices to avoid self-examination, choices that compound over time until the capacity for self-awareness is severely diminished. Drawing on Martin Buber's taxonomy of evil myths, Peck describes evil as a process of sliding — not a sudden fall but a gradual narrowing of the will.

### Narcissism and Will
Peck aligns his clinical analysis with theological categories. Evil arises from the refusal to submit will to any authority — truth, conscience, love, or God. This connects to the same spectrum as Satan's refusal to submit to God's judgment in Judeo-Christian mythology, and to Erich Fromm's analysis of necrophilia (the desire to control, fossilise, and prevent the living variety of others).

### The Methodology of Love
Peck's prescription for encountering and treating evil — whether in clinical practice, exorcism, or civic life — is love. Not sentimental love, but the disciplined willingness to be wounded by another's evil, to absorb it without being destroyed, and to offer healing without capitulating to the demand to affirm what is destructive. He uses the cross as the paradigm: the victim who suffered without becoming the perpetrator, whose suffering "absorbs" evil and transforms the relational field.

## Connections to Other Thinkers

**Carl Jung**: Peck explicitly endorses Jung's insight that evil is frequently the failure to meet one's own shadow — to acknowledge one's own darkness rather than projecting it. The scapegoating mechanism is the clinical expression of Jung's shadow projection. See [Shadow](../concepts/shadow.md).

**Erich Fromm**: Peck's primary theoretical mentor on evil. [Fromm](erich-fromm.md)'s *[The Heart of Man](../sources/heart-of-man.md)* (1964) and *The Anatomy of Human Destructiveness* (1973) provided the concept of [malignant narcissism](../concepts/individual-and-group-narcissism.md) and the analysis of [necrophilia](../concepts/necrophilia-and-biophilia.md) as the deepest expression of the death-oriented character. Peck acknowledges Fromm as one of his two teachers in evil (the other being Malachi Martin). Peck's diagnosis absorbs only Fromm's narcissism strand into his own single-mechanism account of evil, leaving out Fromm's necrophilia and [incestuous-symbiotic fixation](../concepts/incestuous-symbiotic-fixation.md) strands — see [Human Evil § Fromm's Three-Strand Account](../concepts/human-evil.md) for the fuller comparison.

**Martin Buber**: Peck draws on Buber's *Good and Evil* (1953) for the distinction between two types of myths of evil — the slide (gradual descent) and the catastrophic fall — and for the formulation that evil is the compulsive insistence on one's own reality at the expense of others'.

**Malachi Martin**: Peck's guide in the phenomenon of possession and exorcism. [Martin](malachi-martin.md)'s *[Hostage to the Devil](../sources/hostage-to-the-devil.md)* (1976) provided Peck with case descriptions, theological framework, and practical guidance he found confirmed in his own exorcism experience. Peck's process model compresses Martin's four-stage entry framework and six-stage ritual sequence into a shorter vocabulary (Pretense, Breakpoint) and documents only two cases in far less detail than Martin's five. See [Martin's Stages of Possession](../concepts/possession-process-martin.md) for the fuller framework Peck draws from.

**Caroline Myss and Norman Shealy**: Peck's framework parallels Myss and Shealy's in one important respect: both locate disease (physical or spiritual) in the pattern of energy-investment choices a person makes over time. Where Myss traces the path of illness from biographical choices to cellular biology, Peck traces the path of evil from choices about self-knowledge to the hardening of conscience. The [five myths of healing](../concepts/five-myths-of-healing.md) that Myss identifies in *Why People Don't Heal* — particularly woundology and the refusal to release the wound — describe a less extreme version of the same refusal Peck sees in his evil patients.

**A Course in Miracles**: Peck mentions ACIM as an example of the nondualist model of evil — the view that evil has no ultimate reality, being only an illusion created by the belief in separation. Peck explicitly rejects this position, arguing that evil is real and must be engaged directly rather than dissolved theoretically. See [Separation and Atonement](../concepts/separation-and-atonement.md) and [Ego vs. Holy Spirit](../concepts/ego-vs-holy-spirit.md) for ACIM's contrasting view.

## Critique

**The evil/illness distinction**: Peck acknowledges that labelling a person "evil" rather than "ill" risks weaponising psychiatric authority in the service of moral judgment — the same abuse Soviet psychiatry committed by labelling dissidents. He argues the solution is not to avoid the concept but to develop it rigorously, with safeguards. Critics note these safeguards are not well specified.

**Selection bias**: Peck's case studies are drawn from his own practice, which is a highly selected sample. Extrapolating a theory of evil from a handful of cases is methodologically weak.

**The exorcism evidence**: Peck's description of two witnessed exorcisms is the most controversial element of the book. He acknowledges he cannot offer this as scientific evidence and frames it as personal testimony — but it shapes the book's theological conclusions significantly.

**The victim-blaming risk**: As with Myss's [psychology of illness](../concepts/the-psychology-of-illness.md), Peck's framework can be misread to suggest that the victims of evil (children, partners, soldiers) are responsible for their situation through their failure to resist or escape. Peck is aware of this risk and addresses the structural dimensions (group evil, institutional complicity) in his MyLai chapter — though the clinical sections focus on individual cases.
