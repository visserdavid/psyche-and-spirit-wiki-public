# Martin Buber

**Summary**: Austrian-Israeli Jewish philosopher and theologian (1878–1965), best known for *I and Thou* (1923), whose *Good and Evil* (1953) argues that evil is fundamentally a refusal of direction toward the divine — cited by M. Scott Peck alongside Fromm's concept of malignant narcissism as a foundational image for Peck's own clinical psychology of evil.

## Biography

Martin Buber (1878–1965) was born in Vienna and became one of the twentieth century's most influential Jewish philosophers and theologians. He is best known for *I and Thou* (1923), which distinguishes the "I-Thou" relation (a mode of genuine, mutual encounter between persons) from the "I-It" relation (treating another as an object to be used or analysed) — a distinction that has influenced existentialist philosophy, theology, and psychotherapy broadly. Buber later emigrated to Jerusalem, where he taught social philosophy at the Hebrew University.

## Good and Evil (1953)

In *Good and Evil*, Buber presents two linked interpretations of the problem of evil — "Right and Wrong," addressing evil's place in the human social and moral world, and "Images," addressing its place in individual psychological development. Buber's central claim is that evil is not an independent force but fundamentally a refusal of *direction*: because the only ultimate direction available to a person is toward God, evil consists in turning away from that direction — a self-enclosure that mirrors, in miniature, the same refusal of authentic I-Thou relation that *I and Thou* identifies as the deepest human failure.

## Use in the Wiki

[M. Scott Peck](m-scott-peck.md) cites Buber's image of evil as compulsive **self-affirmation regardless of evidence** — the refusal of genuine encounter with one's own faults — as one of the two theoretical touchstones (alongside [Erich Fromm](erich-fromm.md)'s concept of malignant narcissism) for his own clinical diagnosis of human evil in *[People of the Lie](../sources/people-of-the-lie.md)*. See [Human Evil (Psychology of Evil)](../concepts/human-evil.md) for how Peck's narcissism-centred clinical account relates to Buber's more purely theological framing, and to Fromm's and Jung's competing accounts within the wiki's evil cluster.

## Critique

Buber's theological framing of evil as a refusal of relation and direction is a philosophical-religious claim, not an empirical or clinical one, and Peck's use of it is illustrative rather than diagnostic — Buber supplies an image Peck finds resonant, not a testable mechanism. As with the wiki's other accounts of evil (Jung's *privatio boni*, Fromm's syndrome of decay, Peck's own narcissism-centred diagnosis), Buber's framework cannot be adjudicated by controlled evidence; its value lies in the coherence and moral seriousness of the picture it offers rather than in falsifiable clinical claims.
