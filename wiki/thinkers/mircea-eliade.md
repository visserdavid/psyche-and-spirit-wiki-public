# Mircea Eliade

**Summary**: Romanian historian of religion (1907–1986) whose comparative, "morphological" method sought the recurring structures of sacred experience across cultures and eras — best known for the sacred/profane distinction, the theory of *hierophany*, and his foundational studies of initiation and shamanism.

## Overview

Mircea Eliade held the chair in History of Religions at the University of Chicago from 1956 until his death, and was, alongside [Carl Jung](carl-jung.md) and [Joseph Campbell](joseph-campbell.md), one of the twentieth century's most influential comparativists of myth and religion. His scholarship spans yoga, shamanism, alchemy, cosmology, and initiation, always pursuing the same underlying method: identifying a structural pattern (a "morphology," in his term) that recurs across historically unconnected cultures, and treating that recurrence as evidence of a universal religious sensibility rather than mere coincidence or diffusion.

Eliade's central theoretical apparatus — developed across his career but foundational to *[Rites and Symbols of Initiation](../sources/rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md)* (1958) — is the distinction between the **sacred** and the **profane**. For archaic and traditional societies, Eliade argues, reality itself is not homogeneous: certain places, times, objects, and states of being are charged with sacred power (what he calls *hierophany*, a "showing" of the sacred) while the rest of existence is profane, ordinary, undifferentiated. Religious behavior — ritual, myth, initiation — is the technology by which archaic humanity accesses, repeats, and renews contact with the sacred.

## Rites and Symbols of Initiation (1958)

Originally delivered as the 1956 Haskell Lectures at the University of Chicago under the title "Patterns of Initiation," this book is Eliade's most systematic single treatment of initiatory ritual: puberty rites, entrance into tribal secret societies, and the mystical vocation of shamans and medicine men, surveyed across Australian, African, Melanesian, North and South American, and Eurasian material, with a final chapter extending the pattern into India, Greece, and Christianity. See the [source page](../sources/rites-and-symbols-of-initiation.md) and [Symbolic Death and Rebirth](../concepts/symbolic-death-and-rebirth.md) for the book's central argument.

## Method and Its Critics

Eliade's comparative "morphological" method — assembling structurally similar rites, myths, or symbols from unconnected cultures and treating the pattern itself as religiously significant, independent of any one culture's specific historical context — invites the same objection repeatedly leveled at [Campbell](joseph-campbell.md)'s monomyth (see [Hero Myth § Critique](../concepts/hero-myth.md)) and at [van Gennep](arnold-van-gennep.md)'s tripartite schema (see [The Rites of Passage Schema § Critique](../concepts/rites-of-passage-schema.md#critique)): flattening cultural and historical specificity into a universal template drawn largely from secondhand ethnographic reports rather than the historian's own fieldwork. Eliade is explicit in his foreword that the lectures are addressed to "the nonspecialist reader" and depict "only the broadest outline" of an immense field — a hedge similar to van Gennep's own concession that his schema was offered for testing, not as established law.

**Biographical controversy**: Eliade's early career in interwar Romania included documented association with the far-right, antisemitic Iron Guard movement, a matter of ongoing scholarly controversy (needs verification for specifics — sources dispute the depth and duration of his involvement, and Eliade himself never publicly addressed it in detail). This biographical fact is relevant context for evaluating the ideological undercurrents of his broader project — including his romantic idealization of "archaic" societies and his sometimes uncritical nostalgia for a lost cosmic religiosity — even though it does not, by itself, invalidate his scholarship on initiation and the sacred.

## Connections Across the Wiki

- [Arnold van Gennep](arnold-van-gennep.md): Eliade builds directly on van Gennep's tripartite rite-of-passage structure, citing *Les rites de passage* (1909) in his first chapter; see [The Rites of Passage Schema](../concepts/rites-of-passage-schema.md).
- [Joseph Campbell](joseph-campbell.md): Campbell's Belly of the Whale motif in [*The Hero with a Thousand Faces*](../sources/the-hero-with-a-thousand-faces.md) parallels Eliade's swallowed-monster initiatory motif directly; see [Symbolic Death and Rebirth](../concepts/symbolic-death-and-rebirth.md) and [Hero Myth](../concepts/hero-myth.md).
- [Victor Turner](victor-turner.md): Turner's [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md) elaborates the same middle, transitional phase that Eliade treats as the psychologically decisive core of initiatory death.
- [Carl Jung](carl-jung.md): Eliade explicitly cites Jung's concept of [individuation](../concepts/individuation.md) in his concluding chapter, reading modern man's dreams and crises as unconscious survivals of the initiatory pattern.
- Eliade's later book *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* (1951, not yet in this wiki) is previewed in Chapter V of *Rites and Symbols of Initiation*; see [Shamanic Healing](../concepts/shamanic-healing.md), where [Jeanne Achterberg](jeanne-achterberg.md) already cites Eliade's "technician of ecstasy" formulation secondhand.
