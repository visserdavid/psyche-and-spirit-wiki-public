# Moses de León

**Summary**: Spanish Jewish mystic and kabbalist (c. 1240–1305) credited by modern scholarship as the primary author of the Zohar, the central text of Kabbalah, which he circulated pseudepigraphically under the name of the ancient sage Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai.

## Biography

Moses de León (Moses ben Shem Tov of León) was born around 1240 in the city of León in Castile, Spain, and died in 1305 in Arévalo. He lived during a turbulent period for Spanish Jewry: the Reconquista had been completed, the Jewish community faced increasing legal restrictions, and within the educated class, Maimonidean rationalism was eroding traditional observance.

Moses initially engaged with Neoplatonic philosophy, copying a translation of Maimonides' *Guide for the Perplexed* in 1264. But by the late 1270s he had turned decisively to Kabbalah, immersing himself in the emerging tradition of the Bahir and the school of Gerona. He settled in Guadalajara around 1275–1280 and began producing Hebrew kabbalistic works under his own name (including the *Sefer ha-Rimmon*, *Mishkan ha-Edut*, and others).

Beginning around 1280–1286, he produced the main body of the Zohar — a sprawling mystical commentary on the Torah in a distinctive pseudo-Aramaic. He circulated this text in portions, claiming to be copying an ancient manuscript composed by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. The attribution gave the work the authority of a second-century sage; the pseudepigraphic strategy had deep roots in Jewish literary tradition (the Bahir was similarly attributed).

### The Authorship Question

Isaac ben Samuel of Acre, a contemporary who investigated the Zohar's origins, records that Moses de León's widow admitted her husband had "written [the Zohar] from his own head" — attributing the work to financial motivation. Moses's friend Joseph Abulafia confirmed the role of a meditative writing technique (writing in an altered state using divine names) in the composition.

Modern scholarship, pioneered by Gershom Scholem in his 1925 lecture "Did Moses de León Write the Zohar?" and confirmed by Isaiah Tishby and Daniel Chanan Matt, has established beyond reasonable doubt that Moses de León was the primary (though possibly not sole) author. The Aramaic of the Zohar, while archaic-seeming, contains systematic Hebraisms, grammatical errors, and neologisms characteristic of Moses's Hebrew style.

### The Zohar's Purpose

Moses was not a fraud. He experienced himself as channeling ancient wisdom — a vessel for a tradition that, in his understanding, stretched back to Sinai and Adam. He also had a polemical purpose: to counter the rationalist erosion of Jewish practice by revealing the Torah's mythological and mystical depths. The Zohar was an act of cultural and spiritual preservation at a moment of severe communal pressure.

## Core Contributions

### Mythologising Torah
Moses returned Jewish thought from philosophical allegory back to living myth. Maimonides had demythologised the Torah; Moses re-mythologised it. Verses become descriptions of divine dynamics; the Patriarchs become living embodiments of sefirot; the Sabbath becomes a cosmic wedding.

### The Sefirot as Divine Drama
Moses gave the sefirot system its fullest narrative expression. The ten aspects of the divine personality enact an ongoing drama of love, judgment, exile, and reunion. The central couple — Tif'eret (the Blessed Holy One) and Shekhinah (the Divine Presence) — provides the emotional and erotic heart of the system. Their union is constantly threatened by human sin and restored by prayer and righteous action.

### Exile as Cosmic Condition
Moses's Zohar transforms the historical condition of Jewish exile into a cosmic metaphysic: the Shekhinah herself is in exile, separated from the divine masculine, accompanying Israel in its dispersion. The task of tikkun (repair) — restoring divine unity — is assigned to every Jew through Torah observance, prayer, and ethical action.

### The Four Levels of Torah
The Zohar introduces the teaching that Torah has four levels — garment (story), body (commandment), soul (hidden wisdom), and soul of the soul (the ineffable mystery). "Fools see only the garment." This hermeneutic invites endless depth-reading and became the foundation for all subsequent Kabbalistic exegesis.

## Relation to Other Thinkers

[Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md) draws on the Zohar — specifically this Matt anthology — as her primary source for the Kabbalistic column of her three-traditions synthesis. Moses de León's sefirot system provides the Hebrew-mystical parallel to the chakra system and Christian sacraments in [Anatomy of the Spirit](../sources/anatomy-of-the-spirit.md).

[Daniel Chanan Matt](daniel-chanan-matt.md)'s translation makes Moses de León's vision accessible to non-Hebraists and non-Aramaists.

The creative genius Moses de León displays — composing a masterwork while attributing it to an ancient sage — has parallels in this wiki: Helen Schucman experienced herself as scribing rather than composing [A Course in Miracles](../sources/a-course-in-miracles.md); Barbara Brennan received her [Holographic Healing Model](../concepts/holographic-healing-model.md) through channeled guidance. Moses represents the same phenomenon in a medieval Jewish key.
