# O. Carl Simonton

**Summary**: American radiation oncologist who pioneered psychological and imagery-based approaches to cancer treatment; founder of the Cancer Counseling and Research Center in Fort Worth, Texas; co-author of *Getting Well Again* (1978) with Stephanie Matthews-Simonton; the primary clinical influence on Bernie Siegel and a foundational figure in the mind-body oncology movement.

**Sources**:
- [Siegel, B. S. (1986). *Love, medicine and miracles: Lessons learned about self-healing from a surgeon's experience with exceptional patients*. Harper & Row.](../sources/love-medicine-miracles.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

## Biography

O. Carl Simonton, M.D. (1942–2009) was an American radiation oncologist who, in the early 1970s, began integrating psychological techniques into cancer treatment at Travis Air Force Base in California. His clinical interest began with the observation that patients' attitudes, emotional states, and willingness to engage with their illness measurably affected treatment outcomes — observations that mainstream oncology at the time neither recognised nor rewarded.

He founded the Cancer Counseling and Research Center (later the Simonton Cancer Center) in Fort Worth, Texas, with his then-wife Stephanie Matthews-Simonton. Their work combined meditation, mental imagery, group psychotherapy, and lifestyle modification with conventional radiation therapy. Their 1978 book *Getting Well Again*, co-authored with James Creighton, documented their approach and results.

In June 1978, Simonton and Stephanie Matthews-Simonton conducted a workshop at the Elmcrest Institute in Portland, Connecticut, titled "Psychological Factors, Stress and Cancer." [Bernie Siegel](bernie-siegel.md) attended this workshop — and found himself the only physician among 75 participants of psychologists, counsellors, and patients. This experience was the decisive turning point in Siegel's shift from surgeon-mechanic to the founder of [ECaP](../concepts/exceptional-cancer-patients.md).

## Key Contributions

### Imagery-Based Cancer Treatment

Simonton's core therapeutic innovation was guided mental imagery applied to cancer. Patients were taught to enter a relaxed (alpha) state through meditation, then to visualise their immune system actively engaging and destroying cancer cells. The imagery was adapted to each patient's idiom — some used warrior imagery; others used natural metaphors of digestion, absorption, or transformation.

In 1971, Simonton's first major case: a man with advanced throat cancer who the specialists had given a 5% chance of surviving five years. Under Simonton's imagery protocol, the man experienced no side effects from radiation; his cancer disappeared; and he went on to cure himself of arthritis and sexual impotence using the same imagery technique. This case gave Simonton confidence to develop the method further.

### Clinical Results

Simonton's published results, reported in *Getting Well Again* and described by Siegel:

Of 159 patients treated at the Cancer Counseling and Research Center, none were expected to live more than one year at the start of treatment:
- 19 percent saw their cancer regress
- 22 percent stabilised
- The remainder succumbed, but the average survival time of those who died was double the statistical prediction

These results — achieved in patients with no expected survival — attracted both enthusiasm and scepticism. The patient self-selection problem is significant: people who travel to Fort Worth for an imagery programme are not representative of the general cancer population.

### Psychological Profile of Long-Term Survivors

Working from the clinical research of [Lawrence LeShan](lawrence-leshan.md) and Elida Evans, Simonton developed a description of patients who responded well to treatment — characteristics that overlapped with Siegel's eventual survivor personality taxonomy and with Myss's four challenge areas:

- Career involvement maintained or returned to during illness
- Strong sense of self-esteem and adequacy
- Degree of self-love and retained control over one's life
- A clear sense of reality
- Welfare of others as a motivator (not purely self-interest)
- Tendency toward nonconformity
- Appreciation of diversity in people

### The Simonton Method

The programme described in *Getting Well Again* became the template for mind-body cancer work in the late 1970s and 1980s:

1. Education about the mind-body connection in cancer
2. Relaxation training and entry into the alpha state
3. Guided imagery (visualising the immune system, cancer cells, and treatment)
4. Group psychotherapy addressing psychological factors
5. Lifestyle changes (nutrition, exercise, stress reduction)
6. Setting personal goals and life-meaning work

ECaP (Siegel's programme) extended this model by emphasising authentic emotional expression, the healing partnership with the physician, and the developmental dimension of the survivor personality.

## Critique

Simonton's work attracted significant criticism from mainstream oncology:

- The clinical results were based on self-selected, motivated patients who cannot be compared to population norms
- No controlled randomised trial tested the imagery method against conventional treatment alone
- The claim that attitude and imagery extend cancer survival was not supported by peer-reviewed prospective research at the time

Simonton acknowledged these limitations and continued refining the programme throughout his career. He did not claim that imagery cures cancer; he claimed that it improved outcomes in patients who were simultaneously receiving conventional treatment, and that it dramatically improved quality of life regardless of survival time.

**Note**: Mainstream oncology does not endorse imagery or meditation as cancer treatments, though psychological interventions for quality of life, anxiety, and treatment adherence are widely supported in the research literature.

## Relationship to the Wiki's Thinkers

Simonton's influence runs through multiple wiki sources:

- [Siegel](bernie-siegel.md) credits the 1978 workshop as the origin of ECaP; Siegel's work is an extension and humanisation of Simonton's imagery-based model
- The imagery techniques Simonton developed overlap with [Brennan](barbara-ann-brennan.md)'s healing work in terms of visualising the energy field
- The psychological profile of the long-term survivor Simonton described converges with [Myss](caroline-myss.md)'s account of what it means to claim one's power
