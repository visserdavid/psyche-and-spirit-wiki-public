# Paul Radin

**Summary**: American anthropologist (1883–1959), a pioneering ethnographer of the Winnebago (Ho-Chunk) people, whose translation and analysis of the Winnebago Trickster cycle became the primary ethnographic source both Jung and Kerényi used to build their psychological and mythological theories of the Trickster archetype.

**Sources**:
- [Radin, P. (1956). *The trickster: A study in American Indian mythology* (K. Kerényi & C. G. Jung, Commentaries). Philosophical Library.](../sources/the-trickster.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

## Overview

Paul Radin (1883–1959) was a Polish-born American anthropologist trained under Franz Boas, whose fieldwork centered on the Winnebago (Ho-Chunk) people of Wisconsin and Nebraska. Over a long career (his fieldwork began around 1908) Radin translated and analyzed a large body of Winnebago myth, ritual, and autobiography, becoming one of the most important sources of primary Native American ethnographic material available to non-specialist readers in the mid-20th century — the same period in which [Carl Jung](carl-jung.md) and [Karl Kerényi](karl-kerenyi.md) were developing their comparative approaches to myth.

## Contribution to *The Trickster*

Radin's 1956 volume, *[The Trickster: A Study in American Indian Mythology](../sources/the-trickster.md)*, presents his translation of the Winnebago Trickster cycle (the adventures of Wakdjunkaga), supplementary Hare-cycle material, and summarized Assiniboine and Tlingit trickster myths, together with his own extended analytical essay. Radin argues that the Winnebago material represents something close to a "pure" trickster type — appetite, wandering, and sexuality without the moral or creative overlay found in many other North American trickster cycles — and situates it comparatively against Northwest Coast Raven cycles and cases of trickster figures demoted from an earlier divine status by later mythographers (e.g., the Oglala Iktomi/"Spider").

Radin invited both Kerényi and Jung to contribute independent interpretive essays to the same volume without attempting to reconcile their readings — a deliberately plural editorial structure unusual for its time. He himself remained more cautious than either commentator about generalizing a single ethnographic case into a universal psychological or mythological archetype; his own essay stresses the wide variability of trickster material across tribes even as it makes the comparative case for a shared core pattern. See [Trickster Archetype](../concepts/trickster-archetype.md) for the full content and the wiki's critique of the universalizing moves both commentators make on top of Radin's fieldwork.

## Connections to Other Thinkers

**Carl Jung**: Jung's essay in this volume, "On the Psychology of the Trickster Figure," is explicitly a commentary on Radin's material and was later reprinted in [*The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious*](../sources/collected-works-9-1.md) (CW 9.1) — the wiki's original, second-hand source for the Trickster concept before this ingest.

**Karl Kerényi**: Kerényi's companion essay in the same volume reads Wakdjunkaga against the Greek trickster-god Hermes; see [Karl Kerényi](karl-kerenyi.md) for his fuller mythological method.

**Joseph L. Henderson**: Henderson's chapter in [*Man and His Symbols*](../sources/man-and-his-symbols.md) also draws directly on Radin's Winnebago fieldwork for its account of the Trickster as the most primitive of four sequential Winnebago hero figures.

## Critique

Mainstream anthropology regards Radin's fieldwork as historically significant but cautions against treating a single narrator's (Sam Blowsnake's) rendition of one tribe's cycle as evidence for a universal psychological or mythological structure — a move made more emphatically by Jung and Kerényi than by Radin's own, more cautious comparative chapter.
