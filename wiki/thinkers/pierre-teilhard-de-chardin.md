# Pierre Teilhard de Chardin

**Summary**: French Jesuit priest and paleontologist (1881–1955) who argued in *The Phenomenon of Man* (written 1938–1940, posthumously published 1955) that cosmic evolution has a built-in directionality toward increasing complexity and consciousness, converging on a final unifying, personal state he called the "Omega Point" — the framework Kenneth Ring adopts wholesale as the evolutionary destination toward which he believes near-death experiences are propelling humanity.

## Biography

Pierre Teilhard de Chardin (1881–1955) was a French Jesuit priest, paleontologist, and philosopher who participated in significant paleoanthropological fieldwork, including the discovery of the Peking Man fossils in China. Ordained in 1912, he served as a stretcher-bearer in the First World War (earning the Military Medal and Legion of Honour) before pursuing a career in geology and paleontology under Marcellin Boule at the Institute of Human Paleontology in Paris. He spent much of his career attempting to synthesise Catholic theology with an evolutionary understanding of the cosmos, a project the Church's hierarchy viewed with suspicion during his lifetime: his religious superiors forbade him from teaching or publishing his philosophical-theological writings after his 1920s lectures on original sin and evolution were judged unorthodox, and he was later blocked from a professorship at the Collège de France and from publishing a planned recasting of *The Phenomenon of Man*. He spent much of the 1920s–40s in China as an advisor to the Geological Survey of China, and his final years (from 1951) in New York, working with the Wenner-Gren Foundation. His major theological-scientific works appeared only after his 1955 death, per his own prior arrangement with a literary executor.

## The Law of Complexity-Consciousness and the Noosphere

In *[The Phenomenon of Man](../sources/the-phenomenon-of-man.md)* (written 1938–1940; published 1955/1959), Teilhard argued that evolution is not merely biological but has an "inner face" — consciousness — correlated with and deepening alongside the increasing physical complexity of matter, a claim he formalized as the **Law of Complexity-Consciousness**. He traced this progression through the geosphere, the biosphere, and what he called the **noosphere** (the emerging sphere of collective human thought, a term he coined in 1925), proposing that the entire cosmic process is oriented toward a final convergent state of maximum consciousness, love, and unity: the **Omega Point**, which Teilhard identified theologically with Christ as the goal and endpoint of creation. See [Noosphere and the Law of Complexity-Consciousness](../concepts/noosphere-and-complexity-consciousness.md) for the full treatment, including his "Within/Without" account of matter and his treatment of evil as a structural by-product of an evolving, converging universe.

## Use in the Wiki: Ring's Omega Hypothesis

[Kenneth Ring](kenneth-ring.md) borrows Teilhard's vocabulary and framework wholesale for his [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md) in *[Heading Toward Omega](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md)*: Ring argues that the systematic transformation he documents in near-death-experience survivors — decreased ego-centrism, increased compassion, diminished fear of death, deepened cosmic unity — closely resembles the qualities Teilhard attributed to the approaching Omega. If millions of people are undergoing this transformation through NDEs, Ring suggests, the species may be accelerating toward the state Teilhard predicted on theological and paleontological grounds decades earlier.

## Critique

Teilhard's teleological framework — that the universe is metaphysically "aimed at" a final convergent point — is a theological commitment that the great majority of evolutionary biologists reject; standard evolutionary theory has no mechanism by which evolution could be goal-directed in this sense, and Teilhard's synthesis was controversial within his own Church during his lifetime for related reasons. Ring's appropriation of Teilhard's vocabulary for the Omega Hypothesis inherits this problem: Ring uses "Omega Point" language without committing to (or fully engaging) the Catholic metaphysics that gave the term its original meaning, leaving the philosophical foundation of his own hypothesis ambiguous, as the wiki's [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md) critique notes.
