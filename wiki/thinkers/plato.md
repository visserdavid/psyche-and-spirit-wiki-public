# Plato

**Summary**: Ancient Greek philosopher (c. 428–348 BCE) whose Myth of Er — a near-death narrative in which souls choose their next life before drinking from the River of Forgetfulness — is one of Myss's primary philosophical precedents for the concept of Sacred Contracts.

## Relevance to the Wiki

Plato appears in *Sacred Contracts* as a key historical precedent for the idea that souls enter life with a pre-agreed purpose. His philosophical framework — particularly the theory of archetypal Forms and the Myth of Er — provides Myss with Western philosophical grounding for concepts she develops through contemporary spiritual language.

## The Myth of Er (*Republic*, Book 10)

In the closing section of the *Republic*, Socrates recounts the story of Er, a soldier who returned to consciousness twelve days after apparently dying on the battlefield and described what he witnessed in the realm between lives.

Souls waiting to reincarnate were presented with a range of life templates — different conditions of wealth, poverty, health, illness, power, and obscurity. They chose from these templates in an order determined by lots. Three Fates then ratified each choice: Clotho (who spins the thread of life), Lachesis (who determines its length), and Atropos (who cuts it). After choosing, souls drank from the River of Unmindfulness (Lethe) and forgot the particulars of their choice before being born.

The myth establishes several ideas central to Myss's framework:
- **Pre-birth choice**: Souls select the key conditions of their incarnation, not as victims of fate but as active agents.
- **Guided selection**: A divine figure (the Prophet, the Fates) assists in the choosing.
- **Forgetting as part of the design**: The loss of explicit memory is structural — the soul lives its Contract without knowing its terms in advance.
- **Responsibility**: "The responsibility lies with the chooser; God is without blame" (Republic 617e).

## James Hillman's Extension

Myss cites [James Hillman](james-hillman.md)'s *The Soul's Code* (1996) as a contemporary extension of Plato's myth. Hillman calls the pre-birth image the *acorn* — the soul's unique form, which it carries into life and spends a lifetime trying to actualise. The soul is accompanied by a *daimon* (guardian spirit) that knows the soul's purpose and guides it toward fulfilment.

## Plato's Theory of Forms

While the Myth of Er is the primary text Myss draws on, the broader Platonic framework is relevant: Plato's belief in eternal, non-material Forms (archetypes) as the true reality behind the world of appearances is the philosophical ancestor of Jung's archetypal theory and, through Jung, Myss's own. The historian Richard Tarnas, whom Myss cites, argues that Plato's Forms were not mere abstractions but active principles shaping reality — "the gods and goddesses of tradition, now understood as symbols of deep patterns."

## Critique

**Textual interpretation**: Scholars debate whether the Myth of Er is intended literally or as a philosophical metaphor. Plato uses it to close an argument about justice (the *Republic* argues that justice is intrinsically worthwhile, and the myth shows that choosing a just life has cosmic support). Reading it as a literal account of pre-birth soul selection exceeds what the text itself claims.

**Historical distance**: Plato wrote in a specific cultural and philosophical context. Applying his myth directly to contemporary spiritual frameworks requires translation and interpretation that may distort the original meaning.
