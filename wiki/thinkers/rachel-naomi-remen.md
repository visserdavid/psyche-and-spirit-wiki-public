# Rachel Naomi Remen

**Summary**: American physician, medical educator, and pioneer of integrative medicine whose work on the healing relationship, illness as meaning, and the doctor-patient bond makes her a key figure in the convergence of medicine and spirituality.

## Overview

Rachel Naomi Remen, M.D. is a clinical professor of family and community medicine and the co-founder and medical director of the Commonweal Cancer Help Program in California. She has lived with Crohn's disease since childhood — an illness that profoundly shaped her understanding of medicine, suffering, and the limits of cure — and brought an autobiographical authenticity to her practice and writing.

*Why People Don't Heal and How They Can* is dedicated to Remen and Daniel Lowenstein, M.D., with the words "love and appreciation for having come into my life." This dedication signals the close intellectual and personal relationship between Myss's work and Remen's integrative medical practice.

## Key Contributions

### Kitchen Table Wisdom (1994)

Remen's best-known work is *Kitchen Table Wisdom: Stories That Heal* (1994), a collection of stories from her decades in medicine that explores illness as a passage, suffering as a teacher, and the healing power of authentic human relationship. The book helped establish the narrative medicine movement in the United States.

### Beyond Cure to Healing

Remen draws a sharp distinction — parallel to [Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md)'s own — between **curing** (the elimination of disease through medical intervention) and **healing** (the restoration of wholeness at the level of meaning, relationship, and spirit). She argues that medicine has confused the two and that patients and doctors alike suffer from the conflation.

### The Healing Relationship

Remen places the quality of the human connection between healer and patient at the centre of therapeutic efficacy. She critiques the depersonalising tendencies of modern medicine and teaches physicians to be present with suffering rather than managing it from a clinical distance.

### Meaning and Illness

Like Myss, Remen argues that illness can be a catalyst for the discovery of meaning and that the most important healing often occurs at a level medicine does not measure. She does not claim that meaning causes disease or prevents death; she argues that the orientation toward meaning transforms the experience of illness and can mobilise healing resources unavailable to purely biomedical intervention.

## Relationship to Borysenko's Work

Remen is listed by name in the acknowledgments of [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)'s *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* (1987) as a valued colleague, alongside Dean Ornish, MD, and others working at the intersection of medicine and meaning. This places Remen within the same Cambridge/Boston network of mind-body medicine practitioners from which the Harvard Mind/Body Clinic emerged — a community Borysenko explicitly credits with shaping the program. The [healing vs. curing](../concepts/healing-vs-curing.md) distinction that Remen develops in her own work parallels the distinction Borysenko illustrates through Sam's story.

## Relationship to Myss's Work

Remen and Myss occupy convergent territory: both situate healing beyond the biomedical cure model, both emphasise the spiritual and relational dimensions of illness, and both draw on their own experience of illness or limitation as practitioners. Where Myss focuses on the energy anatomy framework and archetypal patterns, Remen tends toward narrative, relationship, and the phenomenology of suffering.

Their collaboration/mutual reference represents the broader dialogue between energy medicine, integrative oncology, and narrative medicine that characterised the American holistic health movement of the 1990s.

## Critique

Mainstream medicine has generally been receptive to Remen's work on the doctor-patient relationship and the importance of meaning — these are well-supported areas in psycho-oncology and palliative care. The narrative medicine movement she helped inspire has been widely adopted in medical education. Her work is less controversial than Myss's because it operates within an interpersonal and psychological frame rather than making claims about energy fields or intuitive diagnosis.
