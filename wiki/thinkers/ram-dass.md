# Ram Dass

**Summary**: American spiritual teacher (1931–2019), born Richard Alpert, whose journey from Harvard research psychologist to devoted student of an Indian guru is used by Myss as a primary illustration of the five stages of Sacred Contract unfolding in a contemporary Western life.

## Relevance to the Wiki

Ram Dass appears in *Sacred Contracts* as Myss's extended case study for the five stages of the Sacred Contract in action (see [Five Stages of Sacred Contract](../concepts/five-stages-of-sacred-contract.md)). His biography — academic stage, psychedelic research stage, spiritual transformation, decades of teaching and service — illustrates how a Contract unfolds across distinct life phases, each building on and making sense of the previous one.

## Biography

Born Richard Alpert in Boston in 1931 into a wealthy Jewish family, he became a psychology professor at Harvard in the late 1950s alongside Timothy Leary. Together they conducted research on the psychological effects of psilocybin and LSD, attracting notable intellectuals including Aldous Huxley and Allen Ginsberg. Both were dismissed from Harvard in 1963.

Alpert travelled to India in 1967 with Leary and the poet Allen Ginsberg. In India, he encountered the guru Neem Karoli Baba (Maharaji), who knew inexplicable details about Alpert's deceased mother — an event that shattered Alpert's analytical defences in a way that psychedelics had not. Maharaji gave him the name Ram Dass ("Servant of God") and instructed him in yoga and meditation.

Returning to the United States, Ram Dass became one of the most influential Western teachers of Hindu-derived spirituality. His 1971 book *Be Here Now* was a defining text of the consciousness movement. He founded the Prison-Ashram Project (which brings meditation into prisons) and the Dying Project (which supports people facing death with mindfulness practices). In 1997 he suffered a severe stroke that left him partially paralysed; he continued teaching until his death in 2019.

## Ram Dass and the Five Stages

Myss reads Ram Dass's biography as a textbook illustration of the Sacred Contract's stages:

1. **Contact**: The encounter with Maharaji, who named his mother's cause of death. Ram Dass describes his mind stopping — "it went kind of…white" — in a moment of inexplicable recognition.
2. **Heeding the Call**: Becoming a disciple, receiving instruction, returning to the United States as Ram Dass rather than Richard Alpert.
3. **Renaming**: The literal receiving of the name Ram Dass — "Servant of God" — marking a soul-level identity shift.
4. **Assignments**: Decades of teaching, writing, founding organisations, mentoring students, facing the stroke and its aftermath.
5. **Surrender**: Most fully expressed after the stroke, which Ram Dass described as a *stroke of grace* that forced him into a new dimension of surrender and teaching — learning from radical vulnerability and dependency.

Ram Dass himself described his life as a "lotus flower unfolding" — what seemed like three separate stages (the science stage, the yogi stage, the teacher stage) was a single progression that only made sense in retrospect.

## Ram Dass and Mindfulness

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) quotes Ram Dass — under his birth name, Richard Alpert — in the mindfulness chapter of *Minding the Body, Mending the Mind* (1987) as a practical guide to present-moment awareness. His teaching distilled to: "Put up signs around the house as reminders. The practice is just remembering what's happening right now." This situates him within the clinical mind-body literature as a teacher of [mindfulness](../concepts/mindfulness.md) as a moment-to-moment practice, not only a formal meditation. The Borysenko-Ram Dass connection represents the bridge between Harvard's experimental psychology tradition (Alpert's original academic home) and the Mind/Body Clinic's clinical application of the same contemplative insights he brought West from India.
