# Raymond Moody

**Summary**: American philosopher and physician who coined the term "near-death experience" and, in *Life After Life* (1975), founded the modern field of near-death studies by synthesizing roughly 150 interviews into a composite model of the experience.

## Overview

Raymond A. Moody Jr. earned a PhD in philosophy from the University of Virginia in 1969, specializing in ethics, logic, and philosophy of language, and taught philosophy for three years before entering medical school with the intention of becoming a psychiatrist. His interest in what became near-death studies began almost by chance in 1965, as an undergraduate, when he heard a psychiatry professor's account of being pronounced dead twice with an unusually vivid experience each time. Years later, teaching a course on Plato's *Phaedo*, a student independently described a strikingly similar experience following her grandmother's death. Moody began collecting cases informally, eventually interviewing around 150 people who had been resuscitated from clinical death, come very close to death, or (secondhand) described a dying relative's experience.

*Life After Life* (1975) became an unexpected bestseller and coined the term "near-death experience," giving the field its founding vocabulary — the tunnel, the being of light, the life review — and its founding composite model of roughly fifteen recurring elements. Moody was explicit that this was not a scientific proof of an afterlife, only a description of a strikingly consistent experiential pattern, and he anticipated and directly addressed both religious and scientific criticism. The sequel, *Reflections on Life After Life* (1977), expanded the case base, documented additional elements (the "vision of knowledge," negative/bewildered-spirit accounts, supernatural rescues), and proposed a methodological research agenda for future, more rigorous NDE studies.

[Elisabeth Kübler-Ross](elisabeth-kubler-ross.md), whose own research with dying patients ran in parallel, wrote the foreword to both books and was an early, prominent supporter of Moody's work; her independent corroboration was, by Moody's own account, decisive in convincing him the phenomenon was not an isolated coincidence of his own case-finding. [Kenneth Ring](kenneth-ring.md) read *Life After Life* and set out to give Moody's anecdotal observations a systematic, controlled empirical foundation, publishing *Life at Death* (1980) and *[Heading Toward Omega](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md)* (1984) — work Moody himself, in *Reflections*, had called for in his own methodological appendix.

## Key contributions

- **[Near-death experiences](../concepts/near-death-experiences.md)**: coined the term and established the founding composite model of the phenomenon — a fifteen-element sequence later refined and quantified by Ring's sixteen-element "core NDE."
- **The life review**: identified the panoramic, non-linear re-experiencing of one's whole life, conducted in the presence of a being of light, as a recurring core structure — self-judgment through felt knowledge rather than external condemnation.
- Explicit methodological humility: repeatedly stressed the retrospective, self-selected, uncontrolled nature of his sample, and in *Reflections*' appendix laid out a detailed proposal for prospective, controlled research — anticipating the empirical work Ring, [Melvin Morse](melvin-morse.md), and later researchers carried out.
