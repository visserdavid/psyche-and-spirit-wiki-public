# Robert Bly

**Summary**: American poet and men's-movement leader (1926–2021), National Book Award winner, whose *A Little Book on the Human Shadow* (1988) popularised Jung's shadow concept as the "shadow bag" everyone drags through life — the populist complement to Jung's clinical shadow that Joan Borysenko draws on for her account of masks and disowned material in inner-child work.

## Biography

Robert Bly (1926–2021) was an American poet, essayist, and activist who won the 1968 National Book Award for Poetry (*The Light Around the Body*) before becoming, from the mid-1980s onward, the most visible figure of the mythopoetic men's movement. His best-known prose work, *Iron John: A Book About Men* (1990), spent over a year on the *New York Times* bestseller list and used a Grimm fairy tale, read through a Jungian lens, to explore contemporary masculine psychology. Two years earlier, *A Little Book on the Human Shadow* (1988) mixed essay and verse to popularise Carl Jung's concept of the [shadow](../concepts/shadow.md) for a general readership.

## Key Contribution: The Shadow Bag

Bly's central image — the **shadow bag** — describes the sack everyone drags through life containing everything shamed into hiding: anger (typically bagged in boys), sexuality (typically bagged in girls), and whatever else family or culture declares unacceptable, including creativity, need, spontaneity, courage, and grief. Bly's own formulation: "We spend the first twenty years of our lives deciding what to put in the bag, and the rest of our lives trying to get it back out." What remains bagged does not disappear; it "composts," growing more pressurised and autonomous until it erupts as accidents, illness, sudden rage, or self-defeating behaviour — the bagged material making its way out by force rather than by integration.

## Use in the Wiki

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md), in *[Guilt Is the Teacher, Love Is the Lesson](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md)*, presents Bly's shadow bag as the populist complement to [Carl Jung](carl-jung.md)'s clinical [shadow](../concepts/shadow.md) concept, using it alongside Eric Berne's transactional-analysis "masks" and Roberto Assagioli's psychosynthesis "subpersonalities" to describe how children develop protective strategies against shame that later preclude genuine intimacy and drive projection onto others. See [Inner Child](../concepts/inner-child.md) for the fuller synthesis.

## Critique

Bly's shadow bag is a popularisation rather than a clinical or empirical construct — a vivid and widely resonant metaphor for Jung's shadow rather than an independently operationalised psychological category. The mythopoetic men's movement Bly led was itself controversial, criticised by some feminist and academic commentators for essentialising gender difference (e.g., the specific claim that anger is characteristically "bagged" in boys and sexuality in girls) even as it offered many men a first accessible language for emotional and psychological work.
