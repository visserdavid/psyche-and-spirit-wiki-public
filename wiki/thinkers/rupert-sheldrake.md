# Rupert Sheldrake

**Summary**: English biologist (b. 1942) who proposed the hypothesis of morphic resonance in *A New Science of Life* (1981) — that the form and behaviour of systems from crystals to organisms are shaped by non-local "morphogenetic fields," reinforced across time by resonance with all previous similar systems — adopted by Kenneth Ring as the proposed mechanism by which individual near-death transformation could propagate across the whole human species, and cited by Barbara Ann Brennan and Caroline Myss as a scientific-sounding parallel to their own field-based frameworks.

## Biography and the Morphic Resonance Hypothesis

Rupert Sheldrake (b. 1942) studied natural sciences at Cambridge and philosophy at Harvard (as a Frank Knox Fellow) before returning to Cambridge for a PhD in biochemistry and becoming a Fellow of Clare College and a Rosenheim Research Fellow of the Royal Society. In the early 1970s, rather than pursue a conventional academic career studying isolated plant cells in the laboratory, he moved to the International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics in Hyderabad, India, to study whole plants growing in the field — a move he later described as the beginning of his dissatisfaction with mechanistic biology's capacity to explain living form.

*A New Science of Life: The Hypothesis of Morphic Resonance* (1981), largely drafted during an eighteen-month stay at the Shantivanam Ashram in Tamil Nadu, proposes that biological form and behaviour are organised not solely by genes and known physical laws but by [morphic resonance](../concepts/morphic-resonance.md): systems are shaped by a cumulative, non-energetic resonance with all previous relevantly similar systems, unattenuated by distance or the passage of time. His central claim is that when members of a species (or any self-organising system) learn or develop something new, the field associated with that form or behaviour is progressively strengthened, making the same form or behaviour easier for other members of the species to adopt — even without direct contact or communication.

The most-cited supporting evidence is [William McDougall's decades-long Harvard rat-learning experiments](../concepts/morphic-resonance.md#the-mcdougall-rat-experiments), in which a control line of untrained rats showed the same generation-on-generation improvement in maze-learning as the deliberately trained line — a result two later replications (Crew, Agar) partially confirmed and partially complicated. The popular "hundredth monkey effect" illustrates the same claimed principle informally, though the underlying primatology has since been shown not to support it.

## Use in the Wiki: The Omega Hypothesis

[Kenneth Ring](kenneth-ring.md), in *[Heading Toward Omega](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md)*, adopts morphic resonance — via Peter Russell's application of it to human consciousness in *The Global Brain* (1983) — as the propagation mechanism for his broader [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md): if millions of near-death-experience survivors have each accessed a state of universal love and expanded consciousness, morphic resonance would predict that this collective transformation strengthens a field of that consciousness, making it progressively easier for the rest of humanity to access the same state.

## Use in the Wiki: Energy Anatomy and Archetypes

[Barbara Ann Brennan](barbara-ann-brennan.md), in *[Hands of Light](../sources/hands-of-light.md)*, cites Sheldrake's morphogenetic fields as a parallel to the organising function of the [Human Energy Field](../concepts/seven-layer-auric-field.md) — both propose field-like structures that shape biological form beyond known material anatomy. [Caroline Myss](caroline-myss.md), in *[Archetypes: Who Are You?](../sources/archetypes-who-are-you.md)*, cites Sheldrake in her Visionary-archetype chapter as an example of a credentialled scientist whose ideas run ahead of what mainstream science is willing to accept.

## Critique

Morphic resonance is a genuinely fringe hypothesis within mainstream biology and physics. Its 1981 publication produced a notably public dispute — including *Nature* editor John Maddox's editorial calling the book "the best candidate for burning" in years — centred on whether the hypothesis is falsifiable in any practical sense, or whether any negative experimental result can always be explained away by an unmeasured field (see [Morphic Resonance § Reception and Critique](../concepts/morphic-resonance.md#reception-and-critique) for the full exchange, including physicist Brian Josephson's defence and developmental biologist Lewis Wolpert's rebuttal). No mechanism by which morphic fields could encode or transmit information across space and time has been reconciled with known physics, and Sheldrake's supporting evidence — including the McDougall rat experiments and the "hundredth monkey" story — is contested or, in the monkey case, has been shown to be apocryphal. Sheldrake's proposals have not been replicated to the satisfaction of the mainstream scientific community and are generally classified as pseudoscience by mainstream biologists, though Sheldrake himself has continued an active, self-funded programme of research and public advocacy. Ring's extension of morphic resonance to human consciousness evolution is, by Ring's own acknowledgment, speculative extrapolation beyond what Sheldrake's original hypothesis (itself unestablished) claims — a layering of one unproven hypothesis atop another, which the wiki's [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md) page flags explicitly as the weakest link in Ring's synthesis.
