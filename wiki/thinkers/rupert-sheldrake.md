# Rupert Sheldrake

**Summary**: English biologist (b. 1942) who proposed the hypothesis of morphic resonance in *A New Science of Life* (1981) — that self-organising systems, including living species, are shaped by non-local "morphic fields" that carry a kind of inherited memory of form and behaviour — adopted by Kenneth Ring as the proposed mechanism by which individual near-death transformation could propagate across the whole human species.

## Biography and the Morphic Resonance Hypothesis

Rupert Sheldrake (b. 1942) trained as a biochemist and cell biologist at Cambridge before proposing, in *A New Science of Life: The Hypothesis of Morphic Resonance* (1981), that biological form and behaviour are organised not solely by genes and known physical laws but by "morphic fields" — organising fields that transcend ordinary space and time. Sheldrake's central claim is that when members of a species (or any self-organising system) learn or develop something new, the morphic field associated with that behaviour is strengthened, making the same behaviour or form progressively easier for other members of the species to adopt — even without direct contact or communication between them.

The most-cited supporting evidence Sheldrake offers is William McDougall's Harvard experiment, in which successive generations of rats learned a water maze faster over twenty generations, with the improvement eventually appearing in unrelated rat populations tested for the first time; the popular "hundredth monkey effect" illustrates the same claimed principle informally.

## Use in the Wiki: The Omega Hypothesis

[Kenneth Ring](kenneth-ring.md), in *[Heading Toward Omega](../sources/heading-toward-omega.md)*, adopts morphic resonance as the propagation mechanism for his broader [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md): if millions of near-death-experience survivors have each accessed a state of universal love and expanded consciousness, morphic resonance would predict that this collective transformation strengthens a field of that consciousness, making it progressively easier for the rest of humanity to access the same state — whether through NDEs, meditation, or other transformative events. Ring draws an explicit parallel to Peter Russell's application of the same hypothesis to human consciousness generally in *The Global Brain* (1983).

## Critique

Morphic resonance is a genuinely fringe hypothesis within mainstream biology: no mechanism by which morphic fields could encode or transmit information across space and time has been reconciled with known physics, and Sheldrake's supporting evidence (including the McDougall rat experiments and the "hundredth monkey" story) is contested or, in the monkey case, widely regarded as apocryphal. Sheldrake's proposals have not been replicated to the satisfaction of the mainstream scientific community and are generally classified as pseudoscience by mainstream biologists, though Sheldrake himself continues an active program of research and public advocacy. Ring's extension of morphic resonance to human consciousness evolution is, by Ring's own acknowledgment, speculative extrapolation beyond what Sheldrake's original hypothesis (itself unestablished) claims — a layering of one unproven hypothesis atop another, which the wiki's [Omega Hypothesis](../concepts/omega-hypothesis.md) page flags explicitly as the weakest link in Ring's synthesis.
