# Sogyal Rinpoche

**Summary**: Tibetan Buddhist master (born c. 1947 in Kham, Tibet) whose 1992 *The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying* became the most widely read presentation of Tibetan Buddhist death teachings in the West; founder of the Rigpa organisation; student of Jamyang Khyentse Chokyi Lodro, Dudjom Rinpoche, and Dilgo Khyentse Rinpoche.

## Biography

Sogyal Rinpoche was born c. 1947 as Sogyal Lakar in the Kham region of eastern Tibet. He was recognised as the reincarnation of Terton Sogyal (Lerab Lingpa, 1856–1926), the mystic teacher and terma revealer who served as personal teacher to the Thirteenth Dalai Lama.

He grew up in the household of his root teacher, Jamyang Khyentse Chokyi Lodro (1893–1959), whom he describes in *The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying* as the towering master of his childhood — an encyclopedic authority across all schools of Tibetan Buddhism, recognised as the emanation of multiple previous masters. Jamyang Khyentse's influence shaped Sogyal's entire understanding of what enlightened presence looks like.

After the fall of Tibet in 1959, Sogyal went into exile. He later studied at Cambridge University and began teaching in the West in the 1970s. He founded Rigpa, an international network of Buddhist meditation centres based in the Nyingma tradition, with its European headquarters at Lerab Ling in southern France.

*The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying* was published in 1992 and became an international bestseller, translated into dozens of languages. A revised and updated edition was published in 2002. The book was adopted in hospices, medical schools, and universities across many countries.

In 2017, Sogyal Rinpoche faced multiple credible allegations of sexual, physical, and emotional abuse of students, spanning decades. He resigned from Rigpa in 2017. The allegations were independently investigated and found credible. This history requires acknowledgment alongside any engagement with his teachings.

## Core Teachings

### The Nature of Mind (Rigpa)

Sogyal's central teaching — drawn from the Dzogchen tradition of the Nyingmapa school — is the recognition of [Rigpa](../concepts/rigpa-nature-of-mind.md): the fundamental awareness that underlies all thought and experience. The sky-and-clouds metaphor: thoughts and emotions are the clouds; Rigpa is the sky itself, which is never touched by them. All Buddhist practice, in this framework, is the gradual recognition and stabilisation of this nature.

### The Four Bardos

The bardo (*bar do*, intermediate state) framework divides existence into four transitional phases: the [bardo of life](../concepts/bardos.md), bardo of dying, bardo of dharmata (intrinsic radiance), and bardo of becoming (approaching rebirth). Each bardo presents opportunities for liberation that can be prepared for during life through meditation practice.

### Ground Luminosity

At the moment of death, the [Ground Luminosity](../concepts/ground-luminosity.md) — the Clear Light of the fundamental nature of mind — dawns when all veils dissolve. The practitioner who has recognised Rigpa during life will recognise this light as their own nature and be liberated. The teaching is the motivation for all meditation practice: to practise recognition now, so that recognition is possible at the most critical moment.

### Caring for the Dying

Sogyal developed extensive practical guidance for accompanying dying people — addressing fear, helping clear unfinished business, creating a sacred environment, and offering phowa practice (consciousness transference) at the moment of death. This guidance was widely adopted in Western hospice and palliative care contexts in the 1990s and 2000s.

## Key Teachers

- **Jamyang Khyentse Chokyi Lodro** (1893–1959): Sogyal's root teacher; the central figure of his childhood; one of the great masters of twentieth-century Tibetan Buddhism
- **Dudjom Rinpoche** (1904–1987): meditation master, Nyingma lineage holder; Sogyal's principal teacher after leaving Tibet
- **Dilgo Khyentse Rinpoche** (1910–1991): prolific teacher, poet, and Nyingma master; one of Sogyal's closest guides
- **Nyoshul Khen Rinpoche** (1932–1999): Dzogchen master whose poem *Autobiography in Five Chapters* appears in the book

## Connections to Other Wiki Thinkers

**[Kenneth Ring](kenneth-ring.md)** and **[Elisabeth Kübler-Ross](elisabeth-kubler-ross.md)** are explicitly cited as Western pioneers whose NDE research and death studies Sogyal regards as corroboration of bardo teachings. Sogyal dedicates a full chapter (Chapter 20) to mapping NDE elements onto the bardos.

**[Willis Harman](willis-harman.md)**: Harman's M-3 metaphysics (consciousness primary) and Sogyal's bardo teachings are parallel frameworks: both argue that consciousness is the fundamental reality and that the physical world is a secondary condensation.

**[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)**: Borysenko's [centering prayer](../concepts/centering-prayer.md) and the [crisis-as-initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) framework parallel Sogyal's teaching that meditation trains the mind for the "crisis" of dying.

**[Helen Schucman](helen-schucman.md)**: Both Sogyal and Schucman transmitted material described as coming from a source beyond the ordinary ego — Schucman through inner dictation, Sogyal through the Tibetan lineage of transmission. The [Ground Luminosity](../concepts/ground-luminosity.md) parallels ACIM's [Real World](../concepts/real-world-acim.md) as the mind's unveiled natural state.
