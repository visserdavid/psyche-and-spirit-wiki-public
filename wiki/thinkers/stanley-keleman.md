# Stanley Keleman

**Summary**: American somatic psychologist and founder of Formative Psychology, whose work on the body's pulsatory structure and emotional shaping provides a secular, developmental parallel to the energy medicine frameworks of Myss and Shealy.

## Overview

Stanley Keleman (born 1931) is an American somatic psychologist, educator, and author based in Berkeley, California, where he directs the Center for Energetic Studies. He has spent over thirty years developing what he calls **Formative Psychology** — an approach to human experience grounded in the body's biological processes of shaping, organization, and pulsation.

Keleman's work spans the body-centered psychotherapy tradition that includes [Wilhelm Reich](wilhelm-reich.md) and [Alexander Lowen](alexander-lowen.md), but his emphasis is on biological formation rather than character analysis or catharsis — where Reich's *[Character Analysis](../sources/character-analysis.md)* derives [somatic patterns](../concepts/character-armor.md) from psychoanalytic drive theory and infantile conflict, Keleman derives his [somatic distress patterns](../concepts/somatic-distress-patterns.md) from embryology and pulsatory biology, treating them as biographical solutions rather than defenses against repressed content. His primary focus is on how life makes shapes — how the body's form is continuously organized and reorganized by developmental history, stress responses, relational experience, and the fundamental biological rhythm of pulsation.

## Embodying Experience (1987): The Methodology

*Embodying Experience* is the companion to *Emotional Anatomy* — where EA establishes the theory, EE presents the practice. Its core contribution is the **[HOw Process](../concepts/how-process.md)**: a five-step methodology for engaging somatic patterns directly.

The five steps:
1. What is my somatic image/situation? (recognition)
2. How do I organize/maintain it? (intensification)
3. How do I stop doing this? (incremental disorganization — the accordion step)
4. What happens when I stop? (incubation — the somatic pause)
5. What do I do with what I have learned? (forming a new response, practice)

The methodology is built on two key insights: first, that insight alone does not change somatic patterns — one must work at the muscular level; second, that **inhibition is a core competency**, not a problem. The brain's most sophisticated function is *stopping* — delaying, redirecting, and differentiating impulses. The HOw process is an education in controlled stopping.

EE also introduces **[somagrams](../concepts/somagrams.md)** — drawings of one's somatic image, representing the felt interior of one's bodily organization. Used alongside the five steps, somagrams make visible the somatic story that verbal language obscures.

The book includes ten detailed case studies (Morris, Jim, Sarah, Joel, Louise, Betty, Angela, Ellen, Larry, Mary) demonstrating the HOw process applied to conflict, relational patterns, gender identity, and somatic distress patterns.

## Core Contributions

### The Pulsating Body

Keleman's foundational insight is that the body is not a machine or a container but a living, pulsating process. Beginning from embryology, he traces how all life organizes itself as tubes, layers, and pouches — and how the rhythmic [pulsation](../concepts/pulsation.md) of cells and tissues is the ground of feeling, self-identity, and consciousness.

Emotion is not a mental event that affects the body secondarily; it is a somatic event — a change in the quality of pulsatory movement that then generates the images, thoughts, and experiences we recognize as feeling.

### Life Makes Shapes

*Emotional Anatomy* (1985) introduces the principle that biographical experience is structurally encoded in the body's form. The shapes a person's body assumes — posture, muscular tone, facial expression, organ function — are the cumulative record of their developmental history, the insults they have sustained, and the protective strategies they have adopted.

This is Keleman's version of what Myss calls ["biography is biology"](../concepts/biography-is-biology.md) — but grounded in observable somatic anatomy rather than subtle energy. The body's shape tells the story of a life.

### The Four Distress Patterns

Keleman identifies four characteristic patterns in which chronic stress distorts the body's pulsatory organization. See [Somatic Distress Patterns](../concepts/somatic-distress-patterns.md) for the full analysis. Each pattern — rigid, dense/compact, swollen, collapsed — represents a biographical solution to a particular kind of relational or developmental stress, not a disease category.

### Formative Psychology

Keleman's clinical approach, which he calls [Formative Psychology](../concepts/formative-psychology.md), works by reorganizing pulsatory patterns rather than analysing the past or imposing an ideal form. The aim is to restore the range of expansion and contraction — the aliveness — that chronic stress has narrowed or extinguished.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers in the Wiki

Keleman's "anatomy is destiny" / "life makes shapes" is the most direct somatic parallel in the wiki to Myss's "biography is biology." Both locate the origin of illness and distress in biographical encoding — but their frameworks are almost entirely non-overlapping:

| Aspect | Keleman | Myss/Shealy |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Medium of encoding | Physical tissue, pulsation, structure | Energy field, chakras |
| Diagnostic tool | Somatic observation, anatomy | Medical intuition, energy reading |
| Healing modality | Somatic reorganization, formative work | Energy medicine, spiritual practice |
| Frame | Secular, developmental, phenomenological | Spiritual, archetypal, energy-based |

The frameworks are complementary: together they describe the same biographical-biological link from two radically different angles.

## Critique

Keleman's work is not empirically validated in the conventional sense — his typology of somatic distress patterns (rigid, swollen, collapsed) is clinical and observational, not derived from controlled studies. The tradition he works in (body-centered psychotherapy) remains marginal in mainstream psychology, which is dominated by cognitive-behavioral and pharmacological approaches. The claim that body structure directly reflects psychological history — while intuitive to somatic therapists — has not been established through outcome research. Keleman makes no supernatural claims, which means his work is in principle testable, but it has not yet attracted the systematic research attention that would confirm or refute its central propositions.
