# Thomas Keating

**Summary**: American Trappist monk, abbot, and teacher (1923–2018); one of the founders of the Centering Prayer movement; author of *Open Mind, Open Heart* (1986); Borysenko's primary guide to contemplative prayer in *Fire in the Soul* — the person who systematised the Christian mystical tradition's direct encounter with divine presence into a form accessible to ordinary people outside the monastic context.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Biography

Thomas Keating was born on March 7, 1923, in New York City. He entered the Cistercian (Trappist) order in 1944 and was ordained a priest in 1949. He became abbot of St. Joseph's Abbey in Spencer, Massachusetts, in 1961, a position he held until 1981, when he moved to St. Benedict's Monastery in Snowmass, Colorado, where he resided until his death.

During his years at Spencer, Keating initiated an unprecedented series of meetings between the abbot and spiritual teachers from Buddhist, Hindu, and Jewish traditions — an early and influential step in the contemporary contemplative dialogue across traditions. This encounter deepened his conviction that contemplative experience underlies and unites the world's major spiritual traditions, and shaped his approach to making Christian contemplative prayer accessible to non-specialists.

In the 1970s Keating co-founded the Centering Prayer movement with Fathers William Meninger and Basil Pennington. Drawing on the 14th-century anonymous English mystical treatise *The Cloud of Unknowing*, Keating systematised a simple practice for entering the prayer of silence that had been the living heart of Christian monasticism but was largely inaccessible to laypeople. His principal text, *Open Mind, Open Heart: The Contemplative Dimension of the Gospel*, was published in 1986.

Keating taught internationally until his final years and was instrumental in founding Contemplative Outreach, an organisation that has introduced centering prayer to hundreds of thousands of people worldwide. He died on October 25, 2018, at Snowmass.

## The Centering Prayer Method

Keating's method is described in detail at [Centering Prayer](../concepts/centering-prayer.md). In essence:

A sacred word is chosen — any word that the meditator associates with their intention to rest in God's presence. The word is not repeated as a mantra but used as a "pointer": when thoughts arise and claim attention, the word is gently reintroduced as a reminder of the meditator's intent, and then released as silence returns.

The central metaphor: consciousness is a river; thoughts are boats floating on the river. Ordinary thinking means having one's attention entirely on the boats (thoughts, emotions, plans, images). Centering prayer trains the practitioner to identify with the river — the consciousness itself — rather than with any particular content. The boats are not suppressed; they are allowed to float past.

The practice does not *create* the divine presence; it removes the obstacles to noticing it. For Keating, the divine presence is always already present; the noise of ordinary thinking conceals rather than creates the distance. "Resist no thought, hang on to no thought, react to no thought."

## Theological Framework: Open Mind, Open Heart

Keating's theology is rooted in the Christian mystical tradition — the apophatic (or negative) theology that recognises God as beyond all concepts, images, and theological categories. This tradition is represented by:

- The desert fathers and mothers (4th–5th century Egypt and Syria)
- The anonymous author of *The Cloud of Unknowing* (14th century England)
- Meister Eckhart (13th–14th century Germany)
- John of the Cross (16th century Spain) — whose writing Borysenko also draws on
- Teresa of Ávila (16th century Spain)

The common thread: the divine cannot be grasped conceptually; it can only be encountered experientially, in the silence beyond thought. All concepts, theological formulations, even interior experiences (visions, consolations, feelings of divine presence) are, in this tradition, recognised as secondary — the gift of the divine, but not the divine itself. The practitioner's role is to release even the desire for spiritual experience, resting in pure intention and availability.

The phrase "open mind, open heart" captures the two movements:
- **Open mind**: releasing the grip on concepts, theologies, expectations, and the interpretive overlay that the ego brings to experience. Not emptiness as nihilism, but availability as receptivity
- **Open heart**: surrender to the divine will — what the tradition calls "consent" to God's presence and action. The practitioner does not demand or produce the encounter; they make themselves available

## The Crisis of Contemplative Prayer in Western Christianity

Keating's project arose from a historical observation: the contemplative dimension of Christianity — the direct experiential encounter with God beyond words and doctrines — had been largely available only to monastics for the past several centuries. The Reformation's rejection of monastic structures, the Counter-Reformation's emphasis on institutional devotion, and the modern period's increasing psychological self-consciousness had left ordinary Christians without access to the contemplative practices that were the living core of the tradition before these divisions.

The results, in Keating's diagnosis: Christianity increasingly offered two modes — institutional religiosity (services, sacraments, doctrine) without experiential depth, or emotional revivalism (charismatic experience) without contemplative grounding. Neither produces what the mystical tradition promised: the gradual transformation of the person through sustained encounter with divine love.

Keating's Centering Prayer movement addresses this gap — not by adding something new but by recovering something very old and making it teachable outside the monastery.

## Relationship to Other Contemplative Traditions

Keating's ecumenical engagement and his conviction that contemplative experience underlies the world's traditions position him in relationship to:

**Buddhism**: The river-of-consciousness metaphor and the instruction to "resist no thought, hang on to no thought" are structurally parallel to Buddhist mindfulness practice — though the intent differs (mindfulness: awareness of what is; centering prayer: rest in divine presence). Keating explicitly acknowledges the parallel and sees it as evidence that the contemplative dimension accesses a level of reality that is not denominationally bounded.

**Hesychasm** (Eastern Orthodox): The hesychast tradition — centred on the Jesus Prayer and the cultivation of inner stillness (*hesychia*) — is centering prayer's closest Eastern Christian analogue. Keating was in dialogue with the Eastern Orthodox contemplative tradition throughout his ministry.

**Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah)**: The *devekut* ("cleaving to God") practice of the Hasidic tradition, particularly as expressed in the teaching of Rabbi Pinchas Koretz (whom Borysenko cites: "Prayer is not addressed *to* God — prayer *is* God"), is a direct parallel to the intent of centering prayer.

## Relationship to Borysenko's Work

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) credits Thomas Keating's *Open Mind, Open Heart* as the text that brought her contemplative practice to its deepest level after years of mantra-based meditation and Buddhist mindfulness. She describes settling into centering prayer after encountering it "for its clarity, simplicity, and effectiveness."

For Borysenko, centering prayer occupies a specific place in her broader framework:
- The [relaxation response](../concepts/relaxation-response.md) (Benson) established the physiological baseline: a nervous system calm enough for sustained quiet attention
- [Mindfulness](../concepts/mindfulness.md) (Kabat-Zinn, Thich Nhat Hanh) developed the Witness stance: the capacity to observe thought without identification
- Centering prayer opens the level below thought: the silence in which, for Borysenko, the divine is encountered not as concept or experience but as presence

Her Chapter Nine in *Fire in the Soul* teaches centering prayer as a complete practice, including setting, letting-go breath, the connecting memory, the sacred word, and the daily schedule — making Keating's monastic practice available to the general reader.

## Critique

**Protestant critique**: Some Protestant theologians view centering prayer's Buddhist-adjacent technique and its emphasis on direct experience without doctrinal mediation as a deviation from the Reformation's emphasis on Scripture-centred faith. Keating's response: the tradition he is recovering is older than the Reformation and is present throughout the New Testament and patristic literature.

**Syncretism**: Critics note that Keating's dialogue with Buddhist and Hindu teachers, and his willingness to find common ground across traditions, can shade into an implicit equivalence that erases real theological differences. Keating himself maintained that the traditions are genuinely different in their doctrinal content while sharing the contemplative core.

**Passive prayer**: The instruction to "resist no thought" and to make oneself "available" rather than actively seeking God can be misunderstood as quietism — the 17th-century heresy condemned by the Church for suggesting that spiritual passivity is sufficient and active virtue unnecessary. Keating carefully distinguishes active from passive contemplation and emphasises that centering prayer supports, rather than replaces, active love of neighbour.
