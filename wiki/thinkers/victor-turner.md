# Victor Turner

**Summary**: British cultural anthropologist (1920–1983), known for fieldwork among the Ndembu of Zambia and for his theory of ritual as a three-phase rite of passage (separation, liminality, reincorporation) culminating in *The Ritual Process* (1969) — the foundational text for liminality and communitas, and the anthropological framework Joan Borysenko adopts as the structural backbone of her "crisis as initiation" reframe of personal catastrophe.

## Biography

Victor Witter Turner (1920–1983) conducted extensive fieldwork among the Ndembu people of what was then Northern Rhodesia (now Zambia), documenting their rituals of initiation, healing, and sacrifice over months of residence in Ndembu communities. Building on Arnold van Gennep's earlier model of rites of passage, Turner developed his most influential concepts — **liminality** and **communitas** — in *The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure* (1969), work that has since influenced fields well beyond anthropology, including performance studies, theology, and education.

## Key Contribution: The Three-Phase Rite of Passage

Building on [Arnold van Gennep](arnold-van-gennep.md)'s 1909 schema, Turner's model divides the rite of passage into three phases:

1. **Separation**: the initiate is stripped of their previous role, identity, and social position.
2. **Liminality**: the threshold state (from the Ndembu *mwadi*, "one who is between") — a formless, boundary-crossing condition in which ordinary social categories do not apply.
3. **Reincorporation**: return to society bearing a new identity, new knowledge, and new authority earned by having survived the liminal threshold.

Turner's key observation — central to how [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) uses him — is that the liminal state's stripping-away of accustomed roles and possessions is not punishment but the necessary precondition for the larger identity that follows. In *[The Ritual Process](../sources/the-ritual-process.md)* (1969), Turner elaborates this into the full theory of [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md) — including his famous table of oppositions between liminal and structured status, and his analysis of the Ndembu Kanongesha chieftainship installation, in which the chief-elect is ritually stripped, humiliated, and reviled before enthronement — and its dialectical partner, [Communitas](../concepts/communitas.md), the egalitarian bond liminality generates, which he traces historically through the Franciscan poverty movement and the Bengali Sahajiya-Vaishnava devotional movement.

## Use in the Wiki: Crisis as Initiation

Borysenko, in *[Fire in the Soul](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)*, adopts Turner's three-phase structure as the anthropological backbone of her central reframe: that personal catastrophe (illness, addiction, bankruptcy, trauma) is not pathology to be fixed but a rite of passage to be undergone. She uses Turner to argue that contemporary psychiatric and medical culture, lacking any equivalent to the liminal category, tends to work against the transformative process — treating the person in crisis as simply "disordered" rather than holding space for the emergence of a new identity. See [Crisis as Initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) for the full synthesis, including its integration with Joseph Campbell's night-sea journey, Viktor Frankl's logotherapy, and Stanislav Grof's perinatal-stage research.

## Critique

Turner's anthropological model itself is mainstream and well-regarded within cultural anthropology, though it has been critiqued (within the discipline, and by Turner himself in his own 1977 preface) for over-generalising a structure derived from a particular set of traditional-society rituals to all rites of passage cross-culturally, and for idealizing communitas as pure egalitarian fellowship when real liminal periods can also produce coercion and scapegoating (see [Liminality](../concepts/liminality.md#critique) and [Communitas](../concepts/communitas.md#critique) for the full discussion). The wiki's own critique concerns Borysenko's extension rather than Turner's original work: contemporary Western individuals undergoing personal crisis typically lack the communal elders, ritual container, and cultural sanction that made rites of passage transformative (rather than simply chaotic and unsupported) in the societies Turner studied. The initiation frame, applied without that missing social structure, may work best when — as Borysenko acknowledges — some equivalent of "the tribe" shows up to hold the space.
