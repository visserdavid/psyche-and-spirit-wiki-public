# Victor Turner

**Summary**: British cultural anthropologist (1920–1983), known for fieldwork among the Ndembu of Zambia and for his theory of ritual as a three-phase rite of passage (separation, liminality, reincorporation) — the anthropological framework Joan Borysenko adopts as the structural backbone of her "crisis as initiation" reframe of personal catastrophe.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

## Biography

Victor Witter Turner (1920–1983) conducted extensive fieldwork among the Ndembu people of what was then Northern Rhodesia (now Zambia), documenting their rituals of initiation, healing, and sacrifice over months of residence in Ndembu communities. Building on Arnold van Gennep's earlier model of rites of passage, Turner developed his most influential concepts — **liminality** and **communitas** — in *The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure* (1969), work that has since influenced fields well beyond anthropology, including performance studies, theology, and education.

## Key Contribution: The Three-Phase Rite of Passage

Turner's model divides the rite of passage into three phases:

1. **Separation**: the initiate is stripped of their previous role, identity, and social position.
2. **Liminality**: the threshold state (from the Ndembu *mwadi*, "one who is between") — a formless, boundary-crossing condition in which ordinary social categories do not apply.
3. **Reincorporation**: return to society bearing a new identity, new knowledge, and new authority earned by having survived the liminal threshold.

Turner's key observation — central to how [Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) uses him — is that the liminal state's stripping-away of accustomed roles and possessions is not punishment but the necessary precondition for the larger identity that follows.

## Use in the Wiki: Crisis as Initiation

Borysenko, in *[Fire in the Soul](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)*, adopts Turner's three-phase structure as the anthropological backbone of her central reframe: that personal catastrophe (illness, addiction, bankruptcy, trauma) is not pathology to be fixed but a rite of passage to be undergone. She uses Turner to argue that contemporary psychiatric and medical culture, lacking any equivalent to the liminal category, tends to work against the transformative process — treating the person in crisis as simply "disordered" rather than holding space for the emergence of a new identity. See [Crisis as Initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) for the full synthesis, including its integration with Joseph Campbell's night-sea journey, Viktor Frankl's logotherapy, and Stanislav Grof's perinatal-stage research.

## Critique

Turner's anthropological model itself is mainstream and well-regarded within cultural anthropology, though it has been critiqued (within the discipline) for over-generalising a structure derived from a particular set of traditional-society rituals to all rites of passage cross-culturally. The wiki's own critique concerns Borysenko's extension rather than Turner's original work: contemporary Western individuals undergoing personal crisis typically lack the communal elders, ritual container, and cultural sanction that made rites of passage transformative (rather than simply chaotic and unsupported) in the societies Turner studied. The initiation frame, applied without that missing social structure, may work best when — as Borysenko acknowledges — some equivalent of "the tribe" shows up to hold the space.
