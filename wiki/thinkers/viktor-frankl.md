# Viktor Frankl

**Summary**: Austrian psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor (1905–1997); founder of logotherapy, the psychotherapy centred on the human search for meaning; author of *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946); Borysenko invokes Frankl as the definitive demonstration that finding meaning in suffering is the pivotal variable between survival and destruction — and that this capacity is available even under conditions of the most extreme horror.

**Sources**:
- [Borysenko, J. (1993). *Fire in the soul: A new psychology of spiritual optimism*. Warner Books.](../sources/fire-in-the-soul.md)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-24

## Biography

Viktor Emil Frankl was born in Vienna, Austria, on March 26, 1905. He showed exceptional intellectual gifts early, corresponding with Freud as a teenager and publishing his first paper in a psychoanalytic journal at eighteen. He trained in medicine and psychiatry at the University of Vienna, where he worked under Alfred Adler before developing his own framework.

In the 1930s Frankl developed the concept of logotherapy — a psychotherapy based on the premise that the fundamental human drive is not pleasure (Freud's Eros) or power (Adler's will to power) but *meaning*. He established suicide prevention centres in Vienna and worked extensively with adolescents.

In September 1942 Frankl, his wife, his parents, and his brother were arrested and deported to concentration camps. He spent approximately three years as a prisoner in four camps, including Auschwitz and Dachau. His parents, brother, and wife died in the camps. He survived.

After liberation in 1945, Frankl dictated *...trotzdem Ja zum Leben sagen* ("Nevertheless, Say Yes to Life") in nine days. The book was published in German in 1946 and in English as *Man's Search for Meaning* in 1959. It has sold more than ten million copies in twenty-four languages and was listed by the Library of Congress as one of the ten most influential books in America.

Frankl was Professor of Neurology and Psychiatry at the University of Vienna and held visiting professorships at Harvard, Stanford, and Dallas. He died in Vienna on September 2, 1997.

## Logotherapy

Logotherapy (from Greek *logos*, meaning "word," "reason," or "meaning") is based on three foundational premises:

**1. Freedom of will**: Human beings have the capacity to choose their attitude toward any given set of conditions. This freedom is not unlimited — circumstances, biology, and history constrain it significantly — but it is never eliminated. The concentration camp demonstrated this most starkly: even the prisoner who could not avoid physical suffering retained the capacity to choose how they related to it.

**2. Will to meaning**: The primary motivational force in human beings is the search for meaning. When this drive is thwarted, the organism suffers a specific form of distress that Frankl calls the "existential vacuum" or "metapathology" — a pervasive sense of meaninglessness, boredom, and emptiness that is distinct from neurotic anxiety (generated by unresolved inner conflict) or physical suffering (pain). The existential vacuum underlies much contemporary addiction, depression, and aggression.

**3. Meaning of life**: Life has meaning under all circumstances, even the most miserable. Meaning can be found in three ways:
   - Creating a work or accomplishing a deed
   - Experiencing something (beauty, truth, love) or encountering someone (a loving relationship)
   - The attitude one takes toward unavoidable suffering

The third source of meaning is Frankl's most original contribution: *one can find meaning in suffering itself*, if one can understand it as a task — something to be endured with dignity, offered as a sacrifice, or converted into an act of witness. This is not masochism or resignation; it is the deepest available human response to conditions that cannot be changed.

## The Camp Observation

From direct observation in Auschwitz and Dachau, Frankl documented the variable that predicted survival: the presence of a *reason to live*. This was not correlated with physical health (strong men died faster than weak men if they lost their sense of purpose), social status, or psychological history.

Specific observations:
- Prisoners who gave up hope died within days, typically of a seemingly minor illness. The body seemed to follow the soul's surrender.
- The prisoner who had a manuscript to complete, a child to return to, or a task that only they could perform — or who found meaning in bearing witness to the suffering around them — maintained the will to survive under physically comparable conditions
- The loss of a "why" to live produced a characteristic syndrome: appetite loss, withdrawal, the giving away of possessions (which Frankl identified as the signal that a prisoner had decided to die)

The summary Frankl draws from Nietzsche: *"He who has a why to live can bear almost any how."*

## The Existential Vacuum and Contemporary Life

Frankl's post-war writing addresses what he saw as the growing prevalence of the existential vacuum in modern Western life. Having survived conditions designed to strip meaning away, he was struck by how many people in freedom and abundance voluntarily inhabited a similar emptiness.

The existential vacuum manifests as:
- Chronic boredom and the search for stimulation
- Conformism (doing what others do because there is no internal compass)
- Totalitarianism (doing what others *tell you to do* for the same reason)
- Sunday neurosis (depression that emerges when the week's activities stop and the emptiness is no longer masked by busyness)
- Addiction as meaning-substitute
- Aggression as the expression of frustrated meaning-seeking

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)'s patient Jack — a grandfather in his forties with everything a person might want and yet empty, not living, waking up without a reason to get up — is Frankl's existential vacuum in clinical form. Jack's depression was not neurotic (no identifiable anxiety or unresolved conflict) but existential: the meaning drive was frustrated. The treatment was not psychological repair but the reopening of meaning — meditation practice, Amnesty International volunteer work, contemplative community.

## Relationship to Other Thinkers

**[Abraham Maslow](abraham-maslow.md)**: Maslow's hierarchy of needs places "self-actualisation" at the top; his concept of "meta-needs" (the need for beauty, truth, justice, meaning) corresponds to Frankl's third source of meaning. Both identify a level of human striving that transcends pleasure and pain. Maslow's "metapathology" (the suffering caused by deprivation of meta-needs) is structurally parallel to Frankl's "existential vacuum."

**C. G. Jung**: Jung's concept of individuation — the lifelong process of becoming what one truly is — shares with logotherapy the premise that meaninglessness is a fundamental form of human suffering, not merely a symptom of other conditions. Jung said that "the central neurosis of our time is emptiness." Frankl's systematic clinical and empirical treatment of this insight complements Jung's analytical framework.

**[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)**: Borysenko uses Frankl as the primary evidence that the [crisis-as-initiation](../concepts/crisis-as-initiation.md) framework is not naive optimism but demonstrated clinical reality. Frankl did not survive Auschwitz because he had the right attitude toward something survivable; he survived the most extreme conditions of the 20th century because meaning proved more powerful than circumstance. This is Borysenko's fundamental argument for [spiritual optimism](../concepts/spiritual-optimism.md): not that bad things don't happen, but that the response to them determines whether they destroy or transform.

## Critique

**Survivor bias**: The observation that people with strong sense of meaning survived is vulnerable to selection effects — those who died could not report whether they had or lacked meaning. Frankl's data is clinical observation under impossible research conditions, not controlled study.

**Universalising from the extreme**: Auschwitz is the most extreme human experience Frankl could have witnessed. Translating the survival variable from that context to ordinary clinical practice requires care. "What gave meaning in Auschwitz will give meaning in daily life" may be true in principle but requires translation for the very different conditions of contemporary Western distress.

**Voluntary suffering**: Frankl's framework risks being used to suggest that people should find meaning in suffering they can and should resist or escape. The logotherapeutic insight is about *unavoidable* suffering; applied to avoidable harm (domestic violence, structural oppression, medical mistreatment), the meaning framework becomes dangerous if it substitutes for action.
