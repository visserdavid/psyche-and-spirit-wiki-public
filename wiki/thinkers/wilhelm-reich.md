# Wilhelm Reich

**Summary**: Austrian psychiatrist and psychoanalyst (1897–1957), originally part of Freud's inner circle, who argued that repressed emotion is held in the body as chronic muscular "armoring" and proposed orgone — a pulsating bioenergy governing the tension-charge-discharge-relaxation cycle — as its underlying physical substrate; his character-armoring framework is the direct ancestor of Lowen's bioenergetics, Pierrakos's Core Energetics, Brennan's auric character structures, and Keleman's somatic distress patterns.

## Biography

Wilhelm Reich (1897–1957) trained under Freud in Vienna and was for a time a leading figure in the psychoanalytic movement, known for his clinical rigor on the technique of character analysis. He broke from orthodox psychoanalysis in the 1930s, increasingly convinced that neurosis was not just a matter of ideas and memories but was physically locked into the body as chronic muscular tension — "character armor." His later work turned toward a controversial bioenergetic theory of a universal life-energy he called "orgone," and toward orgone-accumulator devices claimed to concentrate it; this phase brought him into conflict with the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, a court injunction, the burning of his books, and his death in federal prison in 1957. Mainstream psychiatry and science rejected this later work as unscientific.

## Key Contributions

### Character armor
Reich proposed that each characteristic way a person defends against overwhelming early experience becomes fixed not only psychologically but somatically — as a patterned rigidity in specific muscle groups and body regions ("armor"). Releasing this armor through direct bodywork, not only verbal analysis, became the basis of his therapy (vegetotherapy) and the entire body psychotherapy tradition that followed him. His 1945 text *Character Analysis* is the foundational document of this approach — see [Character Structures (Auric)](../concepts/character-structures-auric.md).

### Orgone energy and the pulsation cycle
Reich proposed that living organisms contain a specific biological energy — orgone — that flows in pulsating waves and governs a cycle of tension, charge, discharge, and relaxation. He held that the orgasm is the organism's fundamental discharge mechanism, releasing accumulated bioelectric charge; chronic muscular tension (armoring) was, in his framework, blocked or stagnant orgone. Brennan's [Mask Self, Lower Self, Higher Self](../concepts/mask-self-lower-self-higher-self.md) model borrows Reich's term "DOR" (deadly orgone energy) directly for the compressed, stagnant energy she associates with the Lower Self. See [Bioelectromagnetism](../concepts/bioelectromagnetism.md) for how his early bioelectric research on skin and muscle relates to later, more mainstream findings in that field.

### Precursor to bioenergetics and energy-field character typology
Reich's character types were elaborated by his student [Alexander Lowen](alexander-lowen.md) into bioenergetics, and by John Pierrakos (also a student of Reich, and Lowen's co-founder of the Bioenergetic Institute before founding Core Energetics) into an explicitly energy-field-based framework. Both lines feed directly into [Barbara Ann Brennan's](barbara-ann-brennan.md) five auric character structures and into [Stanley Keleman's](stanley-keleman.md) somatic distress patterns, making Reich the common root of most of this wiki's body-centered psychotherapy material.

## Critique

Reich's early clinical work on character armor and the body-mind connection is still cited with respect in body psychotherapy circles, and some of his early bioelectric research (on the electrical properties of skin and the piezoelectric effects of muscle tension) anticipated genuine later findings in bioelectromagnetism. However, his orgone theory itself — the existence of a measurable, universal bioenergy, and the orgone accumulator's claimed therapeutic and even meteorological effects — has never been validated by mainstream science and is generally regarded as a pseudoscientific turn in an otherwise serious career. Note: mainstream psychiatry and academic psychology do not recognize character armoring or orgone as diagnostic or physical constructs; the character-typology tradition survives today mainly within body psychotherapy modalities (bioenergetics, Core Energetics) rather than in clinical or research psychology.
