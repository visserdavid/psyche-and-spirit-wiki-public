# William James

**Summary**: American philosopher, psychologist, and physician (1842–1910); father of American psychology and pragmatism; author of *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902), which introduced the term "soul sickness" for guilt-driven psychological suffering and provided the first systematic empirical treatment of mystical experience — a foundational reference for Borysenko's psychospiritual framework.

## Biography

William James (1842–1910) was trained as a physician at Harvard Medical School (MD, 1869) but never practiced medicine. He joined the Harvard faculty in 1873, teaching physiology and then psychology and philosophy, becoming the first person to offer an experimental psychology course in the United States (1875). He founded the first American psychological laboratory and is widely regarded as the father of American psychology.

His major works:
- *The Principles of Psychology* (1890) — the foundational text of American psychology
- *The Will to Believe* (1897)
- *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) — the Gifford Lectures at Edinburgh
- *Pragmatism* (1907)
- *A Pluralistic Universe* (1909)

James suffered his own prolonged psychological crisis in his twenties (depression, insomnia, fear of insanity) and emerged from it through what he described as an act of will — a philosophical decision to act as if free will were real. This biographical experience shaped his lifelong interest in the psychology of religious conversion and the therapeutic power of changed belief.

## "Soul Sickness" and Its Cure

In *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (the 1901 Gifford Lectures), James examined hundreds of first-person accounts of religious and mystical experience, distinguishing between what he called "healthy-mindedness" (the natural capacity for positive engagement with life) and "sick-souledness" — a state of chronic inner division, self-criticism, anxiety, and the haunting sense of something fundamentally wrong.

James borrowed the term **"soul sickness"** from the New Thought movement (Phineas Parkhurst Quimby, Mary Baker Eddy's predecessors) to name this chronic condition of guilt and unworthiness. He documented accounts of its healing — through conversion, mystical experience, prayer, or philosophical shift — and argued that such healing constituted some of the most dramatic evidence of the mind's transformative power over its own states.

A letter James cited from a woman healed through New Thought principles:
> "Life seemed... one time — I had breaking attacks called nervous prostration, insomnia, verging on insanity, having stomach troubles... I sent home under the charge of doctors... I never recovered until New Thought took me in hand... I think the one thing impressed me most was learning... the fact of my relation... to touch the very word and essence of life that we call God. I live in a constant turning of consciousness toward God for illumination — as turning to the sun for light and warmth and invigoration."

James framed this as an account of the healing of soul sickness through conversion to a new orientation toward the universe — not through doctrine but through *direct experience* of connection to something larger.

[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md) explicitly credits James with naming the syndrome she is treating clinically in her guilt work. Where Sigmund Freud mapped the same territory in terms of neurosis and libido, James mapped it in terms of spiritual orientation and the capacity for religious experience — a frame that Borysenko finds more clinically useful for the patients she describes.

## The Varieties of Religious Experience

Now ingested directly as a primary source — see [*The Varieties of Religious Experience* (source)](../sources/the-varieties-of-religious-experience.md) for the full treatment. The Gifford Lectures were structured around James's characteristic pragmatism: not "is religion true?" but "what are religion's psychological effects?" James opens with a foundational methodological distinction between the **existential judgment** (what caused this state of mind?) and the **spiritual judgment** (what is it worth?), arguing the two are logically independent — a state's organic or pathological origin settles nothing about its value. He names the reductive conflation of the two "medical materialism": explaining away Saint Paul's conversion as an epileptic lesion, or George Fox's spiritual restlessness as a disordered colon.

Key findings and arguments:
- **Healthy-mindedness and the sick soul**: James's core typology of religious temperament — see [Healthy-Mindedness and the Sick Soul](../concepts/healthy-mindedness-and-sick-soul.md) for the full account of the once-born/twice-born distinction and conversion as the movement between them
- **Mysticism's four marks**: ineffability, noetic quality, transiency, and passivity — see [Mysticism's Four Marks](../concepts/mysticism-four-marks.md)
- **Saintliness**: the psychological fruits of the saintly character (charity, purity, asceticism, obedience) and their pathological excesses, evaluated by the same fruits criterion
- **Conversion as a real psychological event**: the subliminal/subconscious self (following F. W. H. Myers and Pierre Janet) as the intermediary through which transformative experience arrives; James takes religious experience seriously as data without committing to metaphysical conclusions about its source
- **The pragmatic test**: religious beliefs and practices that produce increased vitality, compassion, and psychological wellbeing are validated "by their fruits, not their roots," regardless of their metaphysical truth-status

James's concluding position: whatever the ultimate nature of the religious experiences he documented, they are *real effects* on real people. "The conscious person is continuous with a wider self through which saving experiences come." James remained agnostic about whether this "wider self" was the Jungian collective unconscious, God, or some other dimension of consciousness — but he was certain that the experiences were genuine and their healing effects documented.

## Relation to Other Thinkers in the Wiki

**[Carl Jung](carl-jung.md)**: James's analysis of the collective dimensions of religious experience directly influenced Jung's development of the collective unconscious. Jung read James carefully; James's "wider self" through which saving experiences come is a recognisable precursor to Jung's psychic totality — the [Self](../concepts/the-self.md) as the ground from which healing arises.

**[Joan Borysenko](joan-borysenko.md)**: Borysenko explicitly invokes James's term "soul sickness" as the name for the guilt-shame syndrome she is treating in [*Guilt Is the Teacher*](../sources/guilt-is-the-teacher.md). She also cites his argument (via Carl Jung's letter to Bill W.) that the deepest forms of soul sickness require a spiritual conversion — not merely cognitive or behavioural change. Her three-eras-of-treatment framework (psychological, spiritual, relational) echoes James's conclusion that the psychological dimension of healing requires spiritual resources that pure psychology cannot supply.

**[Larry Dossey](larry-dossey.md)**: Dossey's Era III medicine (non-local consciousness, prayer effects, shared mind) is a clinical development of James's philosophical position that consciousness is not reducible to individual brain function and that "saving experiences" come through a wider self.

## Critique

**The evidential standard**: James's method was to take first-person testimony seriously as data, subject only to internal consistency and pragmatic fruits. Contemporary psychology would require more rigorous experimental controls. James would likely acknowledge this limitation while arguing that the phenomena deserve investigation even under looser standards.

**The pragmatic test**: James's claim that beliefs are validated by their fruits is a philosophical position rather than an established empirical principle. A belief can produce short-term wellbeing and long-term harm (e.g., sects that produce peace and stability while maintaining harmful power structures). The pragmatic test requires specifying which fruits, over what time horizon, and for whom.

**Influence**: Despite these limitations, *The Varieties of Religious Experience* remains one of the most widely read and cited texts in the psychology of religion more than a century after its publication. Its basic framework — taking religious experience seriously as psychological data without requiring metaphysical commitments — continues to shape empirical research in positive psychology, transpersonal psychology, and the psychology of spirituality.
