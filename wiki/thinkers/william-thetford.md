# William Thetford

**Summary**: American psychologist (1923–1988) at Columbia University who served as typist, editor, and collaborative motivator during the seven-year scribing of *A Course in Miracles*; his declaration that "there must be another way" to resolve interpersonal conflict is identified in ACIM lore as the catalyst that initiated the entire project.

**Sources**:
- Schucman, H. (1976). *A course in miracles*. Foundation for Inner Peace.

**Last updated**: 2026-06-25

---

## Life and Career

William Thetford (1923–1988) was a clinical psychologist and director of clinical psychology at Columbia University's College of Physicians and Surgeons, where he was [Helen Schucman](helen-schucman.md)'s colleague and department head. He held a doctorate in psychology from the University of Chicago. In biographical accounts he is described as skilled, ambitious, and driven — and like Schucman, not oriented toward spiritual practice before the ACIM project began.

## Role in the Scribing

In 1965, following an extended period of professional conflict between himself and Schucman, Thetford made an unusual declaration — described by both as out of character — that "there must be another way" to relate to colleagues and to work. Schucman later described this statement as the event that "opened the door" to the scribing process that would begin shortly after.

When Schucman began receiving the inner dictation, Thetford became her collaborative partner. He typed out the shorthand she had scripted each session, organized the material, and provided the sustained institutional support that allowed the work to continue over seven years. He was also a careful editor who helped with chapter titles, headings, and early structuring decisions.

Unlike Schucman, Thetford embraced the material more fully in later life, eventually leaving Columbia to join the Foundation for Inner Peace in California, where he worked on distribution and study materials until his death in 1988.

## Significance

Thetford's contribution to ACIM is primarily practical: without his sustained commitment to the typing, organization, and dissemination of the material, the text might never have reached publication. His role illustrates the collaborative dimension of channeled or intuited works — the scriber and the typist together constitute the publishing channel.

His personal trajectory — from a psychologist who found the material "useful" in a human-relations sense to someone who gave the last decade of his life to its dissemination — contrasts markedly with Schucman's more ambivalent relationship to the text she produced. This contrast is itself of psychological interest: the person who typed and edited the material integrated it more readily than the person who received it.
